## Overview

Released on April 5, 2005 through Victory Records, *Alaska* is the Between the Buried and Me album that announced the band as a serious force in progressive metal. Clocking in at over 73 minutes and spanning death metal, jazz, folk, and prog rock within a single album — often within a single song — *Alaska* established the compositional template BTBAM would refine across every subsequent record. For Blake Richardson, it was the full-length statement of a drummer already capable of navigating music of staggering technical and emotional complexity.

Richardson's drumming on *Alaska* is remarkable for its musicality above its technique. The album opens with "All Bodies" — nearly seven minutes of shifting time signatures and dynamic swings — and never settles into a predictable groove. Throughout the record, Richardson deploys double-bass passages compositionally, shifts between blast beat sections and jazz-inflected interludes, and executes metric modulations with precision that never sounds labored.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** DW Collector's Series (early configuration), double 22" bass drums, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, 16" floor tom
- **Snare:** DW Collector's Series Maple 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Extra Dry series — 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 17" Thin Crash, 18" Medium Thin Crash, 20" Ride, 18" China
- **Pedals:** DW 9002 Double Bass Pedal (chain-drive, adjustable cam)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth American Classic 5B
- **Heads:** Evans G2 Coated (snare batter), Remo Emperor Coated / Ambassador Coated (toms), Remo Powerstroke 3 (kick)

### DW Collector's Series: The Early Configuration

For *Alaska*, Blake Richardson used the DW Collector's Series kit that would anchor his setup through the band's formative breakthrough period. DW's Collector's Series uses North American maple shell construction with precision-machined hardware — delivering consistent performance across the extreme demands of progressive metal touring and recording.

The double bass drum configuration on *Alaska* is deployed with Richardson's characteristic compositional intelligence. Unlike drummers who run uninterrupted double-bass ostinatos, Richardson varies kick density to follow the music's emotional arc: explosive double-bass bursts during the album's most aggressive passages, near-silence during the jazz and folk interludes. The three rack toms (10", 12", 13") and single floor tom (16") provide the pitch range needed for the fill architecture that marks BTBAM's structural transitions.

### Meinl Byzance Extra Dry: Precision for Progressive Metal

Richardson's Meinl Byzance Extra Dry setup on *Alaska* is optimized for precision. The Extra Dry sub-series delivers drier, more focused cymbal voices than the standard Byzance Traditional or Dark lines — less wash, faster decay, more articulate attack. For the rhythmically complex passages that define *Alaska*, this controlled character is functional: it allows Richardson's metric modulations and odd-time patterns to read clearly.

The 14" Extra Dry Medium Hi-Hats are the rhythmic center of his *Alaska* performance — dense ghost-note patterns, frequent open-close transitions as compositional accents, and use of the hi-hat foot as an independent voice in polyrhythmic passages.

### Jamie King at Basement Studios

Producer Jamie King recorded *Alaska* at Basement Studios in Winston-Salem — the home studio environment that would capture every major BTBAM record. King's intimate knowledge of Richardson's kit allowed him to capture the nuanced dynamic range that *Alaska* demanded: from the near-silent folk sections to the full-force death metal passages, all within a coherent drum sound that serves the album's 73-minute arc.

## Key Track Analysis

- **All Bodies** — Album opener; nearly seven minutes of shifting time signatures. Richardson's compositional kick deployment is immediately audible — density varies with the music's emotional temperature.
- **The Primer** — Demonstrates *Alaska*'s genre-fluid approach most clearly; transitions from progressive metal to jazz to folk and back. Richardson's anticipatory fill work prepares each transition before it arrives.
- **Selkies: The Endless Obsession** — The album's most melodic, progressive statement; Richardson's dynamic restraint is as technically demanding as the heaviest passages.
- **Alaska** — Title track; the full BTBAM compositional template in one piece. Richardson's performance here is the definitive introduction to his approach.

## Metric Modulation on Alaska

*Alaska* contains some of the clearest early examples of Richardson's metric modulation technique — shifting tempo perception by reinterpreting note values within an existing pulse. In practice, he internalizes phrase shapes of each section and plays to the song's emotional arc, executing these modulations while maintaining forward momentum. ## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Blake Richardson use on Alaska?**
Blake Richardson recorded Between the Buried and Me's *Alaska* (2005) using a DW Collector's Series kit — double 22" bass drums, three rack toms (10", 12", 13"), and a 16" floor tom — paired with Meinl Byzance Extra Dry cymbals and a DW 9002 double bass pedal. Recorded at Basement Studios, Winston-Salem, NC with producer Jamie King. Full profile at [Blake Richardson at MetalForge](/drummer/blake-richardson).

**Q: What cymbals did Blake Richardson play on Alaska?**
Blake Richardson used Meinl Byzance Extra Dry series cymbals on *Alaska* — 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 17" Thin Crash, 18" Medium Thin Crash, 20" Ride, and 18" Byzance China. The Extra Dry voicing delivers fast decay and articulate attack suited to Alaska's rhythmically complex progressive metal arrangements. The Byzance series' hand-hammered Turkish construction provides complex, nuanced tone for his jazz-influenced hi-hat work.

**Q: How does Blake Richardson handle Alaska's odd time signatures?**
Blake Richardson navigates *Alaska*'s odd time signatures by treating them as musical shapes rather than mathematical problems. His metric modulation technique — shifting tempo perception by reinterpreting note values — allows him to move between time signatures without losing forward momentum. Songs like "The Primer" demonstrate movement between 7/8, 4/4, and compound time that sounds musical rather than mathematical.

**Q: What makes Alaska important in BTBAM's discography?**
*Alaska* (2005) is BTBAM's breakthrough record — the album that established the compositional template refined across every subsequent release. At 73 minutes spanning death metal, jazz, folk, and prog rock, it was the first full statement of the genre-fluid approach that defines BTBAM. It leads directly to [The Parallax II: Future Sequence drum setup](/articles/the-parallax-ii-future-sequence-drum-setup) as the next major milestone in Richardson's progressive metal arc.

**Q: How does Alaska connect to the rest of BTBAM's catalog?**
*Alaska* sits at the start of the most productive arc in BTBAM history. It establishes the compositional template that *Colors* (2007) refines, *The Great Misdirect* (2009) expands, and *The Parallax II: Future Sequence* (2012) brings to its full ambition. For drummers studying Blake Richardson, *Alaska* is the essential entry point — the earliest full documentation of an approach that would be refined across eight studio albums.
