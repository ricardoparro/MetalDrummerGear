## Overview

Released in 2000 on Century Media Records, "And Then You'll Beg" represents the apex of Flo Mounier's technical ambition. Cryptopsy's fourth studio album pushed complexity beyond what the band had achieved on [None So Vile](/articles/none-so-vile-drum-setup) (1996) or [Whisper Supremacy](/articles/whisper-supremacy-drum-setup) (1998), introducing extended odd-time passages in 11/8 and 13/8, blast beat variations that had never appeared on record before, and progressive compositional arcs that demanded complete command of the entire kit.

The album also marked Cryptopsy's first significant lineup change: Lord Worm, the original vocalist, was replaced by Mike DiSalvo. The lineup upheaval put additional pressure on Flo and the instrumentalists to anchor the band's identity — and the drums rise to that challenge with authority. Where previous albums showed a band at the top of its brutality, "And Then You'll Beg" showed a band in controlled evolution.

Flo's gear had evolved from the Pearl MX days of None So Vile. By 2000 he was behind a Pearl Masters BRX kit anchored by a 26-inch bass drum — significantly larger than the 22-inch configuration of 1996 — producing a lower, more cavernous low-end that sits beneath the complex upper-register patterns. Producer Pierre Rémillard returned to Studio Victor in Montreal to capture the performance, applying the same precision-focused approach he had pioneered with the band on earlier records.

The result is widely regarded by drum scholars as Flo Mounier's most technically demanding recorded performance. The odd-time signatures are not decorative — they run for full song sections, forcing the blast beat vocabulary into non-standard subdivision territory where most extreme metal drummers cannot follow. This article breaks down the gear, technique, and recording decisions that made it possible.

### Flo's 2000 Arsenal: Pearl Masters BRX with 26" Bass Drum

By 2000, Flo Mounier had upgraded from the Pearl MX Series of None So Vile to the Pearl Masters BRX — Pearl's professional flagship line featuring hybrid maple/birch shell construction and a full hardware overhaul. The most significant change visible on "And Then You'll Beg" is the 26-inch bass drum, a configuration unusual in death metal at the time.

The 26-inch diameter generates a fundamentally different low-frequency profile: longer wavelength, more room-filling low-end, and a slower initial transient compared to the 22-inch drums common in extreme metal. Flo compensated by dampening the head aggressively and tuning the resonant head lower than typical, preserving the attack click while exploiting the deeper chamber for sustained low-frequency weight beneath his complex upper-pattern work.

The Masters BRX shell construction — Pearl's Superior Shell Technology laminating alternating maple and birch plies — delivered tighter manufacturing tolerances than the MX Series. At the extreme tempos Flo demanded, shell consistency translates directly to predictable resonance, which matters when you are executing 11/8 blast beat patterns where any unpredictable ring buildup would obscure individual strokes.

The rack tom configuration (10" and 12") remained consistent with his previous setup, reflecting Flo's established preference for compact, fast-responding toms that he treats as melodic accent instruments rather than rhythm anchors. The floor toms at 14" and 16" provided the kit's lower melodic register for the progressive passage fills that characterize the album's more compositionally adventurous sections.

### The Precision Core: Pearl Free-Floating Steel Snare

For the more complex blast beat vocabulary of "And Then You'll Beg," Flo required a snare that could deliver consistent articulation across a wider dynamic range and through more complex polyrhythmic contexts than any previous Cryptopsy album demanded. The deeper 14" x 6.5" steel shell — compared to the 5.5" depth of his None So Vile configuration — provided marginally more body and sustain without sacrificing the cutting attack essential to blast beat clarity.

Pearl's Free-Floating snare system isolated the shell from the lugs, eliminating the dampening effect that conventional lug mounting places on shell resonance. This produced a more consistent pitch and response across the drumhead surface — critical for Flo's playing, which covered the full head surface rather than centering all strokes.

In the odd-time sections that define the album — blast beats running across 11/8 and 13/8 bar structures — the snare was tuned to a high but not choked tension, allowing sufficient sustain for the stroke to speak clearly in the mix while decaying fast enough to prevent washout between strokes at blast beat tempos. Pierre Rémillard's microphone placement, using the same close-mic philosophy that had served the band since None So Vile, captured both the steel shell's metallic crack and the snare wire's crisp articulation.

### The Paiste Alpha Foundation

The shift from Sabian AA cymbals to Paiste Alpha for "And Then You'll Beg" represents a subtle but meaningful change in Flo Mounier's sonic palette. The Paiste Alpha series — built from CuSn8 bronze, the same alloy as Paiste's professional 2002 line — delivers a brighter, more overtone-rich sound profile than the Sabian AA, with a slightly more complex decay that benefits the album's progressive passages without compromising blast beat clarity.

The 14" Alpha Medium Hi-Hats formed the rhythmic core of every blast beat on the album. At the tempos Flo executed — pushing past 260 BPM on the faster passages — hi-hat articulation is the primary differentiator between technical and indistinct playing. The Alpha's focused top hat profile produced tight, consistent "chick" sounds even at extreme hand speeds.

For crashes, Flo paired a 16" and an 18" Alpha Medium Crash. In the odd-time blast sections, he deployed the 16" as a punctuation mark at bar boundaries, exploiting its fast attack and quick decay to mark time signature shifts without creating wash buildup. The 18" handled broader dynamic moments — the album's momentary spacious passages where a larger crash voice could breathe.

The 20" Alpha Metal Ride provided bell definition for the album's more compositionally intricate ride patterns, while the 18" Alpha China delivered the aggressive, spitting accents that punctuate Cryptopsy's most intense section transitions. The China's irregular surface and turned-up edge generated the raw, unpredictable character that contrasts with the controlled precision of Flo's primary pattern work.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Flo Mounier use on And Then You'll Beg?**

A: On Cryptopsy's And Then You'll Beg recorded in 2000, Flo Mounier played a Pearl Masters BRX kit with a 26-inch bass drum — significantly larger than the 22-inch configuration of his 1996 None So Vile recording. The BRX shell pack used Pearl's Superior Shell Technology maple/birch hybrid construction for improved shell consistency. Rack toms were 10 and 12 inches; floor toms were 14 and 16 inches. The 26-inch bass drum generated a deeper, more cavernous low-frequency profile that supported the album's progressive passages while Flo's muffling technique preserved the kick drum attack transient needed for blast beat clarity.

**Q: What cymbals did Flo Mounier use on And Then You'll Beg?**

A: On And Then You'll Beg in 2000, Flo Mounier used the Paiste Alpha series — a shift from the Sabian AA cymbals of his None So Vile era. His configuration included Paiste 14-inch Alpha Medium Hi-Hats, a 16-inch Alpha Medium Crash, an 18-inch Alpha Medium Crash, a 20-inch Alpha Metal Ride, and an 18-inch Alpha China. The Paiste Alpha's CuSn8 bronze construction delivers a brighter, overtone-rich character compared to the Sabian AA. This profile suited the album's more compositionally complex passages, where cymbal sustain contributed to the musical texture between blast beat sections.

**Q: What double pedal did Flo Mounier use on And Then You'll Beg?**

A: On And Then You'll Beg, Flo Mounier used the DW 9000 double pedal — a transition from the Pearl Eliminator he had used on None So Vile. The DW 9000's dual-chain drive and floating rotor system provided a different mechanical feel from Pearl's design, with more inherent rebound that Flo exploited for the layered foot patterns in the album's odd-time sections. The pedal's adjustable beater angle was particularly useful on the larger 26-inch bass drum, allowing Flo to tune the impact point for maximum attack on the deeper shell.

**Q: Why is And Then You'll Beg considered Flo Mounier's most technically demanding album?**

A: And Then You'll Beg is considered Flo Mounier's most technically demanding recording because it introduces sustained blast beats in odd time signatures — specifically 11/8 and 13/8 — that require the drummer to maintain blast beat vocabulary across bar boundaries that fall at non-standard positions in the blast pattern. In standard 4/4 blast beats, bar boundaries occur predictably every eight eighth notes. In 11/8 or 13/8, the bar boundary falls at a different point in the pattern on each repetition, requiring simultaneous management of the physical blast and the metric position. By 2000, few drummers globally could execute this at the tempos Flo sustained throughout the album.
