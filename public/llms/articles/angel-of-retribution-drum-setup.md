# Scott Travis's Drum Setup on Judas Priest's Angel of Retribution (2005)

> Inside Scott Travis's mid-2000s drum rig on Angel of Retribution — the album that marked Rob Halford's long-awaited return to Judas Priest after a 13-year departure, bridging the 15-year arc from Painkiller (1990) to Nostradamus (2008) and the 'Eulogy/Lochness' epic.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Scott Travis](/llms/drummers/scott-travis.md)
**Band / Album:** Judas Priest — *Angel of Retribution* (2005)
**Genre:** Heavy Metal

## Overview

When Rob Halford rejoined Judas Priest in 2003 after a 13-year departure that began in 1992, the metal world braced for what would either be a triumphant homecoming or an awkward nostalgia trip. "Angel of Retribution," released on February 28, 2005 through Epic Records and Sony BMG, turned out to be the former — a deliberate, song-focused reunion record that hit #1 in Germany and #13 on the Billboard 200. For Scott Travis, the album represented something subtler but equally significant: his first studio album with the classic Halford-Tipton-Downing-Hill lineup that had been his original goal when he joined the band in 1989.

Recorded at a series of California studios and co-produced by Roy Z alongside the band itself, Angel of Retribution sits in a unique place on the Judas Priest timeline. It closes the 15-year arc gap between 1990's blistering "Painkiller" and the orchestral concept work of 2008's "Nostradamus," before the band's eventual return to brute-force metal on 2018's "Firepower." Travis's drumming on the record — particularly on the 13-minute closing epic "Eulogy/Lochness" — is the connective tissue that holds those eras together.

The gear story on Angel of Retribution is one of transition. By the mid-2000s, Travis had shifted away from the Tama Artstar II setup that powered Painkiller and into the Pearl camp, where he would stay for much of the 2000s before later moving toward Tama again in the Firepower era. Photographs and live footage from the 2004-2005 sessions and supporting tour show him behind a Pearl Reference Series kit — the company's flagship hybrid-shell line — with the long-running Sabian cymbal endorsement intact and Vater signature sticks in hand. Tracks like the lead single "Revolution," the anthemic "Worth Fighting For," and the sprawling "Eulogy/Lochness" each showcase a different facet of that mid-period rig.

This is the album where Travis stopped trying to repeat Painkiller and started playing for the song. The double-kick fury is still there when the material demands it, but Angel of Retribution leans on groove, dynamics, and patience — exactly what a reunion record needed to be taken seriously rather than dismissed as a victory lap.

### Travis's Pearl Reference Series Rig

By 2005, Scott Travis had moved away from the Tama Artstar II that defined his Painkiller-era sound and settled into Pearl's flagship Reference Series. Photos and rehearsal footage from the Angel of Retribution sessions and the supporting 2005 tour consistently show him behind a Pearl Reference kit, with a Pearl Masters MCX appearing on some tracking dates as a backup. This transition is important: it's the rig that bridges Travis's blistering speed-metal years and the later Tama-era Firepower setup, and it's tuned for a fundamentally different goal — songs over spectacle.

The Reference Series uses a hybrid shell construction — maple, birch, and mahogany plies arranged so each drum is tuned for its role in the kit. The toms get more maple for warmth and sustain, the bass drums get more mahogany for low-end weight, and the snare position emphasizes birch for cutting attack. For an album like Angel of Retribution, which oscillates between mid-tempo grooves on "Worth Fighting For" and double-kick blasts on "Hellrider," this approach paid off: the kit could speak quietly during the verses and explode without changing tuning.

Travis ran a streamlined four-tom configuration — 10" and 12" up top, 14" and 16" on the floor — a noticeable simplification from the three-rack-tom Painkiller setup. The reduced tom count let him commit to bigger, more architectural fills rather than the rapid-fire melodic runs of 1990. The dual 22" x 18" bass drums (deeper than the 22" x 16" Painkiller-era kicks) gave him more air and a fuller bottom end suited to the album's heavier mid-2000s production aesthetic.

The Pearl Masters MCX, used on alternate sessions, is the all-maple counterpart to the Reference Series and would have provided a slightly warmer, more rounded tom voice on any tracks that called for it. Both kits sit in the same family — pro-level professional Pearl gear that defined Travis's middle period.

### Brass Authority

For Angel of Retribution, Travis paired his Pearl Reference kit with a Pearl Reference brass snare — a 14" x 6.5" beaded brass drum that delivers a darker, more complex backbeat than the steel Artstar II he used on Painkiller. Brass shells sit between steel and bronze tonally: more body than steel, more crack than bronze, and a midrange focus that cuts through the modern mid-2000s mix without sounding thin.

The 6.5" depth gives the snare enough body for the album's slower, heavier material — "Worth Fighting For," "Eulogy," and the closing stretch of "Lochness" all benefit from a snare that can sustain a backbeat without disappearing. At the same time, the die-cast hoops keep rim shots sharp and tight, which matters during the double-kick passages on "Judas Rising" and "Hellrider" where Travis still needs Painkiller-style snap on the 2 and 4.

Travis tuned the drum medium-high — not as tight as the 1990 Artstar steel, but tighter than a typical rock tuning — to find a middle ground between projection and tone. The combination of brass shell, beaded reinforcement, and die-cast hoops produced one of the most consistent snare sounds in Travis's recorded catalogue and is a big part of what makes Angel of Retribution feel modern next to its 1990 predecessor.

### Sabian HH and AA: The Long-Running Endorsement

Scott Travis has been a Sabian endorser for decades, and Angel of Retribution captures him deep into that relationship. The setup combines Sabian's hand-hammered HH series (darker, more complex, more vintage in character) with the machine-hammered AA line (brighter, more cutting, faster response) — a hybrid approach that gives Travis tonal options without sacrificing the projection he needs to sit on top of K.K. Downing and Glenn Tipton's dual-guitar wall.

The 14" HH Regular Hats are a noticeably warmer choice than the Paiste Signature 14" Heavy Hi-Hats of the Painkiller era. The hand-hammered finish produces a chick-and-wash character that suits Angel of Retribution's groove-leaning material — particularly the verse of "Revolution" and the long instrumental passages of "Lochness," where hi-hat color matters as much as articulation.

The crash array spans 16" to 20", giving Travis a range from quick AA Medium accents to larger sustaining hits. The 22" HH Raw Bell Dry Ride is the centerpiece of the right side — a dry, articulate ride with a pronounced bell that drives the ride patterns on the verses of "Deal With the Devil" and the slow build of "Eulogy." The 20" AA Chinese provides the trashy accent voice for the heavier moments on "Hellrider" and the climactic sections of "Lochness."

This setup represents Travis's mature cymbal voicing: less brightness than Painkiller, more tonal variety, and a deliberate balance between cutting attack and musical sustain. It's the cymbal language of a drummer who has stopped chasing the loudest possible sound and started shaping a real ensemble role.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Scott Travis use on Angel of Retribution?**

A: For the 2005 Angel of Retribution sessions, Scott Travis played a Pearl Reference Series kit — Pearl's flagship hybrid-shell line that combines maple, birch, and mahogany plies tuned per drum. The configuration featured dual 22 inch by 18 inch bass drums, 10 and 12 inch rack toms, and 14 and 16 inch floor toms. A Pearl Masters MCX served as an all-maple alternate on some tracking dates. This rig represented a clear transition away from the Tama Artstar II setup he used on 1990's Painkiller and toward the modern Priest sound that would carry through Nostradamus and into the Firepower era.

**Q: Why did Scott Travis switch from Tama to Pearl by 2005?**

A: Travis moved into the Pearl camp in the early-to-mid 2000s as part of an endorsement shift. The Pearl Reference Series, introduced in 2004, was Pearl's most ambitious shell design at the time — its hybrid construction tunes each drum for its role rather than using a single shell material throughout. For a drummer whose work spans the speed-metal extremes of Painkiller and the song-oriented material on Angel of Retribution, that flexibility was a meaningful upgrade. Travis would later transition back toward Tama by the Firepower era, but the mid-2000s through early 2010s are firmly his Pearl years.

**Q: What cymbals did Scott Travis use on Angel of Retribution?**

A: Travis has been a long-running Sabian endorser, and his 2005 setup combined the hand-hammered HH series with the machine-hammered AA line. The rig included 14 inch HH Regular Hi-Hats, AA Medium Crashes from 16 to 20 inches, an HH 19 inch Thin Crash for darker accents, a 22 inch HH Raw Bell Dry Ride, and a 20 inch AA Chinese for trashy accents on tracks like Hellrider and the Lochness section of the closing epic. The HH/AA hybrid approach gave him both vintage warmth and modern cut.

**Q: How does Angel of Retribution fit between Painkiller and Firepower in Scott Travis's career?**

A: Angel of Retribution closes a 15-year arc gap on the Judas Priest timeline: Painkiller in 1990, Angel of Retribution in 2005, Nostradamus in 2008, and Firepower in 2018. For Travis specifically, it's the album where he stops being purely the speed-metal phenom of Painkiller and becomes the long-arc Priest drummer. The Pearl Reference kit, the Sabian HH/AA hybrid setup, and the more restrained song-serving approach all point toward the mature drumming he'd bring to the band's later work. It's also Rob Halford's first studio album back with Judas Priest after a 13-year departure, making it as much a comeback record for the band as a transition record for Travis's gear.

**Q: What is significant about the 'Eulogy/Lochness' track for drum performance?**

A: Eulogy/Lochness is a 13-minute closing epic that gives Travis more long-form space than almost any other Priest track. Eulogy opens as a slow, brooding ballad with minimal kit work, then Lochness detonates into a doom-paced epic that builds through tom pulses, china accents, and a climactic double-kick section. It's drumming as long-form arrangement, and it foreshadows the orchestral ambitions Priest would pursue on 2008's Nostradamus. For a 2005 reunion record that needed to prove the band still had reach, it's the showcase track.
