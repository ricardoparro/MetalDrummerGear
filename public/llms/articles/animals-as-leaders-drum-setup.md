# Animals as Leaders (2009) Drum Setup: Programmed by Tosin Abasi

> Tosin Abasi's self-titled Animals as Leaders debut (2009) features fully programmed drums — sequenced by Abasi himself using Toontrack samples and a MIDI workflow. Honest breakdown of the programmed drum production behind the djent-defining instrumental album.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** Tosin Abasi (programmed)
**Band / Album:** Animals as Leaders — *Animals as Leaders* (2009)
**Genre:** Progressive Metal / Djent

## Overview

Animals as Leaders' self-titled debut, released April 28, 2009 on Prosthetic Records, defined a generation of progressive instrumental metal — but it was made without a live drummer. Every drum track on the album is programmed: sequenced in MIDI by Tosin Abasi himself, voiced through Toontrack samples (primarily Drumkit From Hell and related libraries), and produced in collaboration with Misha Mansoor of Periphery. This is a critical fact about the record that often gets obscured by the band's later live reputation.

The album began as a solo guitar project. Tosin Abasi had recently been offered a record deal by Prosthetic after his work in the band Reflux, and he initially declined — feeling he needed time to develop as a guitarist before fronting a solo record. He spent that time studying jazz at Atlanta Institute of Music, then returned to the project and built the album in his home studio. With no full band assembled and a tight production budget, programming drums was the practical and editorial choice.

This is editorially honest framing that the Animals as Leaders fan base has long known: the debut is a guitar-led solo album with sequenced drums. The drums are excellent — Tosin's programming work is musical, dynamic, and informed by his deep knowledge of progressive metal vocabulary — but no human drummer played them. Navene Koperweis (later of Entheos and Animosity) joined the live touring lineup after the album's release and toured with the band for the debut cycle, but did not appear on the recording. Matt Garstka, the band's current drummer, did not join until 2012 for the Weightless sessions.

This article documents the programmed drum production on the Animals as Leaders self-titled debut: the sample libraries used, the MIDI workflow Tosin employed, the production approach Misha Mansoor brought to mixing, and the editorial reasoning behind the programmed-drums approach. For a guide on a live-drummer Animals as Leaders record, see the Weightless or The Joy of Motion drum setup articles.

### The Sampled Kit: Toontrack Drumkit From Hell

The drum kit heard on Animals as Leaders is not a physical drum kit. It is a sample library, voiced through software and triggered by MIDI sequencing in Tosin Abasi's DAW. The primary sample source is Toontrack's Drumkit From Hell library — the de facto industry standard for programmed metal drums in the late 2000s, used by countless djent and progressive metal records of the era including the early Periphery demos.

Drumkit From Hell was originally recorded at Dug-Out Studios in Sweden with engineer Daniel Bergstrand, capturing real Tama Starclassic shells with high-tension tuning, multiple microphone perspectives, and the dry, focused metal-production aesthetic that defined the late-2000s progressive metal sound. Each drum was sampled across multiple velocity layers and articulations, giving the library a level of expressiveness that distinguished it from earlier programmed drum offerings.

For the Animals as Leaders sessions, Tosin programmed every kick, snare, tom, and cymbal hit in MIDI. The double-bass passages — which are extensive on tracks like "CAFO" and "Tempting Time" — were sequenced with humanizing velocity variation to avoid the mechanical feel that plagues amateur programmed drums. The fills were composed measure-by-measure to match the complex polyrhythmic guitar arrangements.

This is the honest gear answer for the album: there is no Tama Starclassic, no Pearl Reference, no Mapex Saturn that someone hit with sticks to make Animals as Leaders. The drums are programmed, and they are well-programmed.

### The Programmed Snare: Sampled, Layered, Replaced

The snare sound on Animals as Leaders is a programmed sample — and like the rest of the kit, it is built from the Toontrack Drumkit From Hell library voiced through MIDI sequencing. The underlying source recording was a 14x6.5 steel-shell snare captured at Dug-Out Studios with multiple microphone perspectives (top, bottom, and room) and multiple velocity layers per articulation.

What distinguishes the snare sound on the album from a typical programmed record is the layering. Misha Mansoor's production approach — which became foundational to the djent sound through his Periphery work — frequently involved layering a programmed snare sample with a triggered secondary sample for additional weight, then EQ-matching the result against reference snare tones from Meshuggah and other reference records. The Animals as Leaders snare benefits from that approach: it has both the cutting top-end of a steel shell and the body of a layered sample stack.

The programming choices are also musical: Tosin used ghost notes, accents on syncopated upbeats, and dynamic variation to give the snare a feel that programmed drums often lack. On "On Impulse," the snare placement against the polyrhythmic guitar phrasing demonstrates a level of programming musicality that informed an entire generation of bedroom-producer djent.

### Programmed Cymbals: Sample Library and MIDI Trigger

The cymbals on Animals as Leaders are programmed samples drawn from the Toontrack Drumkit From Hell library, sequenced in MIDI alongside the kick, snare, and toms. The underlying samples were recorded with real cymbals (Zildjian and Sabian sources, depending on the specific cymbal voice) at Dug-Out Studios with multiple velocity layers per articulation.

Programming convincing cymbal work is one of the hardest aspects of sequenced drumming — and one where Tosin's approach on this album stands out. Real drummers vary their stick angle, accent placement, and dynamic across every cymbal hit; static MIDI velocities and identical samples produce the unmistakable "machine cymbals" sound that plagues amateur programming. Tosin used velocity humanization, alternated between articulation samples (e.g., different crash positions and stick contact areas), and varied timing micro-adjustments to avoid the worst of those artifacts.

The hi-hat programming is particularly musical: across the album, the closed/open variation tracks the song dynamics rather than locking to a static pattern. On tracks like "Soraya" — which features more spacious, jazz-influenced sections — the hi-hat articulations shift to match the breath of the music.

The China cymbal accents above the floor tom are a djent-era constant, and Tosin's programming uses them sparingly — for section transitions and accent points rather than continuous trash.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Did Animals as Leaders use a live drummer on the self-titled album?**

A: No. The Animals as Leaders self-titled debut (2009) features fully programmed drums — sequenced in MIDI by Tosin Abasi himself, voiced through Toontrack sample libraries (primarily Drumkit From Hell). No live drummer played on the recording. Navene Koperweis joined the live touring lineup after the album's release to play the programmed material in concert, but he did not appear on the studio recording. Matt Garstka, the band's long-term drummer, did not join until 2012 for the Weightless sessions. This editorial fact is part of the album's identity and history.

**Q: What drum samples were used on the Animals as Leaders debut?**

A: The drums on the Animals as Leaders self-titled debut were programmed using Toontrack's Drumkit From Hell sample library — the industry-standard programmed-drums solution for metal records in 2009. The library was recorded at Dug-Out Studios in Sweden with engineer Daniel Bergstrand, capturing real Tama Starclassic shells across multiple velocity layers and microphone perspectives. The same library was used extensively on the early Periphery demos by Misha Mansoor, who co-produced and mixed the Animals as Leaders debut.

**Q: Who produced the Animals as Leaders self-titled album?**

A: The album was produced collaboratively by Tosin Abasi (composition, performance, drum programming) and Misha Mansoor of Periphery (mixing, technical production guidance). It was recorded in Tosin Abasi's home studio rather than a commercial recording facility — a bedroom-studio approach typical of the early djent scene. The album was released April 28, 2009 on Prosthetic Records.

**Q: When did Animals as Leaders start using a live drummer on records?**

A: Matt Garstka joined Animals as Leaders for the Weightless sessions in 2012 and has been the band's drummer ever since. Weightless (2011) was the transitional album that moved away from programmed drums to live drum tracking. Every Animals as Leaders studio album after the self-titled debut features Matt Garstka performing the drums live — including The Joy of Motion (2014), The Madness of Many (2016), and Parrhesia (2022). The 2009 debut remains the only programmed-drums record in the catalog.

**Q: Why did Tosin Abasi program the drums instead of hiring a drummer?**

A: The Animals as Leaders debut began as Tosin Abasi's solo project — he had recently returned from studying jazz at Atlanta Institute of Music and had no full band assembled. Programming drums was the practical choice given the minimal production budget and the absence of a permanent rhythm section. Tosin had also developed sufficient programming skill through working with Misha Mansoor on early Periphery material to handle the drum production himself. The approach was editorially valid for a solo-project debut and produced excellent results, but it is honest framing to note that this is a sequenced-drums album, not a live-drums album.
