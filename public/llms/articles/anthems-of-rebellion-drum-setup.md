## Overview

Released October 27, 2003 on Century Media Records, Arch Enemy's fifth studio album Anthems of Rebellion marks a defining moment in the band's history: Angela Gossow's first full album with Arch Enemy — the record where her presence shaped the songwriting and identity of the band from the ground up, rather than being grafted onto material already in progress. Where Wages of Sin (2002) was the breakthrough that introduced Gossow to the world, Anthems of Rebellion is the album where the Angela Gossow–era Arch Enemy established its fully developed creative identity. The results confirmed that the chemistry between Gossow's extreme vocal approach and the band's melodic death metal architecture was not a one-record phenomenon — it was the defining formula of Arch Enemy's most commercially successful and critically recognised era.

Behind the kit, Daniel Erlandsson brought the same compositional precision that had characterised his playing on Wages of Sin, but now operating within arrangements that had been written from the outset with Gossow's vocal style in mind. The interplay between Erlandsson's double-kick patterns and the melodic guitar work of Michael Amott is sharper and more purposeful on Anthems than on its predecessor: the drums answer the vocal phrasing, frame the harmonic motion of the guitar leads, and drive the album's most anthemic moments — including the defining Gossow-era statement "We Will Rise" — with the locked-in precision that became Arch Enemy's trademark.

Erlandsson's setup for the Anthems sessions centred on his Pearl Masters MMX kit — Pearl's flagship mixed-shell configuration of the era, combining maple and mahogany shells for a warmer, fuller tonal character than pure-maple designs. His cymbals had evolved toward Meinl's Byzance series, bringing the hand-hammered B20 bronze complexity of that line to Arch Enemy's studio and live configuration. The Pearl Eliminator double bass pedal remained his foot technique foundation, providing the mechanical consistency that sustained double-kick passages demanded across full studio tracking days.

This article breaks down Daniel Erlandsson's drum setup for Anthems of Rebellion — the kit, cymbals, hardware, recording approach, and technique — and places the album within the arc of the Angela Gossow era and the Gothenburg melodic death metal tradition that shaped both Erlandsson brothers' careers.

### Daniel Erlandsson's 2003 Kit: Pearl Masters MMX Configuration

Daniel Erlandsson's Pearl Masters MMX kit on Anthems of Rebellion represents a distinct chapter in his gear evolution. The MMX (Masters Maple/Mixed) designation refers to Pearl's flagship mixed-shell configuration of the era — shells constructed from a combination of maple and mahogany that delivers a warmer, fuller tonal character than pure-maple designs, with more low-mid body and a slightly longer sustain that suits melodic material. Where pure maple tends toward brightness and definition, the maple/mahogany blend produces a rounder, more envelope-rich drum voice that sits differently in a dense guitar mix.

For Arch Enemy's Anthems of Rebellion, that tonal character serves the album's compositional direction. The arrangements on Anthems are more melodic and structurally varied than the more linear aggression of Wages of Sin — "We Will Rise," "Dead Eyes See No Future," and "Exist to Dominate" all feature melodic passages where the drums need to breathe and articulate against guitar harmonies rather than simply drive a relentless groove. The MMX's fuller, warmer character suits these passages: the toms sing with more body, the bass drums carry more sub-bass warmth, and the overall kit voice complements Amott's layered guitar harmonics without fighting for the same bright frequency space.

The double 22" x 18" bass drum configuration continues Erlandsson's established template. Two independent kick drums provide the per-foot articulation and resonance independence that Arch Enemy's sustained double-kick passages require — the independence audible on the extended footwork sections of "Exist to Dominate" and in the driving rhythmic foundation of "We Will Rise." At the recording level, two separate kick drums produce true stereo width in the low end that reinforces the album's three-dimensional mix character.

The 10"/12" rack tom and 14"/16" floor tom configuration gives Erlandsson the four-piece tom spread he uses across all Arch Enemy recordings: compact enough for physical efficiency at velocity, ranged enough for melodic fill content that follows guitar contours. The MMX shells' added warmth makes the toms sound particularly full in the tom-fill passages that mark structural transitions throughout the album.

### Pearl Free-Floating Brass 14" x 6.5": Brightness Through Melodic Density

On Anthems of Rebellion, Daniel Erlandsson's snare work continued with his preference for the Pearl Free-Floating Brass 14" x 6.5" — the same fundamental approach he had used on Wages of Sin, chosen for the brightness and cut that brass shell construction delivers in dense guitar environments. The Free-Floating system's shell-isolation design — in which the shell is suspended away from the hardware — preserves sustain and resonance by decoupling the shell's vibration from mechanical dampening.

In the context of Anthems of Rebellion's production, the brass shell's cutting character is essential. The album's guitar arrangements are layered and harmonically rich, with Amott's melodic leads occupying significant mid-frequency space. A warmer, wood-shell snare would be absorbed into that mid-frequency density; the brass shell's brighter attack speaks above it, giving the snare a distinct voice in the mix even when surrounded by full-production guitar layers.

The 6.5" depth produces the balance of crack and body that professional melodic death metal requires. Shallower snares speak fast but can sound thin in full-band contexts; deeper snares have more body but can slow the attack at velocity. The 6.5" Free-Floating Brass sits at the practical optimum — fast enough for the blast beat passages on "Exist to Dominate," full enough for the mid-tempo groove sections on "We Will Rise."

### Meinl Byzance: Daniel Erlandsson's 2003 Cymbal Evolution

The Meinl Byzance series that Daniel Erlandsson deployed on Anthems of Rebellion represents a significant shift in the tonal philosophy of his cymbal work relative to the Sabian AA/HH setup of the Wages of Sin era. The Byzance line is hand-hammered B20 bronze — the same alloy as most premium cymbals, but processed with more surface variation than machine-hammered designs. The result is a cymbal character that is warmer, darker, and more musically complex than the bright-cutting Sabian AA approach: more overtones, longer sustain, and a tonal depth that rewards dynamic playing.

For Arch Enemy's Anthems of Rebellion sessions, the Byzance character suits the album's more melodic compositional direction. The hand-hammered surface produces a rich wash that blends with Amott's layered guitar harmonics rather than cutting aggressively above them. The hi-hats speak with warmth and definition, the crashes sustain with musical complexity rather than fast decay, and the ride provides a textured canvas for groove passages that feels compositionally integrated rather than purely functional.

The 14" Byzance Traditional hi-hats drive the album's rhythmic foundation with the articulate clarity that Meinl's Byzance Traditional series is known for — every stroke registers distinctly even within the blast beat passages, where hi-hat definition separates competent drumming from exceptional drumming. The 16" and 18" crash configuration provides the accent range from fast-decay structural markers to longer, more musical sustain for the bigger section moments on "We Will Rise."

The Byzance 18" China maintains the Gothenburg melodic death metal china accent vocabulary that Erlandsson established on earlier albums — the aggressive, trashy character for riff transitions and dynamic punctuation — but with the additional tonal complexity of the hand-hammered Byzance surface. And the 20" Byzance Medium Ride provides the low-frequency texture that the more spacious passages on Anthems require, offering a contrasting sonic character to the crashes that expands the album's tonal palette.

**Q: Why is Anthems of Rebellion considered Angela Gossow's first full Arch Enemy album?**

Wages of Sin (2002) was her debut as Arch Enemy's vocalist, but the band had already begun development of that material before she joined; the Wages sessions adapted existing material to her presence. **Q: What cymbals did Daniel Erlandsson use on Anthems of Rebellion?**

**Q: What is the Pearl Masters MMX and how does it differ from the Pearl Reference Pure?**

A: The Pearl Masters MMX was Pearl's flagship mixed-shell configuration of the early 2000s — shells constructed from a combination of maple and mahogany that produces a warmer, fuller tonal character with more low-mid body than pure-maple designs. The MMX designation stands for Masters Maple/Mixed, indicating the hybrid shell construction. The Pearl Reference Pure (which Erlandsson used on Doomsday Machine in 2005 and subsequent albums) shifted back to all-maple construction for maximum tonal clarity, definition, and projection — a brighter, more articulate character than the MMX's warmer, rounder voice. The progression from MMX (2003) to Reference Pure (2005 onward) reflects both the evolution of Pearl's product lines and the development of Erlandsson's tonal preferences toward the cleaner, more defined character that Andy Sneap's production approach prioritised.
