# Sepultura Arise Drum Setup: Igor Cavalera's 1991 Gear Breakdown

**Band / Album:** Sepultura — *Arise* (1991)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Death Metal

## Overview

Released in April 1991, "Arise" stands as Sepultura's most technically demanding album and the definitive bridge between the raw Brazilian thrash of "Beneath the Remains" and the groove-metal revolution of "Chaos A.D." It is the album where Igor Cavalera transformed from a prodigiously gifted teenager into one of the most formidable drummers in extreme metal.

Returning to Morrisound Recording in Tampa, Florida, and once again working with producer Scott Burns, Sepultura arrived in 1990 as a band on the verge of international breakthrough. Two years of relentless touring behind "Beneath the Remains" had forged Igor into a technically precise, physically powerful drummer. His practice regimen had intensified, his double-bass control had deepened, and his musical vocabulary had expanded far beyond the straight thrash attack of the previous record.

What Igor delivered on "Arise" shocked listeners who thought they had already heard his ceiling. The title track opens with one of thrash metal's most punishing drum introductions — a double-bass barrage that sets the tempo for nine songs of unrelenting aggression. "Dead Embryonic Cells" showcases blast beats that rival anything in contemporary death metal. "Subtraction" pushes the tempo past anything Sepultura had recorded before. Yet through all of this intensity, Igor never loses the pocket — every hit lands with purpose, every fill serves the arrangement.

"Arise" also marks the moment where listeners can hear the first seeds of Igor's future evolution. Songs like "Desperate Cry" — an emotional mid-paced track that veered away from pure thrash — demonstrate a drummer beginning to explore dynamics and groove over raw speed. The contrast between the album's thrashiest moments and these more measured passages reveals an artist who had already begun thinking beyond genre constraints.

The album charted in several European countries and went Gold in Brazil, exposing Igor's drumming to its widest audience yet. For many metal fans worldwide, "Arise" remains the definitive Sepultura album — and Igor Cavalera's definitive performance. This article examines every piece of gear he used to record these legendary tracks, the recording techniques Scott Burns employed at Morrisound, and the technical approach that made "Arise" an enduring masterclass in extreme metal drumming.

### Igor's 1991 Thrash Peak: The Tama Artstar II

By the time Sepultura entered Morrisound to record "Arise," Igor Cavalera had upgraded to a Tama Artstar II kit — a significant step up from the Pearl Export used on "Beneath the Remains" and one of the premium professional drum kits available in 1990. The Artstar II was renowned throughout the metal and rock world for its birch shells, which delivered a sharper, more aggressive attack than the maple-shelled alternatives favored by rock drummers. For Igor's thrash metal assault, birch was the perfect choice.

The dual 22" x 16" bass drums remained the cornerstone of Igor's setup. The touring years between the two Morrisound sessions had refined his double-bass technique into something genuinely frightening in its precision and power. Where the double bass on "Beneath the Remains" occasionally revealed the enthusiasm of a teenager pushing his limits, the kick drum work on "Arise" is the playing of a mature technician who has fully internalized the mechanics of speed, control, and stamina. The title track, "Dead Embryonic Cells," and "Altered State" all feature double-bass passages that demonstrate this leap in command.

Tama's Star-Cast mounting system — which suspends the shells at two points per tom rather than clamping them — allowed the drums to resonate more freely than conventional mounted designs. This engineering detail contributed to the fuller, more resonant tom sound audible on "Arise" compared to "Beneath the Remains." The toms ring with authority, adding melodic interest to fills without muddying the mix.

Igor expanded his tom configuration for "Arise," adding a third rack tom (13" x 11") to the two-tom setup he had used on the previous album. This additional voice allowed for more elaborate tom sequences and gave him the option of descending tom runs that sweep from the high rack down through the floor tom — a technique used to dramatic effect in several of the album's most intense passages.

Scott Burns and the Morrisound engineers knew exactly how to capture the Artstar II's natural character. The birch shells were tuned medium-tight — tighter than rock tuning but not choked like some thrash productions — which preserved both the attack transient and the sustain needed for the studio environment. The result is a drum sound that is simultaneously punishing and musical: you can feel every hit land, but the drums also have enough body to support the album's dynamics.

### The Precision Strike: Steel Authority

The snare on "Arise" is arguably Igor Cavalera's finest studio snare tone — a cutting, authoritative crack that sits perfectly in Scott Burns' dense mix. The steel shell delivers the brightness and projection that thrash metal demands while providing enough body to support the album's faster, more intense passages.

The 6.5" depth gives the drum substantial presence: this is not a thin, high-pitched snare that gets lost in the guitars. Every backbeat commands attention. On the militaristic sections, like the verse of the title track, each hit lands with ceremonial precision. On the blasting passages, the snare cuts through the wall of guitar with unwavering clarity.

Burns' approach to miking the snare was consistent with his Morrisound philosophy: close-mic it to capture every nuance of the attack, and let the natural character of the drum speak. The steel shell's inherent brightness meant less EQ was needed to achieve the cutting tone — Burns could focus on capturing the performance rather than sculpting the sound artificially.

Igor tuned the snare tight for "Arise" — tighter than the medium-high tuning he would adopt for the more groove-oriented "Chaos A.D." sessions. This choice prioritized articulation at extreme speeds: at the tempos required for "Dead Embryonic Cells" or "Subtraction," the head tension needed to be high enough that the drum could respond cleanly to rapid strokes without the pitch dropping between hits.

### Zildjian Aggression: Cutting Through the Mix

For "Arise," Igor Cavalera moved to Zildjian cymbals — specifically the A Series, which had been a staple of aggressive drumming for decades. The A Series' bright, cutting tone was well suited to Morrisound's recording environment, where the cymbals needed to slice through dense multi-tracked guitars without becoming harsh or brittle.

The 14" Heavy Hi-Hats drive the majority of "Arise" with relentless precision. Throughout the album's fastest sections, Igor maintains 16th-note patterns on the hi-hat while his feet execute complex double-bass figures beneath — a coordination feat that demands not just technique but significant physical conditioning. The Heavy model provides the resistance and projection needed at these tempos.

The crash cymbal pairing — 16" and 18" — gave Igor options for different dynamic contexts. The 16" responds instantly and decays quickly, perfect for the rapid punctuation between riffs. The 18" has more body and sustain, suited for the bigger moments where the entire band explodes simultaneously. Both were positioned for fast access, reflecting the rapid-fire crash demands of thrash composition.

The A 20" Ride's clear bell articulation is audible throughout "Arise" in passages where Igor shifts from hi-hat driving to ride accenting. The bell cuts through even the densest guitar sections, making it a useful voice for defining beat one in complex riff structures.

The 18" China cymbal remains a signature element of the Sepultura sound. Its trashy, immediate decay creates explosive punctuation at riff transitions — an effect that Scott Burns captured with close miking to maximize the china's aggressive character without allowing it to wash over the mix.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Igor Cavalera use on Sepultura's Arise?**

A: On Sepultura's Arise recorded in 1990 and released in 1991, Igor Cavalera played a Tama Artstar II kit with birch shells. The setup included dual 22x16 inch bass drums, three rack toms in 10, 12, and 13 inch sizes, and a single 16 inch floor tom. This was a significant professional upgrade from the Pearl Export he used on Beneath the Remains, with the Artstar II's birch shells delivering the sharper attack and greater resonance needed for the album's technically demanding material. The Tama Star-Cast mounting system allowed the shells to resonate more freely, contributing to the fuller tom sound audible throughout the album.

**Q: What cymbals did Igor Cavalera use on Arise?**

A: On Arise in 1991, Igor Cavalera used Zildjian A Series cymbals. His setup included Zildjian A 14 inch Heavy hi-hats, A 16 and 18 inch Medium Crashes, an A 20 inch Medium Ride, and an A 18 inch China Boy High. The A Series' bright, cutting tone was well suited to Morrisound Recording's environment and Scott Burns' production approach, allowing the cymbals to slice through the dense guitar arrangements without becoming harsh. This represented a change from the Paiste 2002 series he used on Beneath the Remains.

**Q: Where was Sepultura's Arise recorded and who produced it?**

A: Arise was recorded at Morrisound Recording in Tampa, Florida, the same studio where Sepultura recorded Beneath the Remains in 1989. The album was once again produced by Scott Burns, who by 1990 had become the definitive producer of American extreme metal, having worked with Death, Obituary, Morbid Angel, and Deicide. Burns's philosophy of capturing drum performances with maximum clarity and without electronic triggers or samples meant every sound on Arise is Igor Cavalera performing live in the Morrisound live room.

**Q: How does Igor Cavalera's drumming on Arise compare to Beneath the Remains?**

A: Igor's drumming on Arise is noticeably more advanced and powerful than on Beneath the Remains, reflecting two years of constant touring and practice. The double-bass work is tighter and more sustained, the blast beats are more precise, and the overall control at extreme tempos is significantly greater. Arise also shows Igor beginning to explore dynamics and musical phrasing — particularly on the mid-paced track Desperate Cry — hinting at the groove-metal direction he would fully pursue on Chaos A.D. in 1993. The drum sound itself also benefits from the upgraded Tama Artstar II kit, which has more resonance and definition than the Pearl Export used on Beneath the Remains.

**Q: How much does a replica of Igor Cavalera's Arise drum kit cost?**

A: At the time of recording in 1990 to 1991, a Tama Artstar II kit was priced at approximately $2,000 to $3,000 depending on configuration. Today, vintage Tama Artstar II kits sell on the used market for $1,500 to $4,000 depending on condition and configuration. Period-correct Zildjian A Series cymbals matching the Arise setup run $600 to $1,200 on the vintage market. A Tama steel snare of the era adds $200 to $400 used. A complete replica of the Arise-era setup can be assembled for approximately $2,500 to $5,500.
