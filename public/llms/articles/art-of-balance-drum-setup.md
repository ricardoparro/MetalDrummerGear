# Jason Bittner's Drum Setup on Shadows Fall's The Art of Balance (2002)

> Complete breakdown of the drum kit, snare, cymbals, and hardware Jason Bittner used to record Shadows Fall's NWOAHM breakthrough The Art of Balance (2002). Tama Starclassic Performer B/B, Sabian HHX cymbals, DW 9002 double bass pedal, and the double-kick technique behind "Idle Hands."

**Type:** Album Drum Setup

**Band / Album:** Shadows Fall — *The Art of Balance* (2002)
**Genre:** NWOAHM / Metalcore

## Overview

Released on September 17, 2002 through Century Media Records, Shadows Fall's *The Art of Balance* is the album that put the New Wave of American Heavy Metal on the map. It was the band's commercial breakthrough on Century Media, opening at #15 on Billboard's Top Independent Albums chart, and set up the run of releases that would eventually carry Shadows Fall to a major-label deal with Atlantic Records five years later for *Threads of Life* (2007). The album's opening track, "Idle Hands," has since become the genre's defining technical drumming showcase.

"Idle Hands" is the single most-cited Shadows Fall track in metal drumming discussion. Its double-kick verse pattern — a rapid, syncopated 16th-note figure locked against the guitar's palm-muted riff — became a reference point for an entire generation of NWOAHM and metalcore drummers. "A Fire in Babylon," the album's longest and most expansive track, is the record's other essential drummer's showcase, pairing Bittner's dynamic control and stamina with the album's most ambitious arrangement.

Shadows Fall recorded *The Art of Balance* with producer Zeuss (Chris Harris) at Planet Z Studios in Hadley, Massachusetts. Zeuss's production on this record is rawer and more aggressive than the polish that came later on *The War Within* (2004): the drums sit forward and hot in the mix, giving Bittner's double-bass work an immediacy that suits the album's breakthrough energy.

### The Art of Balance Kit: Tama Starclassic Performer B/B

Jason Bittner's drum kit on *The Art of Balance* was his Tama Starclassic Performer B/B — the birch/bubinga hybrid shell kit that would become his signature setup throughout Shadows Fall's peak NWOAHM period. Zeuss's production pushed the kit's attack character hard — the drums sit forward and aggressive in the mix, rawer than the more polished production that would follow on *The War Within* two years later.

The double 22" x 18" bass drum configuration is the mechanical foundation of "Idle Hands"'s signature verse pattern — a rapid, syncopated double-kick figure that alternates feet in a 16th-note subdivision against the guitar's palm-muted riff. The four-tom configuration (10", 12", 14", 16") supplies the fill vocabulary for the album's frequent tempo and dynamic shifts, letting Bittner sweep across a full tonal range on the extended arrangement of "A Fire in Babylon."

### Snare: Steel Crack for the Breakthrough Record

Bittner's 14" x 6.5" steel snare on *The Art of Balance* concentrates resonance at higher frequencies than wood, producing a crack that sits naturally above dense, distorted guitar. On "Idle Hands," the snare's role is structural: it marks the backbeat against the double-kick pattern's constant subdivision, giving the listener a fixed rhythmic reference point inside a dense verse.

### Sabian HHX: Cutting Through the Breakthrough Mix

Bittner's Sabian HHX and HH cymbal setup had to compete with one of the hottest, most forward guitar mixes in the Shadows Fall catalog. On "Idle Hands," the HHX hi-hats carry the rhythmic pulse above the palm-muted riff and double-kick pattern, staying audible through the verse's densest moments. The 16" and 18" HHX crashes split duty across the album's dynamic range, the HH 20" ride's defined bell gives a pitched alternative to the hi-hat pulse, and the HHX china punctuates the heaviest breakdown passages.

### DW 9002: The Engine Behind "Idle Hands"

The DW 9002 double bass pedal is the mechanical engine behind "Idle Hands"'s signature verse pattern. The pattern's impact depends entirely on both feet delivering matched beater velocity; the 9002's dual-chain drive system delivers that matched action, translating Bittner's foot technique into consistent, even beater strokes on both sides.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums did Jason Bittner use on The Art of Balance?**
A: On *The Art of Balance* (2002), Jason Bittner played a Tama Starclassic Performer B/B kit — birch/bubinga hybrid shells in a double bass drum configuration (two 22" x 18" kicks), with 10" and 12" rack toms and 14" and 16" floor toms. This kit remained Bittner's setup throughout Shadows Fall's peak NWOAHM period, carrying through to *The War Within* (2004). See [Jason Bittner at MetalForge](/drummer/jason-bittner) for full career gear context.

**Q: What cymbals did Jason Bittner use on The Art of Balance?**
A: Jason Bittner played Sabian HHX and HH series cymbals — HHX 14" hi-hats, HHX 16" and 18" crash cymbals, an HH 20" ride, and an HHX china. The HHX series's hand-hammered B20 bronze construction and brilliant finish emphasize upper harmonics that project above Zeuss's hot, forward guitar mix.

**Q: Who produced The Art of Balance?**
A: *The Art of Balance* was produced by Zeuss (Chris Harris) at Planet Z Studios in Hadley, Massachusetts. Zeuss's production is rawer and more aggressive than the polish that followed on *The War Within* (2004) — drums sit hot and forward in the mix. The album debuted at #15 on Billboard's Top Independent Albums chart and helped set up Shadows Fall's eventual major-label move to Atlantic Records.

**Q: What makes "Idle Hands" a technically distinctive drum track?**
A: "Idle Hands" is built around a syncopated double-kick verse pattern that alternates feet in a rapid 16th-note figure against the guitar's palm-muted riff — a pattern that requires matched, even beater velocity from both feet to read as precise rather than uneven. Jason Bittner's execution of that pattern on the DW 9002 double bass pedal made "Idle Hands" the most widely cited reference track for NWOAHM double-kick technique.
