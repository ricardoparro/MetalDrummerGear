# Mike Portnoy's Drum Setup on Dream Theater's Awake (1994)

> Complete breakdown of Mike Portnoy's drum gear on Dream Theater's Awake (1994). Discover the Tama Artstar II/Granstar kit, Sabian cymbals, Iron Cobra pedals, and the darker, heavier sonic shift that defined the band's post-Images and Words era.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup

**Band / Album:** Dream Theater — *Awake* (1994)
**Genre:** Progressive Metal

## Overview

Released on October 4, 1994 by EastWest/Atlantic, "Awake" is Dream Theater's third studio album and the recording that proved Images and Words was no fluke. Where the 1992 breakthrough had balanced melodic accessibility with progressive ambition, Awake leaned hard into darkness, weight, and complexity. The album reached #32 on the Billboard 200 — the highest charting position any Dream Theater record would achieve for over a decade — and singles "Lie," "6:00," and "The Mirror" carried the band's heavier new identity onto MTV and rock radio at a moment when grunge and alternative still dominated the cultural conversation.

Recording took place in the spring and summer of 1994 at Devonshire Studios in North Hollywood, California, with John Purdell and Duane Baron co-producing alongside the band themselves. Purdell and Baron had recently produced Ozzy Osbourne's "No More Tears" and Alice Cooper's "Hey Stoopid," and their sonic fingerprints are unmistakable on Awake — fatter low end, denser guitar tones, a more compressed and aggressive overall presentation than the airier Images and Words sound. Mike Portnoy's drums sit at the center of this shift. Where the BearTracks sessions had captured a relatively dry, articulate kit, Devonshire wrapped his drums in a heavier ambient field that matched the album's confrontational atmosphere.

Awake also carries a specific historical weight: it is keyboardist Kevin Moore's final album with Dream Theater. Moore had been a founding member and co-wrote much of the album's most emotionally resonant material — "Space-Dye Vest," "Lifting Shadows Off a Dream," and the haunting passages of "Scarred" — and his decision to leave the band shortly after the recording wrapped imbued the album with a sense of farewell that listeners and critics noticed immediately. Portnoy's drumming reflects this gravity. The grooves are heavier, the dynamics more architectural, and the melodic tom work — already a signature on Images and Words — became more compositional and less ornamental.

Gear-wise, the Awake sessions document Mike Portnoy still firmly in his Tama era — Artstar II and Granstar shells, Sabian cymbals (he had been a Sabian endorser throughout the 90s), and the then-new Tama Iron Cobra pedal that had launched in 1993. This is the bridge configuration between the Images and Words birch sound and the Starclassic Maple kit he would eventually adopt later in the decade. This article breaks down every piece of gear Mike used to record Awake and traces how his choices served the album's darker, heavier vision.

### Mike's Studio Kit: The Tama Artstar II / Granstar Configuration

By the spring 1994 Awake sessions, Mike Portnoy had refined and expanded the Tama setup he had used on Images and Words. The core kit remained Tama Artstar II / Granstar birch — Tama's flagship professional shells of the early-to-mid 90s — but the configuration grew to match the more architecturally demanding music. The expanded tom range, with 8", 10", 12", 14", and 16" sizes, gave Portnoy nearly two full octaves of melodic vocabulary and let him construct the wide-arc fills that defined tracks like "6:00" and "Erotomania."

The double 22" bass drum configuration carried over from Images and Words, but the playing approach evolved. Where the 1992 album used double bass as syncopation and accent, the Awake sessions deployed it as sustained low-end pressure — the relentless eighth-note pulse under "The Mirror," the driving sixteenth patterns in the "A Mind Beside Itself" suite, and the punishing footwork in "Lifting Shadows Off a Dream" all required a kit that could deliver weight as well as articulation. The birch shells of the Artstar II/Granstar handled this well: birch's pronounced low end and crisp high attack let the bass drums punch through Devonshire's dense ambient capture without losing definition.

The toms themselves were tuned slightly lower than on Images and Words, matching the heavier overall tonal palette. The 8" and 10" rack toms still spoke with the bright crack Portnoy preferred for fast melodic passages, but the 12" and 14" rack toms and 16" floor tom were tensioned to give up more body and sustain — useful for the more cinematic fill writing across "Scarred" and "Space-Dye Vest."

The Star-Cast mounting system — Tama's then-recent innovation that suspended each tom from its lugs rather than drilling through the shell — preserved shell resonance and was crucial for the longer sustain Portnoy needed in this era. Visually, the black lacquer finish replaced the Images and Words purple, signaling the darker thematic identity of the new record.

### The Bell Brass Snare That Defined the Sound

The single most important sonic upgrade Mike Portnoy made for Awake was the snare drum. He moved from the Artstar II brass snare he had used on Images and Words to the legendary Tama Bell Brass — a heavy, dense brass shell prized in the 80s and 90s for its combination of cracking attack, fat body, and almost gong-like sustain. The Bell Brass was the same shell formula behind some of the most celebrated rock snare sounds of the era, and on Awake it gave Portnoy a snare voice that could anchor both whisper-quiet passages and full-throated double-bass attacks.

At 14" x 6.5", the Bell Brass had the depth to project under heavy guitars and the sensitivity to articulate the rapid ghost-note work that runs through "6:00," "Caught in a Web," and "Voices." The snare's tonal weight is one of the first things that distinguishes Awake's sound from Images and Words — the crack is fatter, the sustain is denser, and the drum sits lower in the spectrum without sacrificing definition.

Co-producers John Purdell and Duane Baron captured the snare with both a top microphone for attack and a bottom microphone for snare-wire response, then compressed and EQ'd the result heavily to match the album's denser overall mix. This is a more processed snare sound than David Prater achieved on Images and Words, but the processing serves the music — Awake's darker emotional palette demanded a snare with more presence and weight.

Portnoy tuned the Bell Brass in the medium range for this recording, slightly looser than his Images and Words approach. The looser tuning gave the drum more body and resonance, supporting the longer melodic phrases that recur across the album.

### The Sabian Setup: HH and AA Series

Mike Portnoy was a Sabian endorser throughout the 1990s, and the Awake sessions document the deepest version of his Sabian setup before the eventual move toward the Artisan series later in the decade. The 1994 rig combined the brighter, more cutting AA series (Sabian's automated-hammered cymbals) with the darker, hand-hammered HH series for tonal variety across the album's wide emotional range.

The 14" AA Rock Hi-Hats were heavier than the standard AA pair, giving Portnoy the weight to chop through Devonshire's denser mix without losing articulation. Listen to the opening of "6:00" — those hi-hats cut through everything around them, even as the guitars stack into a wall of low end.

The HH 17" Medium-Thin Crash and the HH 19" Crash provided the darker, more musical accent voices on the kit. Hand-hammered cymbals produce a more complex overtone structure than machine-hammered models, and Portnoy used these for the more emotionally weighted moments — the climactic crashes in "Space-Dye Vest," the layered accents across "Scarred," and the transition moments in "The Mirror."

The AA 16" and 18" crashes filled the brighter accent roles — quick, cutting hits for tempo changes and fill endpoints. The variety between AA and HH gave Portnoy a palette where every crash had a slightly different tonal character, which served the through-composed sectional writing of tracks like "A Mind Beside Itself."

The HH 22" Raw Bell Dry Ride is one of the signature cymbal choices of the Awake era. Dark, dry, and articulate, with a raw unlathed bell that cuts through any density, this ride became closely associated with Portnoy's mid-90s sound. It rides cleanly through "Lifting Shadows Off a Dream" and provides the bell accents that punctuate "Caught in a Web."

The AA 18" Chinese added trashy aggression — essential for the heaviest passages, where Portnoy uses it as a punctuation mark on top of the bass drum hits.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Mike Portnoy use on Awake?**

A: Mike Portnoy recorded Awake in 1994 using a Tama Artstar II / Granstar kit with birch shells and Star-Cast mounting. The setup featured dual 22x16 inch bass drums, rack toms at 8, 10, 12, and 14 inches, and a 16 inch floor tom. The Artstar II / Granstar series was Tama's flagship professional shell platform of the early-to-mid 90s, and its birch construction delivered the punchy low end and crisp attack needed for the album's heavier sonic identity. Portnoy stayed with Tama Artstar II / Granstar shells before eventually transitioning to the Starclassic Maple series later in the decade.

**Q: What snare did Mike Portnoy use on Awake?**

A: Mike Portnoy used a Tama Bell Brass 14x6.5 inch snare on Awake, replacing the Artstar II brass snare he had used on Images and Words. The Bell Brass shell is a dense, heavy brass alloy prized for its combination of cracking attack, fat body, and long sustain. Co-producers John Purdell and Duane Baron captured the snare with top and bottom mics plus heavy compression, producing one of the most distinctive snare sounds of the mid-90s progressive era. The Bell Brass voice anchors tracks like "The Mirror," "6:00," and "Lie."

**Q: What cymbals did Mike Portnoy use on Awake?**

A: Mike Portnoy was a Sabian endorser throughout the 1990s and used a combined HH and AA series setup on Awake. The rig included AA 14 inch Rock Hi-Hats, AA crashes at 16 and 18 inches, HH Medium-Thin and Medium crashes at 17 and 19 inches, a Sabian HH 22 inch Raw Bell Dry Ride, an AA 18 inch Chinese, and AA splashes at 8 and 10 inches. The combination of brighter AA cymbals and darker hand-hammered HH cymbals gave Portnoy tonal variety across the album's wide emotional range.

**Q: What pedals did Mike Portnoy use on Awake?**

A: Mike Portnoy used the then-new Tama Iron Cobra Power Glide double pedal on the 1994 Awake sessions. The Iron Cobra series had launched in 1993, making Awake one of the earliest major studio recordings to capture the pedal in action. The Power Glide cam gave Portnoy a heavier, more powerful feel ideal for the album's sustained double-bass passages — most prominently the relentless eighth-note pulse under "The Mirror" and the rapid sixteenth-note work in "Erotomania."

**Q: How does the Awake drum sound differ from Images and Words?**

A: Awake is heavier, denser, and darker than Images and Words. Three factors drove the shift. (1) The snare changed from a Tama Artstar II brass snare to a Tama Bell Brass — a fatter, more sustained voice. (2) The studio changed from BearTracks (Suffern, NY) to Devonshire (North Hollywood, CA), which has a denser room signature. (3) The production team changed from David Prater to John Purdell and Duane Baron, whose work on Ozzy Osbourne and Alice Cooper records brought heavier compression, fatter low end, and a more aggressive overall presentation. The toms are also tuned slightly lower on Awake, and the Iron Cobra pedal gave the bass drums more weight than the earlier pedal Portnoy used.
