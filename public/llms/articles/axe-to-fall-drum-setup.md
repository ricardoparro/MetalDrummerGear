# Converge Axe to Fall Drum Setup: Ben Koller's 2009 Gear Breakdown

> Discover the drum setup Ben Koller used to record Converge's Axe to Fall — a genre-spanning metalcore landmark featuring members of Neurosis and Cave In.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Ben Koller](/llms/drummers/ben-koller.md)
**Band / Album:** Converge — *Axe to Fall* (2009)
**Genre:** Metalcore / Post-Hardcore

## Overview

Released on October 20, 2009 on Epitaph Records, Converge's *Axe to Fall* is the band's most genre-spanning record — moving between blast-beat hardcore, grinding doom, post-hardcore melody, and progressive metalcore within a single cohesive statement. Guest vocalists Scott Kelly (Neurosis), Stephen Brodsky (Cave In), and Hamilton Jordan (Genghis Tron) each pushed [Ben Koller](/drummer/ben-koller) to adapt his approach to different musical contexts while maintaining Converge's identity. Produced by Kurt Ballou at Godcity Studio in Salem, MA. The album reaches the widest audience of any Converge record while losing none of the intensity that defines the band.

### Tama Starclassic Performer B/B: The Mature Setup

By the time Converge recorded *Axe to Fall* in 2009, Ben Koller's setup had evolved significantly from the utilitarian touring kit of the *Jane Doe* era. The Tama Starclassic Performer B/B had become his primary instrument — a professional-grade kit with birch/bubinga shell construction that provided the tonal complexity his increasingly nuanced playing demanded.

The birch/bubinga combination is particularly well-suited to *Axe to Fall*'s dynamic range. Birch shells provide the focused, punchy attack needed for the album's blast beat sections and hardcore passages. Bubinga adds warmth and a deeper fundamental that gives the toms body in the album's slower, doom-influenced moments. When Scott Kelly's Neurosis-influenced passages require Koller to play with heavy, deliberate strokes rather than rapid-fire aggression, the bubinga component provides the tonal weight those moments demand.

The compact 10"/12"/16" tom configuration that Koller favors serves *Axe to Fall*'s compositional demands efficiently. Kurt Ballou's production on *Axe to Fall* gave the Starclassic Performer B/B its most defined recorded sound: toms with both impact and pitch definition, a kit that remains recognizably consistent across the album's diverse tempos and textures.

### The Snare: Authority Across Genre Contexts

The snare on *Axe to Fall* must serve multiple musical masters within a single album. In the hardcore and blast beat passages, it needs cutting attack and fast decay. In the doom-influenced passages where Scott Kelly's Neurosis influence is most audible, it needs weight and body. The Tama S.L.P. (Studio Legendary Percussion) snare at 14" x 6.5" covers both extremes.

The 6.5" depth is key to the snare's versatility. Deeper than the standard 5.5" shell, the extra depth provides more air column — giving the drum fuller body on rimshots played at medium velocity, while still producing a cutting crack when hit with full force. At the album's slower tempos, that body is audible; at full blast beat speed, the crack asserts itself.

Kurt Ballou's snare recording on *Axe to Fall* benefits from the more refined approach he had developed by 2009. The snare sits clearly in the mix across the album's dynamic range — audible in the quietest passages without becoming overwhelming in the most intense, and consistent in character whether Koller is playing ghost-note grooves or flat-out blasts.

### Zildjian K and A Custom: The Genre-Spanning Arsenal

Ben Koller's Zildjian K Custom and A Custom cymbal setup on *Axe to Fall* serves the album's genre breadth with the same tonal intelligence as the kit configuration. The K Custom series provides complexity and darkness suited to the album's post-hardcore and doom passages; the A Custom series provides the brightness and explosive attack of classic hardcore crashes.

The K Custom Dark Hi-Hats (14") anchor the album's rhythmic foundation across all tempos. In the faster hardcore passages, they produce the controlled chick needed for rapid closed-hat patterns without excessive wash. In the slower doom-influenced sections — particularly those co-written with the Neurosis-influenced energy Scott Kelly brings — the K Custom's darker character adds tonal depth to the hi-hat work that standard A series hats couldn't provide.

The A Custom crash pairing (18" and 19") are Koller's primary section-ending tools throughout the album. The A Custom series's bright, cutting attack ensures dynamic transitions are unmistakable, registering clearly over even the densest Converge guitar arrangements.

The K Custom Ride (21") earns its place on *Axe to Fall* more than on most Converge records because of the album's extended slower passages. Where *Jane Doe* rarely called for sustained ride patterns, *Axe to Fall*'s doom-influenced tracks require Koller to use the ride as a sustained timekeeping voice. The K Custom's complex bell definition and washy body serve that function precisely.

The A Custom China (18") marks the album's moments of absolute peak aggression — where Jacob Bannon's vocals peak, where Kurt Ballou's guitars reach maximum density, and where the collaboration with doom metal influences like Scott Kelly reaches its most intense expression. The China's fast, trashy decay adds violence without lingering sustain.

## FAQ

**What drum setup did Ben Koller use on Converge's Axe to Fall?**
Ben Koller recorded Converge's Axe to Fall (2009) on his Tama Starclassic Performer B/B kit — birch/bubinga shell construction with 10", 12", and 16" toms and a 22" bass drum. His cymbal setup included Zildjian K Custom Dark Hi-Hats (14"), A Custom Crashes (18" and 19"), K Custom Ride (21"), A Custom China (18"), and K Splash (10"). The album was produced by Kurt Ballou at Godcity Studio in Salem, MA. Axe to Fall represents Koller's mature setup at the point where his gear and playing philosophy had fully crystallized.

**Who are the guest vocalists on Converge's Axe to Fall?**
Converge's Axe to Fall (2009) features guest vocalists from three significant heavy music acts: Scott Kelly of Neurosis appears on "Worms Will Feed / Rats Will Feast," bringing doom-influenced post-metal intensity; Stephen Brodsky of Cave In contributes to "Losing Battle," connecting the track to Cave In's melodic post-hardcore character; and Hamilton Jordan of Genghis Tron appears on "Cruel Bloom," adding mechanical mathcore precision. These collaborations required Ben Koller to adapt his drumming to each guest's musical context while maintaining Converge's identity throughout.

**How does Axe to Fall differ from Jane Doe in terms of drumming?**
Ben Koller's drumming on Axe to Fall (2009) is more genre-versatile than his work on Jane Doe (2001). Jane Doe demands sustained ferocity — 47 minutes of relentless intensity at the ceiling of hardcore drumming. Axe to Fall demands versatility: the ability to shift between blast-beat hardcore, slow doom passages, melodic post-hardcore, and progressive metalcore, often within a single track. The production also differs — Axe to Fall's Kurt Ballou mix is more refined and defined than Jane Doe's rawer approach, with cleaner separation between kit elements and a tighter low-end drum sound that serves the album's dynamic range.

**What label released Converge's Axe to Fall?**
Converge's Axe to Fall was released on October 20, 2009 by Epitaph Records — a major independent punk and metal label known for Bad Religion, NOFX, Rancid, and many others. The Epitaph partnership gave Axe to Fall the widest commercial release of any Converge album to that point, helping the band reach audiences from doom metal, post-hardcore, and progressive metal backgrounds. Axe to Fall remains one of Epitaph's most critically acclaimed heavy music releases.

**Is Axe to Fall considered Converge's best album?**
Converge's Axe to Fall (2009) is frequently cited alongside Jane Doe (2001) as the band's finest work. Jane Doe is more influential and historically significant — widely considered the more important album for the hardcore and mathcore genres. Axe to Fall is arguably more musically versatile and accessible, reaching a broader audience through its genre-spanning approach and the Epitaph Records platform. The two albums represent different peaks: Jane Doe as the definitive statement of extreme hardcore, Axe to Fall as the demonstration of how Converge could expand without compromising.
