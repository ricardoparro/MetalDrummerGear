## Overview

Released on November 1, 1993, *Bastards* marked a pivotal moment in Motörhead history — the first studio album to feature Mikkey Dee behind the kit. When Phil "Philthy Animal" Taylor departed Motörhead for the second time in early 1992 due to health issues, the band faced a genuine crisis. Taylor had been one-third of the classic Motörhead lineup alongside Lemmy Kilmister and "Fast" Eddie Clarke, and his chaotic, thundering style was deeply embedded in the band's DNA.

The decision to recruit Mikkey Dee — a Gothenburg-born Swede who had already impressed the metal world with King Diamond's *Abigail* (1987) and subsequent albums — was inspired. Where Taylor brought barely-controlled fury, Dee brought precision power: the ability to deliver devastating volume with technical discipline, maintaining pocket-locked grooves even at extreme tempos. Lemmy recognized immediately what Dee brought to the table. "Mikkey can do anything," Lemmy once said. "He's the best drummer I ever played with."

*Bastards* served as Mikkey Dee's audition tape to the world. The album showcased tracks like the relentless "Burner," the funk-metal experiment "Born to Raise Hell" (featuring Ice-T and Ugly Kid Joe vocalist Whitfield Crane), and the thundering "Death or Glory." What unified every track was a drum foundation that was simultaneously heavier and more controlled than anything in Motörhead's previous decade.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Artstar II / Swingstar (maple shells, Black finish)
- **Bass Drum:** 22"
- **Toms:** 10", 12", 13" rack toms; 16", 18" floor toms
- **Snare:** Tama Artstar II Steel, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Paiste Giant Beat 15" hi-hats; Paiste 2002 crashes (18", 20"), 22" ride, 18" China
- **Pedals:** Tama Iron Cobra (chain-drive)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (toms/snare), Remo Powerstroke 3 (kick)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension for crack and projection

## Gear Details

### Tama Kit: Precision for the World's Loudest Band

During his early Motörhead years, Mikkey Dee built his arsenal around Tama drums — specifically the Artstar II and Swingstar series, which were among the finest professional kits available in the early 1990s. Tama's Japanese engineering brought a level of consistency and durability that suited the punishing demands of Motörhead's touring and recording schedule.

The 22" bass drum was the foundation of Dee's setup on *Bastards*. Unlike the oversized bass drums popular among death and thrash metal drummers of the era, Dee kept to the classic 22" configuration — delivering the focused, punchy low-end thump that Motörhead's hard rock approach demanded, a drum sound that locked with Lemmy's bass guitar without competing with it.

The multi-tom setup gave Dee tonal range for both Motörhead's straightforward rock tracks and more complex arrangements. Three rack toms (10", 12", 13") and dual floor toms (16", 18") provided options for dramatic fills without overwhelming the song-focused arrangements that defined the *Bastards* material.

Tama's Artstar II shells were prized for even sustain and attack — qualities that worked in the studio environment. Where Motörhead's rawness had sometimes masked kit limitations in earlier recordings, Dee's Tama setup delivered a drum sound with genuine definition and character.

### The Steel Snare: Cutting Through the Loudest Band Alive

Dee's preference for steel-shell snares dated back to his King Diamond years. The steel construction delivers an attack and projection that aluminum and wood cannot match at high volumes. In the Motörhead context — where Lemmy's bass often occupied a "third guitar" frequency range — the snare needed to claim its own sonic space emphatically.

The 6.5" depth provided body and sustain, while the tight steel shell kept the high-frequency attack prominent. Dee tuned the drum on the higher side of medium — enough crack for live volumes without sacrificing the musicality that distinguishes his playing from simpler battering.

On tracks like "Burner" and "Death or Glory," the snare's articulation is remarkable. Ghost notes emerge between primary hits, giving the groove a pocket that Taylor's more chaotic approach rarely provided.

### Paiste Giant Beat and 2002: Period-Correct Bronze for Maximum Volume

Dee's cymbal setup during the *Bastards* era combined Paiste's Giant Beat line with the more aggressive Paiste 2002 series. Both share Paiste's CuSn8 bronze alloy, but the Giant Beat's warmer character contrasts with the 2002's brighter, more cutting attack.

The Giant Beat 15" hi-hats — larger than the standard 14" — provided more body and volume essential when playing next to Lemmy's bass rig. The Paiste 2002 crashes (18" and 20") and 22" ride handled accents and time-keeping duties. The 2002 has been a fixture in rock and heavy metal since the 1970s, used by Lars Ulrich on *Kill 'Em All* (1983) and Dave Lombardo on *Reign in Blood* (1986) — its combination of cutting attack and warm sustain made it ideal for Dee's role in Motörhead's mix.

### Tama Iron Cobra: Mechanical Precision for Deliberate Kick Placement

The Tama Iron Cobra was among the most respected bass drum pedals of the early 1990s. Its chain-drive mechanism delivered a consistent feel suited to rock drumming applications. For the *Bastards* sessions, Dee's kick technique emphasized placement over speed — heavy, deliberate hits that anchored the groove rather than machine-gun double-bass runs.

## Track Analysis

### Burner
High-tempo track that established Dee's Motörhead credentials immediately. Driving eighth-note hi-hat pattern with precise backbeat placement — notably more controlled than Taylor's approach. The kick drum locks with Lemmy's bass on the downbeat, demonstrating the pocket feel Dee brought to the band. Fills are economical: short, precise, placed to emphasize riff changes.

### Born to Raise Hell
Mid-tempo groove track featuring Ice-T and Whitfield Crane — the most commercially recognizable cut on *Bastards*. Dee adapts his playing for a funk-adjacent feel, demonstrating versatility beyond pure metal. Snare on 2 and 4 with ghost notes throughout — funk-influenced technique applied to hard rock. The groove matters more than the power here; Dee demonstrates restraint as a creative tool.

### Death or Glory
One of the album's faster tracks. Dee's feet move with precision that Taylor rarely achieved at this tempo. A consistent double bass pattern drives the verses with controlled power, and crash cymbal use is deliberate — only at genuine musical climax points, not on every measure.

## FAQs

**What drums did Mikkey Dee use on Bastards?**
On *Bastards* (1993), Mikkey Dee used a Tama Artstar II / Swingstar kit with maple shells — 22" bass drum, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, and 16"/18" floor toms. This was his first studio album with Motörhead, having joined the band in early 1992 after replacing Phil "Philthy Animal" Taylor. He would later transition to Yamaha drums, but his Tama era produced some of his most visceral Motörhead recordings.

**When did Mikkey Dee join Motörhead?**
Mikkey Dee joined Motörhead in January 1992, replacing Phil "Philthy Animal" Taylor for the second time. Taylor's departure was health-related. Dee came to the band with impressive credentials from his work with King Diamond. Lemmy famously called him "the best drummer I ever played with." *Bastards* (1993) was his first full studio album with Motörhead.

**How did Mikkey Dee's style differ from Phil Taylor?**
Phil Taylor's drumming was characterized by barely-controlled fury — enormous energy with a looseness that sometimes threatened to overwhelm the groove. His performances on *Overkill* (1979) and *Ace of Spades* (1980) have enduring charm precisely because they sound dangerous. Mikkey Dee brought Swedish precision combined with equal power. Where Taylor might flam accidentally and make it work through momentum, Dee placed every stroke deliberately. His ghost note technique and more controlled fills serve the song rather than express the moment. Neither approach is definitively superior — they represent two legitimate creative philosophies applied to the same band.

**What cymbals did Mikkey Dee use on Bastards?**
Mikkey Dee used a Paiste setup combining Giant Beat 15" hi-hats with Paiste 2002 crashes (18" and 20"), a 22" ride, and an 18" China cymbal. Both series use Paiste's CuSn8 bronze alloy, delivering a warm, complex sound that cuts through dense mixes naturally. Dee would later move toward Zildjian cymbals as his endorsement relationships evolved.
