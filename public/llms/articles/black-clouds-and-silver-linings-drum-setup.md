## Overview

Released on June 23, 2009, *Black Clouds & Silver Linings* is Dream Theater's tenth studio album and, as history would reveal, **Mike Portnoy's final studio recording with the band**. Debuting at **#6 on the Billboard 200** and **#1 on the Billboard Top Rock Albums** chart, the record was among the best-charting releases of Dream Theater's career. The album received a **Grammy nomination for Best Metal Performance** for "A Rite of Passage" at the 52nd Grammy Awards in 2010 — the band's second Grammy nomination in three albums, following the *Systematic Chaos* nomination for "The Dark Eternal Night" in 2008.

The album is an extraordinary artistic statement in retrospect: a 77-minute record spanning brutal prog-metal riffing, a 19-minute epic, and the most emotionally devastating performance of Portnoy's Dream Theater career. "The Best of Times" — a 13-minute track Portnoy wrote for his father, Howard, who was dying of pancreatic cancer — contains Portnoy's most personal drumming. Howard Portnoy passed away before the album was released; the track became a tribute rather than a dedication before the world ever heard it.

Gear-wise, *Black Clouds & Silver Linings* continued the platform Portnoy had established on *Systematic Chaos* (2007): Tama Starclassic **Bubinga** shells, Sabian **HHX Evolution** cymbals, and **DW 9000** double pedals. After the significant transitions of the mid-decade, this was Portnoy in a period of gear consolidation. Two years of touring *Systematic Chaos* had proven every component, and the *Black Clouds* sessions reflect a drummer completely comfortable with his entire rig.

The sessions took place at Cove City Sound Studios in Glen Cove, New York — a change from the Avatar Studios environment of *Train of Thought* and *Systematic Chaos*. John Petrucci and Portnoy self-produced again, with the goal of capturing drum performances capable of ranging from the aggressive double-bass assault of "A Rite of Passage" to the intimate, emotionally raw dynamics of "Wither" and "The Best of Times."

This is a complete breakdown of every piece of drum gear Mike Portnoy used to record what would become — though no one knew it yet — his farewell to Dream Theater.

### Mike's 2009 Studio Kit: Tama Starclassic Bubinga

For *Black Clouds & Silver Linings*, Mike Portnoy continued with the Tama Starclassic **Bubinga** kit that had defined his sound on *Systematic Chaos* (2007). Having made the transition from Maple to Bubinga in the mid-decade and spent two years touring the expanded configuration extensively, Portnoy arrived at the *Black Clouds* sessions with a drum kit he knew completely.

Bubinga's sonic character — sharper attack, tighter decay, and more articulate transients compared to Maple — remained the right choice for an album that again demanded extraordinary range. "A Rite of Passage" required the same relentless attack that "Constant Motion" and "The Dark Eternal Night" had demanded on *Systematic Chaos*. "The Count of Tuscany" required the bubinga's clarity to keep individual drum voices distinct across 19 minutes of evolving musical architecture. And "The Best of Times" required the shells to respond with precision and restraint across one of the most emotionally charged performances of Portnoy's career.

The configuration maintained the large ~13-drum layout that had been Portnoy's standard since *Train of Thought*: double 22"x18" bass drums, five rack toms (8" through 14"), and three floor toms (14", 16", 18"). Tama's Star-Cast mounting system preserved the bubinga shells' resonance across all positions. Every drum in the configuration had a clear musical purpose across the album's five main tracks.

### Snare for Portnoy's Final DT Chapter

For *Black Clouds & Silver Linings*, Portnoy continued with the Tama Starclassic Bubinga snare that had served him on *Systematic Chaos*. The 6.5" depth and bubinga construction provided the sharp, punchy attack that the album's more aggressive passages demanded while retaining the sensitivity needed for the dynamic restraint of "Wither" and "The Best of Times."

On "A Rite of Passage" — the Grammy-nominated track — the snare's tight bubinga character anchors the aggressive groove with the authority needed to cut through the dense guitar and keyboard layers. On "The Best of Times," the same snare is played with a completely different touch: measured, deliberate, and emotionally precise — demonstrating the full dynamic range Portnoy could extract from a single instrument.

### The Sabian HHX Evolution Setup: Consolidated and Refined

By *Black Clouds & Silver Linings* (2009), Portnoy's Sabian HHX Evolution setup was a fully proven quantity — two years of *Systematic Chaos* touring had tested every cymbal's character across every conceivable musical context. The HHX Evolution's defining traits — hand-hammered construction, darker baseline character, complex overtone wash — were precisely matched to the dual identity of *Black Clouds*: heavy and aggressive on "A Rite of Passage," atmospheric and emotionally nuanced on "The Best of Times" and "Wither."

The 14" HHX Evolution hi-hats provide warm, smoky articulation that serves both the relentless double-time sections of "A Rite of Passage" and the measured, intimate passages of "Wither." The 17"-through-19" Evolution crashes respond with authority at full dynamics while retaining a complex wash that adds dimension rather than just volume. The Evolution 21" Ride — with its complex bell and hand-hammered bow — is central to the long-form prog architecture of "The Count of Tuscany," providing shimmering sustain across the track's 19-minute arc.

The HHX Evolution setup's ability to serve both heavy and delicate musical contexts without compromise was a key component of what allowed the *Black Clouds* production to accommodate such extreme dynamic range within a single 77-minute album.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What gear did Mike Portnoy use on Black Clouds & Silver Linings?**

A: Mike Portnoy recorded Black Clouds & Silver Linings (2009) on a Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit — the same proven platform as Systematic Chaos (2007), seasoned over two years of touring. The configuration maintained approximately 13 drums: double 22"x18" bass drums, five rack toms (8", 10", 12", 13", 14"), and three floor toms (14", 16", 18"). Cymbals were Sabian HHX Evolution — hand-hammered, darker-toned, and capable of serving both the album's heaviest and most atmospheric passages. Pedals were DW 9000 double pedals, known for smooth roller-bearing cam action and dynamic sensitivity. Sticks were his Vic Firth Mike Portnoy Signature model. For his full career profile, see the [Mike Portnoy drummer profile](/drummer/mike-portnoy).

**Q: Why did Mike Portnoy leave Dream Theater after this album?**

A: Mike Portnoy announced his departure from Dream Theater in September 2010, approximately 15 months after Black Clouds & Silver Linings was released. In his public statement, Portnoy cited exhaustion from the relentless pace of the band — he had been touring and recording with Dream Theater for 25 years, often simultaneously managing side projects including Transatlantic, Flying Colors, and Liquid Tension Experiment. He had asked the band for a break during discussions about the next album cycle; when the other members decided to move forward without pausing, he felt he had no choice but to step away. The departure shocked the prog metal world, as Portnoy was widely considered the band's emotional engine and co-founding presence. Mike Mangini was subsequently announced as his replacement in April 2011. For the post-Portnoy era, see the [A Dramatic Turn of Events drum setup article](/articles/a-dramatic-turn-of-events-drum-setup).

**Q: What is the saddest song on Black Clouds & Silver Linings?**

A: 'The Best of Times' is universally considered the most emotionally devastating track on Black Clouds & Silver Linings — and arguably in all of Dream Theater's catalog. Mike Portnoy wrote the song for his father Howard, who was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer during the album's recording sessions. Howard Portnoy passed away before the album was released in June 2009, meaning the song became a tribute rather than a dedication before the world ever heard it. The drumming on 'The Best of Times' is measured, restrained, and emotionally precise — fundamentally different from anything else Portnoy recorded with Dream Theater. See the [Mike Portnoy drummer profile](/drummer/mike-portnoy) for the complete story of his Dream Theater era.

**Q: Was Black Clouds & Silver Linings nominated for a Grammy?**

A: Yes. Dream Theater received a Grammy nomination for Best Metal Performance at the 52nd Grammy Awards (February 2010) for 'A Rite of Passage' from Black Clouds & Silver Linings — the band's second Grammy nomination in three albums, following the Systematic Chaos nomination for 'The Dark Eternal Night' at the 50th Grammy Awards in 2008. 'A Rite of Passage' features some of Portnoy's most aggressive and disciplined double-bass drumming — a relentless foundation beneath the album's most direct prog-metal arrangement. See the [Systematic Chaos drum setup article](/articles/systematic-chaos-drum-setup) for the 2007 Grammy nomination comparison.

**Q: How does the Black Clouds kit differ from Systematic Chaos?**

A: The two setups are closely related — Black Clouds & Silver Linings continued the Tama Starclassic Bubinga / Sabian HHX Evolution / DW 9000 platform that Portnoy had established on Systematic Chaos (2007). The primary differences were contextual rather than gear-based: the Black Clouds kit arrived at the sessions with two years of touring experience behind it, and the recording environment shifted from Avatar Studios (Systematic Chaos) to Cove City Sound Studios in Glen Cove, NY. Musically, the same gear served a far wider emotional range on Black Clouds — from the Grammy-nominated aggression of 'A Rite of Passage' to the intimate restraint of 'The Best of Times' and 'Wither.' See the [Systematic Chaos drum setup article](/articles/systematic-chaos-drum-setup) for the full 2007 comparison.
