# Blunt Force Trauma Drum Setup: Igor Cavalera's Cavalera Conspiracy Second Strike (2011)

> Complete drum gear breakdown for Cavalera Conspiracy's Blunt Force Trauma (2011). Igor Cavalera's Tama Starclassic kit, Paiste RUDE cymbals, and the heavier, tighter follow-up to Inflikted — the brothers-reunited arc continues.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Igor Cavalera](/llms/drummers/igor-cavalera.md)
**Band / Album:** Cavalera Conspiracy — *Blunt Force Trauma* (2011)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Death Metal

## Overview

Released in March 2011 through Roadrunner Records, "Blunt Force Trauma" is Cavalera Conspiracy's second album — and it is a harder, more focused record than its predecessor. Where "Inflikted" had the energy of reunion, the excitement of brothers making metal together again after more than a decade apart, "Blunt Force Trauma" has something different: conviction. This is a band that knows exactly what it is and exactly what it wants to say.

For Igor Cavalera, the three years between albums were productive in ways that show throughout "Blunt Force Trauma." The Cavalera Conspiracy live experience — touring extensively behind "Inflikted" and reconnecting with the physicality of performing aggressive metal every night — sharpened his playing. The drum performances on this album are tighter, more decisive, and more dynamically varied than on the debut. Igor sounds like someone who has fully committed to this creative partnership rather than testing whether it still works.

Producer Logan Mader returned for the second album, maintaining continuity with the debut's sound while pushing the production toward greater clarity and weight. The drums on "Blunt Force Trauma" sit with more authority in the mix. The kick is heavier, the snare cuts deeper, and the cymbals breathe more openly than on "Inflikted." These are incremental improvements in production, but they accumulate into an album that sounds more fully realized as a recording.

The Cavalera brothers' creative dynamic had deepened by 2011. Max's riff writing and Igor's groove interpretation were aligning more naturally. On "Inflikted," there were moments where the parts felt assembled. On "Blunt Force Trauma," the symbiosis is more complete — the drums and guitars move together with the instinctive coordination of people who learned to play music as children in the same house.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Tama Starclassic Maple (Dark stain finish)
- **Snare:** Tama Tama Steel Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Paiste — Paiste RUDE Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Tama Speed Cobra HP910LSW; Tama Iron Cobra Hi-Hat Stand HH905N; Roc-N-Soc Nitro; Promark 2B Wood Tip
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (batter), Remo Ambassador Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension — consistent with Inflikted setup; tighter than Sepultura groove-era tuning

### Igor's Tama Starclassic: Refined and Battle-Tested

Igor Cavalera continued with his Tama Starclassic Maple kit for "Blunt Force Trauma," the same platform he had used on "Inflikted" and refined through years of touring. By the time the band entered the studio for the second album, the relationship between Igor and this kit was fully established — he knew exactly how to tune it, how it responded in different rooms, and how to get the sounds he wanted without extended setup time.

The configuration remained consistent with the Inflikted sessions: single 22" x 18" bass drum, two rack toms, and two floor toms. This minimal-but-complete setup reflects Igor's philosophy. Metal drumming does not require eight toms and four bass drums. It requires the right number of well-tuned drums placed where they are genuinely used.

For "Blunt Force Trauma," the Tama Starclassic's maple shells were tuned slightly differently than on the debut. Listening to the records, the toms on "Blunt Force Trauma" sound slightly tighter and more punchy — tuned higher in the shell for more attack with less sustain. This was a deliberate choice that suits the album's more compressed, direct sonic character.

The Starclassic's Star-Cast mounting system continued to deliver the open, resonant shell quality that keeps the drums from sounding choked or dampened under the compression of professional metal recording. The toms ring cleanly before being gated and processed, giving Logan Mader options in the mix that inferior hardware would not provide.

Three years of touring had also given Igor the chance to experiment extensively with head choices and tuning configurations in live environments. The drumming on "Blunt Force Trauma" benefits from that experimentation — the decisions made in the studio were informed by thousands of performances on the same kit.

### Tama Steel: Authority Maintained

Igor stayed with his Tama 14" x 6.5" steel snare for "Blunt Force Trauma" — the same type that had delivered on "Inflikted." In the context of an album that doubled down on the debut's direction without radical gear changes, consistency in the snare was the right call.

The steel shell snare's characteristic brightness and cut remained essential for "Blunt Force Trauma"'s dense guitar arrangements. Logan Mader's production on the second album gives the snare slightly more room in the mix compared to "Inflikted," allowing the drum's natural steel resonance to be more audible. The result is a snare that cracks with authority but also has more body than a hyper-gated studio snare sound.

Igor's tuning approach was consistent — medium-high tension on the batter, tight snare wires for fast response, minimal head damping. Over the three years of touring between albums, these tuning preferences had become second nature. The studio sessions for "Blunt Force Trauma" benefited from that muscle memory — less time spent dialing in, more time performing.

The Tama steel snare is a durable, reliable instrument that can handle the physical demands Igor places on it. Hard hitters who play multiple sets per week test hardware to its limits. The Tama held up through the "Inflikted" touring cycle and came back ready for the studio.

### Paiste RUDE: The Conspiracy's Sonic Identity

The Paiste RUDE cymbal setup remained Igor's choice for "Blunt Force Trauma" — the same configuration he had used on "Inflikted" and throughout much of the classic Sepultura era. By 2011, the Paiste RUDE series was essentially part of Igor's sonic identity, as closely associated with his playing as his Tama Starclassic kit.

For a band that was explicitly not trying to reinvent its sound on the second album, cymbal continuity made sense. The RUDE series' aggressive character, raw surface texture, and high-volume projection are defining elements of Cavalera Conspiracy's sonic profile. Switching to a different cymbal line would have altered the band's character without obvious musical justification.

The 14" RUDE hi-hats drive the album's thrash and death metal sections with consistent power. The multiple crash options give Igor the ability to match crash size to musical intensity — smaller crashes for rapid accent hits, larger crashes for full-band climaxes. The 20" ride provides weight and articulation for sections where a crash sound would be excessive.

Listening across both Cavalera Conspiracy albums, the RUDE cymbals create a sonic thread that connects the two records. Fans who attended Cavalera Conspiracy shows in 2008 and 2009 recognized the same cymbal character on "Blunt Force Trauma" — intentional continuity that reinforces the band's identity.

Paiste's RUDE series remains available today, making it accessible for drummers looking to capture Igor's Cavalera Conspiracy era sound. The series has not significantly changed since Igor began using it in the mid-1990s.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What albums did Cavalera Conspiracy make?**

A: Cavalera Conspiracy released four studio albums on Roadrunner Records: Inflikted in 2008, Blunt Force Trauma in 2011, Pandemonium in 2014, and Psychosis in 2017. All four albums feature the founding Cavalera brothers partnership — Max Cavalera on vocals and guitar, Igor Cavalera on drums — returning to the aggressive thrash and death metal roots that defined early Sepultura. The band formed after both brothers had left Sepultura, Max in 1997 and Igor in 2006, and reunited as creative partners outside of the band they had co-founded.

**Q: What drum kit did Igor Cavalera use on Blunt Force Trauma?**

A: On Cavalera Conspiracy's Blunt Force Trauma recorded in 2010 and released in 2011, Igor Cavalera played a Tama Starclassic Maple kit — the same platform he had used on Inflikted and refined through three years of touring. The setup included a single 22x18 inch bass drum, rack toms in 10 and 12 inches, and floor toms in 14 and 16 inches. The maple shells were tuned slightly tighter than on the debut, producing a punchier, more attack-forward sound that matched the album's more focused production.

**Q: How does Blunt Force Trauma compare to Inflikted?**

A: Blunt Force Trauma is generally regarded as a more focused and consistent album than Inflikted. Where the debut had the energized looseness of a reunion, Blunt Force Trauma benefits from three years of the brothers performing together live. Igor's drumming is tighter and more decisive, the production by Logan Mader is incrementally clearer with better dynamic range, and the songs reflect a band that knows its identity rather than discovering it. The drum sound specifically is heavier and more authoritative — tour-hardened and studio-refined in equal measure.

**Q: What cymbals did Igor Cavalera use on Blunt Force Trauma?**

A: On Blunt Force Trauma in 2011, Igor Cavalera continued with the Paiste RUDE series that had been his primary cymbal choice since the Sepultura Roots era. His setup included Paiste RUDE 14 inch hi-hats, RUDE 16, 18, and 19 inch crashes, a RUDE 20 inch ride, and a RUDE 18 inch China. The RUDE series remained consistent across both Cavalera Conspiracy albums, reinforcing the sonic identity between Inflikted and Blunt Force Trauma.

**Q: Why did Max and Igor Cavalera start Cavalera Conspiracy instead of returning to Sepultura?**

A: Max and Igor Cavalera formed Cavalera Conspiracy rather than returning to Sepultura because Sepultura — the legal entity, recording contract, and band name — continued without them after their respective departures. Max left in January 1997 following a dispute over management; Sepultura elected to continue with a new vocalist rather than dissolve. Igor left in 2006. By then, a different lineup had recorded four albums under the Sepultura name. Cavalera Conspiracy gave the founding brothers a fresh vehicle for their creative partnership without the legal or emotional complications of reclaiming the Sepultura name.
