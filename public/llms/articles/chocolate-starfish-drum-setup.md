# Chocolate Starfish Drum Setup — John Otto's Gear on Limp Bizkit's Record-Breaking Album (2000)

> Complete breakdown of John Otto's drum gear on Limp Bizkit's Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water (2000). The Pearl/OCDP transitional kit, Zildjian cymbals, and the nu-metal drumming behind 'Rollin'', 'My Way', 'Full Nelson', and a first-week record of 1.05 million copies.

## Overview

Released on October 17, 2000, *Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water* is the most commercially successful album Limp Bizkit ever made. In its first week of US release, it sold 1,055,000 copies — a record for a rock album debut at the time — and debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200. John Otto's drumming drives every track on the record.

The album arrived at the absolute peak of nu-metal's mainstream dominance. *Significant Other* (1999) had established Limp Bizkit as a commercial force; *Chocolate Starfish* confirmed them as the genre's biggest commercial act. Otto's response to that pressure was his most controlled, powerful drumming to that point in his career.

His approach on *Chocolate Starfish* builds directly on *Significant Other* while expanding the dynamic range. "Rollin' (Air Raid Vehicle)" deploys the hip-hop groove pocket at its most refined — a four-on-the-floor kick and 16th-note hi-hat patterns borrowed directly from hip-hop drum machine programming. "My Way" demonstrates the atmospheric restraint that Otto's jazz background makes possible. "Full Nelson" delivers full-force nu-metal aggression. "Boiler" achieves an emotional intensity that few nu-metal tracks attempted.

For the *Chocolate Starfish* sessions, Otto was in the transitional phase between his Pearl setup (established on *Significant Other*) and the OCDP custom configuration he would formalize through the 2000s. Zildjian A Series and A Custom cymbals and Gibraltar hardware remained consistent across both records, giving the two albums a related sonic character.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl / early OCDP custom transition — double bass configuration
- **Snare:** Pearl or OCDP custom snare, 14" x 5.5" – 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series and A Custom Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Double bass pedal; Gibraltar Professional Series; Zildjian sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (tom batter), Remo Powerstroke 3 (kick batter), Remo Coated Ambassador (snare batter)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium to medium-high — projection with dynamic flexibility across the album's wide range

| Rack Toms | 10"x8", 12"x9" |
| Floor Toms | 14"x14", 16"x16" |
| Shell Material | Transitional: Pearl → OCDP custom maple |
| Configuration | Double bass setup |

The *Chocolate Starfish* sessions took place during John Otto's transition from the Pearl setup that had defined his *Significant Other* work toward the OCDP custom kit he would use throughout the 2000s. Orange County Drum and Percussion (OCDP) builds drums to artist specification — a natural step for a drummer whose commercial standing gave him the ability to commission instruments built precisely to his requirements.

The tom configuration remained consistent: double bass, two rack toms, two floor toms. In nu-metal, where the rhythmic relationship between kick, bass guitar, and groove is the musical foundation, a clean four-tom spread keeps the drumming focused on pocket and power. The double bass configuration was essential for "Hot Dog" and "Full Nelson," where continuous kick patterns provide rhythmic energy beneath Borland's riff work.

### Cymbal Setup

| Hi-Hats | Zildjian A 14" New Beat | Left side |
| Crash | Zildjian A Custom 16" Crash | Left |
| Crash | Zildjian A 18" Medium | Right of toms |

The Zildjian cymbal setup carried from *Significant Other* into the *Chocolate Starfish* sessions without significant change — a continuity that reflects Otto's established endorsement relationship and the sonic consistency across both albums. A Series cymbals' bright, projecting character was essential for Limp Bizkit's live performance demands at arena and festival scale, while the A Custom crashes provided fast attack suited to syncopated hip-hop influenced accent work.

## Key Tracks

**"Rollin' (Air Raid Vehicle)"** — Four-on-the-floor kick, snare on 2 and 4, 16th-note hi-hats. Hip-hop drum machine template applied to rock. Became one of Limp Bizkit's signature songs of 2000. Deceptively simple — the groove's effectiveness comes from pocket, not complexity.

**"My Way"** — Mid-tempo groove with melodic content. Ghost notes beneath the main groove add texture. Chorus escalation demonstrates controlled power increase. Strong argument for Otto's musical sophistication.

**"Full Nelson"** — Heaviest track alongside "Hot Dog." Full double bass aggression. China cymbal accents riff transitions. Four-tom fills deployed across the complete spread.

**"Boiler"** — Slow-burn emotional intensity. Restrained playing through most of the track before the climax. Ghost notes and near-brush dynamics from Otto's jazz background. Emotional arc across the track demonstrates mature musical judgment.

**"Hot Dog"** — Album opener and most aggressive track. Continuous double bass patterns. The track that framed the album's commercial identity upon release.

## Recording Context

*Chocolate Starfish* was produced primarily by DJ Lethal, continuing the partnership that had defined *Significant Other*'s sound. The production built on the previous album's hip-hop influenced approach while expanding the dynamic palette — more variety in tempo, tone, and intensity.

The hip-hop groove pocket methodology continued throughout. "Rollin'" is the clearest example: Otto's kick placement, snare placement, and hi-hat patterns follow 2000-era hip-hop production conventions — a deliberate choice that made the song work simultaneously as a rock hit and a hip-hop crossover. Compared to *Significant Other*, the kick drum sits more prominently in the *Chocolate Starfish* mix, reflecting the arena-scale production demands that followed the album's record-breaking commercial debut.

## Chart Context: 1.05 Million First Week

The 1,055,000 first-week US sales figure (October 2000) placed *Chocolate Starfish* as the fastest-selling rock album debut of its era. This commercial scale translated directly into sustained global arena and festival touring — a touring operation where hardware reliability, kit durability, and consistent gear performance are not optional. Otto's Gibraltar hardware and Remo head choices reflect exactly these priorities.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did John Otto use on Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water?**
A: John Otto was in a transitional period between Pearl and OCDP custom drums during the Chocolate Starfish sessions. His configuration used double 22" bass drums, two rack toms (10", 12"), and two floor toms (14", 16"). Zildjian A Series and A Custom cymbals and Gibraltar hardware remained consistent with the Significant Other setup. For the full OCDP-era breakdown, see the [John Otto drum setup guide](/articles/john-otto-drum-setup).

**Q: What drum pattern did John Otto play on "Rollin'"?**
A: The "Rollin' (Air Raid Vehicle)" pattern uses a four-on-the-floor kick drum, snare on beats 2 and 4, and 16th-note hi-hat patterns with selective openings — a hip-hop drum machine template applied to rock production. Otto's jazz and hip-hop training made this approach natural. The pattern's commercial success demonstrates how groove-forward, hip-hop informed drumming crosses genre boundaries. See [John Otto's drummer profile](/drummer/john-otto) for the full musical background.

**Q: How many copies did Chocolate Starfish sell in its first week?**
A: Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water sold 1,055,000 copies in its first week of US release in October 2000 — a record for a rock album debut at the time. The album debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200. This remains Limp Bizkit's most commercially successful album.

**Q: What cymbals did John Otto use on Chocolate Starfish?**
A: John Otto continued with Zildjian A Series and A Custom cymbals — consistent with Significant Other. Setup: A 14" New Beat hi-hats, A Custom 16" crash, A 18" Medium crash, A 20" Medium ride, A 18" China. The A Series' bright, projecting character suited the arena and festival volumes that followed the album's record-breaking debut. See the [John Otto drum kit guide](/articles/john-otto-drum-setup) for the complete endorsement picture.

**Q: How does Chocolate Starfish compare to Significant Other drum-wise?**
A: Both albums share Otto's hip-hop groove pocket and the same Zildjian/Gibraltar/Remo configuration, but Chocolate Starfish shows evolution: the kick drum is more prominent in the mix (arena production demands), the dynamic range is wider (from "Boiler"'s atmospheric restraint to "Hot Dog"'s full aggression), and the Pearl → OCDP transitional gear shift contributes marginally warmer drum character. Both records are documented in the [Significant Other drum setup article](/articles/significant-other-drum-setup) and [John Otto drum kit guide](/articles/john-otto-drum-setup).
