# Chocolate Starfish Drum Setup: John Otto's Limp Bizkit 2000 Gear Breakdown

> Complete breakdown of John Otto's drum setup on Limp Bizkit's Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water (2000). Pearl drums, Zildjian A Series cymbals, Gibraltar hardware — the nu-metal rig behind "Rollin'," "My Way," and Limp Bizkit's 1-million-first-week record-setter.

## Overview

Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water arrived on October 17, 2000, and immediately shattered records. The album sold 1.05 million copies in its first week — making it the fastest-selling rock album in history at the time — and debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200. Certified 6×Platinum in the United States, Chocolate Starfish was the high-water mark of the nu-metal era's mainstream dominance, and John Otto's drumming was its rhythmic engine.

Where Significant Other (1999) had been a breakthrough, Chocolate Starfish was a consolidation at scale. By the time Limp Bizkit entered the studio, they had spent over a year touring behind Significant Other's 7 million US copies. Otto's drumming on the 2000 album is the playing of a drummer who has executed these grooves thousands of times at arena volume: tighter, more assured, with the same jazz-trained sophistication applied with even greater precision.

"Rollin' (Air Raid Vehicle)" became the album's signature track — a driving double-bass groove that anchored one of the era's most commercially dominant singles. "My Way," "Boiler," and "Full Nelson" demonstrated the full range of Otto's playing approach: the hip-hop pocket of "My Way," the dynamic restraint and explosive climax of "Boiler," the aggressive double-kick foundation of "Full Nelson."

Producer Terry Date recorded the album with the same clarity-first approach that had worked on Significant Other, preserving the live energy of Otto's performances — the subtle kick displacement, the ghost notes, the dynamic range control — in a mix that helped sell a million copies in the album's first week.

This article breaks down the specific drum setup John Otto used for the Chocolate Starfish sessions and examines how his performances drove one of the most commercially successful albums in nu-metal history.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Export / Reference (double 22" bass drums, maple shells) — transitional period toward OCDP
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating, 14" x 6.5" maple
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — A New Beat 14" Hi-Hats, A 18" Medium Crash, A Custom 16" Fast Crash, A 20" Medium Ride, A 18" China, A 10" Splash
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Gibraltar Double Bass Pedal; Gibraltar 9607 Hi-Hat Stand; Gibraltar SC-4422 Boom Stands; Zildjian 5A Wood Tip sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear (kick), Remo Emperor Clear (toms), Remo Coated Ambassador (snare)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension consistent with Significant Other — crack and sensitivity balanced for hip-hop ghost notes and metal backbeats

### The Recording Setup: Pearl to OCDP Transition Era

For the Chocolate Starfish sessions, John Otto's drum setup was functionally continuous with Significant Other — the same double 22-inch bass drum foundation, similar maple shell tom configuration, and the same Zildjian/Gibraltar combination that had just driven a 7-million-selling record. By the Chocolate Starfish sessions, the setup had been road-tested extensively and was optimized for the physical demands of Otto's playing style.

The double 22-inch bass drum configuration was the defining physical element of Limp Bizkit's live and recording drum setup. Otto's hip-hop-influenced double-kick technique — laid slightly behind the metric grid — required a pedal setup and kick drum configuration that allowed subtle timing variations rather than mechanically locked precision. The Pearl maple shells provided warm, full fundamentals that complemented the guitar and bass frequencies in Terry Date's production.

The four-tom configuration — 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms — provided the melodic range for "Rollin'"'s fills and the aggressive section changes in "Full Nelson." By the Chocolate Starfish period, the OCDP custom kit had begun appearing in Otto's live setup, but the recording sessions used a configuration consistent with the Pearl/OCDP transitional period.

### Zildjian A Series: Consistent Commercial Peak

John Otto's Zildjian A Series setup for Chocolate Starfish is consistent with the cymbal configuration that drove Significant Other. The A New Beat hi-hats are the most functionally important cymbal on the album: "Rollin'" is fundamentally a hi-hat groove track, with the 16th-note pattern driving the verse before the chorus opens into a fuller kit arrangement. The A New Beats' bright, cutting chick sound sits clearly in Terry Date's production, providing rhythmic definition against Wes Borland's processed guitar layers.

The crash selection — 18" Medium Crash as primary, 16" Fast Crash for quicker responses — gives Otto different accent qualities for the album's emotional contexts. The China cymbal provides the section-change punctuation audible in "Full Nelson" and the heavier transitions of "Rollin'" and "My Generation."

### Terry Date's Production: The Sound of 1M First-Week Sales

Terry Date produced Chocolate Starfish at NRG Studios in North Hollywood and Studio City Sound with the same transparency-first approach as Significant Other. The key difference: by Chocolate Starfish, Otto had been road-tested into a tighter, more confident ensemble. A year-plus of touring the Significant Other material at arena volume made the 2000 sessions more immediate and locked-in.

Date's primary drum production priority was preserving Otto's hip-hop kick displacement — the behind-the-grid kick placement that gives "Rollin'" and "My Way" their forward momentum must be maintained through tracking, editing, and mixing rather than quantized to mechanical precision.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did John Otto use on Chocolate Starfish?**
A: John Otto recorded Chocolate Starfish (2000) on a Pearl drum setup with a double 22-inch bass drum configuration, two rack toms (10" and 12"), and two floor toms (14" and 16"), with maple shells throughout. This was the same essential Pearl-era configuration used on Significant Other (1999), road-tested through extensive touring. Full gear breakdown at [John Otto drum setup](/articles/john-otto-drum-setup).

**Q: What cymbals did John Otto use on Chocolate Starfish?**
A: John Otto used Zildjian A Series cymbals — including A New Beat 14" hi-hats, an 18" Medium Crash, a 16" A Custom Fast Crash, a 20" Medium Ride, an 18" China, and a 10" Splash. The consistent Zildjian A Series endorsement across Significant Other and Chocolate Starfish reflects the same cymbal voice on both peak-era Limp Bizkit records.

**Q: What was Limp Bizkit's fastest-selling album?**
A: Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water (2000) was Limp Bizkit's fastest-selling album, opening with 1.05 million copies in its first week — a record for fastest-selling rock album at the time. The album debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 and is certified 6×Platinum in the United States. John Otto's hip-hop groove meets metal power drumming — informed by formal jazz training at Douglas Anderson School of the Arts — drove the rhythmic foundation behind "Rollin'," "My Way," and the album's other key tracks.

**Q: How does Chocolate Starfish's drum sound compare to Significant Other?**
A: Both albums share the same essential drum setup (Pearl drums, Zildjian A Series cymbals, Gibraltar hardware) and the same producer (Terry Date). The key difference is performance confidence: by Chocolate Starfish, Otto had spent over a year touring the Significant Other material at arena volume, and the 2000 sessions are tighter and more commanding. The same jazz-trained hip-hop groove approach, applied with greater precision. See [Significant Other drum setup](/articles/significant-other-drum-setup) for direct comparison.

**Q: Who produced Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water?**
A: Chocolate Starfish was produced by Terry Date and Limp Bizkit, recorded at NRG Studios in North Hollywood and Studio City Sound in Studio City, California in 1999-2000. Terry Date's drum production preserved the human feel and hip-hop groove displacement in Otto's performances that contributed to the album's commercial success.
