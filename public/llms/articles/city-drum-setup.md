# City Drum Setup: Gene Hoglan's Industrial Precision with Strapping Young Lad

> Complete breakdown of Gene Hoglan's drum performance on Strapping Young Lad's City (1997). The Tama Artstar II, Roland SPD-20 electronic pads, and the techniques behind the industrial extreme metal record that prefigured the djent movement.

**Genre:** Industrial Metal / Extreme Metal

## Overview

Released on January 21, 1997, Strapping Young Lad's "City" is the record that permanently changed extreme metal's relationship with electronics, precision, and industrial texture. At the center of that change was Gene Hoglan — "The Atomic Clock" — executing performances that blurred the line between human drummer and sequenced machine.

By 1997, Hoglan had built his legend with Dark Angel's "Darkness Descends" (1986), Death's "Individual Thought Patterns" (1993), and "Symbolic" (1995). Those albums established him as extreme metal's most precise drummer. "City" asked something different: precision in service of industrial chaos, metronomic accuracy within Devin Townsend's wall-of-sound production, and the integration of electronic percussion pads into an acoustic extreme metal context — a combination that had no real precedent.

Recorded at The Greenhouse in Vancouver, British Columbia, the album captured the intersection of acoustic drumming and industrial texture that no subsequent record has fully replicated. "City" became a direct precursor to the djent movement — Meshuggah's explorations of mechanical-feeling grooves drew on the same principle Hoglan and Townsend established here: that human drumming, executed with absolute precision, can sound more machine-like than a programmed sequence while retaining musical expressiveness.

### Tama Artstar II: Industrial Acoustic Foundation

For "City," Gene Hoglan used a Tama Artstar II kit — the same professional series as his "Symbolic" era, adapted here for Devin Townsend's industrial production context. The Artstar II's birch shells were essential: birch produces naturally bright, forward-sounding tones with fast attack transients that cut through even the densest guitar arrangements. Two 22" x 16" bass drums (shorter shells than some extreme metal configurations, chosen for tighter, more focused low-end) provided the rhythmic engine for tracks like "Detox" and "All Hail the New Flesh."

The power tom configuration — 10" and 12" square-dimension rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms — gave Hoglan the melodic vocabulary for fill work between the album's relentless industrial passages. The square dimensions produced focused, punchy pitch without excess sustain, matching "City"'s production philosophy: attack over ambience, precision over warmth.

### Roland SPD-20: The Industrial Layer

The Roland SPD-20 Total Percussion Pad was the defining addition that distinguished the "City" setup from every previous Gene Hoglan recording. The SPD-20 — an 8-pad electronic percussion controller capable of triggering internal sounds, samples, and MIDI instruments — allowed Hoglan to integrate industrial electronic textures directly into his live performance.

Devin Townsend's compositions deliberately blurred the boundary between acoustic and electronic percussion. The SPD-20 provided the industrial sound design elements — synthesized percussion, processed samples, electronic accents — that made "City" feel like a convergence of extreme metal and industrial music. Where most electronic elements in metal were added post-recording, Hoglan performed them live, integrated into his drumming in real time.

This integration was genuinely novel in 1997 extreme metal. The SPD-20's placement within the kit allowed Hoglan to trigger electronic elements with his sticks while maintaining his acoustic drum performance — requiring the same precision as any other aspect of his playing, with an additional compositional layer that no acoustic kit alone could provide.

### Pearl Free-Floating Snare: The Industrial Crack

The snare sound on "City" is one of extreme metal's most distinctive — a compressed, cracking impact at the intersection of acoustic snare and industrial sample. Hoglan achieved this with a Pearl Free-Floating steel snare, the same model used during the Death "Individual Thought Patterns" sessions in 1993.

Pearl's Free-Floating design decouples the shell from the mounting hardware, producing an open, explosive crack that responds to every nuance of stroke pressure. At 14" x 6.5", the steel shell provided the bright, projecting tone needed to cut through Townsend's multilayered guitar arrangements. In "City"'s production context, the natural Free-Floating character was enhanced through aggressive gate processing and compression — transforming Hoglan's acoustic snare performance into an industrial percussion event.

### Zildjian A Custom Cymbals

Hoglan's cymbal setup for "City" used the Zildjian A Custom series — bright, modern cymbals with excellent attack definition and fast decay times suited to industrial production. The 14" A Custom hi-hats allowed every stroke to register clearly within Townsend's dense mix. The three-crash configuration (17", 18", 19") provided dynamic range across the album's varied intensity levels — A Custom crashes speak clearly and decay quickly, essential when industrial production demands rhythmic legibility at all times.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums did Gene Hoglan use on Strapping Young Lad City?**
A: Gene Hoglan recorded Strapping Young Lad's City (1997) using a Tama Artstar II kit with birch shells, two 22" x 16" bass drums (independent double kick), and a Pearl Free-Floating steel snare at 14" x 6.5". His cymbals were Zildjian A Custom series throughout. The defining addition unique to this setup was the Roland SPD-20 Total Percussion Pad, which allowed Hoglan to trigger industrial electronic sounds and samples directly from his kit during recording.

**Q: Did Gene Hoglan use electronic drums on Strapping Young Lad City?**
A: Yes — Gene Hoglan integrated a Roland SPD-20 Total Percussion Pad into his setup for City. The SPD-20 is an 8-pad electronic controller that allowed him to trigger industrial sounds, samples, and synthesized percussion directly from the kit. This was genuinely novel in 1997 extreme metal: where most electronic elements were added post-recording in production, Hoglan performed them live as part of his drum performance. The acoustic Tama Artstar II kit remained the foundation, with the SPD-20 adding the industrial electronic texture layer that distinguishes City's sound from purely acoustic extreme metal recordings.

**Q: How did Strapping Young Lad get that drum sound on City?**
A: The drum sound on City was created by Devin Townsend at The Greenhouse in Vancouver. Townsend applied aggressive compression and gating to Gene Hoglan's acoustic Tama Artstar II kit — treating each drum strike as an industrial percussion event rather than a natural acoustic sound — while integrating Roland SPD-20 electronic pad outputs directly into the mix. The result transforms what was physically a high-quality acoustic extreme metal performance into something that sits at the boundary of human drumming and industrial programming. Hoglan's metronomic precision was essential to making this approach work: the same production chain applied to a less consistent drummer would have exposed every timing variation.

**Q: Is City by Strapping Young Lad related to djent?**
A: City (1997) is widely considered a direct precursor to the djent movement. Devin Townsend and Gene Hoglan established on City the same aesthetic that Meshuggah formalized in the early 2000s and that Periphery, Animals as Leaders, and other djent bands popularized after 2008: human drumming executed with machine-grade precision, integrated with industrial production values, creating a rhythmic character that sounds simultaneously human and mechanical. The blast beat architecture, the electronic pad integration, and Hoglan's absolute metronomic precision on City demonstrated that extreme metal drumming could serve as the foundation for this industrial-mechanical aesthetic.

**Q: How does City compare to Gene Hoglan's Death recordings?**
A: Both the Death albums and City feature Gene Hoglan at his most precise, but the recordings differ fundamentally in character. On Individual Thought Patterns (1993) and Symbolic (1995), Hoglan's precision served progressive death metal compositions — the precision was in service of musicality, dynamics, and Chuck Schuldiner's compositional vision. On City (1997), the precision serves an industrial aesthetic — Devin Townsend's production transforms Hoglan's acoustic performance into something deliberately mechanical. Same player, same fundamental precision, qualitatively different sonic results due to production approach and the addition of the Roland SPD-20 electronic layer.
