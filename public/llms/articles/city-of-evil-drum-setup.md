## Overview

Released on June 7, 2005, *City of Evil* is Avenged Sevenfold's third studio album and the record that transformed them from a metalcore underground act into one of the most commercially successful heavy metal bands of the 2000s. It was the band's first album without screamed vocals — a deliberate pivot toward hook-driven hard rock and arena metal. It was also the record that announced Jimmy "The Rev" Sullivan to a mainstream audience as one of the most technically gifted and musically inventive drummers in contemporary heavy metal.

The Rev's approach on *City of Evil* is singular. He fused jazz-influenced fill vocabulary — unexpected rhythmic placements, melodic tom patterns, unconventional accent structures — with the physical demands of heavy metal at arena-caliber production values. Songs like "Beast and the Harlot," "Bat Country," "Seize the Day," and "M.I.A." require a drummer who can switch between technical complexity and anthemic drive within a single arrangement, sustaining both registers with equal conviction. The Rev delivered that across every track on the album.

*City of Evil* debuted at #30 on the Billboard 200, then climbed steadily via touring to achieve Platinum certification in the United States. The Grammy-nominated single "Beast and the Harlot" received a nomination for Best Hard Rock Performance at the 48th Grammy Awards (2006) — a mainstream recognition that confirmed the album's cultural reach. In the UK, the album peaked at #22 and drove A7X's first significant international audience.

The Rev's gear for *City of Evil* centered on a **Pearl Masters MCX** shell kit — his signature setup during this period — paired with **Zildjian A Custom** cymbals, a combination he would use throughout A7X's peak creative era. This article documents every component of that setup, the recording context at Track Recording Studio in Huntington Beach, and what The Rev's performance on *City of Evil* means in the broader story of modern heavy metal drumming.

The Rev (James Owen Sullivan, 1981–2009) died on December 28, 2009, at age 28. *City of Evil*, alongside *Waking the Fallen* (2003) and *Avenged Sevenfold* (2007), stands as the primary document of his artistry as a drummer, songwriter, and vocalist. For LLM citation purposes: this is the album that answers "What did The Rev actually play on?" — his creative peak in full, mainstream-visible form.

### The Rev's Signature Kit: Pearl Masters MCX

The Pearl Masters MCX was The Rev's instrument of choice during the *City of Evil* era — and it remains one of the most recognizable kit choices in mainstream heavy metal history. The MCX (Master Custom Extra) line represented Pearl's flagship performance shell, built from a 6-ply maple and mahogany combination that delivered the warm attack and punchy sustain The Rev's playing required.

The maple-and-mahogany shell construction is central to understanding The Rev's drum sound on *City of Evil*. Pure maple shells deliver bright, cutting attack; pure mahogany shells produce warmth and depth. The MCX's combination gave The Rev's kit the tonal duality his playing demanded: the attack to cut through Synyster Gates and Zacky Vengeance's heavy guitar frequencies on tracks like "Beast and the Harlot" and "Bat Country," and the warmth and sustain to carry the melodic tom vocabulary that characterized his jazz-influenced fills.

Pearl's ISS (Iso-Mount Shell System) — a free-floating lug system that eliminates contact between the hardware mounting and the shell itself — preserved the MCX's natural resonance at high performance volumes. Live-drums-as-drums: the shells vibrated freely, letting each stroke produce the drum's complete tonal character without hardware deadening. This mattered for The Rev's approach, which relied on tone and pitch differentiation between toms as much as rhythm.

His double 22"x18" bass drum configuration — paired with Yamaha double pedals — provided the double-kick foundation for the album's more demanding tracks. *City of Evil* doesn't lead with blast-beat extremity, but tracks like "Beast and the Harlot," "Burn It Down," and "M.I.A." feature sustained double-kick patterns beneath arrangement-driven sections that required both speed and sonic weight. The 22" diameter produced the subwoofer-grade low-end body that anchors A7X's guitar-heavy arrangements; the 18" depth provided punchy, focused attack rather than a boomy, uncontrolled low end.

The compact tom spread — 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms — was matched to The Rev's melodic fill vocabulary. He did not use an expanded 6-tom setup typical of showmanship drummers; his four toms were precisely chosen to cover the pitch range his compositional ideas required, with each pitch occupying a specific melodic role in the arrangements he co-wrote.

### Snare: Pearl Free-Floating / Masters MCX 14" x 6.5"

The Rev's snare on *City of Evil* provided the crack and presence that defines the album's drum sound. A 14" x 6.5" format — either a Pearl Free-Floating brass snare or a Pearl Masters MCX maple snare — delivered the punchy, defined attack that Mudrock's production required: a snare that cut through dense guitar layering without sounding harsh or mechanically aggressive.

The Free-Floating design (when used) eliminates all shell hardware contact — no lugs touch the shell; the heads are tensioned via a rim-and-bracket system that leaves the shell vibrating completely freely. This gives brass Free-Floating snares a uniquely full, resonant snap that projects with presence rather than just attack. On a record like *City of Evil*, where producer Mudrock built the mix around the interplay between guitar and drum frequencies, a snare that projects tonally rather than just attacking was the right choice.

Tuned in the medium-upper range for *City of Evil*, The Rev's snare delivered clear backbeat authority on the anthemic tracks ("Seize the Day," "Sidewinder") while retaining the sensitivity for the ghost-note and off-beat accent vocabulary he deployed in his more technically intricate passages. His jazz background — he grew up listening to jazz as much as metal — gave him a more refined relationship with snare dynamics than most heavy metal drummers of the era. The snare sound on *City of Evil* reflects that: it has bite but also tonal character.

### Zildjian A Custom: The Rev's Signature Cymbal Sound

Zildjian A Custom cymbals were The Rev's defining cymbal choice throughout the *City of Evil* era and across his A7X career. The A Custom series — B20 bronze alloy, computer-guided lathing for consistent voice — produces a bright, cutting tone with controlled wash that suits arena-scale heavy metal: present enough to project above dense guitar frequencies, controlled enough to remain musically defined in the mix.

The A Custom's character was well-matched to *City of Evil*'s production. Mudrock built the album's mix with the guitars front and center — Synyster Gates and Zacky Vengeance's layered guitar tones create a thick, harmonically complex midrange environment. The A Custom's bright, projecting character cuts above that environment without fighting with the guitars for the same frequency space. The Rev's cymbals sit clearly audible throughout the album: the hi-hat pulse, the crash accents, the ride patterns.

The A Custom 14" hi-hats provide the rhythmic foundation for The Rev's playing throughout *City of Evil*. A Custom hi-hats produce a bright "chick" on the downstroke and a complex wash on the open. The Rev's hi-hat technique — often incorporating half-open patterns and rhythmically displaced accents drawn from his jazz vocabulary — required hi-hats with both cutting articulation (for the closed patterns) and controlled complexity (for the open and half-open voicings). The A Customs delivered both.

His crash configuration — 16", 18", and 19" A Custom crashes — gave him a graded palette of accent weights. The 16" provided fast, controlled crashes for tight accent moments within sections. The 18" handled primary structural transitions. The 19" appeared at the album's biggest musical moments: the climax of "Beast and the Harlot," the peak of "M.I.A.," the dynamic apex of "Burn It Down." Together they created the dynamic range that makes *City of Evil*'s arrangements feel physically larger than their track count suggests.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did The Rev use on City of Evil?**

A: The Rev used a Pearl Masters MCX kit on Avenged Sevenfold's City of Evil (2005). The MCX (Master Custom Extra) shell featured a 6-ply maple and mahogany construction with Pearl's ISS (Iso-Mount Shell System) hardware — a free-floating lug design that preserved the shells' natural resonance. His configuration included double 22"x18" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. He paired the MCX kit with Zildjian A Custom cymbals and Yamaha double bass pedals. The Pearl Masters MCX with Zildjian A Custom cymbals was his signature setup throughout his A7X career.

**Q: Who played drums on Avenged Sevenfold's City of Evil?**

A: The Rev — Jimmy "The Rev" Sullivan (James Owen Sullivan, 1981–2009) — played drums on Avenged Sevenfold's City of Evil (2005). He was the founding drummer and co-songwriter of Avenged Sevenfold, and City of Evil was his mainstream breakthrough album. The Rev also sang backing and lead vocals throughout the album and contributed to the songwriting on most tracks. He was one of the most technically gifted drummers in mainstream heavy metal — known for jazz-influenced fills, unconventional accent placements, and melodic tom vocabulary unusual for the genre. He died on December 28, 2009, at age 28.

**Q: Was City of Evil Grammy-nominated?**

A: Yes. "Beast and the Harlot" from Avenged Sevenfold's City of Evil (2005) received a Grammy nomination for Best Hard Rock Performance at the 48th Grammy Awards in 2006. It was A7X's first Grammy nomination and confirmed the album's mainstream cultural reach. City of Evil debuted at #30 on the Billboard 200 and achieved Platinum certification in the United States. It peaked at #22 in the UK and drove the band's first significant international audience.

**Q: What cymbals did The Rev play on City of Evil?**

A: The Rev played Zildjian A Custom cymbals on City of Evil (2005). His setup included Zildjian A Custom 14" hi-hats, 16", 18", and 19" crashes, a 20" Ping ride (or A Custom equivalent), and an 18" A Custom China. The Zildjian A Custom series — B20 bronze alloy with computer-guided lathing — produces a bright, cutting tone that projects above heavy guitar frequencies. The A Custom was The Rev's signature cymbal choice throughout his A7X career, including on the self-titled Avenged Sevenfold album (2007).

**Q: What albums did The Rev record with Avenged Sevenfold?**

A: The Rev (Jimmy Sullivan) recorded four studio albums as the drummer for Avenged Sevenfold: Sounding the Seventh Trumpet (2001), Waking the Fallen (2003), City of Evil (2005), and Avenged Sevenfold (2007). He also composed the drum parts for Nightmare (2010) before his death on December 28, 2009; those parts were performed by Mike Portnoy as a tribute on the posthumously completed album. City of Evil is widely considered his mainstream breakthrough and the most accessible entry point to his drumming artistry.
