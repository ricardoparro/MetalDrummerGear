## Overview

Released on July 31, 2015 through Metal Blade Records, *Coma Ecliptic* is Between the Buried and Me's most accessible — and most deliberately melodic — full-length. The album is a narrative rock opera following a protagonist in a coma who travels through alternate realities, each represented by a different musical chapter. It charted in the UK, reached #1 on the iTunes rock chart, and earned the band their strongest mainstream recognition to date.

For Blake Richardson, *Coma Ecliptic* demonstrates complete musical maturity — the same technical vocabulary as *The Parallax II* deployed with greater selectivity, serving a more accessible narrative structure. The album's rock opera format demanded Richardson be a storyteller as much as a technician: his dynamic choices, the contrast between heavy and restrained passages, and the narrative fill work at chapter transitions all contribute to telling the story rather than simply providing rhythmic support.

*Coma Ecliptic* is more melodic and structured than *The Parallax II* (2012), while maintaining BTBAM's progressive metal core. Fewer extreme-metal passages, more sustained dynamic storytelling, greater compositional use of space — all in service of the narrative concept's priority.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** DW Collector's Series (mature configuration after a decade of BTBAM recording), double 22" bass drums, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, 16" floor tom
- **Snare:** DW Collector's Series Maple 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Dark + Extra Dry — 14" Dark Hi-Hats, 17" Extra Dry Thin Crash, 18" Dark Crash, 20" Extra Dry Ride, 10" Dark Splash, 18" Byzance China
- **Pedals:** DW 9002 Double Bass Pedal (chain-drive, adjustable cam)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth American Classic 5B
- **Heads:** Evans G2 Coated (snare batter), Remo Emperor Coated / Ambassador Coated (toms), Remo Powerstroke 3 (kick)

### DW Collector's Series: A Decade of BTBAM Refinement

By *Coma Ecliptic*, Richardson's DW Collector's Series kit had been refined through a full decade of BTBAM touring and six studio albums. The kit was not an instrument being explored; it was an extension of Richardson's musical vocabulary, completely understood and deployed with total confidence.

For *Coma Ecliptic*'s rock opera structure, the DW Collector's maple shells needed to serve varied musical environments within a unified production aesthetic. The album's chapters — each representing a different alternate reality in the protagonist's coma — required the kit to adapt across different emotional temperatures while maintaining BTBAM's sonic identity. The Collector's Series maple handled this convincingly: warm and resonant in the album's more melodic sections, powerful in the heaviest passages, subtle and controlled during the quieter narrative moments.

The three-rack-tom configuration (10", 12", 13") continued to enable the melodic fill architecture that has been Richardson's signature since *Alaska*. In *Coma Ecliptic*'s more melodic context, these fills function as narrative punctuation — marking chapter transitions in the rock opera's story — making the wide pitch range essential for melodically meaningful narrative fills.

Producer Jamie King had now recorded seven BTBAM records with Richardson, making Basement Studios the most familiar recording environment in the band's history. The *Coma Ecliptic* drum sound reflects this familiarity — warm, present, and balanced, with Richardson's kit captured as the natural center of the album's orchestration.

### Meinl Byzance Dark and Extra Dry: The Complete Progressive Metal Vocabulary

Richardson's full Meinl Byzance Dark and Extra Dry configuration continued unchanged into *Coma Ecliptic*, but its deployment evolved to suit the rock opera context. The album's more melodic, narrative-focused character meant greater restraint: the Byzance China appeared less frequently, the Dark Hi-Hats provided more sustained shimmer for melodic passages, and the overall dynamic character leaned warmer than the more aggressive *Parallax II* setup.

The 14" Byzance Dark Hi-Hats are particularly central to *Coma Ecliptic*'s character. The album's melodic chapters demanded hi-hat work that could carry sustained grooves with complex, musical character. The Dark voicing provided the warm, nuanced response these sustained passages required — contributing to the warmth that helped the album reach the wider audience its chart performance reflected.

The Extra Dry 20" Ride maintained clean articulation through *Coma Ecliptic*'s complex passages. Even in the album's more accessible context, BTBAM's arrangements still feature time signature changes and metric modulations that require ride articulation rather than wash. The Byzance Extra Dry delivers that articulation while the hand-hammered construction adds the musical complexity that distinguishes BTBAM's sound.

### Compositional Restraint: The DW 9002 in Rock Opera Context

By *Coma Ecliptic*, Richardson's DW 9002 double bass pedal was completely intuitive — a full decade of BTBAM recording and touring had made the pedal an extension of his musical intent. The rock opera format deployed the DW 9002 with compositional restraint: double-bass passages appeared at narrative climaxes and the album's heavier chapters rather than throughout, creating contrast that made the heavy moments land harder.

## Key Track Analysis

- **Node** — Album opener. Establishes the rock opera's concept immediately. Richardson's dynamic restraint introduces *Coma Ecliptic*'s narrative approach: the drums serve the story.
- **Dim Ignition** — One of the album's most commercially accessible tracks. Richardson's groove work here is more traditional progressive metal than the rhythmic extremity of *The Parallax II*, serving the melody-forward arrangement.
- **Memory Palace** — *Coma Ecliptic*'s most ambitious extended piece. The full BTBAM compositional template applied to the rock opera format: genre transitions marked by Richardson's anticipatory fills.
- **Life in Velvet** — Demonstrates Richardson's dynamic storytelling most clearly; the contrast between the track's softer sections and heavier passages shows how he uses kick density and cymbal choice to mark narrative beats.
- **King Redeem / Queen Serene** — Closing tracks. Richardson's fill architecture across the two-part closing sequence is the album's most compositionally demanding performance — marking the narrative's resolution.

## Rock Opera Drumming: Narrative Before Technique

*Coma Ecliptic*'s chapter-based structure created specific demands that distinguish Richardson's performance here from the earlier BTBAM catalog. Each chapter required the drums to serve a different emotional context — some chapters are aggressive progressive metal, some are melodic and restrained, some are atmospheric. Richardson's ability to shift between these contexts while maintaining BTBAM's sonic identity is the defining achievement of his *Coma Ecliptic* performance.

This narrative approach connects to the broader progressive metal technique of compositional double-bass deployment — see [odd time signatures in metal drumming](/technique/odd-time-signatures) — and to the compositional intelligence Richardson describes in interviews as his primary musical priority.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Blake Richardson use on Coma Ecliptic?**
Blake Richardson recorded *Coma Ecliptic* (2015) using his fully mature DW Collector's Series kit — double 22" bass drums, three rack toms (10", 12", 13"), and a 16" floor tom — now a decade into its refinement through BTBAM touring and recording. Paired with Meinl Byzance Dark and Extra Dry cymbals and the DW 9002 double bass pedal. Producer Jamie King recorded at Basement Studios in Winston-Salem, NC. Full profile: [Blake Richardson at MetalForge](/drummer/blake-richardson).

**Q: How does Coma Ecliptic differ musically from The Parallax II?**
*Coma Ecliptic* (2015) is more melodic, more structured, and more deliberately accessible than *The Parallax II* (2012). Its rock opera format creates a narrative-focused structure where each track represents a chapter in the protagonist's coma journey. Richardson's drumming reflects these priorities with more melodic phrasing and dynamic storytelling, fewer extreme-metal passages. The album charted in the UK and reached #1 on the iTunes rock chart. For comparison, see [The Parallax II drum setup](/articles/the-parallax-ii-future-sequence-drum-setup).

**Q: What is the rock opera concept behind Coma Ecliptic?**
*Coma Ecliptic* follows a protagonist in a coma given the opportunity to choose a new life across different alternate realities — each track representing a chapter in this journey. The chapter-based structure explains the album's varied internal dynamics and Richardson's narrative fill work at chapter transitions: the drums serve the story rather than simply providing rhythmic support. The concept's accessibility relative to *The Parallax II* reflects BTBAM's desire to reach wider audiences without abandoning their progressive metal core.

**Q: How does Blake Richardson's playing connect across Alaska, Parallax II, and Coma Ecliptic?**
The three albums trace Richardson's development across a decade of BTBAM's creative peak. *Alaska* (2005) is the early statement — full technical vocabulary in place, compositional approach developing. *The Parallax II* (2012) is peak compositional density — Richardson's technique fully integrated into service of a 74-minute concept album of maximum ambition. *Coma Ecliptic* (2015) is the mature restraint — the same vocabulary deployed with greater selectivity, serving a more accessible narrative structure. Consistent gear across all three allows the musical evolution to be clearly heard. Full arc overview: [Blake Richardson at MetalForge](/drummer/blake-richardson).

**Q: What makes Coma Ecliptic Blake Richardson's most accessible BTBAM performance?**
*Coma Ecliptic*'s rock opera format prioritizes narrative clarity and melodic accessibility over the technical density of *The Parallax II*. Richardson serves these priorities directly: more melodic fills, more sustained dynamic storytelling, and compositional use of space that lets the album's narrative breathe. The result is a performance where Richardson's complete technical command is deployed in service of melody and narrative rather than compositional complexity. The album's commercial success — UK chart entry, iTunes #1 rock — validates the approach.
