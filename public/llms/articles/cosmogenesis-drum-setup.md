## Overview

When Obscura released "Cosmogenesis" on March 27, 2009, through Relapse Records, the technical death metal landscape shifted. Behind the kit was Hannes Grossmann — a 26-year-old classically trained drummer from Bavaria who had previously played alongside Christian Münzner in Necrophagist's touring lineup. With Obscura's second album, he stepped into the foreground of a generation-defining record.

"Cosmogenesis" was recorded at Woodshed Studio in Landshut, Germany, with V. Santura producing — the same Triptykon and Dark Fortress guitarist whose engineering work would shape the next decade of European extreme metal production. Santura's approach prioritized clarity within complexity: every voice in Obscura's dense arrangements had to remain legible, including drums that were doing more compositionally than any death metal record of the year demanded.

Tracks like "Anticosmic Overload," "Choir of Spirits," and "Universe Momentum" weren't built as songs with drum parts; they were composed as integrated four-voice arrangements where Grossmann's drumming carries melodic and harmonic weight equal to the guitars and Steffen Kummerer's bass. The polyrhythmic construction, open-handed technique, and ghost-note density that defined "Cosmogenesis" became the template that technical death metal drummers would study for the next fifteen years.

### DW Collectors Series: The Precision Foundation

The DW Collectors Series shell precision matters acutely for an album like Cosmogenesis: every tom needed to speak at a distinct, tunable pitch because Grossmann's fills function as melodic counterlines within the band's four-voice compositional architecture. The double bass drum configuration — two 22" x 18" kick drums — was essential. Grossmann does not play double pedal; he uses two complete bass drum instruments for the independence his patterns require.

### DW Collectors Maple Snare

Grossmann's DW Collectors maple snare on Cosmogenesis was selected for one defining capability: dynamic range. His open-handed playing builds patterns where ghost notes sit beneath primary accents within the same hi-hat phrase. The DW Collectors maple shell registers near-silent touches and translates them into musically present but not dominant tones — a sensitivity range that aluminum or steel shells cannot match. The 5.5" depth is the precision choice for technical death metal.

### Meinl Byzance Cymbals

Hand-hammered B20 bronze with natural patina finish produces warm, complex, harmonically rich sounds. For an album where drums function as composed voices within four-part arrangements, that complexity matters. The 14" Byzance hi-hats anchor the open-handed playing — left hand leads on a left-positioned hi-hat, freeing the right hand for snare patterns with ghost-note density impossible with conventional crossed-hands technique.

### DW 9000 Double Pedal

The DW 9000's adjustable cam system allowed Grossmann to dial in the acceleration curve that matched his heel-up technique at Cosmogenesis's specific tempo range. The pedal's mechanical consistency made the polyrhythmic patterns on "Anticosmic Overload" and "Universe Momentum" reproducible across multiple takes without physical deterioration.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Hannes Grossmann use on Cosmogenesis?**
A: A DW Collectors Series kit with custom North American maple shells. His configuration ran two 22" x 18" bass drums for independent two-footed playing, two rack toms (10" and 12"), and two floor toms (14" and 16"). His snare was a DW Collectors maple 14" x 5.5".

**Q: Who produced Obscura's Cosmogenesis?**
A: V. Santura produced and engineered Cosmogenesis at Woodshed Studio in Landshut, Germany. Santura is the guitarist for Triptykon and Dark Fortress, and his production work has shaped European extreme metal for over a decade.

**Q: What cymbals did Hannes Grossmann use on Cosmogenesis?**
A: Meinl Byzance hand-hammered cymbals — 14" hi-hats, 16" Medium Thin and 18" Medium crashes, 22" ride, 18" China, and 10" splash. The Byzance series' tonal complexity gives Grossmann's cymbal work musical character beyond pure projection.

**Q: What technique defines Grossmann's playing on Cosmogenesis?**
A: Open-handed playing (left hand leads on hi-hat), polyrhythmic construction (drum part operates in a different subdivision from the guitar arrangement), and classical voice-leading in fills.
