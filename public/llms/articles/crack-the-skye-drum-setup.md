# Crack the Skye Drum Setup — Brann Dailor / Mastodon (2009)

> Complete breakdown of Brann Dailor's drum setup on Mastodon's Crack the Skye (2009). Tama Starclassic Maple kit, Istanbul Agop Traditional cymbals, single-pedal philosophy — the gear behind progressive metal's most melodically sophisticated drum performance.

## Overview

*Crack the Skye* (released March 24, 2009) is Mastodon's Grammy-nominated progressive concept album — Rolling Stone Top 100 Albums of the 2000s, universally acclaimed as both the band's artistic peak and the most melodically sophisticated drum performance in modern metal. Brann Dailor's drumming on this record is the fullest realization of his "drummer as melodist" philosophy: toms functioning as melodic counter-lines, ghost notes as vocabulary, and a single-kick groove approach that gives the album's complex rhythmic foundation its breath and intention.

Produced by Brendan O'Brien at Doppler Studios in Atlanta, the album's drum sound is room-aware and dimensional — O'Brien's rock production instincts capturing the natural resonance of Dailor's Tama Starclassic Maple rather than gating it out.

## Drum Kit

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple
- **Bass Drums:** 22" x 18" (single kick — Dailor does not use double bass)
- **Toms:** 10", 12", 13" rack toms (tuned in musical intervals for melodic use)
- **Floor Tom:** 16" x 16"
- **Shells:** 100% maple — warm, resonant, melodically focused
- **Snare:** Tama Starphonic or Artstar, 14" x 6.5", steel or brass shell
- **Estimated Kit Value:** $3,000–5,500 (Starclassic Maple shell pack)

## Cymbals

- **Brand:** Istanbul Agop (Traditional Series)
- **Hi-Hats:** Istanbul Agop 14" Traditional Hi-Hats — hand-hammered Turkish bronze, warm and complex
- **Crashes:** Istanbul Agop 18" Traditional Crash; Istanbul Agop 20" Traditional Crash
- **Ride:** Istanbul Agop 22" Traditional Ride — atmospheric, washy bell used as textural layer
- **China:** Istanbul Agop China or Zildjian 18" China (selective use for heavy accent moments)
- **Estimated Cymbal Value:** $2,000–3,500

## Hardware

- **Bass Drum Pedal:** Tama Iron Cobra (single pedal) — groove and placement over double-kick speed
- **Drumsticks:** Vater American Hickory — balance of power and ghost note sensitivity
- **Tom Heads (batter):** Remo Emperor Coated — two-ply warmth for melodic resonance
- **Kick Head (batter):** Remo Powerstroke 3 — controlled attack and low-end
- **Throne:** Roc-N-Soc Nitro

## Recording Context

Recorded at Doppler Studios, Atlanta with producer Brendan O'Brien. O'Brien's approach — natural room capture, dimensional drum sound, no excessive gating — is central to why the *Crack the Skye* drum performance sounds so present and musical. The Starclassic Maple's natural resonance is preserved; the Istanbul Agop cymbals' atmospheric warmth is audible in the mix as texture.

Key engineering notes:
- Kick: inside dynamic + outside condenser for tone
- Snare: SM57 top and bottom for ghost note clarity
- Toms: Sennheiser MD421 — captures Starclassic pitch
- Overheads: large-diaphragm condenser pair (spaced)
- Room mics: ambient capture in Doppler's live room

## Track Highlights

**"Crack the Skye"** — 7/4 and 4/4 alternating polyrhythmic construction; Dailor's most studied rhythmic pattern. The 7-over-4 displacement aligns thematically with the album's time-loop concept.

**"The Czar"** — Ten-minute epic with dramatic tempo changes; melodic tom patterns as opening counter-melody; ghost note density in quieter sections showcasing jazz vocabulary in prog metal context.

**"Oblivion"** — Dailor most prominent as vocalist; groove-focused playing; hi-hat patterns carrying intricate articulation beneath Brent Hinds's vocal melody.

**"The Last Baron"** — Thirteen-minute album closer; builds from near-silence to full-band assault; full Istanbul Agop cymbal palette used across complete dynamic range.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums does Brann Dailor use on Crack the Skye?**
A: Brann Dailor recorded *Crack the Skye* on his long-established Tama Starclassic Maple kit. The 100% maple shells provide the warm, resonant tone his melodic drumming philosophy requires — these drums are tuned to function as melodic instruments. Configuration: single 22" bass drum, three rack toms (10", 12", 13") tuned in musical intervals, and a 16" floor tom.

**Q: What cymbals does Brann Dailor use on Crack the Skye?**
A: Brann Dailor's cymbal setup on *Crack the Skye* was built around Istanbul Agop Traditional series — hand-hammered Turkish cymbals with warm, complex, dark character suited to the album's progressive and atmospheric character. Setup: 14" Traditional hi-hats, 18" and 20" Traditional crashes, 22" Traditional Ride. The ride is used as an atmospheric texture layer throughout the album's quieter passages.

**Q: Does Brann Dailor use double bass on Crack the Skye?**
A: No. Brann Dailor plays single bass drum with a single Tama Iron Cobra pedal across the entire album. His single-kick philosophy treats the bass drum as a groove instrument — precise placement and feel over double-kick velocity. On *Crack the Skye*, single-kick restraint gives the album's complex rhythmic foundation its musical quality and breath.

**Q: What makes Brann Dailor's drumming on Crack the Skye unique?**
A: The "drummer as melodist" concept at its most fully realized: toms tuned in musical intervals and integrated throughout arrangements as counter-melodies; extensive ghost note vocabulary from jazz applied to progressive metal; single-kick groove philosophy across a complex concept album; and Istanbul Agop atmospheric cymbal warmth used as textural colour. He also sings complex vocal parts simultaneously while executing demanding drum arrangements. The drum parts function compositionally, not just rhythmically.

**Q: What are the polyrhythm patterns on Crack the Skye?**
A: The title track alternates between 7/4 and 4/4 time signatures, creating rhythmic displacement that cycles without resolving at a shared downbeat. "The Czar" features metric modulation across its ten-minute length. These patterns align with the album's conceptual themes of time loops and non-linear consciousness — music and narrative sharing a rhythmic philosophy.
