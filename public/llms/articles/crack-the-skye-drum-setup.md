> Discover the exact drum kit, cymbals, and gear Brann Dailor used to record Mastodon's Crack the Skye (2009). Complete breakdown of his DW Collector's Series kit, Meinl Byzance cymbals, DW 9000 double pedal, and the melodic drumming philosophy behind progressive metal's most studied album.

**Genre:** Progressive Metal / Sludge Metal

## Overview

Released on March 24, 2009, Mastodon's *Crack the Skye* stands apart from every other metal album of its era — a progressive concept record about astral projection, time loops, and a paraplegic boy's journey through the spirit world. At its center is Brann Dailor, whose drumming on this album is studied in music schools, dissected on drum forums, and cited by professionals as a masterclass in melodic percussion.

The album was recorded at Doppler Studios in Atlanta, Georgia with producer Brendan O'Brien. The title carries deep personal weight: Brann named the album after his sister Skye Dailor, who died by suicide in 1995. That grief — processed over fourteen years — shaped the album's emotional weight and Brann's approach to every drum part.

*Crack the Skye* received a Grammy nomination and appeared on Rolling Stone's list of the Top 100 Albums of the 2000s. Brann's drumming on this record is considered his finest studio work and the definitive example of the "drummer as melodist" concept in progressive metal.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** DW Collector's Series (Maple shells, Natural/Lacquer finish)
- **Bass Drum:** 22" x 18" (DW 9000 Double Pedal)
- **Toms:** 10", 12", 13" rack toms; 16", 18" floor toms
- **Snare:** DW Collector's Series Maple, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Traditional — 14" Medium hi-hats, 18" and 20" crashes, 22" Medium Ride, 18" China, 10" Splash
- **Pedals:** DW 9000 Double Bass Pedal (chain-drive)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5A American Classic (pre-Meinl stick endorsement)
- **Heads:** Evans UV1 Coated (batter, all drums), Evans EMAD2 Clear (kick batter), Evans EC Resonant (tom resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium tension with tight snare wires for dynamic range

## Gear Details

### DW Collector's Series: Warm, Responsive, Progressive

For *Crack the Skye*, Brann built his setup around DW Collector's Series drums — the flagship line from Drum Workshop, built in Oxnard, California with vertically grained maple shells and VLT (Vertical Low Timbre) construction.

The five-tom configuration (three rack toms, two floor toms) provided the tonal palette Brann's melodic approach demands. He plays sequences across toms that function more like instrumental melodies than conventional fills — the pitch spread from a 10" rack to an 18" floor tom gave him the range for these melodic statements. The 22" bass drum was tuned to blend with the guitar low-end rather than compete with it, producing the cohesive rhythm section sound that defines Mastodon's approach.

Evans UV1 Coated heads provided the playing surface across all drums, their UV coating offering extended durability while maintaining the controlled response Brann's technique required.

### DW Collector's Series Maple Snare: Sensitivity for Melodic Playing

Brann's DW maple snare played a role unlike most metal snares. Rather than functioning as a blunt backbeat accent, it sustained a continuous ghost-note conversation and occasional melodic accent. The maple shell delivered warmth and sensitivity — the 6.5" depth provided body without sacrificing the dynamic range needed for ghost notes at lower volumes. Medium tuning with tight snare wires allowed the full expressive range his playing demands.

### Meinl Byzance: The Sound of Progressive Metal

Brann's long-term Meinl endorsement brought the Byzance Traditional series to the *Crack the Skye* sessions. The Byzance Traditional uses hand-hammered B20 bronze with irregular lathing — creating the warm, complex, dark character that suited O'Brien's atmospheric production philosophy.

The 14" Traditional Medium hi-hats delivered articulation for intricate open/closed patterns without sacrificing warmth. The crash setup (18" and 20") gave Brann dynamic range, while the 22" Traditional Ride provided complex bell tone suited to Mastodon's progressive arrangements. The 18" China added explosive trashy accents for the album's heavier passages.

### DW 9000 Double Pedal: Control Over Speed

The DW 9000's chain-drive mechanism with adjustable cam system provided a heavier, more deliberate feel than direct-drive alternatives. Brann's double bass work emphasizes placement and groove over raw speed — the 9000's power-with-control characteristic was a natural match. The extended footboard provided full range of motion for the technically demanding passages in "Divinations," "The Czar," and "Ghost of Karelia."

## Track Analysis

### Oblivion
Album opener and most accessible track. Brann sings lead vocals while playing one of his most intricate drum parts — one of progressive metal's most impressive dual performances. Flowing tom sequences move melodically across the full kit range. Hi-hat work alternates open and closed with unusual phrasing that creates rhythmic tension. Crash cymbal placement is compositional — used to mark emotional high points, not every measure.

### The Czar
Four-movement suite (Usurper, Escape, Martyr, Spiral) spanning multiple time signatures and emotional registers. Each movement requires different technical and emotional approaches. The "Spiral" section features the album's most complex polyrhythmic layering. Floor tom melodies in the "Martyr" section demonstrate Brann's intervallic thinking. Dynamic range is extraordinary — from near-silent to full power within sections.

### Crack the Skye (title track)
The album's conceptual apex. A 7-over-4 primary polyrhythm creates rhythmic displacement where listeners experience floating time — the groove feels simultaneously grounded and weightless. Brann's fills serve the 7-pulse pattern, creating melodic contours within the polyrhythm. Ghost note density on the snare maintains the underlying 4/4 pulse. One of the most studied polyrhythmic drum tracks in progressive metal. See the [polyrhythm techniques guide](/techniques/polyrhythms) for analysis.

### The Last Baron
Thirteen-minute album closer — the most demanding extended drum performance on the record. Tempo shifts across sections require genuine internal clock mastery. Every tom in Brann's configuration appears at least once across its full length. The climactic final section builds from ambient to full-band devastation before a drum-led decrescendo closes the album's conceptual arc.

## FAQs

**What drums does Brann Dailor use on Crack the Skye?**
On *Crack the Skye* (2009), Brann Dailor used a DW Collector's Series drum kit with maple shells in a natural/lacquer finish. Configuration: 22" bass drum, three rack toms (10", 12", 13"), two floor toms (16", 18"). The five-tom spread was essential for his melodic approach — he plays sequences across toms that function as instrumental melodies. See the [Brann Dailor drummer profile](/drummer/brann-dailor) for complete career gear history.

**What cymbals did Brann Dailor use on Crack the Skye?**
Meinl Byzance Traditional series throughout — 14" Medium hi-hats, 18" and 20" crashes, 22" Medium Ride, 18" China, and a 10" Splash. The Byzance Traditional's hand-hammered B20 bronze provides the warm, complex character that matched O'Brien's atmospheric production at Doppler Studios.

**What makes Brann Dailor's drumming style unique?**
Brann plays melodies on the kit rather than just marking time. His fills are intervallic sequences with harmonic relationship to the guitar and bass lines. He employs dense ghost notes (the snare rarely goes quiet between accents), complex polyrhythms (7-over-4 on the title track), and demonstrates extraordinary independence — he sings lead vocals on "Oblivion" while executing one of his most demanding drum parts. See [Brann Dailor's signature licks](/drummer/brann-dailor/licks) for technique breakdowns.

**What double pedal does Brann Dailor use?**
On *Crack the Skye* (2009), the DW 9000 double bass pedal — chain-drive mechanism with adjustable cam system, matched to his DW Collector's Series kit. The 9000's power-with-control characteristic suited Brann's placement-focused double bass technique, which emphasizes groove over raw speed.
