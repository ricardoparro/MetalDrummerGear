# Crack the Skye Drum Setup: Brann Dailor's Gear & Technique Breakdown

> Complete breakdown of Brann Dailor's drum setup on Mastodon's Crack the Skye. Discover the DW Collector's Series kit, Meinl Byzance cymbals, and polyrhythmic techniques that define progressive metal's most critically acclaimed album.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Brann Dailor](/llms/drummers/brann-dailor.md)
**Band / Album:** Mastodon — *Crack the Skye* (2009)
**Genre:** Progressive Metal / Sludge Metal

## Overview

Released on March 24, 2009, Mastodon's "Crack the Skye" stands as the band's most ambitious artistic achievement and a landmark in progressive metal. Grammy nominated, Rolling Stone-certified (Top 100 albums of the 2000s), and studied in music schools worldwide, the album signaled a sea change in what metal drumming could accomplish. At its center was Brann Dailor — a drummer who doesn't simply keep time, but composes melodies, counter-melodies, and emotional arcs from behind the kit.

The album was recorded at Doppler Studios in Atlanta with producer Brendan O'Brien, whose credits include Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, and Bruce Springsteen. Conceptually, the album traces a paraplegic boy who astral-projects through a wormhole and becomes trapped in Czarist Russia — a conceptual complexity matched beat-for-beat by the rhythmic complexity of the drumming, including 7-over-4 polyrhythms in the title track and shifting metric groupings throughout "The Czar."

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** DW Collector's Series (North American maple, natural satin finish)
- **Configuration:** 22" x 18" bass drum (single, with DW 9000 double pedal); 10", 12", 14" rack toms; 16" floor tom
- **Snare:** DW Collector's Series Maple, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Traditional series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW 9000 Series Double Pedal; DW 9000 Series Hi-Hat Stand; Roc-N-Soc Nitro Throne; Vic Firth 5A American Classic (pre-Meinl stick endorsement)
- **Heads:** Evans UV1 Coated (snare/tom batter), Evans Hazy 300 (snare resonant), Evans EMAD2 Clear (kick batter), Evans EQ3 Smooth White (kick front)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high for articulation, moderate snare wire tension

### DW Collector's Series: The Studio Arsenal

For Crack the Skye, Brann Dailor played DW Collector's Series drums — DW's flagship professional line, handcrafted in Oxnard, California. The natural satin maple shells provided warmth and fundamental depth that allowed Brann's fills to sing through the mix.

Brann runs a compact setup by progressive metal standards: a single 22" bass drum, three rack toms (10", 12", 14"), and one floor tom (16"). This configuration demands maximum efficiency — where other drummers rely on six or eight toms, Dailor generates equivalent drama from fewer voices, a reflection of his compositional thinking.

### Meinl Byzance Traditional Cymbals

The Byzance Traditional series is hand-hammered in Turkey using B20 bronze, producing complex, dark, warm overtones that suited the album's progressive and atmospheric character. Setup: 14" Medium hi-hats, 18" Medium Thin Crash, 20" Medium Crash, 22" Medium Ride, 18" China, 10" Splash.

The Byzance's musical quality allowed Brann's cymbals to sing in relation to the drums — essential for a drummer whose cymbal work is as melodically conceived as his tom fills.

### DW 9000 Double Pedal

Brann uses the DW 9000 double pedal on a single 22" bass drum, meaning all double-bass patterns come from the same shell for tonal consistency. The 9000's adjustable spring tension and cam shape tuned the pedal feel to Brann's technique — controlled power and musical precision over maximum velocity.

## Recording: Brendan O'Brien at Doppler Studios

O'Brien's production philosophy centers on capturing performances rather than constructing them. The drums on Crack the Skye have genuine dynamics — softer in verses, harder in climactic sections — with minimal trigger use and natural room ambience from Doppler Studios' large live room.

Tom tuning was an important element: Brann tunes his toms to specific intervals, creating a melodic relationship between the drums that becomes audible in flowing multi-tom phrases throughout "The Czar" and "The Last Baron."

## The Drummer as Melodist

Brann Dailor's defining characteristic is treating the drums as a melodic instrument. His fills are melodic phrases — with shape, direction, and emotional content — not merely rhythmic transitions. The title track's 7-over-4 polyrhythmic patterns are particularly studied: Brann feels both the 7-beat grouping and the band's 4/4 pulse simultaneously, creating the sensation of rhythmic displacement that mirrors the album's concept of being caught in a time loop.

Brann also sings while playing at full technical intensity — a level of independence that directly shapes how he conceives the music. His fills on tracks like "Oblivion" carry narrative weight that complements rather than competes with the vocal melodies above them.
