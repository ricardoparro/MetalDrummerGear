# Opeth Damnation Drum Setup: Martin Lopez's 2003 Progressive Rock Gear

> Discover the drum setup Martin Lopez used to record Opeth's Damnation — the acoustic progressive companion to Deliverance, featuring his most subtle and nuanced performance.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Martin Lopez](/llms/drummers/martin-lopez.md)
**Band / Album:** Opeth — *Damnation* (2003)
**Genre:** Progressive Rock

## Overview

Released on April 28, 2003, Opeth's *Damnation* stands apart from everything else in the band's catalog. Where *Blackwater Park* (2001) and *Deliverance* (2002) wove acoustic beauty through brutal death metal architecture, *Damnation* abandoned metal entirely — no distorted guitars, no blast beats, no death growls. Pure progressive rock, recorded simultaneously with *Deliverance* at Studio Fredman in Gothenburg with producer Steven Wilson.

For Martin Lopez, *Damnation* represented his most demanding performance challenge. Playing softly with perfect control, across an album that offers nowhere to hide behind distortion or density, demands an entirely different discipline. *Damnation* is 44 minutes of exposed drumming — every ghost note audible, every brush stroke deliberate, every dynamic shade visible in the stark acoustic landscape Steven Wilson created.

Lopez's drumming on *Damnation* draws entirely from his jazz vocabulary. The influence of Tony Williams, Stewart Copeland, and Billy Cobham — always present in his Opeth playing — surfaces here without the competing context of death metal. Brushwork features on several tracks. The hi-hat is used as a melodic voice. Ghost notes create rhythmic depth beneath clean guitar passages. The kick drum, when it appears, registers as a deliberate compositional choice rather than a rhythmic constant.

The gear was the same Sonor Designer Series kit Lopez had used on *Blackwater Park* and *Deliverance* — but in Wilson's production for this album, the drums sound different. Without the compression required to blend drums with distorted death metal guitars, the Sonor's natural acoustic character is fully exposed. The shells breathe. The Sabian HH series warmth becomes even more apparent on *Damnation*'s quieter canvas.

This is Martin Lopez at his most purely musical — the jazz vocabulary fully expressed without extreme metal as either medium or constraint.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Sonor Designer Series (Natural Maple finish)
- **Snare:** Sonor Designer Maple Snare, 14" x 5.5"
- **Cymbals:** Sabian HH (Hand Hammered) Series — no China cymbal
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW 5000 Single Pedal (no double bass); Vic Firth jazz wire brushes; Vic Firth 5A sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), minimal muffling for natural acoustic tone

### The Sonor Designer Series: Exposed in Daylight

Martin Lopez used the same Sonor Designer Series kit on *Damnation* as he had on *Blackwater Park* and *Deliverance*. But *Damnation* revealed the Sonor in a way neither companion album had: without compression and distorted guitar mass, the Designer Series' natural acoustic character was fully exposed.

Maple shells are chosen for warmth and sustain — qualities that *Damnation*'s pure progressive rock context demanded above all else. Each tom speaks with natural resonance, the bass drum's body is fully audible rather than compressed into attack, and the full tonal character of the shells is present in Wilson's mix. The minimal tom usage (each move deliberately compositional) and the single kick configuration (no double bass anywhere on the album) reflect Lopez's complete commitment to the album's acoustic identity.

### Sabian HH Series: Jazz Warmth as Primary Voice

The Sabian HH (Hand Hammered) series had been central to Lopez's Opeth cymbal palette since *Still Life* (1999). On *Damnation*, the HH cymbals' defining characteristic — dark, complex, warm tone — was more important than ever. On an album with no distorted guitars, every cymbal choice is fully audible as a musical statement.

The HH 20" Medium Ride is the album's defining cymbal voice. Lopez uses it as a jazz drummer would — not as time-keeping backdrop but as a melodic foreground. On "Windowpane" and "In My Time of Need," the ride's warm wash and complex overtones create harmonic texture beneath clean guitar. Significantly absent is the China cymbal that appeared on *Blackwater Park* for aggressive accents — on an album with no aggression to punctuate, the China had no role.

### Brushes: Jazz Technique Fully Expressed

Brushes on *Damnation* mark the full expression of Lopez's jazz vocabulary without extreme metal as context. The wire brush technique Lopez employs on tracks like "Death Whispered a Lullaby" draws from jazz tradition — circular sweeping patterns on the coated Ambassador snare head, delicate hi-hat taps, a rhythmic texture that supports acoustic guitar without dominating the groove. This is drumming that functions as atmosphere rather than architecture.

## Key Facts

- Recorded simultaneously with Deliverance at Studio Fredman, Gothenburg, 2002–2003
- Produced by Steven Wilson (Porcupine Tree) — same producer as Blackwater Park (2001)
- No distorted guitars, no blast beats, no double bass — Opeth's only entirely acoustic album
- Same Sonor Designer Series as Blackwater Park — captured differently without death metal compression
- Sabian HH series throughout — no China cymbal (no aggression to punctuate)
- Brushes introduced on several tracks: most direct expression of Lopez's jazz training
- Single DW 5000 pedal only — no double bass anywhere on the album
- Steven Wilson's acoustic production philosophy: minimal compression, natural room sound

## FAQ

**What drum setup did Martin Lopez use on Opeth's Damnation?**
Martin Lopez recorded Damnation (2003) using his Sonor Designer Series kit — maple shells, Natural Maple finish — the same configuration as Blackwater Park (2001) and Deliverance (2002). His setup included a 22" x 18" bass drum with single DW 5000 pedal (no double bass on this album), 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. Cymbals were Sabian HH series throughout: 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 20" Medium Ride, 16" Medium Thin Crash, and 18" Medium Crash — no China cymbal. Sessions took place at Studio Fredman in Gothenburg, produced by Steven Wilson.

**Why did Martin Lopez use brushes on Damnation?**
Lopez used brushes on several Damnation tracks (most audibly on "Death Whispered a Lullaby") because the album's purely acoustic progressive rock character demanded it. Damnation contains no distorted guitars, no death growls, and no blast beats — Opeth's single entirely acoustic record. Stick playing on the snare would have been too aggressive and defined for many of the album's quieter passages. Lopez's jazz training included extensive brush technique (influenced by Tony Williams and Billy Cobham), and Damnation was the Opeth album where that training was most fully expressed.

**How does Damnation compare to Blackwater Park as a drum performance?**
Damnation and Blackwater Park represent opposite ends of Martin Lopez's dynamic spectrum, recorded with the same kit, same producer, and same studio within roughly two years of each other. Blackwater Park (2001) demonstrates Lopez's ability to navigate between death metal brutality and acoustic sensitivity within a single album. Damnation (2003) eliminates the metal entirely, leaving only the acoustic progressive rock vocabulary. Technically, Damnation is arguably the more demanding performance: every note is exposed in an acoustic mix without distorted guitars or compression to provide context or cover.

**Was Damnation recorded at the same time as Deliverance?**
Yes. Opeth's Damnation (2003) and Deliverance (2002) were written and recorded simultaneously at Studio Fredman in Gothenburg with producer Steven Wilson. The two albums were conceived as companion pieces — Deliverance as the heavy album, Damnation as the acoustic counterpart. Martin Lopez tracked drums for both during the same recording sessions. The contrast between the two albums is one of metal history's most instructive studies: same drummer, same kit, same studio, same producer — but Deliverance drums are compressed and powerful, Damnation drums are open and warm.
