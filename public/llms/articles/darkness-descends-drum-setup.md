## Overview

When Dark Angel released "Darkness Descends" on October 6, 1986 on Combat Records, Gene Hoglan was 19 years old. The album's opening title track opened at 247 BPM — widely documented as the fastest recorded drum performance in thrash metal at the time. Before his work with Death, before Strapping Young Lad, before the Grammy-nominated Testament reunion, there was this: a teenager in North Hollywood laying down the album that would define extreme metal drumming for the next decade.

"Darkness Descends" was recorded at Track Record Studios in North Hollywood with producer Bill Metoyer, who had already worked with Slayer on their early Combat releases. Metoyer understood extreme metal drum production — the close-mic technique, the compressed attack, the tight snare character that made underground thrash legible at speed. What he captured on Darkness Descends was something neither he nor Hoglan could have fully anticipated: a foundational document of what extreme metal drumming could be.

The album's historical significance is difficult to overstate. Hoglan's blast-beat precursor techniques — alternating kick and snare at high velocity, integrated into full musical arrangements rather than deployed as simple noise — represented a conceptual leap beyond the straightforward thrash of the period. Metallica, Slayer, and Anthrax were fast; Hoglan was architectural. The patterns he played on tracks like "Perish in Flames" and "Hunger of the Undead" weren't just drumming — they were rhythmic compositions.

Gene Hoglan's gear for "Darkness Descends" reflected his age and the resources available to a Southern California extreme metal band in 1986. His primary kit was Tama Imperialstar with custom components — the accessible professional line that provided the reliability he needed for sessions of this intensity. This article documents every piece of equipment used on that recording and explains why, nearly 40 years later, Darkness Descends remains the essential starting point for understanding one of metal's greatest drummers. ### Tama Imperialstar: The Tool That Built the Foundation

For "Darkness Descends," Gene Hoglan recorded using a Tama Imperialstar kit augmented with custom components from his personal setup. The Imperialstar was Tama's mid-professional line in the mid-1980s — not the flagship Artstar (which Hoglan would use later in his career), but a reliable, well-constructed kit that could handle the session intensity extreme metal drumming demanded.

The twin 22" x 16" bass drums were already Hoglan's preference at this stage. Unlike some thrash drummers of the era who used single bass drums with double pedals, Hoglan was committed to independent double kick — two complete, separate bass drum instruments, each with its own pedal and tuning. This setup gave him the natural independence his patterns required. The 247 BPM title track opening was achieved acoustically, with two independent feet executing patterns that single-pedal setups could not replicate with the same organic feel.

The Imperialstar's poplar/basswood shell construction produced a warmer, more midrange-focused sound than the maple drums that would later become Hoglan's preference. In the context of Bill Metoyer's production and the close-mic technique standard at Track Record Studios, this shell character translated to drums that cut through dense guitar arrangements without excessive high-frequency aggression. The result was extreme speed that remained musical — every strike intelligible even at the album's fastest passages.

The configuration — two rack toms and two floor toms — established the four-tom spread that Hoglan would maintain throughout his career. Even at 19, his instincts for melodic fill construction were evident: listen to the tom work on "Black Prophecies" or "Merciless Death" and notice that the fills descend melodically, each drum voice contributing to a phrase rather than simply marking time.

### Ludwig Acrolite: The Crack That Defined an Album

The Ludwig Acrolite was one of the most ubiquitous snare drums in American music by the mid-1980s — a budget-to-mid-range aluminum shell snare that had been in continuous production since the 1960s. Its selection for "Darkness Descends" was pragmatic: Hoglan needed a snare that would crack with authority at the speeds he was executing, and the Acrolite's aluminum shell delivered exactly that character.

Aluminum shells produce a bright, cutting sound with fast response — different from the warmth of steel or the complexity of brass. For a 19-year-old playing at 247 BPM, response speed mattered. The Acrolite's shallow 5" depth kept the snare wire tension manageable at extreme velocities, maintaining a consistent "crack" character rather than the smeared attack that deeper, more resonant drums can produce at high speed.

Hoglan tuned the Acrolite medium-high for the Darkness Descends sessions — a tension setting that maximized the aluminum shell's natural brightness while maintaining enough body to stand out in Metoyer's dense production. The snare sound on this album is one of its defining characteristics: sharp, precise, almost military in its articulation, driving patterns that could have devolved into noise and keeping them legible.

The choice of an Acrolite rather than a more expensive boutique drum reflects the realities of a Combat Records thrash band in 1986. Gene Hoglan was not yet an endorser with free equipment; he used what worked. That the Acrolite worked as well as it did on this recording is a testament to both the drum's inherent quality and Hoglan's ability to extract maximum character from his tools.

### Zildjian A Series: Standard Tools, Extraordinary Results

Gene Hoglan's cymbal selection for "Darkness Descends" centered on Zildjian's traditional A series — the hand-hammered standard that dominated professional drumming in the 1980s. For extreme metal at these speeds, the A series provided two essential qualities: fast response and musical definition.

The Zildjian A 14" New Beat hi-hats were foundational. The New Beat design — heavier bottom, standard top — produced the tight "chick" sounds that Hoglan's precision hi-hat patterns required, while offering enough complexity for the open and half-open work in more mid-tempo sections. At 247 BPM, hi-hat clarity is not a luxury; it's the difference between a comprehensible musical statement and auditory chaos. Hoglan's hi-hat work on Darkness Descends navigates that distinction repeatedly and brilliantly.

The two-crash configuration (16" and 18") gave Hoglan the dynamic range that compositions spanning multiple tempo and intensity levels required. The 16" Medium Thin provided fast, punchy accents for quick syncopated work; the 18" medium handled the album's larger section boundaries. This paired approach — lighter crash for agility, heavier crash for weight — was intuitive and effective.

The 20" A Medium Ride, used primarily for bell accents and mid-tempo groove sections, added textural variety that kept the album from becoming a single-dynamic exercise in extreme tempo. Even at 19, Hoglan understood that sustained maximum intensity is less impactful than intensity earned through contrast.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Gene Hoglan use on Darkness Descends?**

A: Gene Hoglan recorded Darkness Descends (1986) using a Tama Imperialstar kit as his primary setup, augmented with custom components. His configuration included two 22" x 16" bass drums for his signature independent double-kick setup — separate drums with separate pedals, not a double pedal on a single drum. His snare was a Ludwig Acrolite aluminum model at 14" x 5", chosen for its fast response and bright crack at extreme speeds. Cymbals were Zildjian A Series — the hand-hammered standard of the era — and he used single pedals (one per kick drum) from Tama's Iron Cobra line.

**Q: How fast is the opening of Dark Angel's Darkness Descends?**

A: The opening title track of Dark Angel's Darkness Descends (1986) runs at approximately 247 BPM — widely documented as the fastest recorded drum performance in thrash metal at the time of its release. Gene Hoglan was 19 years old when this was recorded. The tempo was achieved acoustically with twin bass drums and no electronic triggers or samples. This speed record contributed significantly to Darkness Descends becoming a foundational document of extreme metal, and established Hoglan's reputation as The Atomic Clock years before that nickname was coined.

**Q: What is Gene Hoglan's connection to Dark Angel?**

A: Gene Hoglan served as Dark Angel's drummer from the band's formation in the early 1980s through their dissolution in 1992. He recorded all of their studio albums including We Have Arrived (1984), Darkness Descends (1986), Leave Scars (1989), and Time Does Not Heal (1991). Dark Angel was his primary band before his work with Death, Strapping Young Lad, and later Testament. Darkness Descends is widely considered his breakthrough performance — the recording that established his reputation in extreme metal and led directly to Chuck Schuldiner recruiting him for Individual Thought Patterns (1993).
