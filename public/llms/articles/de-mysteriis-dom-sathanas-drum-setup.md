## Overview

Released on May 25, 1994, on Deathlike Silence Productions — the label founded by Mayhem guitarist Øystein Aarseth (Euronymous), who had been murdered nine months earlier — *De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas* arrived carrying more mythology than almost any album in metal history. Bassist Varg Vikernes (Burzum), who killed Euronymous before the album shipped, had his bass lines retained on the final pressing. Vocalist Attila Csihar, a Hungarian black metal figure recruited after the 1991 death of prior vocalist Dead (Per Yngve Ohlin), delivered the album's inhuman vocal performances separately before the final mix.

None of this mythology diminishes what Hellhammer accomplished on this album. Recorded at Grieghallen Studio in Bergen, Norway, with producer Pytten (Eirik Hundvin), the drum performances on *De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas* remain the defining blast beat benchmark in black metal. Songs like "Freezing Moon," "Buried by Time and Dust," and the title track demonstrate blast beat drumming that is simultaneously ferocious and precise.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl (early professional configuration, pre-Reference Series)
- **Snare:** Ludwig Custom Acrolite, 14" × 5" — aluminum shell
- **Cymbals:** Paiste 2002 Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Gibraltar double bass pedal (chain-drive), Gibraltar hi-hat stand and cymbal stands
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth American Classic 5B
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (snare batter), Remo Powerstroke 3 (bass batter)

### Ludwig Custom Acrolite: Black Metal's Most Imitated Snare Sound

The snare sound on *De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas* is instantly recognizable — a cold, cutting crack with enough presence to slice through the album's dense guitar layers without artificial brightness. Hellhammer achieved this with the Ludwig Custom Acrolite, an aluminum-shelled snare whose construction made it one of the most recorded drums in professional studios throughout the late 1980s and early 1990s.

The Acrolite's aluminum shell produces a naturally bright, focused sound with a fast decay that prevents sustained ring between strokes. In Grieghallen's close-miked environment, this fast decay was essential: each stroke in a rapid blast sequence needed to be heard as a discrete event rather than a sustained wash. Producer Pytten captured this with a Shure SM57 positioned close to the drum, angled toward the shell to minimize hi-hat bleed while maximizing attack.

### Paiste 2002 Cymbals: Cold Brightness for the Norwegian Sound

Hellhammer's cymbal choice fell on Paiste's 2002 series — the professional standard for extreme metal production in the early 1990s. The 2002's characteristic sound — bright, cutting, with a fast initial transient — was ideally suited to Grieghallen's dry recording environment and served a specific function against Euronymous's dense, layered guitar production.

Setup: 14" 2002 Hi-Hats, 16" Crash, 18" Crash, 20" Ride, 18" China.

The 14" hi-hats anchored the blast beat patterns. The 2002's medium-weight construction provided articulation at Hellhammer's extreme tempos, distinguishing individual hi-hat strokes within the blast sequence.

### Gibraltar Hardware and the Double Bass Foundation

Gibraltar hardware was the professional standard for extreme metal recording in the early 1990s. The chain-drive double bass pedal formed the mechanical platform for Hellhammer's blast beat sequences — the sustained double-kick patterns on "Pagan Fears," "Life Eternal," and the title track represent the ceiling of what chain-drive engineering could produce in 1992–1993.

### Grieghallen Studio Recording Context

Grieghallen Studio (Bergen, Norway) produced the foundational records of Norwegian black metal: Mayhem's *De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas*, Emperor's *In the Nightside Eclipse*, Burzum's early albums, Darkthrone's *Under a Funeral Moon*, Immortal's *Pure Holocaust*. These records share a sonic signature — dry, cold, precise — directly attributable to Pytten's production approach and Grieghallen's acoustic properties.

Pytten's drum capture philosophy prioritized definition over ambience, working close to the drums and capturing immediate transient attack without room reverb decay. The Ludwig Acrolite's fast-decaying aluminum character was perfectly matched to this production philosophy.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum setup did Hellhammer use on De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas?**
A: On Mayhem's De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas (1994), Hellhammer used an early Pearl shell kit with double 22" bass drums. His snare was a Ludwig Custom Acrolite — an aluminum-shelled drum whose fast decay and cold brightness became the defining snare sound of Norwegian black metal. Cymbals were Paiste 2002 series; hardware was Gibraltar. The drum tracks were recorded at Grieghallen Studio in Bergen, Norway, with producer Pytten (Eirik Hundvin). This pre-dates his later Pearl Reference endorsement. For his current setup, see the [Hellhammer drum setup guide](/articles/hellhammer-drum-setup).

**Q: Where was De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas recorded?**
A: De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas was recorded at Grieghallen Studio in Bergen, Norway, with producer Pytten (Eirik Hundvin) — the same facility that recorded Emperor's In the Nightside Eclipse, Burzum's early albums, and Darkthrone's Under a Funeral Moon. Grieghallen's dry, close-miked recording approach produced the cold, precise drum sound that defined Norwegian black metal production across the early-to-mid 1990s.

**Q: What snare drum did Hellhammer use on De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas?**
A: Hellhammer used a Ludwig Custom Acrolite snare on De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas (1994). The Acrolite's aluminum shell construction produces a naturally bright, fast-decaying crack that cuts through dense guitar production without sustained ring. Its cold, precise character became the production reference point for Norwegian black metal snare sound.

**Q: Why is De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas considered the defining black metal album?**
A: De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas is considered the defining black metal album because it represents the fully realized synthesis of blast beat extremity, atmospheric melodic composition, and cold production aesthetic that Norwegian black metal had been building toward since the late 1980s. Hellhammer's blast beats set the standard for the genre. Combined with the album's extraordinary biographical context — Euronymous's murder, Varg Vikernes's bass lines on the final pressing, Dead's prior suicide — it became both a musical benchmark and a historical artifact that appears in virtually every discussion of black metal's origins.

**Q: How does Hellhammer's blast beat technique on De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas compare to his later work?**
A: The De Mysteriis blasts (1994) capture Hellhammer at an earlier developmental stage — recorded on chain-drive Gibraltar pedals rather than the direct-drive Pearl Demon Drive he later adopted. The 1994 performances are arguably rawer and more aggressive-feeling than the more refined later work on Ordo Ad Chao (2007) and Daemon (2019). De Mysteriis documents the foundational template; his later work represents the technical refinement built on that foundation.
