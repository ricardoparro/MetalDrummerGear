# Lars Ulrich's Drum Setup on Metallica's Death Magnetic (2008)

> Lars Ulrich's return to thrash — how Rick Rubin and a Tama Starclassic Birch kit helped Metallica recapture the aggression of their classic era on Death Magnetic.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Lars Ulrich](/llms/drummers/lars-ulrich.md)
**Band / Album:** Metallica — *Death Magnetic* (2008)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Heavy Metal

## Overview

Released on September 12, 2008, Death Magnetic was Metallica's most direct attempt to reconnect with their thrash roots since the early 1990s. Coming five years after the divisive St. Anger — an album notorious for Lars Ulrich's steel snare sound and stripped-down approach — Death Magnetic was explicitly marketed as Metallica "going back to their roots."

The choice of Rick Rubin as producer was itself a statement of intent. Unlike Bob Rock, who had helmed the radio-friendly Black Album through Load and ReLoad, Rubin was known for stripping productions back to raw power. He pushed Metallica to write longer, more aggressive songs in the vein of Master of Puppets and ...And Justice for All.

Lars Ulrich's drum performance on Death Magnetic marked a return to double bass intensity, extended fills, and thrash-paced tempos abandoned during the groove metal middle period. His kit — anchored by a Tama Starclassic Birch — produced a more open, punchier sound than the compressed tones of the Bob Rock era.

The album is also infamous for the Loudness War controversy. The CD master was heavily brick-walled, clipping the transients and reducing dynamic range to near zero. Ironically, the Guitar Hero III version — sourced from less-compressed stems — is widely considered to sound better, spawning one of the earliest mainstream conversations about digital mastering practices.

### Lars's 2008 Studio Kit: Tama Starclassic Birch

For Death Magnetic, Lars Ulrich recorded with a Tama Starclassic Birch kit. The Starclassic line uses six-ply birch shells known for their fast attack, punchy midrange, and dry, focused projection. The birch construction was ideal for Rubin's approach: close-miked, minimal processing, letting the natural tone of the drums speak.

The single 22" bass drum driven by a double Iron Cobra pedal remained Lars's standard configuration. The deeper 18" shell depth provided additional low-end resonance, giving the kick drum authoritative punch without requiring heavy EQ. The four-tom configuration (10", 12", 14", 16") mirrors the ...And Justice for All setup, giving Lars the tonal range needed for the album's longer, more arrangement-intensive compositions.

### The Tama LU1465: Lars Returns with Authority

After the infamous steel-rimmed, resonance-free snare of St. Anger, Lars's snare approach on Death Magnetic was a deliberate corrective. The Tama LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature Snare — developed in collaboration with Tama — provided the tight, cutting crack needed for thrash-tempo passages without the lifeless clunk of the St. Anger snare.

The 14" x 6.5" steel shell delivers brightness and focused attack. The die-cast hoops add tuning stability and a harder rimshot character. Tuned medium-high, the snare cuts through dense guitar arrangements on tracks like "That Was Just Your Life" and "Broken, Beat & Scarred."

### Zildjian A Custom: Lars's Modern Voice

By 2008, Lars Ulrich had been a committed Zildjian A Custom user for over a decade. The 14" A Custom Hi-Hats provide quick response and bright stick definition. Multiple crash cymbal sizes (16", 18", 19") give Lars a wide dynamic palette. The 20" A Custom Ride handles clear bell patterns and crash-riding during intense passages with good projection and stick articulation.

## Recording Approach: Rick Rubin's Philosophy

Rick Rubin's approach to recording drums on Death Magnetic differed fundamentally from the Bob Rock methodology. Primary drum tracking took place at Shangri-La Studios in Malibu — a large, natural-sounding room providing ambience without artificial reverb. Additional sessions used HQ Studios in Novato, Metallica's own rehearsal complex.

Rubin used significant room microphone presence — a departure from the dry Justice approach — and minimal gating, letting the natural Starclassic Birch tone breathe. He pushed Lars toward performances that valued instinct and aggression over technical perfection, a significant shift from the meticulous Bob Rock era.

## The Loudness War Controversy

Death Magnetic became a landmark case in the Loudness War debate. The CD release measures a Dynamic Range (DR) score of approximately 3 — among the lowest ever recorded for a major label rock release. The Guitar Hero III version, sourced from pre-mastered stems, measures DR8 and is widely considered the superior listening experience, with audible drum transients and genuine dynamic variation. Mastering engineer Ted Jensen acknowledged the problematic levels publicly. This controversy accelerated mainstream awareness of digital mastering practices.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums did Lars Ulrich use on Death Magnetic?**
Lars Ulrich recorded Death Magnetic in 2008 using a Tama Starclassic Birch kit. The configuration included a single 22x18 inch bass drum played with a Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal, 10 and 12 inch rack toms, and 14 and 16 inch floor toms. The Starclassic's six-ply birch shells provided a fast attack and punchy midrange suited to the album's return-to-thrash approach under producer Rick Rubin.

**Q: Why does Death Magnetic sound so loud and distorted?**
Death Magnetic became a landmark case in the Loudness War debate. The CD release was mastered at extreme loudness levels, resulting in a Dynamic Range score of approximately 3 — meaning the audio was heavily brick-walled and drum transients clipped. The Guitar Hero III version, sourced from pre-mastered stems, has a DR score around 8 and is widely considered to sound significantly better, with audible dynamics. Mastering engineer Ted Jensen acknowledged the problematic levels in a public statement.

**Q: What snare did Lars Ulrich use on Death Magnetic?**
Lars Ulrich used the Tama LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature Snare — a 14x6.5 inch steel-shell snare with die-cast hoops developed in collaboration with Tama. This was a marked departure from the snare approach on St. Anger (2003). The LU1465 remains available commercially and is still Lars's primary snare.

**Q: What cymbals did Lars Ulrich use on Death Magnetic?**
Lars Ulrich used Zildjian A Custom cymbals: 14" A Custom Hi-Hats, A Custom crashes in 16, 18, and 19 inch sizes, a 20" A Custom Ride, and an 18" A Custom China.

**Q: How did Rick Rubin change Lars Ulrich's drum sound on Death Magnetic?**
Rick Rubin pushed Metallica back toward their thrash roots and applied a more naturalistic recording philosophy. Tracking at Shangri-La Studios in Malibu with significant room microphones and minimal post-production gating, Rubin let the natural ambience of the Tama Starclassic Birch breathe. He also encouraged Lars to prioritize feel and aggression over technical perfection — resulting in Lars's most energetic recorded performance since ...And Justice for All.
