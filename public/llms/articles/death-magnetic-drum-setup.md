# Lars Ulrich's Drum Setup on Metallica's Death Magnetic (2008)

> Lars Ulrich's return to thrash — how Rick Rubin and a Tama Starclassic Birch kit helped Metallica recapture the aggression of their classic era on Death Magnetic.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Lars Ulrich](/llms/drummers/lars-ulrich.md)
**Band / Album:** Metallica — *Death Magnetic* (2008)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Heavy Metal

## Overview

Released on September 12, 2008, "Death Magnetic" was Metallica's most anticipated comeback in years — a deliberate, loudly-marketed return to the aggressive thrash sound that had made the band legends. After the divisive "St. Anger" (2003) stripped down production and famously omitted guitar solos, Death Magnetic swung hard in the opposite direction: guitar solos returned, song structures grew complex again, and Lars Ulrich played with an energy not heard since the late 1980s.

The choice of Rick Rubin as producer was the loudest signal Metallica could send. Rubin — who had helmed landmark records for Johnny Cash, Slayer, and Red Hot Chili Peppers — was known for capturing raw, powerful performances rather than over-engineering them. For Metallica, the partnership meant stepping away from the Bob Rock era (1990–2003) and into a more direct, stripped-back recording approach.

The Loudness War controversy surrounding Death Magnetic is inseparable from the album's legacy. The CD release was mastered by Ted Jensen at Sterling Sound with aggressive limiting that pushed levels so high that waveforms visibly clipped. Ironically, the Guitar Hero III downloadable version — sourced from pre-mastered stems — sounded dramatically more dynamic and less distorted. The resulting online furor helped accelerate mainstream awareness of the loudness war problem.

- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (snare batter), Remo Emperor Clear (tom batter), Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear (kick)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension for punch and attack

### The Tama Starclassic Birch Kit

Lars Ulrich used his Tama Starclassic Birch kit for Death Magnetic — birch/bubinga shell construction with die-cast hoops. The configuration was a standard four-tom setup: 10", 12", 14", and 16" toms over a single 22" x 18" bass drum driven by a Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal. Birch shells are known for their tighter, drier sound compared to maple, producing a more focused low end and brighter attack — characteristics that suited both Rick Rubin's direct production approach and Lars's aggressive playing style.

### The Lars Ulrich Signature Snare

The Tama LU1465 is Lars's signature snare: a 14" x 6.5" steel shell with die-cast hoops. Steel delivers brightness and cut; the 6.5" depth prevents it from sounding thin. Die-cast hoops add rigidity and tuning consistency. The LU1465 was Lars's primary snare through the Death Magnetic sessions.

### Zildjian A Custom Cymbals

Lars had used the Zildjian A Custom series since the mid-1990s. For Death Magnetic: 14" A Custom hi-hats, 16", 18", and 19" A Custom crashes, a 20" A Custom ride, and an 18" A Custom China. The brilliant finish and bright, fast response suited the album's punchy production.

## FAQ

**Why does Death Magnetic sound so loud and distorted?**
The CD was mastered with extreme limiting by Ted Jensen at Sterling Sound, pushed so hard that waveforms visibly clipped. The mix was delivered at very high levels, leaving minimal headroom. The Guitar Hero III stems, sourced before final mastering, sounded dramatically more dynamic — this comparison sparked significant online discussion about the loudness war. The CD version has a DR rating of approximately DR3-DR5.

**What drums did Lars Ulrich use on Death Magnetic?**
Tama Starclassic Birch kit: 10", 12", 14", 16" toms over a 22" x 18" bass drum with Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal. Snare: Tama LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature (14" x 6.5" steel). Cymbals: Zildjian A Custom. Sticks: Ahead Lars Ulrich Signature.

**How did Rick Rubin change Lars Ulrich's drum sound on Death Magnetic?**
Rubin valued raw performance energy over polished studio craft. Lars was encouraged to play with forward momentum and aggression. More room ambience than the dry Justice approach gave the drums a fuller, more three-dimensional sound. The combination of the Tama Starclassic Birch's natural punch and Rubin's philosophy produced Lars's most energetic studio work since the late 1980s.

**Is Death Magnetic a return to the Master of Puppets sound?**
Death Magnetic is closer to the Master of Puppets and Justice era than any Metallica album since the Black Album, but isn't a direct replica. Guitar solos returned, song structures are more complex, and Lars plays with genuine aggression. However, the production approach is more contemporary. See the [Master of Puppets drum setup](/articles/master-of-puppets-drum-setup) and [...And Justice for All drum setup](/articles/and-justice-for-all-drum-setup) for comparison.

**What is the Lars Ulrich Signature snare drum?**
The Tama LU1465 — 14" x 6.5" steel shell, die-cast hoops. Lars's primary snare through Death Magnetic and available as a production model. Popular for its high-attack, bright steel character with proven studio and live pedigree.

## Key Facts

- First Metallica album with Rick Rubin as producer
- Explicitly marketed as return to thrash after the divisive St. Anger
- Guitar solos returned after being absent on St. Anger
- CD mastering sparked loudness war controversy; GH version sounded more dynamic
- Lars's most energetic double-bass studio performances since ...And Justice for All
- Recorded at HQ Studios, San Rafael, CA and Clinton Recording Studios, New York
- Ted Jensen publicly acknowledged mastering limitations due to delivery levels
- DR rating approximately DR3-DR5 on CD release
