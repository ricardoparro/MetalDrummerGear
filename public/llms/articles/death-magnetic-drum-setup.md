## Overview

Death Magnetic (2008) was Metallica's deliberate return to their thrash metal roots after the polarizing St. Anger (2003). Producer Rick Rubin replaced longtime collaborator Bob Rock, bringing a live-tracking philosophy and a preference for natural room acoustics. Lars Ulrich used a Tama Starclassic Birch kit throughout the sessions — a return to birch shells after years on maple — paired with his signature Tama LU1465 snare, Zildjian A Custom cymbals, and Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal.

The album's recording approach was notably more organic than recent Metallica productions: Lars and James Hetfield tracked together whenever possible, with natural bleed and room ambience intentional rather than corrected. Sound City Studios in Van Nuys was selected specifically for its large live room and natural acoustics.

Despite the strong raw recordings, Death Magnetic became one of the most prominent examples of loudness war mastering excess. The CD master was brickwall-limited to approximately -3 dBFS RMS, causing severe digital clipping. A Guitar Hero version released alongside the album used a less-compressed mix and became the audiophile reference version. Mastering engineer Ted Jensen publicly expressed dissatisfaction with the final CD master.

## Drum Kit: Tama Starclassic Birch

**Brand:** Tama  
**Model:** Starclassic Birch  
**Finish:** Platinum Fade Sparkle  
**Shell Material:** Birch with Star-Cast mounting system

**Configuration:**
- Bass Drum: 22" x 18" (single kick, double pedal)
- Rack Toms: 10" x 8", 12" x 10"
- Floor Toms: 14" x 14", 16" x 16"

**Why birch?** Birch shells produce a punchy, mid-forward attack with fast decay — ideal for cutting through heavily distorted guitars and suited to the album's aggressive tempos. This contrasts with the warmer maple kits Lars used during the Black Album era. The Starclassic's Star-Cast mounting kept shells round under tension, improving tuning stability during long sessions.

**Estimated value (2008):** $3,500–$5,000  
**Estimated value (used today):** $4,000–$6,500

## Snare: Tama LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature

**Brand:** Tama  
**Model:** LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature  
**Size:** 14" x 6.5"  
**Shell:** Steel with die-cast hoops  
**Batter Head:** Remo Ambassador Coated  
**Resonant Head:** Remo Ambassador Snare Side  
**Tuning:** Medium-high tension — attack-focused

The LU1465 was a definitive return to conventional snare characteristics after St. Anger's wires-removed experiment. Steel shell, proper wire response, die-cast hoops for tuning stability. Lars tuned it at medium-high tension for maximum crack without losing body. The LU1465 has remained his primary snare through Hardwired...to Self-Destruct (2016) and 72 Seasons (2023).

**Estimated value:** $350–$450 (2008) / $400–$550 (new today)

## Recording Techniques

**Producer:** Rick Rubin  
**Studios:** HQ Recording Studios (San Rafael) + Sound City Studios (Van Nuys)

### Microphone Setup
- **Kick:** AKG D112 inside port, Neumann FET outside
- **Snare:** Shure SM57 top and bottom
- **Toms:** Sennheiser MD421 close-miked on each tom
- **Hi-Hat:** AKG C451 small-diaphragm condenser
- **Overheads:** Neumann U87 spaced pair
- **Room:** Room mics blended more aggressively than prior Metallica productions

### Key Approaches
- **Live tracking:** Lars and James Hetfield tracked simultaneously as much as possible
- **Natural bleed:** Guitar bleed into drum mics was intentional, adding cohesion
- **Room ambience:** Sound City selected for large live room and natural acoustics
- **Minimal processing:** The raw recordings were relatively unprocessed; controversy arose at mastering

### The Loudness Controversy
The CD master was delivered with extreme brickwall limiting, resulting in:
- Severe digital clipping throughout the album
- Audible distortion in quiet passages and cymbal swells
- Dynamic range loss that made the album fatiguing at volume
- A Guitar Hero version (less-compressed mix) becoming the audiophile reference
- Ted Jensen publicly stating his dissatisfaction with the final release
- A widely-circulated fan petition demanding a remaster

This episode became one of the most cited examples of loudness war damage to a commercial recording.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums did Lars Ulrich use on Death Magnetic?**  
A: Tama Starclassic Birch kit — single 22x18" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms. Tama LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature snare. Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal. Zildjian A Custom cymbals throughout.

**Q: Why does Death Magnetic sound so loud and distorted?**  
A: The CD master was brickwall-limited to near-maximum loudness levels, causing severe digital clipping and audible distortion. This was a product of the loudness war in commercial mastering. The Guitar Hero version of the album used a significantly less-compressed mix and became the preferred listening version. Mastering engineer Ted Jensen publicly expressed dissatisfaction with the final CD release.

**Q: What cymbals did Lars use on Death Magnetic?**  
A: Zildjian A Custom series — 14" hi-hats, 16"/17"/18" crashes, 20" ride, 18" China. Lars has used A Custom cymbals since the mid-1990s.

**Q: How is Death Magnetic different from St. Anger drumming?**  
A: St. Anger used a steel snare with wires removed (producing the infamous "trash can" sound), was tracked live in a rehearsal space, and featured mostly mid-tempo playing. Death Magnetic returned to the LU1465 signature snare with proper wires, was tracked at proper recording studios, and brought back thrash-speed tempos. The contrast was deliberate — Rick Rubin was explicitly hired to help reconnect with classic Metallica.

**Q: Who produced Death Magnetic?**  
A: Rick Rubin, marking the first Metallica album without Bob Rock since ...And Justice for All (1988). Rubin prioritized live ensemble recording and natural room sound, including sessions at the legendary Sound City Studios.

**Q: What is the "Death Magnetic GH vs CD" debate?**  
A: The Guitar Hero version of Death Magnetic used a different, less-compressed mix than the commercial CD release. The CD master was brickwall-limited to extreme loudness levels, causing digital clipping. Side-by-side comparisons revealed the GH version had significantly more dynamic range and less distortion. This comparison became widely shared in audiophile and music production communities and remains one of the clearest demonstrations of loudness war damage.
