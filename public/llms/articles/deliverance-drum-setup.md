# Deliverance Drum Setup: Martin Lopez's Most Extreme Opeth Performance

> Discover the drum kit, cymbals, and gear Martin Lopez used to record Opeth's Deliverance (2002) — the heaviest album in the Opeth catalog and Lopez's most brutal recorded performance. Complete setup breakdown with gear details and track analysis.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup

**Band / Album:** Opeth — *Deliverance* (2002)
**Genre:** Progressive Death Metal

## Overview

Released on November 11, 2002, *Deliverance* arrived as one half of a remarkable companion pair alongside *Damnation*. Where *Damnation* explored acoustic prog with barely a drum in sight, *Deliverance* went the opposite direction — the most brutal, death-metal-driven record Opeth ever made. It was Martin Lopez at his most extreme, a performance that demanded everything he had developed over years of jazz-influenced metal drumming and then pushed it further.

The companion-album concept was Mikael Åkerfeldt's: record two simultaneous albums occupying opposite poles of Opeth's identity. *Damnation* would be the acoustic, dreamlike half. *Deliverance* would be the drums-only half in spirit — relentless, muscular, uncompromising. For Lopez, this meant embodying both extremes within the same sessions. Sustaining blast-beat intensity across 13-minute tracks while also playing the near-silent acoustic passages on *Damnation* in the same studio, on the same days, required an extraordinary command of self and instrument.

Lopez has described his drumming philosophy as "architecture" — building a structure the rest of the music inhabits, rather than merely accompanying it. This philosophy is most clearly expressed on *Deliverance* because the demands are greatest there. When tracks run 13 and 14 minutes at extreme tempos, a drummer who just keeps time loses the listener. Lopez creates landmarks, marks transitions, shapes anticipation — and does it while sustaining the most brutal performance of his Opeth career.

Recording took place at Fredman Studio in Gothenburg alongside *Damnation*, with Steven Wilson and Mikael Åkerfeldt co-producing. Wilson adapted his approach for *Deliverance* — tighter compression, less room ambience, more attack on kick and snare — matching the album's uncompromising character.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Masters Premium (Natural Maple finish)
- **Kit Configuration:** 20" x 18" Bass Drum; 10" x 8" and 12" x 10" Rack Toms; 14" x 14" and 16" x 16" Floor Toms
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Maple Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Paiste 2002 Series — 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 16" and 18" Crashes, 20" Medium Ride, 18" China
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW 5000 Double Pedal; DW Hard Felt Beaters; Roc-N-Soc Nitro Throne; Vic Firth American Classic 5B
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Clear (batter), Remo Ambassador Clear (resonant); Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear (kick)
- **Snare Tuning:** Medium-high for maximum cut and attack

### The Pearl Masters Premium: Built for Brutality

For *Deliverance*, Lopez switched from the Sonor Designer Series used on *Blackwater Park* to a Pearl Masters Premium — a change that reflected the album's harder character. The Pearl's 6-ply maple shells delivered more attack and faster transient response than the Sonor, qualities essential for *Deliverance*'s relentless tempos.

The most significant configuration change from *Blackwater Park* was the kick drum: 20" diameter versus the 22" of the previous album. This smaller kick produced a punchier, more immediate attack without the low-frequency weight of the larger drum. On an album where the guitars are thick and distorted, a 20" kick punches through the mix more cleanly. Lopez and Wilson understood this when shaping the drum sound for the recording.

The tom configuration remained similar — 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms — giving Lopez the same melodic voice range across his fills. Pearl's Masters Premium tuned to a slightly higher pitch than the Sonor, enhancing articulation for the rapid tom runs *Deliverance* contains in abundance.

### The Paiste 2002 Arsenal

Lopez chose Paiste 2002 series cymbals for *Deliverance* — a departure from the darker Sabian HH/AAX palette he had used on *Blackwater Park*. The 2002's brighter, more cutting character perfectly matched the album's aggressive production intent.

The 14" Medium Hi-Hats articulate clearly even under blast-beat intensity. The 16" and 18" Crash cymbals speak quickly and decay at a pace suited to *Deliverance*'s rapid section changes — Lopez uses crashes as structural markers here, punctuating riff transitions throughout "Wreath" and "Master's Apprentices." The 20" Medium Ride balanced wash and bell definition, serving both the grinding mid-tempo sections and the album's more melodic passages. The 18" China provided controlled bursts of aggression for the most intense transitions.

### The Snare and Heavier Beaters

*Deliverance* demanded a snare with more authority than the Sonor used on *Blackwater Park*. Lopez chose a deeper Pearl Free-Floating Maple snare — at 14" x 6.5", it delivered the crack needed to cut through dense guitar arrangements. The Free-Floating shell design isolated the shell from hardware contact, producing fuller sustain and a more open, powerful crack.

The heavier DW felt beaters were another deliberate upgrade for *Deliverance*. The extra mass produced more fundamental attack on kick drum passages — the difference is audible: *Deliverance*'s kick has a denser, more physical punch than *Blackwater Park*'s.

## Key Facts

- Recorded at Fredman Studio, Gothenburg in 2002
- Produced by Steven Wilson & Mikael Åkerfeldt (same producers as *Blackwater Park*)
- Released simultaneously with companion album *Damnation* (2002)
- *Damnation* is acoustic/prog with minimal drums; *Deliverance* is Opeth's heaviest and most death-metal album
- Pearl Masters Premium with 20" kick — slightly smaller and punchier than the *Blackwater Park* configuration
- Paiste 2002 cymbals — brighter and more cutting than the Sabian cymbals on *Blackwater Park*
- Lopez's "drumming as architecture" philosophy most clearly demonstrated on this album
- Title track is 13 minutes; "A Fair Judgement" exceeds 14 minutes
- No triggers used — Lopez's technique was consistent enough for natural dynamics at all tempos
- Estimated kit value: $3,000-4,500 (2002) / $3,500-5,500 (comparable setup today)

## FAQ

**What drums did Martin Lopez use on Deliverance?**
Martin Lopez played a Pearl Masters Premium kit on Opeth's *Deliverance* (2002), configured with a 20" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. This was a shift from the Sonor Designer Series used on *Blackwater Park* — the Pearl offered a punchier, more aggressive attack suited to the album's brutal production. See the [Blackwater Park drum setup article](/articles/blackwater-park-drum-setup) for full comparison.

**Is Deliverance heavier than Blackwater Park?**
Yes. *Deliverance* is Opeth's heaviest and most death-metal-driven album. Where *Blackwater Park* alternated between brutal and acoustic passages, *Deliverance* leans heavily toward the extreme end. "Wreath" represents the most sustained death metal intensity Opeth ever recorded. The production is also tighter — Wilson dialed back the room ambience of *Blackwater Park* in favor of a more direct, punchy drum sound. Lopez's drumming reflects this: more blast beats, faster double-bass patterns, less space between heavy sections.

**What's the difference between Deliverance and Damnation?**
*Deliverance* and *Damnation* (both 2002) were recorded simultaneously as companion albums representing opposite poles of Opeth's identity. *Deliverance* is the band's heaviest, most death-metal-oriented record. *Damnation* is almost entirely acoustic and prog-rock, with minimal percussion. Martin Lopez barely appears on *Damnation*; on *Deliverance*, his drumming is the structural center. Both albums were released on the same day as a double-album statement about the full range of Opeth's musical identity.

**What cymbals did Martin Lopez use on Deliverance?**
Lopez used Paiste 2002 series cymbals — 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 16" and 18" Crashes, a 20" Medium Ride, and an 18" China. The Paiste 2002's brighter character suited *Deliverance*'s aggressive production: the hi-hats articulate above dense guitar distortion, and the crashes speak quickly enough to mark rapid section changes. See the [Martin Lopez drummer profile](/articles/martin-lopez-drum-setup) for a full career cymbal breakdown.

**What is Lopez's 'drumming as architecture' philosophy?**
Lopez treats drums as architecture — building a structure the music inhabits, rather than merely accompanying what other instruments play. On *Deliverance*, this philosophy is most clearly demonstrated across the album's longest tracks. Pattern choices and fills serve as structural markers, orienting the listener through each section change. This architectural thinking is what separates Lopez from drummers who just keep time, and it's why the 13-minute title track works as an epic rather than an endurance test.
