## Overview

*Diamond Eyes* arrived in May 2010 under the most difficult circumstances in Deftones' history. In November 2008, bassist Chi Cheng was critically injured in a car accident, leaving him in a coma. The band had nearly completed a different album — later released as *Eros* — but chose to shelve it and start fresh, channeling their grief, uncertainty, and hope into what would become one of their most focused and emotionally charged records.

Recorded at Studio 606 in Northridge, California — Dave Grohl's legendary facility — with producer Nick Raskulinecz (Foo Fighters, Alice in Chains, Rush), *Diamond Eyes* marked a decisive creative pivot. Where *Saturday Night Wrist* (2006) had been fractured and difficult to complete, *Diamond Eyes* emerged with remarkable clarity and cohesion. The band played to each other and to Chi Cheng, dedicating the album to their stricken bandmate.

At the center of the album's sonic identity was Abe Cunningham's drumming. By 2010, Abe had evolved from the restrained, atmospheric approach of *White Pony* into a player capable of broader dynamic swings and more assertive groove work. *Diamond Eyes* demanded exactly that — songs like "Rocket Skates" and "CMND/CTRL" needed drums that could anchor mid-tempo aggression, while tracks like "Sextape" and "Prince" required the same whispered intensity Abe had perfected a decade earlier.

Nick Raskulinecz's production drew out a more upfront, punchy drum sound than Terry Date's work on *White Pony*. The snare sits higher in the mix, the kick drum has more defined attack, and the overall drum picture is brighter and more immediate. Yet Abe's essential quality — serving the song over self-expression — remained unchanged.

"Rocket Skates" became the album's commercial breakthrough, charting on Billboard's Modern Rock Tracks and introducing Deftones to a new generation. Its driving, mid-tempo drum pattern exemplifies Abe's 2010 approach: metronomic control, precise dynamics, and a pocket so deep the groove feels inevitable.

The album earned a Grammy nomination for Best Hard Rock Performance ("Rocket Skates") and was named one of 2010's best albums by critics worldwide. It demonstrated that Deftones didn't just survive adversity — they were galvanized by it. And Abe Cunningham's drumming was the bedrock of their resurgence.

### Drum Kit: Tama Starclassic Maple

For the *Diamond Eyes* sessions at Studio 606, Abe continued with his long-standing Tama Starclassic Maple setup. The all-maple shell construction delivered the warmth and body needed for the album's dynamic range — the same fundamental configuration he had used since the *White Pony* era, now dialed in with over a decade of refinement.

Configuration:
- 22" x 18" Bass Drum
- 10" x 8" Rack Tom
- 12" x 9" Rack Tom
- 14" x 14" Floor Tom
- 16" x 16" Floor Tom

Studio 606's live room acoustics contributed significantly to the drum sound. Raskulinecz blended close microphones with room mics, achieving a result that feels both intimate and powerful — punchier and more forward in the mix than Terry Date's approach on *White Pony*.

### Snare: Punchy and Present

The snare sound on *Diamond Eyes* is notably more present than on *White Pony*. Abe used a 14" snare tuned to a medium-high pitch that cuts through the dense guitar arrangements without sounding brittle. On aggressive tracks like "Rocket Skates" and "CMND/CTRL," the snare delivers a satisfying crack with controlled sustain. On atmospheric tracks like "Sextape," Abe pulls back his hitting dynamics to match the song's delicate texture.

Raskulinecz's mic placement captured the full frequency range — from the stick attack transient through the body tone to the snare wire resonance — producing a snare that sounds three-dimensional.
