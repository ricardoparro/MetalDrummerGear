# Digimortal Drum Setup: Raymond Herrera's 2001 Fear Factory Kit Breakdown

> Complete breakdown of Raymond Herrera's drum gear on Fear Factory's Digimortal (2001). The final Herrera studio album — Pearl Reference Series triggered kit, heavier double-kick approach, and how 'Linchpin' and 'What Will Become?' were recorded.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Raymond Herrera](/llms/drummers/raymond-herrera.md)
**Band / Album:** Fear Factory — *Digimortal* (2001)
**Genre:** Industrial Metal / Nu-Metal

## Overview

Released on May 22, 2001, Fear Factory's *Digimortal* is the final studio album Raymond Herrera recorded with the band before his departure in 2002 — and one of the more controversial entries in the Fear Factory catalog. Produced again by Rhys Fulber, *Digimortal* pushed the hybrid industrial metal formula of *Demanufacture* (1995) and *Obsolete* (1998) into new territory: shorter song structures, nu-metal-influenced passages, electronic programming woven more explicitly into the arrangements, and a collaboration with rapper B-Real of Cypress Hill on "Back the Fuck Up."

For drummers, *Digimortal* is a study in how a world-class industrial metal kit sounds when the music around it changes. Herrera's Pearl Reference Series setup with full ddrum/Roland triggering remained consistent with his previous Fear Factory work, but the drum performances were shaped by the album's more varied compositional landscape: tighter, shorter patterns in the nu-metal influenced tracks; heavier double-kick density in the more aggressive passages like "Linchpin" and "What Will Become?"; more integration with programmed electronic elements throughout.

Rhys Fulber, returning as producer, continued the direction he had established on *Obsolete* — treating the triggered drum signals as electronic production material to be shaped and integrated with the album's programmed layers. On *Digimortal*, this approach went further than on *Obsolete*, with the boundary between Herrera's performed drum parts and the electronic programming deliberately blurred in certain tracks.

This article breaks down Herrera's complete drum setup for *Digimortal*, examines how the kit was deployed across the album's varied material, and explains why this final album in the classic Herrera era remains significant for understanding the full arc of Fear Factory's drum sound.

- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension — fast mechanical response consistent with the Demanufacture through Digimortal production approach

### Pearl Reference Series: The Foundation of Herrera's Final Fear Factory Record

The Pearl Reference Series kit Herrera used on *Digimortal* was the same core setup that had defined Fear Factory's drum sound since *Demanufacture* — a continuity that demonstrates how completely this kit had become the foundation of the band's sonic identity. Pearl Reference Series shells, maple construction, consistent trigger response: the acoustic core of a hybrid system that producer Rhys Fulber extended further into electronic integration on *Digimortal* than on any previous Fear Factory record.

The double kick configuration drove the album's most aggressive passages. "Linchpin" and "What Will Become?" feature double-kick patterns in the Herrera tradition — machine-gun grids executing at industrial metal's demanding tempos, the Pearl Eliminator pedal driving both kick drums with consistent action across both beaters, each stroke firing its ddrum trigger. The triggered kick samples on *Digimortal* carry more electronic processing than on *Obsolete*, further blurring the line between acoustic performance and programmed sound.

On *Digimortal*'s nu-metal influenced tracks — shorter compositions with more direct, less complex rhythmic structures — the Pearl Reference kit still provided its characteristic acoustic body. The triggered toms delivered emphatic, sample-enhanced attacks on fills and accents. The kit's tonal consistency across all drums was essential given Fulber's production approach, which required predictable trigger signals from every drum in the setup.

The *Digimortal* sessions were among the last Herrera would record with Fear Factory. His departure in 2002, along with guitarist Christian Olde Wolbers, effectively ended the classic Fear Factory lineup that had defined the band's three-album peak from *Demanufacture* through *Obsolete* and *Digimortal*.

### Snare: Tight Mechanical Authority on Herrera's Departure Album

Herrera's Pearl Custom 14" x 6.5" steel-shell snare with trigger remained the snare of record on *Digimortal*, its fast decay and cutting authority essential for cutting through *Digimortal*'s dense, electronically layered arrangements.

On *Digimortal*, the snare performance was shaped by the album's more varied compositional landscape. In the heavier, more straightforwardly industrial tracks like "Linchpin" and "What Will Become?," the snare delivered the mechanical precision characteristic of the full Fear Factory catalog — tight, controlled backbeats with the triggered sample reinforcing each hit. In the more nu-metal influenced passages, the snare's role shifted: shorter phrases, more direct rhythmic statements, but the same fundamental setup delivering the triggered crack that Fear Factory audiences expected.

Rhys Fulber's production treated the triggered snare signal on *Digimortal* similarly to his approach on *Obsolete* — the electronic sample weighted heavily in the mix, creating a snare sound that is more processed than acoustic but still grounded in Herrera's physical performance. For the tracks that pushed toward more explicit electronic integration — where programmed drum loops appeared alongside or beneath Herrera's performed parts — the snare's triggered signal needed to sit consistently with the programmed material, which required the stable trigger response the Pearl Custom steel shell reliably provided.

### Zildjian Z Custom: Industrial Metal Durability Through the Final Herrera Era

The Zildjian Z Custom cymbal setup was unchanged for *Digimortal* — the same series Herrera had deployed on *Demanufacture* and *Obsolete*, chosen for the same fundamental reasons: upper-frequency brightness to project above Fear Factory's dense guitar and electronic mix, and physical durability for sustained touring.

On *Digimortal*, the cymbals operated in a denser production context than on previous Fear Factory records — Rhys Fulber's more extensive integration of programmed electronic elements created a mix with more competing frequency layers. The Z Custom's brightness, which had always distinguished cymbal frequencies from the guitar mass on *Demanufacture* and *Obsolete*, was even more important on *Digimortal* given the additional electronic programming competing for frequency space.

The china cymbal, prominent on *Obsolete*, continued to serve as the primary aggressive accent marker on *Digimortal*'s most intense tracks. The hi-hats, Z Custom 14" models, drove the pulse on tracks like "Linchpin" and "What Will Become?" — the bright, cutting Z Custom response maintaining clarity at industrial metal's demanding tempos even within *Digimortal*'s more programmed sonic environment.

Fulber's production on *Digimortal* captured the Z Custom cymbals with definition appropriate to their function — articulating Herrera's rhythmic patterns above the programmed electronic layers rather than being absorbed into the mix. This was particularly important on the more experimental tracks, where the acoustic drum performance needed to maintain its identity alongside the programmed elements.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Raymond Herrera use on Fear Factory's Digimortal?**

A: Raymond Herrera used a Pearl Reference Series drum kit on Fear Factory's *Digimortal* (2001), consistent with the setup he had used on *Demanufacture* (1995) and *Obsolete* (1998): two 22" kick drums in a double bass configuration, rack toms at 10" and 12", and floor toms at 16" and 18". All drums were fitted with ddrum and Roland triggers — the electronic layer central to Fear Factory's hybrid drum sound. Producer Rhys Fulber integrated the triggered signals more extensively with programmed electronic elements on *Digimortal* than on previous Fear Factory records, creating the most electronically processed drum sound of the Herrera era. Herrera also used his Pearl Custom 14" x 6.5" steel-shell snare with trigger, Zildjian Z Custom cymbals, and Pearl Eliminator double bass pedals. For the full context: [Raymond Herrera at MetalForge](/drummer/raymond-herrera).

**Q: How does Digimortal's drum sound compare to Demanufacture and Obsolete?**

A: All three albums — *Demanufacture* (1995), *Obsolete* (1998), and *Digimortal* (2001) — used the same core kit: Pearl Reference Series with full ddrum/Roland triggering. The differences are entirely production-based. *Demanufacture* (produced by Colin Richardson, after initial Ross Robinson sessions) has the rawest, most physically driven drum sound — the triggered signals reinforcing rather than dominating. *Obsolete* (produced by Rhys Fulber and Bill Gould) is more electronically polished — Fulber's electronic music background shaping the triggered signals into a cleaner, more processed sound. *Digimortal* (produced by Rhys Fulber) goes furthest: triggered signals integrated with programmed electronic drum elements, the boundary between acoustic performance and programmed production deliberately blurred. The progression across three albums is from raw hybrid to polished hybrid to explicitly programmed integration — all with the same acoustic core driving the system. See [Obsolete drum setup](/articles/obsolete-drum-setup) for the intermediate step.

**Q: Why was Digimortal controversial and how did that affect the drum production?**

A: *Digimortal* was controversial within the Fear Factory fanbase primarily for its incorporation of nu-metal influences — shorter song lengths, simpler structures in some tracks, and the collaboration with rapper B-Real of Cypress Hill on "Back the Fuck Up." For Herrera's drumming, this meant adapting his industrial metal hybrid approach to more varied compositional demands. The heavier tracks like "Linchpin" and "What Will Become?" kept the machine-gun double-kick patterns and complex rhythmic structures that defined *Demanufacture* and *Obsolete*. The nu-metal influenced tracks required tighter, more direct patterns within shorter forms. The drum production under Rhys Fulber integrated programmed electronic elements more explicitly than on previous records, which some listeners found at odds with the acoustic power of the hybrid kit on earlier albums. *Digimortal* remains significant as the end point of the classic Herrera era — his departure in 2002 closed the chapter on the three-album peak that this album completed.

**Q: What are the key drum tracks on Digimortal?**

A: "Linchpin" is the standout Herrera performance on *Digimortal* — a driving industrial metal track featuring the machine-gun double-kick patterns and snare precision that defined the Fear Factory sound at its most aggressive. "What Will Become?" opens the album with double-kick intensity and establishes the more electronically integrated drum sound of the record. "Invisible Wounds (Dark Bodies)" is a mid-tempo track that showcases Herrera's groove sensibility within Fear Factory's industrial context — controlled, precise, the hybrid kit operating at below-maximum intensity. "No One" demonstrates the more straightforward structures of *Digimortal*'s nu-metal influenced material, with Herrera adapting his industrial precision to a shorter compositional form. For the Fear Factory arc across all three Herrera studio albums: [Raymond Herrera at MetalForge](/drummer/raymond-herrera), [Obsolete drum setup](/articles/obsolete-drum-setup).

**Q: What happened to Raymond Herrera after Digimortal?**

A: Raymond Herrera left Fear Factory in 2002, along with guitarist Christian Olde Wolbers. The departure effectively ended the classic Fear Factory lineup that had recorded *Demanufacture*, *Obsolete*, and *Digimortal*. Herrera and Olde Wolbers subsequently formed Arkaea, releasing one album (*Years in the Darkness*, 2009). Herrera continued drumming in various projects, applying his hybrid industrial metal technique — Pearl Reference Series drums, trigger integration, Pearl Eliminator double pedal — in contexts outside Fear Factory's thematic framework. Fear Factory continued and eventually reformed with different lineups, but the three-album Herrera era from 1995 to 2001 remains the definitive period of the band's drum sound.
