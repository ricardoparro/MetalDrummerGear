## Overview

Released on August 10, 2004 via Relapse Records, Necrophagist's "Epitaph" stands as one of the foundational technical death metal albums — a record that established the modern tech-death template and launched Hannes Grossmann's career as one of extreme metal's most respected drummers. Grossmann's performance on the album, particularly on tracks like "Stabwound," "Ignominious & Pale," and "Seven," became reference material for the entire next generation of technical death metal drumming.

The album was a long-awaited follow-up to Necrophagist's 1999 debut "Onset of Putrefaction" (which had been recorded almost entirely by founder Muhammed Suiçmez). Epitaph was the first Necrophagist record with a proper full-band lineup, and Grossmann's arrival as the drummer transformed the band's sonic possibilities. Where Onset of Putrefaction had used programmed drumming with intricate but mechanical results, Epitaph captured a human drummer at the absolute peak of technical capability — and the difference was decisive for the album's impact on the genre.

Hannes Grossmann's approach to Epitaph combined the dense vocabulary required by Suiçmez's neoclassical guitar compositions with the sustained extreme metal velocity that the songs demanded. Every track features sustained double-bass at sixteenth-note tempos, blast beat passages aligned to complex guitar phrasing, and polyrhythmic fills that move between snare, toms, and bass drums with surgical precision. The album's most famous track, "Stabwound," opens with a drum vocabulary so dense it became a benchmark for technical death metal drumming — every fill on the track is now studied by extreme metal drummers as a foundational text.

His setup on the record — Tama Starclassic Performer (or comparable high-end production shells), Meinl cymbals (including early Pure Alloy and developing Byzance configurations), high-speed double pedals, and Vic Firth sticks — was built for the studio precision that Suiçmez's production demanded. Necrophagist produced the album themselves, and the engineering approach prioritized articulation above all else: every individual stroke needed to register as a discrete event, even at the album's most intense passages.

For drummers studying extreme metal, Epitaph remains essential listening more than two decades after its release. The album is one of the most documented Necrophagist records — partially because it was the band's most successful release, and partially because of the magnitude of Grossmann's contribution. This article breaks down the gear behind that performance.

### Hannes's Studio Kit: Tama Starclassic Era Setup

Hannes Grossmann's drum kit on "Epitaph" represented the high-end production shell pack required for studio-grade technical death metal recording in 2004 — a Tama Starclassic Performer or comparable Pearl Reference shell pack chosen for the combination of attack and articulation that Necrophagist's material demanded. Epitaph asks the kit to perform an unusual concentration of high-velocity work: sustained double-bass passages, blast beat sections, and polyrhythmic fill vocabulary all occurring within single tracks at tempos where every stroke must register cleanly.

The double 22" x 18" bass drum configuration is fundamental to the album's rhythmic identity. Tracks like "Stabwound," "Ignominious & Pale," and "Seven" sustain 16th-note double-bass at velocities where each individual stroke needs to occupy its own clear sonic territory. The 22" diameter delivers the low-frequency body the music requires, while the 18" depth provides the punchy attack character that Suiçmez's self-production prioritized.

Grossmann's tom configuration is compact and efficient: 10" and 12" rack toms paired with 14" and 16" floor toms. This four-tom spread covers the pitch range his fill vocabulary requires while remaining tight enough for the rapid articulation Necrophagist's music demands. Tom fills on Epitaph are dense — often involving cross-tom rolls and polyrhythmic patterns at high velocity — and the compact spread keeps these phrases physically reachable without compromising speed.

The shell construction matters significantly in Epitaph's production context. Necrophagist's self-production approach captured the acoustic kit's natural voice with relatively traditional studio techniques, and the high-end shell pack delivered the articulation and projection required to make that approach succeed.

### The Snare on "Stabwound" and Beyond

Hannes Grossmann's snare drum on Epitaph needed to perform across the full vocabulary of high-velocity tech-death drumming: rapid ghost-note patterns, polyrhythmic snare placement against bass drum figures, full-velocity rim-shot accents during blast sections, and sustained groove punctuation through Necrophagist's most complex guitar arrangements. A 14" x 6.5" professional snare drum provided the dimensional balance for this range — enough depth for tonal body, tight enough at the heads for rapid articulation.

The tuning approach on Epitaph emphasized clarity through Necrophagist's dense guitar layering. Muhammed Suiçmez's seven-string and six-string compositions create thick midrange environments, and the snare must cut through without losing tonal identity. Grossmann's snare voice on the album balances brightness and body, registering with consistent character whether played at ghost-note dynamics or full-velocity rim-shot intensity.

On "Stabwound" specifically — one of the most studied drum tracks in modern extreme metal — the snare appears in nearly every conceivable rhythmic position: backbeats, offbeats, polyrhythmic accents, ghost-note flurries, and blast beat strokes. Each of these positions has consistent tonal character across the track, evidence of disciplined tuning and the snare drum's capacity to perform across that dynamic range.

For drummers studying the album, Grossmann's snare playing on Epitaph is one of its most instructive elements — proof that tech-death snare work benefits from professional drums tuned for musical clarity rather than maximum aggression alone.

### Meinl Cymbals: The Developing Signature Sound

Hannes Grossmann's Meinl cymbal setup on Epitaph captured an earlier stage of the signature voice that would fully mature on Obscura's Cosmogenesis five years later. Grossmann is a long-standing Meinl artist, and Epitaph documents the cymbal configuration he was using as his Meinl partnership was developing toward its mature form. The setup combined early Pure Alloy elements with developing Byzance configurations — a transitional period in his cymbal voice that produced the distinctive Epitaph cymbal palette.

The 14" Medium hi-hats anchor the album's rhythmic pulse. At Necrophagist's tempos — frequently approaching or exceeding 220 BPM — the hi-hats must articulate rapidly without losing tonal character. Grossmann's choice of medium-weight Meinl hi-hats produces the cutting attack and articulation his playing demands while remaining musical enough to blend with the dense guitar arrangements.

The 17" and 19" Medium crashes provide the accent palette across the album. Tech-death music is accent-dense — crashes occur at structural transitions, riff changes, and rhythmic emphasis points throughout every track — and the two-crash configuration gives Grossmann tonal contrast for these accents. The 17" delivers fast, focused crashes for rapid transitions; the 19" provides fuller, more sustained crashes for larger structural moments.

The 22" Medium ride sees significant use across Epitaph in the album's more groove-oriented passages. While Epitaph is primarily a blast-and-double-bass album, sections of "Ignominious & Pale" and "Seven" feature ride work that creates textural contrast against the more aggressive sections.

The 18" China appears at the album's most aggressive intensity peaks. Its trashy, explosive character provides the aggressive accent voice that tech-death's climactic moments demand.

**Q: What drum kit did Hannes Grossmann use on Necrophagist's Epitaph?**

The configuration included two 22" x 18" bass drums for sustained double-kick work, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. **Q: Who was the drummer on Necrophagist's Epitaph in 2004?**

A: Hannes Grossmann was the drummer on Necrophagist's Epitaph (2004), and the album was his debut on a Necrophagist studio recording. Necrophagist's previous album, Onset of Putrefaction (1999), had been recorded almost entirely by founder Muhammed Suiçmez using programmed drumming. Epitaph was the first Necrophagist record with a proper full-band lineup, and Grossmann's arrival as the drummer transformed the band's sonic possibilities. His performance on tracks like "Stabwound," "Ignominious & Pale," and "Seven" became reference material for modern technical death metal drumming. Grossmann went on to become one of the most respected drummers in extreme metal, later co-founding Obscura (2007) and Alkaloid (2015).

**Q: What cymbals did Hannes Grossmann use on Epitaph?**

A: Hannes Grossmann used a Meinl cymbal setup on Epitaph that combined early Pure Alloy elements with developing Byzance configurations — a transitional period in his cymbal voice. The setup included 14" Medium hi-hats, 17" and 19" Medium crashes, a 22" Medium ride, and an 18" China. Grossmann was a long-standing Meinl artist throughout his career, and Epitaph documents the cymbal configuration he was using as his Meinl partnership was developing toward its mature Byzance form (which would fully crystallize on Obscura's Cosmogenesis in 2009). The cymbal voice on Epitaph remains a key part of the album's distinctive sonic identity.

**Q: How does Epitaph compare to Onset of Putrefaction in terms of drumming?**

A: Onset of Putrefaction (1999), Necrophagist's debut, used programmed drumming created by founder Muhammed Suiçmez — the songs were performed with mechanical precision but without a human drummer in the conventional sense. Epitaph (2004) transformed this completely by introducing Hannes Grossmann as the band's first proper studio drummer. The shift from programmed to human-played drumming gave Epitaph a dynamic and rhythmically alive quality that Onset of Putrefaction's programmed drums could not match. Grossmann's performance on tracks like "Stabwound" demonstrated the kind of musical nuance, micro-timing variation, and physical force that human drumming brings — and the contrast between the two albums illustrates why human drumming remains central to extreme metal's emotional impact even when the technical demands are extreme.

**Q: Why is Stabwound considered one of the most important tech death drum tracks?**

A: Stabwound is widely considered one of the most important tech death drum tracks because it concentrates an unusually high density of technical vocabulary into a single song while remaining musically composed and emotionally direct. Grossmann's performance includes sustained 16th-note double-bass under neoclassical guitar leads, blast beat sections aligned to Suiçmez's phrasing, polyrhythmic fill vocabulary moving between snare, toms, and bass drums at high velocity, and dynamic transitions between full-intensity and more atmospheric passages — all within roughly four-and-a-half minutes. The track became required listening for serious tech death drummers, and many subsequent extreme metal drummers cite it as a foundational reference.
