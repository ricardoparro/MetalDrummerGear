## Overview

When Necrophagist released "Epitaph" on August 4, 2004, through Relapse Records, the technical death metal underground recognized immediately that the bar had been raised. Behind the kit was Hannes Grossmann — a 21-year-old classically trained drummer from Bavaria whose recording debut on the album would define his international reputation. Before Obscura, before Alkaloid, before his career as a composer and producer, Grossmann's introduction to the global extreme metal audience was this: eight tracks of relentlessly precise, compositionally ambitious technical death metal drumming under maximum technical pressure.

"Epitaph" was recorded at Muhammed Suiçmez's home studio in Karlsruhe, Germany, with Suiçmez himself handling production and engineering. Suiçmez — Necrophagist's vocalist, guitarist, and primary songwriter — had spent six years preparing the material that would become Epitaph; the album represented years of compositional refinement compressed into a single 32-minute statement.

The album's historical significance is the standard it set for tech-death drumming. Tracks like "Stabwound," "Diminished to B," and "Symbiotic in Theory" are still cited as benchmark performances for the genre. The vocabulary Grossmann established here — heel-up double kick precision at extreme tempos, integrated blast beat passages, ghost-note snare density beneath full-intensity hi-hat work — became the template that he himself would develop further on Obscura's [cosmogenesis-drum-setup](https://metalforge.io/articles/cosmogenesis-drum-setup) (2009) and Omnivium (2011).

### Sonor Kit: German Precision

The German precision drum maker Sonor delivered tight engineering tolerances that suited the tuning stability Epitaph's extended studio sessions required. The double 22" x 18" kick drum configuration was essential — even at this early career stage, Grossmann was committed to independent double kick rather than a double pedal on a single drum. The natural feel and independent acoustic response of two complete bass drum instruments was critical at Suiçmez's close-mic capture distance.

### Sonor Maple Snare

The Sonor maple 14" x 5.5" snare delivered the bright, fast-responding crack needed to cut through Muhammed Suiçmez's quadruple-tracked guitar layering. The 5.5" depth produced a balanced attack-to-body ratio: enough sustain to project, fast enough to handle the rapid snare patterns on "Stabwound" and "The Stillborn One" without smearing. Even at 21, Grossmann's developing ghost note vocabulary registered audibly in Suiçmez's production.

### Meinl Cymbals

In 2004, Meinl was rapidly becoming the cymbal manufacturer of choice for German extreme metal, with their hand-hammered Byzance line emerging as a credible alternative to Zildjian and Sabian for technical players. The 14" hi-hats anchored Grossmann's rhythmic pulse across Epitaph's tempo range — from the mid-paced groove sections of "Symbiotic in Theory" through the full-velocity blast and double-kick passages of "Stabwound."

### Tama Iron Cobra Double Pedal

The Iron Cobra's cam-driven mechanism provided the response speed Necrophagist's material demanded. Tracks like "Stabwound" and "Diminished to B" execute sustained double-kick at tempos where pedal consistency is critical: any mechanical inconsistency would expose itself immediately in Suiçmez's close-mic production.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is the drummer on Necrophagist's Epitaph?**
A: Hannes Grossmann is the drummer on Necrophagist's Epitaph (2004). He was 21 when the album was recorded; Epitaph was his recording debut on a major technical death metal release. He would go on to co-found Obscura and Alkaloid. Romain Goulon joined Necrophagist later as a post-Epitaph drummer; Hannes Grossmann played on the actual studio recording of Epitaph itself.

**Q: What drum kit did Hannes Grossmann use on Epitaph?**
A: A Sonor kit with maple shells — the German precision drum maker whose engineering tolerances suited the tuning stability Necrophagist's material required. His configuration ran two 22" x 18" bass drums for independent double-kick, two rack toms (10" and 12"), and two floor toms (14" and 16"). His snare was a Sonor maple 14" x 5.5".

**Q: Who produced Necrophagist's Epitaph?**
A: Muhammed Suiçmez — Necrophagist's vocalist, guitarist, and primary songwriter — produced and engineered Epitaph at his home studio in Karlsruhe, Germany. The studio is sometimes referred to as Studio Suiçmez in album credits.

**Q: What technique made Grossmann's Epitaph performance significant?**
A: Compositional awareness brought to technical death metal drumming. Where many tech-death drummers in 2004 used blast beats and double-kick as default settings, Grossmann deployed them as compositional events — arriving at structural moments for impact, preceded and followed by groove-oriented contrast.
