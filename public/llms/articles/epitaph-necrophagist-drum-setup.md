## Overview

Released on November 22, 2004 through Relapse Records, Necrophagist's *Epitaph* occupies a singular position in extreme metal history: the record that set the technical ceiling for death metal drumming. For Hannes Grossmann — who had joined the Stuttgart-based band in 2001 at 19 years old — *Epitaph* was an international debut that immediately established him as one of the most exceptional drummers in technical death metal.

The album was produced by guitarist and primary composer Muhammed Suiçmez at a home studio in Karlsruhe, Germany. This intimate recording context demanded absolute precision: no audience mix, no room ambience to smooth over imprecision, every note captured at close distance.

Grossmann's playing on *Epitaph* is architecturally sophisticated beyond pure technical display. On tracks like "Stabwound," "Fermented Offal Discharge," and the title track, his drum parts function as counterpoint to Suiçmez's guitar lines — compositional voices in a genuinely contrapuntal arrangement. Classical percussion training informs every phrase: ghost notes borrowed from jazz drumming add dynamic depth, and his open-handed technique enables voicings that crossed-hands technique cannot access.

### Tama Starclassic Performer: Precision Built for Technical Death

Grossmann used a Tama Starclassic Performer — Tama's flagship production shell line of the era, built with all-maple shells and birch reinforcement rings. The Starclassic Performer's maple construction provides midrange focus and fast attack transients that technical death metal demands.

His configuration: two 22" x 16" bass drums (tighter depth for focused punch rather than sub-bass), three rack toms (10", 12", 13"), and a single 16" floor tom. The three-rack-tom spread enables the pitch intervals for melodic fills, while the compact single-floor-tom layout keeps the setup fast — economy of motion between kit elements directly affects pattern speed at 200+ BPM.

### Meinl Pure Alloy: Clarity Through Dense Arrangements

The Meinl Pure Alloy cymbal series on *Epitaph* prioritized one quality above all: clarity. In polyrhythmically dense arrangements like Necrophagist's, every cymbal accent must speak immediately, decay quickly, and leave space for the next event. Pure Alloy's bright B8 bronze construction delivers this through fast attack and relatively short sustain.

The 14" hi-hats are central to Grossmann's open-handed technique — right hand on hi-hat, left on snare — which enables patterns that crossed-hands technique cannot physically replicate. The Pure Alloy's consistent surface response supports both sticks identically.

### The Technical Steel Snare

The snare sound on *Epitaph* — bright, cracking, audible in the densest passages — comes from a Tama steel snare at 14" x 5.5". Steel's inherent brightness gives the drum a cutting fundamental that remains distinct within Suiçmez's stacked guitar frequencies. The 5.5" depth balances crack and body: projecting on heavy backbeats, sensitive enough to register near-silent ghost notes between primary accents.

## FAQ

**What drum kit did Hannes Grossmann use on Necrophagist Epitaph?**
Hannes Grossmann recorded Necrophagist's Epitaph (2004) with a Tama Starclassic Performer kit featuring two 22" x 16" bass drums, three rack toms (10", 12", 13"), and a 16" floor tom. Cymbals were Meinl Pure Alloy series. Snare was a Tama steel model at 14" x 5.5" tuned medium-high. He used Tama Iron Cobra Power Glide single pedals (independent) on each bass drum.

**Who drummed on Necrophagist Epitaph?**
Hannes Grossmann drummed on Necrophagist's Epitaph (2004). He joined the band in 2001 at 19 and recorded Epitaph over the following years. His performance — technically extreme, classically informed, with ghost notes and open-handed technique — immediately established him as one of technical death metal's most exceptional drummers. He left Necrophagist in 2006 to co-found Obscura.

**How difficult is the drumming on Necrophagist Epitaph?**
The drumming on Epitaph is widely considered among the most technically demanding in metal history. Tempos reach 220+ BPM with polyrhythmic passages where hands and feet operate in different meters simultaneously. Ghost notes are integrated between primary blast strokes (borrowed from jazz drumming), and melodic tom fills function as compositional counterpoint to the guitar lines rather than simple rhythmic filler. Grossmann's open-handed technique enables hi-hat voicings that crossed-hands approach cannot physically replicate.

**What cymbals were used on Necrophagist Epitaph?**
The cymbals on Epitaph (2004) were from the Meinl Pure Alloy series — Meinl's bright, raw-sounding B8 bronze line with fast attack and quick decay. Grossmann's setup included 14" Pure Alloy hi-hats, 16" and 18" crash cymbals, a 20" ride, and an 18" China. The series' brightness and fast decay keep every accent audible and distinct within Necrophagist's dense polyrhythmic arrangements.
