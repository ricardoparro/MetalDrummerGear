# Porcupine Tree Fear of a Blank Planet Drum Setup: Gavin Harrison's 2007 Gear

**Genre:** Progressive Rock / Progressive Metal

## Overview

Released on April 16, 2007, *Fear of a Blank Planet* represents Gavin Harrison at the absolute height of his technical and musical powers. The album — Porcupine Tree's seventh studio record — was recorded at Britannia Row Studios in London with Steven Wilson producing, and it stands today as one of the most comprehensive documents of what a modern progressive metal drummer can achieve. Harrison's performance across the album's six tracks is not simply technically accomplished; it redefines what the drum kit is capable of within a rock context.

The album's six long-form compositions — "Fear of a Blank Planet," "My Ashes," "Anesthetize," "Sentimental," "Way Out of Here," and "Sleep Together" — place Harrison at the centre of Steven Wilson's most compositionally ambitious writing. "Anesthetize," at over seventeen minutes, is a singular progressive statement: the drumming cycles through multiple metric layers, shifts time signatures without announcement, and never once loses the groove that keeps the song moving. It has become required study in conservatoire-level drumming programmes.

What separates *Fear of a Blank Planet* from Harrison's earlier Porcupine Tree work is the degree to which polyrhythmic complexity is integrated with emotional directness. *In Absentia* (2002) and *Deadwing* (2005) introduced his metric modulation and ghost note approach to a wider audience. *Fear of a Blank Planet* is the complete synthesis: extreme rhythmic sophistication deployed with the clarity and impact of great rock music.

The gear Harrison brought to Britannia Row Studios was the same rig he had been refining since joining Porcupine Tree — a custom Sonor SQ2 shell pack, Zildjian K Custom Special Dry cymbals co-developed with Zildjian, Sonor Gavin Harrison Signature snares, and the Sonor Perfect Balance pedal. Each component was selected for one core quality: responsiveness to nuance. Steven Wilson's production philosophy demanded that every dynamic shade be captured with forensic clarity, and Harrison's rig was purpose-built for exactly that environment.

This article documents the complete drum setup Gavin Harrison used to record *Fear of a Blank Planet* in 2007 — the instruments, specifications, and musical reasoning behind every choice — and explains why this combination of gear and technique produced one of progressive metal's landmark recordings.

### The Sonor SQ2: Built for Britannia Row

The Sonor SQ2 that Harrison brought to *Fear of a Blank Planet* is a fully bespoke instrument configured to exact specifications, not a production kit with cosmetic changes. Sonor's SQ2 programme allows each shell to be built from scratch for material, thickness, bearing edge angle, and hardware placement. Harrison's configuration centres on thin maple shells — the same approach he had used throughout his Porcupine Tree tenure and the foundation of his ability to play with extreme dynamic range.

Thin maple shells produce a faster, more responsive drum than thicker alternatives. When Harrison plays a whisper-level ghost note on the snare during "Fear of a Blank Planet," the shell registers the stroke cleanly. When he drives a full-force tom accent during "Anesthetize," the same shell projects without becoming hard or brittle. This dynamic consistency across the entire range of playing weight is the essential characteristic of his SQ2 configuration and a prerequisite for the complexity the album demands.

The 22" bass drum is smaller than what many rock drummers choose, but the choice is deliberate and musical. On *Fear of a Blank Planet*, the kick drum frequently operates as a melodic voice within polyrhythmic patterns — locking with bass guitar lines, functioning as one layer in a three-against-four rhythmic structure, or establishing a micro-groove beneath the main snare and hi-hat pattern. A 24" or 26" bass drum built for maximum power would obscure this function; the 22" allows articulation, precision, and dynamic subtlety.

The four-tom configuration — 10", 12", 14", 16" — provides the melodic range Harrison needs for the orchestrated tom work throughout the album. He tunes his toms in musical intervals rather than simply "high, medium, low," and uses them as sustained melodic voices in fills and transitions. The SQ2's mounting hardware, which preserves full shell resonance by avoiding contact clamps, ensures the tuning holds and the shells ring freely. This is audible across the album: Harrison's tom fills have a pitched, musical quality that distinguishes them from percussive filler.

Steven Wilson's production for *Fear of a Blank Planet* was close-mic, detailed, and transparent — every nuance of Harrison's playing is captured on the recording. The SQ2 shells' sensitivity meant that the studio environment heard exactly what Harrison played, at every dynamic level, without compression or artificial enhancement masking the detail. This pairing of kit and production philosophy is why the album documents his technique so completely.

### The Brass Voice: Sonor Gavin Harrison Signature

The 14" x 5.25" Sonor Gavin Harrison Signature snare is the primary snare voice across all of *Fear of a Blank Planet* and the most recognisable single drum on the recording. Harrison developed the drum in close collaboration with Sonor, arriving at a specification that addresses the specific demands of his playing: a brass shell for warmth and body, .25" additional depth over a standard 5" snare for increased fullness on soft strokes, and die-cast hoops for consistent tension and rim click quality.

The choice of brass as shell material is the defining decision. Brass produces warmth and complexity that steel cannot deliver: the fundamental note is fuller, overtones are richer, and the response to soft strokes is more musical. On *Fear of a Blank Planet*, Harrison uses the snare throughout its entire dynamic range — from barely audible ghost notes embedded in hi-hat patterns to full-force rimshots marking section changes. Brass handles both extremes with equal grace. A steel shell at the same tension would sound thinner on soft strokes and harsher on hard hits.

The 12" Protean snare functions as Harrison's studio textural instrument on the album — a tighter, faster drum designed for the close-mic environment at Britannia Row. Its smaller diameter produces a more focused, immediate attack that sits differently in the mix from the 14", allowing for tonal variety within the same song. The ghost note passages in "Anesthetize" and the complex snare figures in the title track use this differentiation.

Both snares are tuned medium to medium-high — responsive at the lowest dynamic levels, assertive at the highest. Harrison's die-cast hoops produce the rim click sounds used throughout the album's more textural passages, particularly in "My Ashes" and "Sentimental" where the snare operates more as a colour than a backbeat instrument.

### Zildjian K Custom Special Dry: The Co-Creation

The Zildjian K Custom Special Dry series was developed in direct collaboration with Gavin Harrison, and the relationship between these cymbals and the drumming on *Fear of a Blank Planet* is foundational. The design principle — dry sound, fast decay, minimal wash — was not an aesthetic preference but a musical requirement that Harrison identified and communicated to Zildjian during the development process.

On *Fear of a Blank Planet*, Harrison routinely layers three or more independent rhythmic ideas simultaneously. The title track runs in 6/4 with a three-against-four snare figure operating simultaneously with a kick pattern implying a third grouping — three distinct rhythmic layers sounding at once. For this to register as music rather than noise, each layer needs sonic space. Cymbals that sustain too long fill that space and blur the layers together. The Special Dry design — raw, hammered finish with minimal lathing — produces a cymbal that speaks immediately and then releases, leaving the field open for the next event.

The 21" Special Dry Ride is the defining cymbal of *Fear of a Blank Planet* and arguably the defining sound of Harrison's Porcupine Tree work. Its clear, articulate ping on the bow appears throughout the album's ride passages; the complex but controlled wash is used for sustained sections; the cutting bell marks specific metric points in "Anesthetize" and "Way Out of Here." The range this single cymbal covers — from delicate texture at low volume to sustained sound mass when driven hard — is central to the album's dynamic architecture.

The 14" Special Dry Hi-Hats drive the intricate rhythmic conversations Harrison builds throughout the album. Their immediate response to variations in stick pressure and angle allows the subtle open/closed gradations that separate his hi-hat technique from simpler approaches. In "Fear of a Blank Planet" and "Anesthetize," the hi-hat functions as a primary compositional voice — not merely time-keeping, but a melodic and rhythmic element in its own right.

The 16" and 18" Special Dry crashes speak and clear quickly, providing the dramatic section-ending accents the album's long-form compositions require without accumulating wash that would muddy the arrangement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums does Gavin Harrison use on Fear of a Blank Planet?**

A: Gavin Harrison recorded Fear of a Blank Planet (2007) using a custom Sonor SQ2 Series kit with thin maple shells: a 22" x 17" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. His cymbals were the Zildjian K Custom Special Dry series (co-developed with Zildjian), anchored by a 21" Special Dry Ride and 14" Special Dry Hi-Hats, with 16" and 18" Special Dry crashes and an 18" K Custom Trash China. His primary snare was the Sonor Gavin Harrison Signature 14" x 5.25" brass shell drum, tuned medium to medium-high. The Sonor Perfect Balance Pedal controlled bass drum dynamics. All recording took place at Britannia Row Studios, London, with Steven Wilson producing.

**Q: What makes the drumming on Fear of a Blank Planet special?**

A: Fear of a Blank Planet is widely studied for Gavin Harrison's integration of extreme polyrhythmic complexity with genuine musical groove. The album's standout drumming achievement is the title track — which runs in 6/4 with a three-against-four snare figure simultaneously operating against a kick pattern implying a third grouping — three distinct rhythmic layers sounding at once. "Anesthetize" extends this approach across seventeen minutes, cycling through multiple metric layers and time signature shifts without losing momentum. Harrison's gear choices are inseparable from the achievement: his Sonor SQ2's thin maple shells articulate each dynamic level clearly, and the Zildjian K Custom Special Dry cymbals decay quickly enough that complex layered patterns remain individually audible.

**Q: What snare does Gavin Harrison use?**

A: Gavin Harrison's primary snare on Fear of a Blank Planet is the Sonor Gavin Harrison Signature 14" x 5.25" brass-shell snare drum, developed in collaboration with Sonor. The brass shell provides warmth and body on soft ghost notes and ghost note passages, while still projecting powerfully on rimshots. He also uses a 12" Sonor Protean signature snare as a studio textural instrument for tighter, more focused snare sounds in close-mic contexts.
