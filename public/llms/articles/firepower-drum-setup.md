# Scott Travis's Drum Setup on Judas Priest's Firepower (2018)

> Complete breakdown of Scott Travis's drum setup on Judas Priest's Firepower (2018) — the Grammy-nominated, UK Gold-certified album that paired Andy Sneap's modern production with classic-Priest producer Tom Allom's return for the first time since 1988's Ram It Down. Scott Travis's Tama Starclassic Maple kit, Sabian HHX cymbals, and Speed Cobra pedals on the strongest Priest chart performance since Painkiller.

## Overview

Released on March 9, 2018 via Epic Records, Judas Priest's eighteenth studio album *Firepower* delivered the band's strongest commercial performance since 1990's *Painkiller*. It hit #5 on both the UK and US album charts, earned UK Gold certification, and saw the single "Lightning Strike" nominated for a 2019 Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance. For Scott Travis — the American drummer who joined Priest in 1989 and powered *Painkiller* the following year — *Firepower* marked his thirty-year anniversary milestone with the band and his first studio Priest album since 2008's *Nostradamus*.

The album's production team was a deliberate, unusual pairing. Andy Sneap — producer to the modern metal generation (Accept, Exodus, Testament) and a guitarist himself — handled the contemporary production at his own Old Mill Recording Studios. Alongside him was Tom Allom, the producer behind every classic Priest studio album from *British Steel* (1980) through *Ram It Down* (1988). Allom's return marked his first Priest production credit in thirty years, and the dual-producer chemistry — Sneap's modern clarity matched with Allom's classic-Priest perspective on song dynamics and arrangement — gave *Firepower* a sound that felt both contemporary and unmistakably Priest.

For Scott Travis, the gear arc from *Painkiller* to *Firepower* spans twenty-eight years and a complete kit evolution. In 1990 he played a Tama Artstar II birch kit with Paiste cymbals and prototype Iron Cobra pedals. By 2018 the configuration was Tama Starclassic Maple, Sabian HHX cymbals, and the Tama Speed Cobra HP910 — modern hardware tuned for modern recording precision while preserving the relentless double-bass authority that defined his Priest debut.

The album's release was overshadowed by news that founding guitarist Glenn Tipton had been battling Parkinson's disease, with Andy Sneap stepping in to handle his live duties moving forward. Despite that, *Firepower* arrived as a celebration of the band's longevity and Travis's enduring role as the engine room. Three decades into his tenure, Travis was still delivering the sustained double-bass workouts, the precise snare backbeats, and the controlled-but-explosive feel that made him the right call to replace Dave Holland in 1989.

This article covers every piece of gear Scott Travis used on *Firepower*, how each component evolved from his *Painkiller* setup, and what made the Sneap-Allom production capture his sound with both modern clarity and classic Priest weight.

### The Modern Tama: Starclassic Maple

By 2018, Scott Travis's main kit for Priest sessions was the Tama Starclassic Maple — a long way from the Pearl-era setups he had used in his early Racer X period and the Tama Artstar II birch kit he used on *Painkiller*. Travis had transitioned to Tama as his primary endorsement during the 1990s and stayed with the brand through the modern era, settling on the Starclassic Maple as his go-to platform.

The shift from birch (Artstar II) to maple (Starclassic) is the most significant tonal change between *Painkiller* and *Firepower*. Birch delivers a hard, focused attack with pronounced high-mids — ideal for cutting through the dry, in-your-face guitar production Chris Tsangarides built in 1990. Maple delivers a warmer, fuller body with smoother low-mid resonance — better suited to the broader, layered guitar arrangements Andy Sneap and Tom Allom constructed on *Firepower*. The 6-ply maple shell preserves articulation while giving each drum more harmonic depth.

The bass drum dimensions also evolved. *Painkiller* used 22"x16" kicks tuned tight for maximum machine-gun articulation; *Firepower* uses 22"x18" — two inches deeper, which adds low-end body and sustain without sacrificing attack. For an album with more mid-tempo groove material alongside the speed showcases, the deeper kicks deliver the weight the songs demand. Travis still runs two independent bass drums rather than a double pedal, preserving the foot independence that has defined his playing since the 1980s.

The tom configuration is more compact than the *Painkiller* setup — two rack toms (10", 12") and two floor toms (14", 16") instead of the three-rack-tom arrangement of 1990. Modern Travis has consistently favored a tighter tom array, prioritizing fluency across fewer drums rather than a sprawling palette. The 10/12 rack and 14/16 floor combination matches what he had been touring with through the 2000s and 2010s.

Finished in piano black with chrome hardware, the kit reads as a direct visual descendant of the *Painkiller* Artstar II — same aesthetic, same brand loyalty, three decades of refinement under the surface.

### The Modern Crack: Starphonic Brass

For *Firepower*, Travis paired the Starclassic Maple kit with a Tama Starphonic 14"x6" brass snare — a modern, focused snare that delivers the high-frequency cut he has favored throughout his career while adding the warm low-mid body that brass shells uniquely produce.

The Starphonic line is Tama's flagship single-ply premium snare series, engineered to maximize resonance from the shell rather than rely on heavy double-ply construction. The brass shell at 14"x6" is slightly shallower than the 14"x6.5" Artstar II steel snare he used on *Painkiller* — a half-inch difference that trades a small amount of body for additional response and articulation. For an album with as much sixteenth-note snare work as *Firepower*, that responsiveness matters.

Brass sits between steel and aluminum in tonal character: brighter than aluminum, warmer than steel. The result is a snare that cuts through Andy Sneap's dense, layered guitar production while retaining enough body to feel substantial in the mix. Travis tunes it medium-high with tight snare wires — the same tuning philosophy he has used since *Painkiller*, refined for a more modern target sound.

The die-cast hoops continue from the *Painkiller* era as well, adding to the drum's overall projection and giving Travis the rim-shot clarity he uses for accents and cross-stick work across the album. On "Lightning Strike," the Grammy-nominated single, every backbeat needs to lock against Sneap's tight kick-and-guitar production — the Starphonic Brass delivers that anchor with the authority Priest's heaviest material demands.

### Sabian HHX: The Modern Travis Arsenal

*Firepower* documents Scott Travis's long-running Sabian endorsement — a partnership that replaced the Paiste setup he used on *Painkiller* and which has defined his cymbal voice through the modern era. The HHX (Hand-Hammered Xtreme) series is Sabian's premier modern dark-side line, hand-hammered for complex overtones while retaining the cut and projection a metal drummer requires.

The 14" HHX Stage Hi-Hats are Travis's pulse layer. Where the *Painkiller* Paiste Signature 14" Heavy Hi-Hats were bright and brilliant-finished for maximum cut at speed, the HHX Stage Hats sit slightly darker in the spectrum — better suited to modern dense productions where excessive high-frequency content can fight the cymbal mics on the guitar tracks. They still cut, but with more harmonic complexity underneath.

The HHX Evolution crash range (16/18/19) provides Travis the dynamic options he uses across the album. Evolution-series crashes are designed for fast response with controlled decay — they speak immediately, peak hard, and clear out of the way for the next phrase. For *Firepower*'s arrangement-dense songs, that quick-clear behavior is essential. The three sizes allow Travis to match accent intensity to song dynamics: 16" for tight punctuation, 18" as the primary, 19" for the bigger structural moments.

The 21" HHX Groove Ride is the album's ride workhorse. It is a relatively dry, defined ride with a strong bell — exactly what Priest's mid-tempo material requires. Where flowery, washy rides would muddy the mix, the Groove Ride delivers articulation that holds its position in the stereo field even when guitars stack to the limit.

The 18" HHX Chinese provides the trashy accent role that the 20" Paiste Novo China filled on *Painkiller*. Sabian's Chinese cymbals in the HHX series have a more controlled trash character than vintage 2002s — useful for tight modern productions that don't want extended cymbal wash.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums does Scott Travis use on Firepower?**

A: On Judas Priest's Firepower (2018), Scott Travis played a Tama Starclassic Maple kit in Piano Black finish — two 22"x18" bass drums, 10"x8" and 12"x9" rack toms, 14"x14" and 16"x16" floor toms, all 6-ply maple shells. His snare was a Tama Starphonic Brass 14"x6" with die-cast hoops. The setup is a notable evolution from his Painkiller-era (1990) Tama Artstar II birch kit — maple shells deliver warmer, fuller body than birch, and the 22"x18" bass drums are two inches deeper than the 22"x16" kicks he used in 1990, adding low-end weight for Firepower's more mid-tempo material.

**Q: Was Firepower Grammy-nominated?**

A: Yes. Firepower's single "Lightning Strike" was nominated for the 2019 Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance. The album itself debuted at #5 on both the UK and US Billboard 200 charts, earned Gold certification in the UK, and represented Judas Priest's strongest commercial chart performance since Painkiller in 1990 — twenty-eight years earlier. The Grammy nomination was the band's first since 2010's "Dissident Aggressor" win from Best Metal Performance in 2010.

**Q: Who produced Firepower?**

A: Firepower was co-produced by Andy Sneap and Tom Allom — a deliberate, historic pairing. Andy Sneap is the modern metal producer behind work with Accept, Exodus, Testament, and Megadeth, and Firepower was recorded primarily at his own Old Mill Recording Studios with additional sessions at Witchroom Sound. Tom Allom was the producer behind every classic Judas Priest studio album from British Steel (1980) through Ram It Down (1988) — Firepower marked his first Priest studio production credit in thirty years. The dual-producer dynamic combined Sneap's contemporary engineering and mixing with Allom's classic-Priest arrangement and dynamics perspective.

**Q: How does Scott Travis's Firepower setup compare to Painkiller?**

A: The gear evolution from Painkiller (1990) to Firepower (2018) spans 28 years and a complete component-level transition. Kit: Tama Artstar II birch became Tama Starclassic maple — warmer body, fuller harmonic content. Bass drums went from 22"x16" to 22"x18" — two inches deeper for added low-end weight. Snare: Tama Artstar II steel 14"x6.5" became Tama Starphonic brass 14"x6" — brass for warmer cut, half-inch shallower for added response. Cymbals: Paiste Signature/2002 became Sabian HHX — darker, more complex overtones better suited to modern dense productions. Pedals: prototype Tama Iron Cobra HP900s became Tama Speed Cobra HP910s — longer footboard, lighter moving mass. Travis still runs two independent bass drums rather than a connected double pedal — that philosophy has not changed since 1990.
