# Cynic Focus Drum Setup: Sean Reinert's 1993 Progressive Death Metal Gear

> Discover the drum kit and gear Sean Reinert used to record Cynic's landmark Focus album — the progressive death metal record that merged jazz fusion with extreme metal.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Sean Reinert](/llms/drummers/sean-reinert.md)
**Band / Album:** Cynic — *Focus* (1993)
**Genre:** Progressive Death Metal / Jazz Fusion

## Overview

Released on September 21, 1993, Cynic's *Focus* is one of the most audacious and influential records in metal history. At its rhythmic core sat Sean Reinert — the same drummer who had already redefined the genre two years earlier on Death's *Human* — now pushing even further into jazz fusion territory with a band built entirely around that ambition.

*Focus* arrived from Morrisound Recording in Tampa, engineered and produced by Scott Burns, featuring Reinert alongside guitarist Paul Masvidal, bassist Sean Malone, and vocalist Masvidal (with vocodered vocals). The result was unlike anything extreme metal had produced: chordal complexity borrowed from jazz fusion, robotic vocoders, and beneath all of it, Reinert's drumming serving the most demanding rhythmic landscape of his career.

Where *Human* demonstrated that death metal could accommodate jazz vocabulary, *Focus* took the next logical step — building the entire architecture of the music around that vocabulary. Reinert's performance features his most complex polyrhythmic work: extended jazz phrasing over 4/4 frameworks, ride cymbal work drawn from Tony Williams and Jack DeJohnette, ghost note architecture creating multiple rhythmic layers, and double-bass deployed as melodic counterpoint.

Sean Reinert passed away on January 24, 2020. *Focus* stands as his most adventurous recorded performance — the fullest expression of his jazz-death fusion vision.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Artstar II (Natural Burst finish)
- **Snare:** Tama Artstar II Birch, 14" x 5.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series + Zildjian K Custom Dry Ride
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW 5000 Double Pedal; Vic Firth 5A sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), Remo Powerstroke 3 (bass drum)

### The Tama Artstar II: Jazz-Death Fusion Platform

Sean Reinert's Tama Artstar II was the same professional kit configuration he had debuted on Death's *Human* (1991). By 1993, this setup was fully refined — Reinert had spent two years touring with it, developing an intimate understanding of how each shell responded under Scott Burns' Morrisound microphone approach.

The birch shells delivered a sharper, brighter attack than maple alternatives — a characteristic that worked directly in favor of Reinert's jazz-influenced playing. In jazz contexts, birch shells provide the articulation needed for complex polyrhythmic playing: each stroke speaks clearly rather than blending into sustained wash. For *Focus*, where rhythmic information is often the primary melodic content, this shell character was essential.

The single bass drum with DW 5000 double pedal reflected Reinert's philosophical approach: rather than two full bass drums for visual maximalism, a single kick with precision mechanics. His double-bass on *Focus* is deployed with compositional intent — integrated into phrases, responsive to harmonic context, never merely sustaining at maximum velocity for effect.

### The K Custom Dry Ride: The Critical Addition

The most significant cymbal on *Focus* is the Zildjian K Custom Dry Ride — the centerpiece of Reinert's jazz vocabulary. Its low-wash, high-articulation design was developed for jazz contexts where cymbal patterns need to be heard clearly without overwhelming other instruments. On *Focus*, the ride cymbal often functions as the album's lead melodic voice: extended jazz patterns, bell accents as harmonic markers, ride-led fills as counterpoint to Masvidal's guitar phrases.

His hi-hats were Zildjian A 14" Quick Beat — tighter and more controlled than the New Beat configuration of *Spiritual Healing* (1990), suited to the precise jazz patterns *Focus* demanded. The remaining A Series crashes (16" and 18") and Zildjian 16" China provided the metal aggression when the compositions required it.

## FAQ

**What drum kit did Sean Reinert use on Cynic's Focus?**
Sean Reinert recorded Cynic's Focus (1993) using a Tama Artstar II kit — birch shells, Natural Burst finish. His configuration included a 22" x 18" bass drum with DW 5000 double pedal, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. Cymbals were Zildjian A and K series, with the K Custom Dry 20" Ride as the central jazz voice. Sessions took place at Morrisound Recording in Tampa with producer Scott Burns — the same team behind Death's Human (1991).

**How did Sean Reinert's drumming on Focus differ from Death's Human?**
While both albums feature Reinert's jazz-death fusion vocabulary, Focus pushed the concept further by making jazz fusion the governing compositional principle rather than an element within death metal. On Human (1991), Reinert introduced jazz ride patterns and ghost notes into a death metal framework. On Focus (1993), Cynic built the entire album's architecture around those jazz elements — greater rhythmic complexity, wider dynamic range (from near-silence to full blast), and the ride cymbal functioning as a lead melodic voice. The addition of the Zildjian K Custom Dry Ride between the two albums signals this shift.

**Why is Cynic's Focus considered a landmark for progressive death metal?**
Focus (1993) was the first death metal album to fully integrate jazz fusion at the compositional level. Previous progressive death metal records (including Human) had introduced jazz elements into a death metal framework. Focus inverted that relationship: jazz fusion vocabulary — polyrhythmic complexity, extended ride cymbal phrasing, dynamic storytelling — was the governing architecture, with death metal intensity as one of several textures within it. Reinert's drumming was central to this achievement: his jazz ride patterns, ghost note architecture, and dynamic control demonstrated what extreme metal drumming could be when jazz training was treated as primary resource rather than supplemental influence.
