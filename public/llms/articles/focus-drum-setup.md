# Cynic Focus Drum Setup: Sean Reinert's 1993 Progressive Death Metal Gear

> Discover the drum kit and gear Sean Reinert used to record Cynic's landmark Focus album — the progressive death metal record that merged jazz fusion with extreme metal.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** Sean Reinert
**Band / Album:** Cynic — *Focus* (1993)
**Genre:** Progressive Death Metal / Jazz Fusion

## Overview

Released on October 19, 1993, Cynic's "Focus" is one of the most singular albums in the history of extreme metal — a record that merged death metal aggression with jazz fusion sophistication, vocoder-processed vocals, and electronic percussion elements in ways no one had imagined possible. At the center of this revolution was Sean Reinert, whose drumming on "Focus" built directly on the innovations he had introduced two years earlier on Death's "Human."

Sean Reinert and guitarist Paul Masvidal had been part of the "Human" sessions that changed death metal drumming forever. After those recordings, they formed Cynic with bassist Sean Malone and guitarist Jason Gobel. Their vision was audacious: a death metal band that didn't just borrow from jazz, but actually merged the two genres at a fundamental level. Reinert's drumming would be the hinge.

Recorded at Morrisound Recording in Tampa with producer Scott Burns — the same studio and engineer responsible for "Human" — "Focus" pushed Reinert's technique into entirely new territory. The album's eight tracks move fluidly between machine-gun death metal blasts and delicate jazz-fusion passages, with Reinert navigating every transition with the instinct of a master improviser. His ride cymbal work on "Veil of Maya," his ghost note architecture in "Sentiment," his brushwork on "Textures" — each represents a different facet of a drumming talent that no single genre could contain.

What made "Focus" technically revolutionary was Reinert's use of electronic drum triggers blended with his acoustic kit. This wasn't the quantized drum machine sound of industrial metal — it was analog trigger pads feeding synthesizer sounds, layered beneath the organic acoustic performance to create textures that still sound futuristic three decades later. The blend required extraordinary precision: the electronic elements only worked if the acoustic foundation was flawless.

Sean Reinert passed away on January 24, 2020. "Focus" stands as the fullest expression of his jazz-death fusion vision — the album where he had complete creative freedom to show what progressive drumming in extreme metal could actually be.

### Reinert's Focus Kit: Tama Artstar II with Electronic Integration

Sean Reinert's kit for the "Focus" sessions was built on the same Tama Artstar II foundation he had used for "Human" in 1991 — birch shells, single bass drum with double pedal, three rack toms providing melodic fill options. But for "Focus," Reinert added a layer that transformed the sonic landscape: electronic drum trigger pads, positioned alongside his acoustic drums, fed into synthesizer modules that layered electronic textures beneath the live performance.

This integration of acoustic and electronic drums was unprecedented in extreme metal. The triggers weren't replacing the acoustic drums — they were augmenting them, adding synthesizer pads, atmospheric tones, and programmatic elements that gave "Focus" its otherworldly production texture. Scott Burns, whose engineering work typically leaned toward bone-dry death metal clarity, found new territory capturing this hybrid setup.

The birch Artstar II shells continued to deliver the warmth and attack balance that defined Reinert's sound. Single bass drum with double pedal remained his signature — his double-kick vocabulary was musical, not merely fast, and the single-drum-plus-pedal setup kept his patterns grounded in compositional thinking rather than pure velocity display.

The three rack toms (10", 12", 13") and single floor tom (16") gave Reinert the same fill vocabulary he had explored on "Human," now deployed across musical contexts ranging from death metal blasting to jazz-fusion comping to near-ambient textural passages. The compact, musical configuration proved as versatile in Cynic's fusion context as it had been in Death's progressive death metal framework.

### Precision in Two Voices

The snare sound on "Focus" occupies a unique sonic territory in Sean Reinert's discography. Where "Human" had a pure acoustic snare captured with Scott Burns' characteristic mid-range clarity, "Focus" adds the possibility of electronic snare layering on certain passages — triggered samples blended beneath the acoustic crack for additional body and attack.

The primary snare was a Tama Artstar II birch model, tuned to provide both the attack required for death metal passages and the sensitivity needed for Reinert's ghost note vocabulary. On "Focus," the dynamic range of his snare playing is even more pronounced than on "Human": passages like the jazz-leaning sections of "Veil of Maya" require whisper-quiet ghost notes, while the death metal blasts of "Hyperdrive" need a sharp, cutting rimshot that registers above the guitar density.

Scott Burns captured this range with particular care on "Focus," allowing the snare dynamics more space in the mix than typical death metal productions of the period. The result is a snare that breathes across the album's varied contexts — cutting through distortion when required, retreating into textural ghost work when the music opens up.

### Zildjian Arsenal for Jazz and Metal

Reinert's cymbal selection on "Focus" carried forward the jazz-rooted Zildjian palette from "Human," deploying it now across an even wider musical range. The K Custom Dry Ride remained the cornerstone of his sound — its dry, articulate character allowed every stick stroke to register clearly whether Reinert was riding the bell, playing the bow in jazz patterns, or using it as an accent surface during blast passages.

The A Series New Beat hi-hats provided the tonal center his playing demanded. Reinert's hi-hat technique on "Focus" is among the most sophisticated in extreme metal: he uses the hats as a harmonic voice, opening and closing them for tonal inflection in ways that jazz drummers would recognize immediately. On tracks like "Sentiment," the hi-hat becomes almost melodic — an approach that had no precedent in death metal.

The crash and China cymbals served the album's death metal sections with characteristic Zildjian clarity — musical crashes with enough body to punctuate section changes, a China with the raw aggression required for blasting passages. The 10" splash appeared in jazz-vocabulary contexts, providing the quick accent responses that larger crashes couldn't deliver.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Sean Reinert use on Cynic's Focus in 1993?**

A: Sean Reinert recorded Cynic's Focus in 1993 using a Tama Artstar II kit with birch shells — the same series he had used on Death's Human two years earlier. His configuration included a single 22" x 16" bass drum with a double pedal, 10", 12", and 13" rack toms, and a 16" floor tom. What made the Focus setup unique was the addition of electronic trigger pads integrated with the acoustic kit, feeding synthesizer modules to create the layered electronic textures that define the album's sound. This blend of acoustic and electronic drums was unprecedented in extreme metal.

**Q: What cymbals did Sean Reinert use on Cynic's Focus?**

A: Sean Reinert used a Zildjian A and K Series cymbal setup on Cynic's Focus, carrying forward the same palette from his Death Human sessions. His setup included Zildjian A 14" New Beat Hi-Hats, A Series 16" and 18" crashes, a Zildjian K 20" Custom Dry Ride (central to his jazz-fusion ride vocabulary), a 16" China for aggressive accents, and a 10" Splash for jazz-vocabulary quick accents. The K Custom Dry Ride was the cornerstone of his sound, its dry, articulate character allowing the jazz ride patterns on tracks like Veil of Maya and Sentiment to speak clearly above the mix.

**Q: How did Sean Reinert's drumming on Focus differ from his work on Death's Human?**

A: On Death's Human (1991), Reinert introduced jazz vocabulary into death metal — ghost notes, ride cymbal work, dynamic shading — but always within the framework of death metal song structures and Chuck Schuldiner's compositional vision. On Cynic's Focus (1993), the jazz and death metal elements were fully equal partners, with no genre hierarchy constraining Reinert's approach. Additionally, Focus introduced electronic trigger pads blended with the acoustic kit — an element absent from Human — creating the synthesizer textures that give Focus its futuristic sonic identity. Reinert had complete creative freedom on Focus in a way that the Death sessions, however groundbreaking, did not allow.

**Q: Who produced Cynic's Focus and where was it recorded?**

A: Cynic's Focus (1993) was recorded at Morrisound Recording in Tampa, Florida, with producer Scott Burns — the same studio and producer who had captured Death's Human in 1991. Scott Burns was the defining engineer of Florida death metal, having recorded Deicide, Obituary, Cannibal Corpse, and numerous other Morrisound classics. For Focus, Burns extended his approach to accommodate Reinert's electronic trigger integration and the album's jazz-fusion hybrid character, producing a drum sound unlike anything else in his catalog.
