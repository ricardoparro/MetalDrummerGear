## Overview

Released on September 25, 2000, *Follow the Reaper* is Children of Bodom's third full-length album and the record widely regarded as the pinnacle of Jaska Raatikainen's technical drumming within the band's catalog. Recorded at Astia Studio in Imatra, Finland — the same studio and production team responsible for *Hatebreeder* — it arrived just a year after CoB's second album and documented a dramatic leap in both compositional sophistication and technical execution.

At the center of *Follow the Reaper*'s relentless precision was Raatikainen at 21, already operating at a level of technical mastery that would define the Children of Bodom sound for years to come. His newly adopted Tama Starclassic Maple kit, paired with Paiste 2002 cymbals and the Tama Iron Cobra double pedals, marked the beginning of the gear era that would reach its commercial peak on *Hate Crew Deathroll* (2003). Tracks like "Kissing the Shadows," "Follow the Reaper," and "Bodom After Midnight" deploy double-bass patterns, blast beats, and neoclassical tom fills at extremes that remain benchmarks for the genre.

*Follow the Reaper* represents the critical bridge in the Jaska Raatikainen gear arc: it is where the early Tama configuration of *Hatebreeder* gave way to the Starclassic era, where Zildjian A Series cymbals were replaced by Paiste 2002, and where the DW 5000 double pedal was superseded by the Tama Iron Cobra. This article breaks down every component of Raatikainen's setup on *Follow the Reaper* — the Starclassic shells, the Paiste 2002 cymbal selection, the Iron Cobra double pedal, and the stylistic approach that made this CoB's most technically demanding record.

### Tama Starclassic Maple: The Technical Era Begins

For *Follow the Reaper*, Jaska Raatikainen made the transition to the Tama Starclassic Maple — Tama's flagship professional series and a significant upgrade over the early Tama configuration of *Hatebreeder*. The Starclassic Maple's six-ply shell construction delivered a combination of attack definition, tonal body, and shell-to-shell consistency that the more demanding arrangements on *Follow the Reaper* required.

The double bass drum configuration — twin 22" x 18" kick drums — remained central to Raatikainen's approach, now supported by the Iron Cobra's superior mechanical consistency over the DW 5000. Children of Bodom's arrangements on *Follow the Reaper* pushed double-kick velocity and endurance further than on any previous record: tracks like "Kissing the Shadows" and "Follow the Reaper" demand sustained blast and double-bass work across extended passages where any mechanical variance in the pedal response would compromise the precision of the patterns.

The compact tom spread — 10" and 12" rack toms paired with 14" and 16" floor toms — continued from the Hatebreeder configuration, giving Raatikainen the tonal range for Children of Bodom's characteristically melodic fill language. The Starclassic Maple's improved shell-to-shell consistency over the early Tama allowed the melody-following tom phrases to register more clearly in Anssi Kippo's mix, supporting the neoclassical keyboard and guitar countermelodies above with greater precision.

### Tama Starclassic 14" x 5.5" Maple Snare

Raatikainen's snare on *Follow the Reaper* was a Tama Starclassic maple model, matched to the kit's shell series and suited to the extraordinary technical demands of the album's arrangements. The 14" x 5.5" dimensions delivered the crack and attack essential for blast beat sections and the rapid single-stroke work that defines tracks like "Kissing the Shadows," with enough body and warmth to remain musical alongside Janne Wirman's dense keyboard layers.

The Starclassic maple snare's construction gave it a warmer, more complex character than steel or brass alternatives — a deliberate choice for an album where the drums needed to serve neoclassical compositions rather than compete with them. Children of Bodom's arrangements placed heavy musical value on the interplay between guitar, keyboard, and drums, and a hyper-aggressive metal snare would have disrupted that balance in Anssi Kippo's mix.

The result is a snare sound on *Follow the Reaper* that is aggressive, precise, and present without overwhelming the melodic content — the correct tool for arguably CoB's most technically demanding studio performance.

### Paiste 2002: The Transition from Zildjian to Finnish Metal's Defining Cymbal

Jaska Raatikainen's cymbal setup on *Follow the Reaper* marked the transition from the Zildjian A Series of *Hatebreeder* to Paiste's 2002 line — a shift that brought a different tonal character to CoB's studio sound and defined the drum sound that would reach its peak on *Hate Crew Deathroll* (2003).

The Paiste 2002 line's CuSn8 bronze alloy produces a bright, cutting sound with enough warmth in the sustain to avoid harshness at high velocities — qualities essential for *Follow the Reaper*'s keyboard-dense arrangements, where Janne Wirman's layers occupied substantial harmonic space across every track. The 2002's natural cut through the mix, without requiring aggressive post-processing, was a significant advantage in Astia Studio's recording environment.

The Paiste Sound Edge 14" hi-hats — with their rippled-edge bottom cymbal for faster air release and more defined chick — gave Raatikainen the articulation precision required at the tempos on *Follow the Reaper*. "Kissing the Shadows" and "Bodom After Midnight" demand that every sixteenth note at the hi-hat registers as a distinct event; the Sound Edge design delivered that precision across the full surface.

The two Paiste 2002 crashes (16" and 18") provided tonal variety for accent work: the 16" for explosive short-decay accents in tight melodic passages, the 18" for section boundaries and builds. The 20" ride served both time-keeping and bell accent functions. The 18" China added the trashy upward-cutting attack deployed at structural riff transitions — always deliberate, never continuous.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Jaska Raatikainen use on Follow the Reaper?**

A: On *Follow the Reaper* (2000), Jaska Raatikainen played a Tama Starclassic Maple kit — marking his transition from the early Tama configuration of *Hatebreeder* (1999) to the Starclassic series. The setup centered on twin 22" x 18" bass drums, rack toms (10" and 12"), and floor toms (14" and 16"), all in the Starclassic's six-ply maple construction. This was the beginning of the Tama Starclassic era that would continue through *Hate Crew Deathroll* (2003). For Raatikainen's complete gear history across CoB's career, see the [Jaska Raatikainen drummer profile](/drummer/jaska-raatikainen).

**Q: What pedals did Jaska Raatikainen use on Follow the Reaper?**

A: Jaska Raatikainen used the Tama Iron Cobra double pedal on *Follow the Reaper* (2000) — a significant upgrade from the DW 5000 of the *Hatebreeder* sessions. The Iron Cobra's chain-drive mechanism and cam geometry delivered superior mechanical consistency at the extreme tempos that *Follow the Reaper* demanded, particularly across the sustained double-kick passages in tracks like "Kissing the Shadows." The Iron Cobra would remain Raatikainen's pedal of choice through the *Hate Crew Deathroll* (2003) sessions. See the [Jaska Raatikainen full kit guide](/articles/jaska-raatikainen-drum-setup) for his pedal evolution across CoB's career.

**Q: What cymbals did Jaska Raatikainen use on Follow the Reaper?**

A: On *Follow the Reaper* (2000), Jaska Raatikainen used Paiste cymbals — specifically Paiste Sound Edge 14" hi-hats paired with Paiste 2002 crashes (16" and 18"), a Paiste 2002 20" ride, and a Paiste 2002 18" China. This marked the transition from the Zildjian A Series of *Hatebreeder* (1999) to the Paiste 2002 era that would continue through *Hate Crew Deathroll* (2003). The Paiste 2002's CuSn8 bronze alloy brought a different, brighter tonal character to CoB's cymbal sound. For context, see the [Hate Crew Deathroll drum setup article](/articles/hate-crew-deathroll-drum-setup).

**Q: Is Follow the Reaper Jaska Raatikainen's most technical album?**

A: *Follow the Reaper* (2000) is widely regarded as Jaska Raatikainen's most technically demanding Children of Bodom studio performance. "Kissing the Shadows" in particular demands sustained blast and double-bass patterns at tempos exceeding 210 BPM across extended song sections — a physical and technical feat that remains one of melodic death metal's most demanding recorded drum performances. The album arrived just one year after *Hatebreeder* and documented a dramatic increase in technical ambition, setting up the refined and commercially successful approach of *Hate Crew Deathroll* (2003).
