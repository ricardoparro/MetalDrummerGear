# God Hates Us All Drum Setup: Slayer's 9/11 Album Gear Breakdown (2001)

> Released the morning of September 11, 2001, Slayer's God Hates Us All marked the band's last album before Dave Lombardo's return on Christ Illusion. Complete gear breakdown of the Tama Starclassic Maple kit, Iron Cobra pedals, and the Matt Hyde production at The Warehouse Studio, Vancouver.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** Paul Bostaph
**Band / Album:** Slayer — *God Hates Us All* (2001)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal

## Overview

On September 11, 2001 — the same morning the World Trade Center towers fell — American Recordings shipped Slayer's God Hates Us All to record stores worldwide. The release date timing was an accident of scheduling, not intent. The album had been finished weeks earlier in Vancouver. But the title, the cover art, and the relentlessly nihilistic tone of the songs made the coincidence one of the strangest in metal history. Few albums have ever been released on a more historically loaded day.

God Hates Us All is also a transitional record in the Slayer catalog. It is the band's last studio album of the Dave Lombardo absence period — the long stretch between Seasons in the Abyss (1990) and Christ Illusion (2006) when Paul Bostaph held the drum chair. It is the album where Slayer's classic-thrash sound met the down-tuned, heavier production aesthetic of the early 2000s metal scene. And it is the album that closed the American Recordings / Rick Rubin Slayer arc that began with Reign in Blood — the next album would be Lombardo's Grammy-winning comeback.

Matt Hyde produced the sessions at The Warehouse Studio in Vancouver, the historic facility built inside a 100-year-old former courthouse and acquired by Bryan Adams in the 1990s. Hyde — known for his work with Porno for Pyros, Bad Religion, and a long list of harder-edged rock acts — brought a thicker, more layered drum sound than the dry Rubin aesthetic of the 1980s Slayer records. The kit choice for the sessions reflected that production direction: a Tama Starclassic Maple kit with double 22x18 bass drums, tuned darker and deeper than the classic Slayer sound.

This article documents the drum gear used on God Hates Us All, the 9/11 release-date context that shaped the album's reception, and the place this record holds as the final pre-Lombardo-return Slayer album in the discography.

### The 2001 Slayer Kit: Tama Starclassic Maple

The drum kit on God Hates Us All centered on a Tama Starclassic Maple — Tama's flagship all-maple shell series in the early 2000s. The choice reflected both the band's long-standing Tama endorsement (going back to Lombardo's Artstar II era) and Matt Hyde's preference for a warmer, more resonant low-end on the drums than Slayer's classic dry sound.

The double bass configuration of two 22x18 kicks gave the album the deep, sustained low-end that suited Hyde's modern production approach. The shells' 18-inch depth — deeper than the 16-inch kicks of the Reign in Blood era — added body and resonance that Hyde captured with port-mic plus inside-mic technique. The low-end on God Hates Us All sits noticeably heavier in the mix than on earlier Slayer records, and the kit specification is one of the reasons.

The tom configuration kept Slayer's classic 4-piece approach: two rack toms (10 and 12) and two floor toms (16 and 18). The slightly larger 18-inch floor tom — instead of the more common 16 — gave the lower fills additional weight, audible on tracks like "Bloodline" and "Disciple" where the tom work punctuates the riff structure.

The Piano Black finish maintained the dark visual aesthetic of Slayer's gear catalog — a constant from Show No Mercy through the band's entire career.

### The 2001 Crack: 14x6.5 Steel

The snare drum on God Hates Us All was a 14x6.5 steel-shell — the standard size and material for thrash metal records of the era. Steel gave the snare the cutting, metallic crack that Slayer's music demands, with the 6.5-inch depth providing the body needed to translate through Matt Hyde's modern, layered production.

The snare sound on God Hates Us All is one of the most aggressive of the entire Slayer catalog. Hyde compressed it heavily — a deliberate departure from the more transparent Rick Rubin approach of the 1980s — and placed it forward in the mix. The result is the snare crack on "Bloodline" and "Disciple" hits with a brutality that defined the album's overall sonic character.

The 6.5-inch depth was a slight increase over the 6-inch and 5.5-inch snares more common on the classic Slayer records, contributing to the lower midrange weight that distinguishes the God Hates Us All sound. The combination of steel shell and deeper depth gave the snare both cut at the top and body underneath — necessary attributes for the album's down-tuned guitar context.

### Paiste 2002 and Signature: The Slayer Cymbal Sound

The cymbal selection on God Hates Us All stayed within the Paiste 2002 / Signature world that defined Slayer's sound throughout the 1990s and early 2000s. Paiste's bright, cutting cymbal character paired naturally with Slayer's down-tuned, riff-driven music: the cymbals project above the dense guitar layers without requiring excessive top-end boost in the mix.

The 14-inch Sound Edge hi-hats provided the immediate, cutting chick that thrash metal demands. The hammered Sound Edge profile — Paiste's design that introduces controlled air gaps between the two cymbals — keeps the chick articulate even at high tempos and prevents the wash that plagues lesser hi-hats in fast playing contexts.

The 18 and 19-inch 2002 crashes — Paiste's signature thrash crash series — punctuated the riff transitions with the trademark cutting bell-and-bow combination. The Signature 22" Power Ride gave the album its ride bell sound: heavier than a standard ride, with a more pronounced fundamental that translated well through Matt Hyde's production.

The 18-inch 2002 China above the floor tom maintained the trashy China accent that has been a Slayer constant since Show No Mercy. Across every drummer who has played in Slayer — Lombardo, Bostaph, and beyond — a China cymbal in roughly that position has remained part of the band's sonic identity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums were used on Slayer's God Hates Us All?**

A: The drums on God Hates Us All (2001) were a Tama Starclassic Maple kit in Piano Black finish, with two 22x18-inch bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 16" and 18" floor toms. The all-maple shells delivered a warmer, deeper tone than the Tama Artstar II birch setup of the classic Reign in Blood era, suited to Matt Hyde's modern production approach at The Warehouse Studio in Vancouver.

**Q: Was God Hates Us All really released on September 11, 2001?**

A: Yes. God Hates Us All was released by American Recordings on Tuesday, September 11, 2001 — the same morning as the World Trade Center attacks. The release date was an accident of scheduling; the album had been finished weeks earlier. The coincidence of an album titled "God Hates Us All" with a bloody, vandalized-Bible cover arriving on that specific day created one of the strangest release-date overlaps in metal history. Slayer did not adjust marketing or release timing in response.

**Q: Was Dave Lombardo on God Hates Us All?**

A: No. God Hates Us All (2001) was recorded by Paul Bostaph during the long period of Dave Lombardo's absence from Slayer. Lombardo had departed after Seasons in the Abyss (1990) and would not return until Christ Illusion (2006). God Hates Us All is the last Slayer studio album before Lombardo's return, and it represents the commercial peak of the Bostaph-era Slayer output (peaking at #28 on the Billboard 200).

**Q: Who produced God Hates Us All?**

A: God Hates Us All was produced by Matt Hyde, known for his work with Porno for Pyros, Bad Religion, and a range of harder-edged rock acts. Hyde brought a thicker, more layered drum sound to the sessions than the dry, minimal production aesthetic of Rick Rubin's 1980s Slayer records. The album was tracked at The Warehouse Studio in Vancouver, Canada — Bryan Adams' historic facility built inside a 100-year-old former courthouse.

**Q: How did God Hates Us All chart commercially?**

A: God Hates Us All debuted and peaked at #28 on the Billboard 200 — Slayer's highest US chart position at the time of release, beating Diabolus in Musica's #31 from 1998. The album was certified Gold by the RIAA in subsequent years. Despite the release-date overlap with September 11, 2001, the album's commercial performance was strong, and it remains one of the most successful Slayer studio records of the post-Reign in Blood era.
