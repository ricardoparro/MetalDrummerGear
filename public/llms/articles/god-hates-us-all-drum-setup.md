## Overview

Released on September 11, 2001 — the same morning as the worst terrorist attack in American history — Slayer's *God Hates Us All* arrived under circumstances that would define its legacy before a single review was written. American Recordings pushed the release through regardless. The album debuted at #28 on the Billboard 200, became Slayer's best-charting record in the United States, and was eventually certified Platinum — making it the most commercially successful album of Paul Bostaph's Slayer tenure.

Behind the kit was Paul Bostaph, who had rejoined Slayer in 2001 after a brief absence. *God Hates Us All* represented a significant evolution in his approach: less pure speed thrash, more emphasis on locked-in groove, mid-tempo power, and what proved to be his most Grammy-relevant performance. The album's track "Disciple" won the Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance at the 2002 Grammy Awards — the only Grammy Bostaph's Slayer work ever received.

For the sessions at The Warehouse in New Orleans and Capitol Studios in Hollywood, Bostaph played a Pearl Masters BRX kit — a professional shell series combining birch and maple for focused, punchy attack. Producer Matt Hyde captured a drum sound tighter and more modern than the Rubin-era Slayer productions, reflecting both the shift in production approach and Bostaph's evolution as a player.

### Paul Bostaph's 2001 Pearl Masters BRX

For *God Hates Us All*, Paul Bostaph played a Pearl Masters BRX kit — a birch-maple hybrid shell series designed for focused attack with tonal depth. The BRX formula combined birch's attack-forward character with maple's warmth and low-end depth, producing a kit that suited the album's more groove-oriented material.

The twin 22" x 18" bass drum configuration remained consistent with Bostaph's established approach. Two separate bass drums — rather than a double pedal — were his preferred setup for Slayer's demanding material. The deep 18-inch shell depth produced a fuller, more resonant kick tone than shallower configurations of earlier Slayer eras.

The four-tom layout (10", 12", 14", 16") provided the full melodic vocabulary Bostaph used throughout the album. His fills on *God Hates Us All* are more compositional than the straight-ahead speed fills of earlier thrash — longer, melodically descending passages that give songs architectural structure beyond the riff. Producer Matt Hyde's drum sound is notably tighter and more modern than the ambient approach of the Rubin era, and the BRX kit's focused attack suited this aesthetic perfectly.

### The Grammy Crack: Steel Snare Precision

The snare sound on *God Hates Us All* is among the most authoritative in Bostaph's Slayer catalog. A steel-shell snare at 14" x 6.5", tuned medium-high for maximum cut, delivered the crack that drives "Disciple" — the Grammy-winning track — and every other backbeat on the album.

Steel shells produce a midrange-forward, cutting tone that slices through dense thrash guitar without the warmth of maple getting in the way. For Matt Hyde's tighter, more modern production approach, a steel snare provided the definition needed to sit in the mix without muddiness.

The Grammy win for "Disciple" brought more attention to Bostaph's drum sound on this album than any previous Slayer release. The locked-in groove, tight snare crack, and precise double-bass foundation became the defining elements of his most commercially visible performance.

### Sabian AAX and Tama Iron Cobra

Bostaph's Sabian AAX setup delivered the brightness and aggression thrash production demands. The AAX series' machine-hammered professional construction provided consistent, bright, cutting tone across the full cymbal range — essential when every cymbal accent must register clearly in Matt Hyde's dense production.

The 14" AAX Stage Hi-Hats drove rhythmic patterns through the album's range of tempos. The 16" and 18" Metal Crash cymbals provided graduated crash options. The 20" Metal Ride served double duty for groove sections and alternate crash when maximum cymbal wash was needed.

The Tama Iron Cobra was the industry standard for professional thrash metal double-kick work in the early 2000s. Its Power Glide cam and adjustable spring system gave Bostaph the mechanical consistency his groove-locked patterns demanded. For "Disciple's" Grammy-winning groove, the Iron Cobra's reliable power stroke maintained the locked-in pocket that defined the track.

## FAQ

**What drums did Paul Bostaph use on God Hates Us All?**
Paul Bostaph recorded God Hates Us All (2001) using a Pearl Masters BRX kit — a birch-maple hybrid shell series designed for focused attack with tonal depth. His configuration included twin 22" x 18" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. His snare was a steel-shell 14" x 6.5" drum. Cymbals were Sabian AAX series and bass drum pedals were Tama Iron Cobra singles (one per bass drum). The album was produced by Matt Hyde at The Warehouse in New Orleans and Capitol Studios in Hollywood.

**Why did God Hates Us All release on September 11, 2001?**
God Hates Us All was scheduled for release on September 11, 2001 — the same morning as the September 11 terrorist attacks. American Recordings pushed the release through regardless, creating one of the most notorious release-date coincidences in metal history. Despite the circumstances, the album debuted at #28 on the Billboard 200 and was certified Platinum — Slayer's most commercially successful album and Paul Bostaph's highest-charting Slayer work.

**Did God Hates Us All win a Grammy?**
Yes — the track "Disciple" from God Hates Us All won the Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance at the 44th Grammy Awards (2002). This was the only Grammy Award associated with Paul Bostaph's Slayer tenure. The award brought significant attention to Bostaph's groove-thrash approach and the album's more commercially accessible sound relative to earlier Slayer material.
