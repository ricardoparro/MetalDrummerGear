## Overview

Released on 9 April 2002, *Hammered* is Motörhead's sixteenth studio album and Mikkey Dee's sixth full studio record with the band. It was also a genuine departure in production personnel: Thom Panunzio, best known for his work with Bruce Springsteen and U2, took the producer's chair, working alongside Motörhead across sessions at Henson Recording Studios in Hollywood and, notably, at Chuck Reed's private house in the Hollywood Hills — an unusually informal recording environment for a band as road-tested as Motörhead.

The album's genesis carried real historical weight. The band arrived in Los Angeles to begin recording just before the September 11, 2001 attacks, and that timing left an unmistakable mark on the record's tone. Dee later described *Hammered* as having "a darker subject matter and reflective tone" compared to the band's previous work — a description borne out across tracks like "Voices from the War" and "Down the Line." It's a rare instance of world events directly shaping the emotional register of a Motörhead album, rather than the band's usual unfiltered aggression standing alone.

*Hammered* also gave Motörhead one of its more unusual pop-culture footnotes: the track "The Game," written by WWE composer Jim Johnston, became wrestler Triple H's entrance theme, and Motörhead performed it live at WrestleMania X-Seven and WrestleMania 21. Triple H returned the favor by contributing spoken-word vocals to the album's closing track, "Serial Killer." Commercially, the record reached #11 on the UK Rock & Metal Albums chart, with additional placements in Sweden (#18), Germany (#39), Finland (#34), and France (#113). It also marked Motörhead's first distribution through Sanctuary Records and its Metal-Is imprint in North America, following the band's earlier CMC International arrangement.

For Mikkey Dee, *Hammered* represents the point where the gear transition begun on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup) fully settled into place — the Tama Starclassic kit and Paiste 2002 Sound Edge hi-hats now his complete, dialed-in setup, two years ahead of reaching their final documented form on [*Inferno*](/articles/inferno-drum-setup) (2004). This article breaks down that settled setup and the gear behind one of Motörhead's more unexpectedly reflective records.

### Tama Starclassic, Fully Settled

By *Hammered*, the transitional Starclassic kit Mikkey Dee introduced on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup) two years earlier had become his complete, settled setup — the same maple/birch hybrid shell configuration that would carry through, largely unchanged, to [*Inferno*](/articles/inferno-drum-setup) in 2004. The core dimensions remained the ones he'd used since 1993: a 22" bass drum, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, and 16"/18" floor toms.

Recording partly at Henson Recording Studios and partly at Chuck Reed's private Hollywood Hills house gave the sessions an unusually intimate, less clinical character. The Starclassic's punchier attack translated well to the more relaxed, house-session environment, capturing a fuller low end without needing the more controlled acoustic treatment of a traditional studio room.

Given the album's darker emotional register, Dee's playing on tracks like "Voices from the War" leans into the kit's fuller resonance — longer tom decays, more deliberate pacing on fills — a contrast to the tighter, faster playing style of earlier-2000s Motörhead material. The Starclassic's more modern voicing gave him the tonal range to serve that mood without sacrificing power on the album's harder-hitting cuts, like "Brave New World" and "Kill the World."

Producer Thom Panunzio's more atmospheric approach — a departure from Howard Benson's leaner 1990s production style — gave the drums room to breathe in the mix, a quality that suited the Starclassic's fuller tonal character better than it might have suited the tighter Artstar II sound of Dee's earlier Motörhead years.

### Steel Snare, Captured in an Unconventional Room

Dee's 14" x 6.5" Tama Starclassic steel snare on *Hammered* is the same drum introduced on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup), now fully broken in and dialed to Dee's preference after two years of use. The steel shell's bright, cutting tone remained his constant signature sound, tracked partly in the more controlled environment of Henson Recording Studios and partly in the looser, more ambient setting of Chuck Reed's house.

The house-session tracks in particular gave the snare a slightly more open, roomy character than the tighter, closely-miked sound of Motörhead's traditional studio recordings — a natural byproduct of recording in a space not purpose-built for isolation. On "Down the Line" and "Mine All Mine," that added room presence gives the backbeat a touch more body without sacrificing the crack Dee had built his sound around since the early 1990s.

Producer Thom Panunzio, working outside his usual rock and pop production context, brought a slightly different mic approach than Cameron Webb would later use on *Inferno* — favoring a warmer, less compressed snare sound that fit the album's more reflective mood.

### Paiste 2002: The Complete, Settled Setup

*Hammered* finds Mikkey Dee's cymbal setup fully settled into the configuration first introduced on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup) — Paiste 2002 Sound Edge 15" hi-hats alongside the 2002 series' ride, crashes, and China cymbal. With two years of use behind them, the Sound Edge hi-hats had become second nature, giving Dee a tighter, more controlled chick sound than the retired Giant Beat pairing he'd used for his first seven Motörhead albums.

The album's more atmospheric production, courtesy of Thom Panunzio, gave the Paiste 2002 cymbals room to ring out more naturally than the tighter, closer-miked approach of earlier records — particularly audible on the more restrained "Voices from the War," where the ride's complex, musical bow carries real prominence in the mix.

On the album's heavier tracks, like "Kill the World" and "No Remorse," the China cymbal's aggressive trash tone punctuates the darker lyrical content appropriately, giving Dee's playing an edge that matches the album's post-9/11 emotional weight without abandoning the band's fundamental sonic identity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Mikkey Dee use on Hammered?**

A: On *Hammered* (2002), Mikkey Dee used a fully settled Tama Starclassic kit — a 22" bass drum, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, and 16"/18" floor toms with maple/birch hybrid shells. This was the same core setup he had introduced in transitional form on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup) (2000), and it would carry forward largely unchanged to [*Inferno*](/articles/inferno-drum-setup) (2004). See the [Mikkey Dee drummer profile](/drummer/mikkey-dee) for his complete gear evolution.

**Q: Why does Hammered sound darker than other Motörhead albums?**

A: *Hammered* was recorded after Motörhead arrived in Los Angeles around the time of the September 11, 2001 attacks, and Mikkey Dee has described the resulting album as having "a darker subject matter and reflective tone" compared to the band's usual work. Tracks like "Voices from the War" reflect that shift, a rare case of world events directly shaping a Motörhead record's emotional register.

**Q: What is the WWE connection to Hammered?**

A: *Hammered* includes the track "The Game," written by WWE composer Jim Johnston, which became wrestler Triple H's entrance theme; Motörhead performed it live at WrestleMania X-Seven and WrestleMania 21. Triple H returned the favor by contributing spoken-word vocals to the album's closing track, "Serial Killer."

**Q: Who produced Hammered and where was it recorded?**

A: *Hammered* was produced by Thom Panunzio, known for his work with Bruce Springsteen and U2, and recorded at Henson Recording Studios in Hollywood as well as at Chuck Reed's private house in the Hollywood Hills. It was mixed at Interscope Studios and mastered at Capitol Mastering, and marked Motörhead's first North American distribution through Sanctuary Records and its Metal-Is imprint.
