# Lars Ulrich's Drum Setup on Metallica's Hardwired...to Self-Destruct (2016)

> Lars Ulrich's most uptempo Metallica recording since the Black Album era — how the DW Collector's Series Maple kit, Zildjian cymbals, and Tama Speed Cobra pedals powered the Hardwired sessions at Metallica's HQ studio.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Lars Ulrich](/llms/drummers/lars-ulrich.md)
**Band / Album:** Metallica — *Hardwired...to Self-Destruct* (2016)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal

## Overview

Released on November 18, 2016, "Hardwired...to Self-Destruct" was Metallica's tenth studio album and the band's most uptempo recording since the Black Album era. An ambitious double album spread across two discs and twelve tracks, Hardwired debuted at #1 in 57 countries — confirming that even three decades into their career, Metallica still commanded the global metal conversation. The lead single, "Hardwired" itself, was a sub-three-minute thrash assault that signaled the band's intent: more speed, more aggression, and a deliberate reach back toward the energy of their classic-era output.

For Lars Ulrich, Hardwired marked a return to relentless tempos. After the slower, more atmospheric "Death Magnetic" (2008) sessions and the long touring cycle that followed, Lars approached the new material with the most sustained uptempo playing he had committed to tape since the early 1990s. Tracks like "Spit Out the Bone," "Atlas, Rise!," and the title track demand the kind of double-bass stamina that Lars had relied on during the "...And Justice for All" era — and the recording captures him in remarkable form.

The production setup itself was a significant departure from previous Metallica records. Where "Death Magnetic" had Rick Rubin at the helm and "Load"/"Reload"/"Black Album" were Bob Rock productions, Hardwired was co-produced by Greg Fidelman alongside James Hetfield and Lars Ulrich themselves. The album was recorded at HQ — Metallica's own studio facility in San Rafael, California — giving the band complete control over the sonic environment and the time to dial in exactly the drum sound they wanted.

That HQ environment, combined with Fidelman's punchy, modern production approach, produced a drum tone that sat squarely in the lineage of "Death Magnetic" but with a slightly fuller, warmer character. The DW Collector's Series Maple kit Lars used through this era delivered a wider tonal palette than the Tama Starclassic Birch of "Death Magnetic," and the dual 22" × 18" bass drums gave Hardwired's most aggressive passages a low-end weight the previous album lacked.

This article covers Lars's complete gear setup for the Hardwired sessions, the HQ studio recording philosophy, and how this album fits within the broader Black Album → Death Magnetic → Hardwired arc of Lars's modern career.

### Lars's Hardwired Kit: DW Collector's Series Maple

For Hardwired...to Self-Destruct, Lars Ulrich anchored his setup on the DW Collector's Series Maple kit — the kit that defined his sound across the Death Magnetic touring cycle and into the Hardwired sessions. Where the earlier Tama Starclassic Birch had emphasized attack and punch, the DW Collector's Series Maple offered a wider tonal palette: warmer fundamentals, longer sustain, and the kind of fullness that suited Hardwired's heavier, riff-dense arrangements.

The defining choice for Hardwired was the move to dual 22" × 18" bass drums. Lars had spent decades running a single 22" kick with an Iron Cobra double pedal, but the dual-kick configuration on Hardwired delivered tonal consistency and visual presence that matched the album's renewed thrash ambitions. Each bass drum could be tuned independently, allowing subtle character differences between the two, and the dual-kick rig eliminated the slight mechanical compromises inherent to slave-beater double pedals.

DW's VLT (Vertical Low Timbre) shell construction, where the outer ply is cut so the grain runs vertically rather than horizontally, produces a lower fundamental pitch than traditional horizontal-grain shells. The result on Hardwired is a kit that hits with more low-end body than the Starclassic Birch of Death Magnetic — fuller toms, kick drums with deeper thump, and a snare drum that sat in a more cushioned environment.

Lars kept his tom configuration economical for Hardwired: a single 12" rack tom up top with 14" and 16" floor toms below. This is the same minimal tom setup he has favored since the Justice era — Lars's playing has always emphasized kick-snare interaction over tom flurries, and the limited tom array reflects that priority. When the toms do appear, as in the fills throughout "Atlas, Rise!" and the explosive moments in "Spit Out the Bone," they punctuate rather than decorate.

DW's True Pitch tuning system gave Greg Fidelman the precise lug-by-lug control needed to dial in consistent fundamental pitches across every drum at HQ studio. Combined with Evans heads tuned for attack and the room treatment Metallica had developed at their own facility, the kit sound on Hardwired stands among the most refined Lars has ever committed to record.

### The DW Collector's Maple Snare: Hardwired's Cracking Voice

For Hardwired, Lars Ulrich's snare drum was a DW Collector's Series 14" × 6.5" maple snare — a continuation of the DW direction he had moved toward through the Death Magnetic touring cycle. The maple shell delivered a fuller body and slightly longer decay than the steel-shell Tama LU1465 of Death Magnetic, sitting beautifully in Greg Fidelman's room-conscious production at HQ.

The 6.5" depth gave the snare enough body to register through the densely layered guitar parts that characterize tracks like "Moth Into Flame" and "Halo on Fire," while die-cast hoops kept rimshots tight and consistent throughout the album's most punishing passages. The maple's natural warmth balanced the brightness of the Zildjian cymbal array, producing a snare voice that cut without being harsh — exactly the tonal balance Hardwired's mix demanded.

Fidelman's snare capture at HQ combined a Shure SM57 close mic on top with a secondary mic underneath to track snare wire response. The HQ studio's controlled acoustic environment, which the band had refined across years of pre-production work in the facility, gave the snare a natural room character without bleed problems. The result was a snare sound that read as Lars's signature voice while feeling unmistakably modern — fuller and warmer than the LU1465 era, with all the attack the thrash arrangements demanded.

Lars's tuning approach for Hardwired stayed in the medium-high range that has defined his playing for decades: enough tension for sharp attack and clarity in fast passages, with the resonant head tuned slightly looser to give the snare body. Evans Heavyweight batter head delivered the durability needed for the album's heaviest backbeats without sacrificing tonal response.

### The Zildjian Z Custom and A Custom Arsenal

Lars's cymbal setup for Hardwired blended the Zildjian A Custom series — his go-to range since the Death Magnetic era — with select Z Custom pieces for added weight. Zildjian has been Lars's endorsement since the Master of Puppets era, and by 2016 the A Custom range had become his established core, with Z Custom crashes added for tracks demanding extra projection.

The 14" A Custom hi-hats remained the rhythmic anchor of the setup. Their tight, bright character cut through the densely layered guitars on tracks like "Hardwired" and "Atlas, Rise!," delivering the kind of cutting 8th-note definition that uptempo thrash drumming requires. The A Custom's brilliant finish gave the hats a presence in the mix that traditional A Series hats lacked.

The crash array on Hardwired expanded slightly versus the Death Magnetic setup. A 16" A Custom delivered fast accents, an 18" Z Custom contributed heavier projection for major section endings, and a 19" A Custom provided full-wash sustain for climactic moments throughout "Spit Out the Bone" and "Halo on Fire." The Z Custom's heavier weight versus the A Custom gave Lars a wider dynamic palette than relying on a single series.

The 20" A Custom Ride carried the rhythmic load during the ride-driven passages on "Moth Into Flame" and the instrumental sections of "Halo on Fire," its clear bell definition cutting through the mix without sacrificing wash sustain. The 18" A Custom China supplied the explosive, trashy accents that have been a Metallica signature since "Master of Puppets" — used for riff endings and dramatic punctuation throughout the album.

The brilliant finish across the A Custom range produced a slightly more aggressive attack profile than traditional A Series cymbals would have delivered — a tonal choice that matched Greg Fidelman's punchy, modern production approach at HQ.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Lars Ulrich use on Hardwired...to Self-Destruct?**

A: Lars Ulrich recorded Hardwired...to Self-Destruct in 2016 using a DW Collector's Series Maple drum kit. The configuration included dual 22x18 inch bass drums, a 12 inch rack tom, and 14 and 16 inch floor toms. The DW Collector's Maple shells, with their VLT (Vertical Low Timbre) construction, delivered a warmer, fuller tone than the Tama Starclassic Birch he used on Death Magnetic — a fit for Greg Fidelman's punchier production at Metallica's HQ studio in San Rafael, California.

**Q: What snare did Lars Ulrich use on Hardwired?**

A: Lars Ulrich used a DW Collector's Series 14x6.5 inch maple snare on Hardwired — a departure from the Tama LU1465 steel-shell signature snare he used on Death Magnetic. The maple shell delivered a fuller body and slightly longer decay than the LU1465, with die-cast hoops keeping rimshots tight and consistent. The snare was tuned medium-high for attack and clarity, paired with an Evans Heavyweight batter head for durability under the album's aggressive backbeats.

**Q: What cymbals did Lars Ulrich use on Hardwired?**

A: Lars Ulrich used a mix of Zildjian A Custom and Z Custom cymbals on Hardwired. His setup included 14 inch A Custom hi-hats, 16 inch and 19 inch A Custom crashes, an 18 inch Z Custom crash for added projection, a 20 inch A Custom ride, and an 18 inch A Custom China. Zildjian has been Lars's endorsement since the Master of Puppets era, and the A Custom range became his established core during the Death Magnetic period, with Z Custom pieces added on Hardwired for heavier projection.

**Q: Why did Lars Ulrich switch to Tama Speed Cobra pedals for Hardwired?**

A: Lars Ulrich switched to Tama Speed Cobra HP910 pedals for the Hardwired era after using DW 9000 pedals during the Death Magnetic touring cycle. The Speed Cobra's long footboard and Cobra Coil spring system deliver fast rebound and effortless response — well suited to the sustained double-bass passages on tracks like "Spit Out the Bone" and "Atlas, Rise!." Used as two single pedals on a dual-kick rig, the Speed Cobras gave Lars consistent feel across both feet at the demanding uptempo arrangements Hardwired required.

**Q: Where was Hardwired...to Self-Destruct recorded?**

A: Hardwired...to Self-Destruct was recorded at HQ — Metallica's own studio facility in San Rafael, California. The album was self-produced by James Hetfield and Lars Ulrich alongside Greg Fidelman, who had worked extensively with the band since the Death Magnetic era as engineer and mixer. Recording at HQ gave the band complete environmental control and the kind of unlimited time for drum-sound refinement that conventional rented studio environments cannot match. The album debuted at #1 in 57 countries upon its November 18, 2016 release.

**Q: How does Hardwired compare to Death Magnetic for drum sound?**

A: Hardwired sits in a clear lineage with Death Magnetic but represents an evolution rather than a repeat. Where Death Magnetic used a Tama Starclassic Birch kit with a single bass drum + double pedal and a steel LU1465 snare under Rick Rubin's production, Hardwired moved to a DW Collector's Maple kit with dual 22 inch bass drums and a maple snare under Greg Fidelman's co-production. The Hardwired drum tone has more low-end weight and slightly fuller body than Death Magnetic, but maintains the same attack-forward, thrash-aligned character. Both albums avoid the over-compressed mastering issues that plagued Death Magnetic's release.
