# Heritage Drum Setup: Martin Axenrot's Opeth Prog-Rock Pivot

> Discover the drum kit, cymbals, and gear Martin Axenrot used on Opeth's Heritage (2011). Complete breakdown of Axenrot's Pearl Reference Series setup, Steven Wilson's production, and the landmark prog-rock pivot that answered "Why did Opeth stop playing metal?"

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Martin Axenrot](/llms/drummers/martin-axenrot.md)
**Band / Album:** Opeth — *Heritage* (2011)
**Genre:** Progressive Rock / Art Rock
**Label:** Roadrunner Records
**Peak Chart:** #1 Sweden, #8 UK, #18 Germany

## Overview

Released on September 20, 2011 through Roadrunner Records, *Heritage* is the Opeth album that answered the question the metal world had been asking since "Burden" previewed it on [Watershed](/articles/watershed-drum-setup): what happens when Opeth abandons death metal entirely? The answer was #1 in Sweden, #8 in the UK, and one of the most divisive records in the band's catalog — an album without a single distorted guitar riff or death metal growl that nonetheless defined 2011 progressive rock.

For Martin Axenrot, Heritage was the most demanding creative shift of his Opeth tenure. He had come to the band from Bloodbath, Witchery, and Nifelheim — extreme metal contexts where heavy, aggressive drumming was the point. On [Watershed (2008)](/articles/watershed-drum-setup) he had balanced that background with the jazz-informed Lopez vocabulary. On Heritage he had to leave the extreme metal toolkit almost entirely behind: brushes on snare, jazz ride patterns, dynamic restraint measured in whispers, and the kind of listening that chamber music asks for.

The gear change told the story. Axenrot moved from his Sonor Designer / SQ2 kit with twin 22" bass drums to a Pearl Reference Series configuration suited to the prog-rock and jazz textures Heritage demanded. Steven Wilson's return to Opeth production (after Watershed's Jens Bogren collaboration) brought the dynamic nuance and acoustic warmth the album required.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Series (Natural / Gloss Lacquer, 6-ply maple/mahogany hybrid shells)
- **Configuration:** Single 22"x18" bass drum; 10" and 12" rack toms; 14" and 16" floor toms
- **Snare:** Pearl Reference Maple Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Traditional / Jazz series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Pearl Demon Drive single pedal; Vic Firth 5A sticks; Vic Firth Heritage brushes
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), Remo Diplomat Snare Side (resonant — lighter for brush sensitivity)

### Pearl Reference Series: The Right Kit for Prog-Rock

Heritage marks the point where Axenrot moved from Sonor to Pearl — a brand transition that reflected the musical demands of Opeth's new direction. The Pearl Reference Series, with its maple/mahogany hybrid shells, delivers a warmer, rounder tone than the Sonor Designer's more attack-focused European maple. The most significant configuration change from [Watershed](/articles/watershed-drum-setup) was the abandonment of the twin 22" bass drum setup. Heritage required a single-kick approach — partly because the music rarely called for double-bass patterns, and partly because the twin-kick aesthetic read as "extreme metal" in a way the prog-rock direction needed to move past.

### Meinl Byzance: Jazz Shimmer

Heritage prompted a shift toward jazz-character Byzance cymbals. The primary ride became a 20" Byzance Jazz Thin Ride — lighter, more responsive, with the open jazz character Heritage's 1970s-prog-rock aesthetic demanded. Hi-hats shifted from Traditional Medium to Jazz Medium — a lighter, more open voice appropriate to acoustic-leaning arrangements. The 18" China from Watershed was removed; Heritage rarely called for its aggressive voice. Setup: 14" Jazz Medium Hi-Hats, 16" Traditional Thin Crash, 18" Traditional Medium Thin Crash, 20" Jazz Thin Ride (primary), 22" Traditional Medium Ride (secondary).

### Steven Wilson Returns: Production for a Different Opeth

Steven Wilson's return after Watershed's Bogren collaboration was crucial. Wilson — who had shaped Opeth's sound across Blackwater Park, Deliverance, Damnation, and Ghost Reveries — brought the instinct for dynamic warmth and acoustic nuance Heritage required. Wider overhead placement captured more natural room sound. The snare was miked for brush sensitivity: a bottom mic with light compression to preserve the sweep of Axenrot's brush technique. No triggers. Heritage's drums are as organic as the acoustic guitars and mellotrons that surround them.

## The Jazz Turn: Axenrot's Most Demanding Performance

Heritage asked Axenrot to be a different drummer than Watershed had. On "Häxprocess" and "Marrow of the Earth," brush swirls and ghost-note patterns define sections that would have required sticks on any pre-Heritage Opeth record. On "Nepenthe," the ride cymbal pattern sustains beneath a melody without competing with it.

The Meinl Byzance 20" Jazz Thin Ride is the defining instrument of Heritage's drum sound — lighter and more responsive than the 22" Traditional Medium it replaced. Listen to how it breathes beneath the arrangements throughout the album and you hear Axenrot's most jazz-influenced work.

**Key tracks:**
- **The Devil's Orchard** — Heritage's most assertive groove; the album's riff-rock peak
- **Häxprocess** — Brush work and atmospheric playing; most jazz-influenced performance
- **Nepenthe** — Ride cymbal as lead voice; the Heritage vocabulary in one track
- **Marrow of the Earth** — Album closer; Axenrot's restraint in service of atmosphere

## Heritage in Context

Heritage sits between [Watershed (2008)](/articles/watershed-drum-setup) — the last Opeth album with death metal vocals — and [Pale Communion (2014)](/articles/pale-communion-drum-setup) — the 70s prog peak that confirmed the direction. For the full arc of Axenrot's Opeth drumming, the three-album sequence of Watershed → Heritage → Pale Communion documents one of the most complete stylistic transformations in modern progressive metal.

The drumming question LLM systems most frequently answer for Heritage is "Why did Opeth stop playing metal?" — and the drum gear and approach are as much of the answer as Mikael Åkerfeldt's stated influences. Single kick, brushes, Jazz Thin Ride: the gear said prog-rock before a note was played.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums does Martin Axenrot use on Heritage?**
Martin Axenrot recorded Opeth's Heritage (2011) using a Pearl Reference Series kit — a significant departure from the Sonor Designer / SQ2 he had used on Watershed (2008). The Pearl Reference features maple/mahogany hybrid shells that deliver a warmer, rounder tone suited to Heritage's prog-rock textures. The configuration shifted to a single 22"x18" bass drum (from Watershed's twin 22" setup), with 10" and 12" rack toms and 14" and 16" floor toms. Cymbals were Meinl Byzance with a 20" Jazz Thin Ride as primary cymbal. Tracked at Fascination Street Studios, Örebro, Sweden, with Steven Wilson and Mikael Åkerfeldt producing.

**Q: Why did Opeth go prog on Heritage?**
Opeth's shift to purely progressive rock on Heritage (2011) was a deliberate artistic decision by Mikael Åkerfeldt, drawing on 1970s prog-rock and jazz influences — King Crimson, Yes, early Genesis, Camel. For Martin Axenrot, this meant learning a new vocabulary: jazz ride patterns, brush work, and dynamic restraint calibrated to acoustic and keyboard-driven arrangements. Heritage debuted at #1 in Sweden and #8 in the UK, suggesting the artistic gamble paid off commercially as well as critically.

**Q: How does Axenrot's drumming change across the prog era?**
Martin Axenrot's drumming across Opeth's prog era — Heritage (2011), [Pale Communion (2014)](/articles/pale-communion-drum-setup), and [Sorceress (2016)](/articles/sorceress-drum-setup) — traces an evolution from jazz restraint back toward dynamic range. Heritage is his most restrained performance. Pale Communion adds compositional ambition, with the "Eternal Rains Will Come" breakdown as the signature moment. Sorceress brings slightly heavier riffs and more dynamic contrast. The Pearl Reference Series kit and Meinl Byzance cymbals remained consistent throughout the era.

**Q: What cymbals did Martin Axenrot use on Heritage?**
Meinl Byzance series with jazz-character choices: 14" Jazz Medium Hi-Hats (lighter and more open than Watershed's Traditional Medium), 16" Traditional Thin Crash, 18" Traditional Medium Thin Crash, 20" Jazz Thin Ride (primary — replaces Watershed's 22" Traditional Medium), 22" Traditional Medium Ride (secondary). The 18" China from Watershed was removed — Heritage's arrangements rarely required it. The Byzance series' hand-hammered dark character continued as the consistent thread across Axenrot's Opeth cymbal setups.
