# Inferno Drum Setup: Mikkey Dee's Gear on Motörhead's 2004 Album

> Discover the exact drum kit, cymbals, and gear Mikkey Dee used on Motörhead's Inferno (2004) — the album that began the band's decade-plus partnership with producer Cameron Webb. Complete breakdown of the Tama kit, Paiste cymbals, and the Steve Vai guest appearance.

## Overview

Released on June 22, 2004, *Inferno* marked Motörhead's first album with producer Cameron Webb — a collaboration that would define the band's sound for the remainder of its 40-year career, running all the way through *Bad Magic* in 2015. Recorded primarily at NRG Studios in North Hollywood with additional sessions at Paramount Recording Studios in Hollywood and Maple Sound Studios in Costa Mesa, *Inferno* found Motörhead sharpening its attack after the comparatively straightforward *Hammered* (2002).

By 2004, Mikkey Dee had been Motörhead's drummer for twelve years, and his rhythmic partnership with Lemmy Kilmister and Phil Campbell had settled into a lethal efficiency. *Inferno* runs through twelve tracks in under 49 minutes, opening with the sprint of "Terminal Show" and closing with the acoustic, country-blues detour "Whorehouse Blues" — a rare moment of restraint that let Dee show off brushwork and dynamic control rather than sheer force. The album also features a guest lead guitar contribution from Steve Vai, a detail that surprised longtime fans and underscored how far Motörhead's reputation had spread across the rock world.

Commercially, *Inferno* charted at #10 in Germany, #17 in Finland, and #95 in the UK — modest by mainstream standards but consistent with Motörhead's reliable, if unspectacular, chart performance throughout the 2000s. What the album lacked in chart impact it made up for in songwriting discipline: Cameron Webb pushed the band toward tighter arrangements and a more modern, punchy drum sound than the somewhat murkier production of earlier 2000s Motörhead records.

Mikkey Dee's playing on *Inferno* still carried the Tama-era foundation established a decade earlier on *Bastards* (1993), even as the band's studio process modernized around him. Tracks like "Killers" and "In the Black" show Dee at his most economical — devastating single-stroke patterns married to a kick technique built for speed without sacrificing the pocket. This article breaks down the drum kit, cymbals, and hardware behind Motörhead's first Cameron Webb-produced record.

- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension for maximum crack and cut through a dense guitar mix

### Tama Power, Late-Era: Dee's Kit Before the Pearl Switch

On *Inferno*, Mikkey Dee was still playing Tama drums — the brand he'd relied on since joining Motörhead in 1992 — though by 2004 his kit had evolved from the Artstar II/Swingstar series of the *Bastards* era to the more refined Starclassic line. The core configuration stayed close to what had worked for over a decade: a focused 22" bass drum, three rack toms, and dual floor toms.

The Starclassic's maple/birch hybrid shell construction gave Dee's drums a punchier, more contemporary attack than the pure maple Artstar II kit he used on *Bastards*, better suited to Cameron Webb's modern production approach. Where earlier Motörhead records sometimes let the drums blur into the wall of guitar, Webb's mix on *Inferno* gave each drum definition — a change Dee's tighter, more consistent tuning helped make possible.

Dee's kick technique on *Inferno* remained rooted in precision over raw speed. Tracks like "Killers" demand a driving, high-tempo pulse, and Dee delivers it with the same placement-first philosophy that defined his *Bastards* performance — every stroke landing exactly where the song needs it, never rushing ahead of Lemmy's bass.

This would prove to be one of Dee's final studio albums on Tama before his transition to Pearl drums took hold around the *Kiss of Death* sessions two years later — making *Inferno* a quiet bridge between two distinct chapters of his Motörhead gear history.

### Steel and Speed: The Snare Behind Inferno's Attack

Mikkey Dee's snare on *Inferno* continued the steel-shell preference he'd carried since his King Diamond days — bright, cutting, and built to slice through Motörhead's dense guitar-and-bass wall. The Starclassic steel snare offered the same fundamental voice as his earlier Artstar II snare from the *Bastards* sessions, refined with more consistent hardware.

At 14" x 6.5", the dimensions matched what Dee had used for over a decade — a testament to how dialed-in his core sound had become by 2004. Cameron Webb's production gave the snare a tighter, more compressed punch in the mix than the somewhat looser sound of Motörhead's late-1990s records, emphasizing the crack over the ring.

On "In the Black" and "Fight," the snare sits high and aggressive, driving the tempo with almost no headroom for the drum to breathe — exactly the effect Webb was after for an album meant to reassert Motörhead's aggression after the more mid-paced *Hammered*. Dee's tuning stayed on the higher side of medium, prioritizing crack and cut over sustain.

### Paiste 2002: Two Decades of Continuity

Mikkey Dee has been a Paiste artist since April 1987, and by *Inferno* that relationship was nearly two decades old — the 2002 series remained his primary cymbal choice, the same alloy family he'd used on *Bastards* in 1993. The bright, cutting CuSn8 bronze construction of the 2002 line continued to give Dee the projection he needed to be heard over Lemmy's mid-heavy bass and Phil Campbell's dense guitar tone.

The switch to 15" Sound Edge hi-hats — with their serrated bottom cymbal — gave Dee a tighter, quicker chick response for the album's faster material, an evolution from the plainer New Beat-style hi-hats of his earlier years. On a track like "Terminal Show," that tighter hi-hat control is audible in the rapid eighth-note patterns driving the verses.

The 2002 series' reputation as a metal and hard rock workhorse — the same cymbals Lars Ulrich used on *Kill 'Em All* and Dave Lombardo used on *Reign in Blood* — made it a natural fit for an album built to reassert Motörhead's aggression. Dee's setup stayed consistent across the *Inferno* sessions and tour: bright, cutting, and durable enough for the punishing schedule Motörhead kept even twelve years into his tenure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Mikkey Dee use on Inferno?**

A: On *Inferno* (2004), Mikkey Dee played a Tama Starclassic kit — an evolution of the Artstar II/Swingstar setup he used on [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup) over a decade earlier. The configuration centered on a 22" bass drum, three rack toms (10", 12", 13"), and dual floor toms (16", 18"), built with maple/birch hybrid shells for a punchier, more modern attack suited to producer Cameron Webb's production style. *Inferno* was one of Dee's final Motörhead albums on Tama before he transitioned to Pearl drums around the [*Kiss of Death*](/articles/kiss-of-death-drum-setup) sessions in 2006. See the [Mikkey Dee drummer profile](/drummer/mikkey-dee) for his complete gear evolution.

**Q: Who produced Inferno and why does it matter for Motörhead's sound?**

A: *Inferno* was produced by Cameron Webb, marking the first of what became a career-defining partnership that lasted through Motörhead's final album, [*Bad Magic*](/articles/bad-magic-drum-setup), in 2015. Webb pushed the band toward tighter arrangements and a more modern, defined drum sound than earlier 2000s Motörhead records like *Hammered* (2002). Recorded across NRG Studios, Paramount Recording Studios, and Maple Sound Studios in the Los Angeles area, *Inferno* set the sonic template Webb and the band would refine across seven more studio albums.

**Q: What cymbals did Mikkey Dee use on Inferno?**

A: Mikkey Dee used Paiste 2002 series cymbals on *Inferno* — the same alloy family he'd played since becoming a Paiste artist in April 1987 and used on [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup) in 1993. His setup included 15" Sound Edge hi-hats, 18" and 20" crashes, a 22" ride, and an 18" China for trash accents. Paiste's bright, cutting CuSn8 bronze construction gave Dee the projection needed to cut through Motörhead's dense guitar-and-bass mix on tracks like "Killers" and "Terminal Show."

**Q: Did Steve Vai really play on a Motörhead album?**

A: Yes — *Inferno* (2004) features a guest lead guitar appearance from Steve Vai, one of several notable guest musicians to appear on Motörhead records during the Mikkey Dee era. The collaboration reflected Motörhead's growing reputation and reach across the rock world by the mid-2000s, twelve years into Dee's tenure behind the kit.
