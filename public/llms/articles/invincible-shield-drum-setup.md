# Judas Priest "Invincible Shield" Drum Setup (Scott Travis, 2024)

> Complete breakdown of Scott Travis's drum setup on Judas Priest's Invincible Shield (2024) — the band's highest-charting UK album ever (#2), Grammy-nominated for "Crown of Horns," and Travis's first Priest album in six years after Firepower. Covers his DW Collector's Series kit, Zildjian A Custom cymbals, and the distributed, city-by-city recording process behind the album.

## Overview

Released on March 8, 2024 through Epic Records (Columbia in Japan two days earlier), *Invincible Shield* is Judas Priest's nineteenth studio album and Scott Travis's first Priest studio record since 2018's *Firepower*. It debuted at #2 on the UK Albums Chart — the highest chart position Judas Priest has ever reached in their home country — and opened at #18 on the Billboard 200 while topping Billboard's Top Hard Rock Albums chart. The album's centerpiece single, "Crown of Horns," earned Judas Priest a nomination for the Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance in 2025, extending a run of Grammy recognition that now spans *Redeemer of Souls* (2014), *Firepower* (2018), and *Invincible Shield* (2024).

For a band fifty-plus years into its existence, and for a drummer thirty-five years into his tenure, *Invincible Shield* is a remarkable outlier: not a victory lap, but the highest-charting album of the band's career. Scott Travis — who joined Priest in 1989 and redefined the band's drumming vocabulary on *Painkiller* the following year — remains the connective tissue across every era this arc represents, from the speed-metal explosion of 1990 through the reunion record *Angel of Retribution* (2005) to this 2024 high-water mark.

*Invincible Shield* was produced primarily by Andy Sneap, continuing the partnership established on *Firepower*, with Tom Allom returning to co-produce the album's closing two tracks, "Sons of Thunder" and "Giants in the Sky." The record's production story is unusual for a modern metal album: rather than tracking together in a single studio, the band worked in near-total geographic isolation. Travis recorded his drum parts alongside guitarist Richie Faulkner in Nashville, Tennessee. Rob Halford tracked vocals separately in Phoenix, Arizona. Bassist Ian Hill contributed his parts while on tour in Europe. Sneap then assembled the pieces and re-amped guitar parts at his own Backstage Studios in England. The album that resulted sounds cohesive and heavy despite never having all four core members in the same room during tracking — a testament to Sneap's production and to how tightly Travis's foundational drum tracks locked the arrangements together for everyone recording around them.

Gear-wise, *Invincible Shield* documents Travis in his DW Collector's Series era, paired with Zildjian A Custom cymbals — a shift from the Tama Starclassic Maple and Sabian HHX rig he used on *Firepower* six years earlier. This article covers that setup in full, how it compares to Travis's prior Priest gear, and what made his drumming on this era-defining record land as hard as it did.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** DW DW Collector's Series (Piano Black lacquer finish)
- **Snare:** DW DW Collector's Series Bell Brass, 14" x 6"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian — Zildjian A Custom

### The DW Collector's Series Era

By the *Invincible Shield* era, Scott Travis had moved from the Tama Starclassic Maple kit he used on *Firepower* (2018) into DW's flagship Collector's Series — the same brand he had briefly sampled on the second leg of the *Nostradamus* tour years earlier, now his primary recording and touring kit. DW's Collector's Series is built around the company's Specialty/Standard Shell Composite (SSC) approach, blending maple and mahogany plies to deliver a fuller, warmer low end than the Tama maple shells while keeping the attack controlled enough to cut through Andy Sneap's dense, modern guitar production.

The two independent 22"x18" bass drums carry over directly from the *Firepower* configuration — Travis has run two separate kick drums, rather than a single drum with a connected double pedal, since the *Painkiller* era. The DW shells' added low-mid warmth suits *Invincible Shield*'s more anthemic, riff-driven material; tracks like the title track and "Panic Attack" benefit from bass drums that carry weight without losing the machine-gun clarity Travis's speed passages require.

The compact four-tom layout (10"/12" rack, 14"/16" floor) is unchanged from every Travis Priest kit since *Angel of Retribution* (2005) — a deliberate, mature choice that favors fluency and architectural fills over a sprawling tom array. The Piano Black finish keeps the visual continuity with every prior Travis-era Priest kit dating back to the *Painkiller* Tama Artstar II.

### DW Collector's Series Bell Brass

Travis's snare for *Invincible Shield* is a DW Collector's Series Bell Brass, 14"x6" — matching the brass-shell preference he has favored since his mid-2000s Pearl Reference Brass era on *Angel of Retribution* and carrying through the Tama Starphonic Brass he used on *Firepower*. Bell brass shells produce a sharper, more focused crack than standard brass, with a pronounced high-frequency lift that helps the drum sit forward in Andy Sneap's tight, modern mix.

The 14"x6" depth mirrors the Starphonic Brass dimensions from *Firepower*, keeping Travis's snare response consistent across the gear transition. Die-cast hoops preserve the rim-shot clarity he relies on for the album's frequent backbeat accents, particularly on "Panic Attack" and "Devil in Disguise," where the snare needs to read clearly against Faulkner and Tipton's layered guitar tracks.

Travis continues tuning medium-high with tight snare wires — the same fundamental approach documented on every Priest album since *Painkiller* — prioritizing cut and articulation over sustained ring.

### Zildjian A Custom: A New Cymbal Voice for Priest

*Invincible Shield* marks Scott Travis's move to Zildjian's A Custom line, replacing the Sabian HHX setup that carried him through *Firepower*. A Custom cymbals are Zildjian's brilliant-finished, brighter-voiced line within the A family — engineered for fast response and pronounced high-frequency cut, which suits Andy Sneap's dry, upfront modern production approach.

The 14" A Custom Hi-Hats give Travis a tighter, more immediate chick than the darker Sabian HHX Stage Hats he used on *Firepower*, useful for the driving hi-hat patterns that carry the verses of "Panic Attack" and "The Serpent and the King." The crash range — 16" through 19" — favors quick attack and fast decay, letting Travis punctuate Sneap's tightly arranged riffs without cymbal wash bleeding into the next phrase.

The 20" A Custom Ride anchors the album's more mid-tempo, groove-driven tracks like "Trial by Fire," delivering clear stick definition and a bell that cuts for accents without overwhelming Ian Hill's bass lines. The 18" A Custom China supplies the trashy accent voice Travis has used across every Priest era, from the Paiste Novo China of *Painkiller* through the Sabian HHX Chinese of *Firepower* to this Zildjian equivalent.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit does Scott Travis use on Invincible Shield?**

A: On Judas Priest's Invincible Shield (2024), Scott Travis plays a DW Collector's Series kit in Piano Black finish — two 22"x18" bass drums, 10"x8" and 12"x9" rack toms, and 14"x14" and 16"x16" floor toms, built with DW's maple/mahogany SSC hybrid shells. His snare is a DW Collector's Series Bell Brass at 14"x6" with die-cast hoops. This is a shift from the Tama Starclassic Maple kit he used on Firepower (2018) — his most significant drum brand change since moving from Tama to Pearl in the mid-2000s.

**Q: Was Invincible Shield Grammy-nominated?**

A: Yes. Invincible Shield's track "Crown of Horns" was nominated for the Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance in 2025. It's the third consecutive Judas Priest studio album to earn Grammy recognition, following Redeemer of Souls (2014, "Halls of Valhalla") and Firepower (2018, "Lightning Strike") — both also nominated for Best Metal Performance. The album itself debuted at #2 on the UK Albums Chart, the highest position in the band's history, and reached #18 on the Billboard 200 while topping Billboard's Top Hard Rock Albums chart.

**Q: What cymbals does Scott Travis use in 2024?**

A: For Invincible Shield, Scott Travis uses Zildjian's A Custom line — a shift from the Sabian HHX series that carried him through Firepower (2018). His setup includes 14" A Custom Hi-Hats, A Custom Crashes from 16" to 19", a 20" A Custom Ride, and an 18" A Custom China. A Custom cymbals are brighter and faster-responding than the darker Sabian HHX line, matching producer Andy Sneap's upfront, modern mix approach on the album.

**Q: How was Invincible Shield recorded, and how did that affect Scott Travis's drumming?**

A: Invincible Shield was recorded with the band members in different locations rather than together in one studio — Scott Travis and guitarist Richie Faulkner tracked in Nashville, Tennessee; Rob Halford recorded vocals in Phoenix, Arizona; and bassist Ian Hill contributed his parts while on tour in Europe. Producer Andy Sneap assembled the pieces and re-amped guitars at his own Backstage Studios in England. Because Travis's drum tracks were recorded early in this process, they functioned as the timing and arrangement reference for the performances layered on afterward — his session consistency was essential to making the distributed recording approach sound cohesive on the finished album.
