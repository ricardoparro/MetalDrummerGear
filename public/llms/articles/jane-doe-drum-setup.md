# Converge Jane Doe Drum Setup: Ben Koller's 2001 Mathcore Gear Breakdown

**Genre:** Mathcore / Metalcore / Hardcore

## Overview

Released in 2001 on Equal Vision Records, Converge's "Jane Doe" is widely considered one of the greatest hardcore albums ever recorded — and Ben Koller's drumming is a central reason why. Recorded at Godcity Studio in Salem, Massachusetts, and produced by guitarist Kurt Ballou, "Jane Doe" marked a quantum leap for Converge as a band and for Koller as a drummer. He was 22 years old when these sessions took place, and the performance he delivered would redefine what was physically and musically possible in hardcore drumming.

"Jane Doe" exists at the intersection of hardcore, post-hardcore, and mathcore — a collision zone where melody and chaos coexist in constant tension. Koller's drumming mirrors this duality perfectly: technically precise blast beats give way to melody-serving tom patterns; driving eighth-note feels explode into polyrhythmic chaos without warning; passages of near-silence are shattered by full-kit attacks. The album runs 50 minutes without a wasted moment, and Koller is present and purposeful throughout every second.

The recording process at Godcity Studio was built around capturing real performances. Ballou's approach — close-miked drums with an emphasis on natural acoustic character and minimal processing — put Koller's raw playing front and center. What you hear on "Jane Doe" is a young drummer performing at the absolute edge of his capabilities, with the technique and musicality to make extreme difficulty sound inevitable. The result set a benchmark for mathcore and metalcore drumming that still stands over two decades later.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama touring kit (professional-grade, pre-Starclassic era) — 22" bass drum, 10"/12" rack toms, 16" floor tom
- **Snare:** Tama steel or wood snare, 14" x 5.5"–6.5", tuned bright and tight
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — 14" hi-hats, 16" and 18" crashes, 20" ride, 18" China
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Tama Iron Cobra (single pedal); Vic Firth 5B sticks
- **Heads:** Remo or Evans coated batter heads; controlled attack bass drum batter
- **Snare tuning:** Bright, high tension for articulation and rimshot authority at extreme tempos

### Ben's 2001 Tama Kit: Raw Hardcore Foundation

At the time of "Jane Doe," Ben Koller was in the early phase of his career with Converge, using a Tama touring kit appropriate for a band at Converge's level in 2001 — not yet the Starclassic Performer B/B setup he would refine over the following decade, but a professional instrument well suited to both the Godcity recording environment and the aggressive touring lifestyle Converge maintained.

Koller's kit configuration for "Jane Doe" was compact and purposeful. The combination of rack toms and a single floor tom reflects the hardcore approach: fewer drums, more impact per piece, and a setup small enough to transport efficiently across the endless touring cycle. Mathcore demands creative use of a minimal palette — the drum fills on "Jane Doe" achieve their drama not through sheer tom count but through the velocity, timing, and musical intention Koller brings to each stroke.

Kurt Ballou's Godcity Studio environment rewarded this compact setup. The close-mic approach — positioning microphones inches from each shell — meant that the tonal character of each drum contributed directly to the album's sound. Koller's ability to manipulate dynamics within a small kit, from feather-light ghost notes to full-force rimshots, gave Ballou exceptional sonic material to work with.

The bass drum setup for "Jane Doe" reflects the album's demands: single kick configuration, driven with the heel-up technique that Koller has used throughout his career, capable of delivering both the sustained blast beat patterns of the album's most aggressive moments and the syncopated groove-oriented kick patterns that define tracks like "Concubine" and "Heaven in Her Arms."

### The Snare: Crack and Authority Through the Chaos

The snare sound on "Jane Doe" is one of the defining elements of the record. In Kurt Ballou's production mix, the snare sits directly in the center of the sonic picture — bright, cutting, and uncompromising. Through passages of dense, down-tuned guitar chaos, Koller's snare remains the rhythmic anchor, providing clarity and definition when everything else is dissolving into noise.

Koller tuned his snare bright and taut for the "Jane Doe" sessions. At the tempos the album demands — many passages exceed 200 BPM — the head tension must be high enough to ensure clean articulation on rapid strokes without the pitch collapsing between hits. This choice also supports the rimshot work that defines Koller's backbeat approach: each rimshot cuts through the mix without EQ assistance.

Ballou's close-mic placement (typically a Shure SM57 on the batter side with a secondary mic underneath) captured the full character of Koller's stroke: the initial transient crack, the body of the shell resonance, and the quick, controlled decay that keeps the drum from cluttering fast passages. For hardcore and mathcore production, snare decay control is critical — a snare that rings too long will wash over the guitar work in dense sections; a snare tuned too dead loses the authority the music demands. Koller's balance threads this needle perfectly throughout the record.

### Cymbal Setup: Functional Aggression

Ben Koller's cymbal setup for the "Jane Doe" sessions was built around functional aggression: cymbals chosen for their ability to project and speak clearly through Converge's dense, heavily distorted guitar arrangements. In 2001, Koller's setup was simpler than the K Custom/A Custom hybrid configuration he would develop over the following decade.

The hi-hat work on "Jane Doe" is among the most demanding on any hardcore record of the era. Koller drives many of the album's fastest passages with relentless 16th-note patterns while executing complex bass drum and snare work simultaneously — a coordination feat that requires both a hi-hat cymbal with consistent response at high speeds and significant physical conditioning.

The crash cymbals in standard A Series configuration need to speak immediately and loudly. The 16" and 18" pairing gives Koller two voices for different dynamic contexts: the 16" for quick, staccato accents between riffs, the 18" for the big section-ending explosions. The China cymbal adds the layer of sonic violence that distinguishes mathcore from more restrained subgenres — used for accent work at the peak intensity moments of "Jane Doe," contributing to the sense of controlled chaos that defines Converge's sound.

## FAQ

**What drum kit did Ben Koller use on Jane Doe?**
Ben Koller recorded Converge's Jane Doe in 2001 using a Tama touring kit — professional-grade drums appropriate for Converge's level at the time. The configuration was compact: a 22 inch bass drum, rack toms in 10 and 12 inch sizes, and a 16 inch floor tom. The sessions took place at Godcity Studio in Salem, Massachusetts, with Kurt Ballou producing and recording using a close-mic philosophy that placed Koller's natural drum sound front and center in the mix. No drum triggers or samples were used.

**Who produced Jane Doe and where was it recorded?**
Jane Doe was produced by Kurt Ballou — Converge's guitarist and longtime recording engineer — at Godcity Studio in Salem, Massachusetts. Ballou's recording approach emphasizes natural acoustic drum sounds with close miking and minimal processing. This philosophy put the full character of Koller's playing directly into the record: every ghost note, every rimshot crack, every blast beat is captured with stark immediacy.

**Why is Jane Doe considered a landmark for hardcore drumming?**
Jane Doe is considered a landmark for hardcore drumming because Ben Koller demonstrated that extreme technical demands — blast beats, polyrhythmic patterns, rapid tempo shifts — could serve musical expression rather than existing as displays of technique. The album operates at the intersection of chaos and precision: Koller's drumming is physically demanding at a level that rivals death metal, yet every passage is calibrated to serve Converge's songs. The combination of mathcore complexity, hardcore aggression, and genuine musicality established a template that influenced a generation of extreme music drummers.
