## Overview

Jocke Wallgren has been the drummer for Amon Amarth since 2013, joining the Swedish melodic death metal institution at a critical point in their career and delivering some of the band's most consistently powerful studio and live performances. A native of Sweden with roots in the Gothenburg metal scene, Wallgren brought technical precision and physical authority to a drum chair that demands both — Amon Amarth's anthemic, riff-driven arrangements run on the drummer's ability to lock the low-end double bass pulse to Johan Hegg's vocal delivery and Olavi Mikkeli and Johan Söderberg's interlocking guitar riffs.

His Amon Amarth tenure spans albums including Deceiver of the Gods (2013), Jomsviking (2016), Berserker (2019), and The Great Heathen Army (2022) — four records across which Wallgren established himself as a foundational part of the band's live and studio sound. The combination of driving double bass work, powerful snare backbeats, and dynamic mid-section restraint that characterized classic Amon Amarth drumming from the Twilight of the Thunder God era carries through each of those records in Wallgren's performances.

His primary setup is built around Tama Starclassic Performer drums, Meinl Byzance cymbals, and DW 9002 double bass pedals — a combination tuned for the melodic death metal context: power and presence without sacrificing the rhythmic clarity that Amon Amarth's anthemic arrangements require. This article covers every component of that setup and explains how the gear choices support the specific demands of viking metal drumming at elite touring level.

### Tama Starclassic Performer: Viking Power on Stage and in the Studio

Jocke Wallgren's Tama Starclassic Performer B/B kit is purpose-built for what Amon Amarth needs from a drum setup: physical impact at arena volumes, rhythmic clarity over dense guitar arrangements, and the endurance to sustain a full headline set across a world tour without sonic degradation.

The Starclassic Performer B/B uses Tama's birch/bubinga hybrid shell construction. Birch provides the sharp attack transient that articulates each stroke clearly in the dense Amon Amarth sonic environment — where two guitarists, a bassist, and Johan Hegg's powerful baritone fill the low-mid frequency range, every drum stroke needs to announce itself cleanly or disappear into the mix. The bubinga inner ply adds warmth and body that prevents the kit from sounding sterile under heavy amplification, preserving the organic character that melodic death metal's dynamic range requires.

The double bass drum configuration is central to Wallgren's setup. Two 22" x 18" kick drums generate the sub-bass mass that powers Amon Amarth's galloping rhythmic figures — patterns that appear throughout the band's catalogue from "Twilight of the Thunder God" to "Raven's Flight." The 22" diameter delivers the low-frequency authority the band's sound demands; the 18" depth provides enough resonant chamber to generate impact felt at the back of large venues. At the melodic death metal tempos Wallgren sustains across a full Amon Amarth live set, the kick drums need the mass to project without triggering — the Starclassic Performer configuration handles both demands.

The four-tom arrangement (10", 12" rack toms; 16", 18" floor toms) covers the full orchestral range of Amon Amarth's arrangements. Melodic death metal drumming employs toms not merely as transitional devices but as compositional elements — the epic, descending tom fills that punctuate album climaxes and build tension across long instrumental sections require the full range Wallgren's four-tom spread provides.

### Snare: The Backbeat That Drives Viking Metal Anthems

In the Amon Amarth context, the snare drum must fulfill two distinct functions: explosive, cutting backbeats on the downbeats of anthemic verse-chorus structures, and controlled, precise strokes in the intricate fills that connect structural sections of the band's epic-length compositions.

Jocke Wallgren's snare setup leans toward bell brass or steel construction for the brightness and projection needed in a mix where Johan Söderberg and Olavi Mikkeli's guitars saturate the mid-low frequency range. A bright, assertive snare cuts through that environment without requiring the drum to be mixed overbearingly loud in relation to the rest of the kit — the metal's natural resonance provides presence through frequency separation rather than raw volume.

The 6.5" depth gives the snare sufficient body to feel full and substantial in large rooms, while the 14" diameter keeps the fundamental pitch high enough to articulate clearly over the bass-heavy foundation the double kick and guitars create. In the live Amon Amarth mix — particularly at the festival headline slots the band regularly occupies — this snare character ensures the backbeat registers across every dynamic range from quiet verse passages to full-power chorus explosions.

The snare is also critical for Wallgren's ghost note work. Amon Amarth's arrangements often employ syncopated rhythmic patterns between the kick and snare that require the snare to produce audible ghost strokes at low dynamics — the bell brass shell responds to light touch with sufficient tone to register in the mix, supporting the polyrhythmic elements that appear in songs like "Destroyer of the Universe" and the groove-driven passages of Jomsviking.

### Meinl Byzance: Dark, Complex, Viking Metal Voice

Jocke Wallgren's Meinl Byzance cymbal setup reflects a deliberate aesthetic choice: the Byzance line's dark, warm, complex tonal character suits the epic, melodic sweep of Amon Amarth's music in ways that brighter, more aggressive cymbal voices do not.

The Byzance series is hand-hammered B20 bronze, a process that produces instruments with layered, harmonically rich sound profiles compared to machine-hammered alternatives. In a genre where the temptation toward harsh, dry cymbal sounds is constant, the Byzance voice adds warmth and depth that supports Amon Amarth's melodic identity — the band's arrangements are built on dark melody and epic scope, not sonic brutality alone, and the cymbals reinforce that character.

The hi-hat selection is particularly important. Amon Amarth's mid-tempo double-bass grooves — the characteristic gallop that drives tracks like "Twilight of the Thunder God" and "Father of the Wolf" — place the hi-hat on upbeats and offbeats that define the rhythmic feel of each riff. The Byzance Traditional hi-hats provide the warmth and definition to make that rhythmic pulse felt rather than merely heard, adding texture to patterns that would sound mechanical with drier, brighter instruments.

The crash configuration covers Amon Amarth's full dynamic range. The 16" medium thin crash responds quickly for accent work within melodic passages; the 18" medium crash delivers the longer sustain and fuller character appropriate for climactic structural moments. The china cymbal is the outlier — Wallgren deploys it selectively at moments of maximum intensity, the one point where raw aggression rather than dark melody is the dominant character. Its use in the set is sparing but effective, reserved for passages where the band's melodic death metal aggression peaks.
