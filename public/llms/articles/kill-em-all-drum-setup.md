# Kill 'Em All Drum Setup: Lars Ulrich's 1983 Budget Kit That Launched Thrash Metal

> Discover the exact drum kit Lars Ulrich used to record Metallica's Kill 'Em All in 1983. A budget Tama Imperial Star, mixed cymbals, no endorsements — the raw gear behind thrash metal's birth.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Lars Ulrich](/llms/drummers/lars-ulrich.md)
**Band / Album:** Metallica — *Kill 'Em All* (1983)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal

## Overview

Released on July 25, 1983 on Megaforce Records, Metallica's debut album "Kill 'Em All" didn't just introduce a new band — it detonated a genre. Thrash metal as a distinct form crystallized here, in a budget studio session in Rochester, New York, powered by four young men playing with total conviction and zero commercial polish.

Lars Ulrich recorded the album in April 1983 at Music America Studios with producer Paul Curcio. The sessions operated on an almost nonexistent budget. There were no endorsement deals, no custom hardware, no triggers. Lars showed up with what he had: a bare-bones Tama Imperial Star touring kit — the entry-level workhorse of working drummers on the cheap touring circuit.

The drum sound on Kill 'Em All is inseparable from this hardware context. Raw, slightly roomy, hitting like a garage rehearsal amplified — it is the sound of a drummer with something to prove and no resources to hide behind. Every pound and crash came directly from the instrument without studio enhancement.

This gear story is fundamentally different from any later Metallica album. By Ride the Lightning (1984) Lars had access to better rooms and more time. By Master of Puppets (1986) he was running vintage Camco maple with a Ludwig Supraphonic snare in a world-class Copenhagen studio. Kill 'Em All belongs to a different era — the era before any of that existed.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Imperial Star 5-piece (Philippine mahogany shells, natural finish)
- **Snare:** Tama Imperial Star wood snare, 14" x 5.5"
- **Cymbals:** Mixed Paiste 2002 and Zildjian A (no formal endorsement)
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Ludwig Speed King single bass drum pedal; Tama standard hardware
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter snare), Remo Ambassador Clear (toms and kick)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium tension — warm and punchy, not the high-tension crack of later albums

### Lars's 1983 Rig: The Tama Imperial Star

The Tama Imperial Star was the entry-level lineup of Tama's early 1980s catalog — a step below the Superstar and Artstar II series Lars would later use. Built with Philippine mahogany (lauan) shells rather than the birch Lars favored from 1988 onward, the Imperial Star produced a warmer, thicker tone that emphasized body over attack.

The kit configuration was a compact 5-piece: 22-inch bass drum, 12 and 13 inch rack toms, one 16-inch floor tom. This minimal layout mirrors the Master of Puppets setup Lars used three years later, but the shell material and construction quality differed significantly. The mahogany shells produced the slightly muffled, thick low-end thud audible throughout the Kill 'Em All recording.

There were no endorsement deals in 1983 — Lars was 19 years old and recording his first album. The Imperial Star was a practical touring kit, not a studio showpiece.

### The Raw Crack: Tama Wood Snare

Lars used a matched Tama wood snare from the Imperial Star lineup — a 14" x 5.5" drum that contributed the warm, organic crack audible on the record. Unlike the Ludwig Supraphonic aluminum snare Lars deployed on Master of Puppets, the wood shell here produced a rounder, less penetrating sound that blends into the mix rather than cutting above it.

The 5.5" depth sits between a piccolo and a power snare. Tuned to medium tension with a coated head, it delivers punching accent sounds behind tracks like "The Four Horsemen," "Phantom Lord," and "Jump in the Fire." The warmer character suited Paul Curcio's raw production approach — surgical snare clarity wasn't the goal.

### Mixed Arsenal: Paiste 2002 and Zildjian A

Lars's cymbal setup on Kill 'Em All was a pragmatic combination of Paiste 2002 and Zildjian A, reflecting the reality of a touring drummer with no endorsements in 1983. Rather than a curated single-brand setup, Lars used whatever quality cymbals he had accumulated through early touring.

The Paiste 2002 series was widely popular in the early 1980s for its bright, cutting tone and durability. The 2002 Sound Edge hi-hats produced the crisp attack and clear stick definition needed for fast thrash patterns. A 2002 crash and China cymbal completed the aggressive high-frequency arsenal.

The Zildjian A cymbals in the mix — a medium crash and 20-inch ride — provided a slightly warmer contrast for musical moments. This mixed-brand approach would give way to an all-Zildjian setup by Master of Puppets as Lars's professional relationships developed.

### Ludwig Speed King: Single Pedal, Full Speed

Lars used a Ludwig Speed King bass drum pedal throughout the Kill 'Em All sessions — a single pedal that powered tracks like "Whiplash" at 190-200 BPM without a double pedal or double bass drum. The Speed King's cam-driven mechanism delivered snappy, direct response suited to aggressive single-pedal technique.

This single-pedal approach continued through the Camco era. Lars didn't switch to a double pedal setup until ...And Justice for All (1988), where the Tama Camco HP35 double pedal enabled the relentless double-bass work on "Blackened" and "One."

## Key Facts

- Recorded April 1983 at Music America Studios, Rochester, NY — Metallica's first professional studio
- Producer Paul Curcio — the only Metallica album not self-produced or recorded with Flemming Rasmussen
- Tama Imperial Star 5-piece: Tama's budget entry-level lineup (below Superstar and Artstar II)
- Philippine mahogany shells — warmer and thicker-sounding than the birch Lars later favored
- Mixed Paiste 2002 and Zildjian A cymbals — no formal endorsement from either brand
- Ludwig Speed King single bass drum pedal — no double pedal, no double bass drum
- Zero triggers — completely acoustic recording throughout
- Approximately two-week session on a minimal budget
- Direct contrast to Master of Puppets (1986): vintage Camco maple + Ludwig Supraphonic in Copenhagen
- Direct contrast to ...And Justice for All (1988): Tama Artstar II birch + Tama steel snare in Los Angeles
- Estimated kit value: $300-500 (1983) / $800-1,500 (vintage today)
- Estimated cymbal value: $800-1,200 total (1983)
