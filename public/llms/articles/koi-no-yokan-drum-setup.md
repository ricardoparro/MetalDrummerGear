## Overview

Released on November 13, 2012, *Koi No Yokan* is widely regarded as the finest album Deftones have made since *White Pony* — and by some measures, their definitive work. The Japanese phrase translates roughly to "premonition of love," a foreknowledge that something significant is coming before it has fully arrived. For Abe Cunningham, the sessions at Studio 606 in Northridge, California felt precisely like that: a sense of the band approaching something it had never quite touched before.

Following the Grammy-nominated success of *Diamond Eyes*, Deftones returned to Nick Raskulinecz and Studio 606 with a clear sense of creative direction. Where *Diamond Eyes* had been shaped by grief and urgency, *Koi No Yokan* was expansive and purposeful — a record that took the atmospheric breakthroughs of *White Pony*, the focused energy of *Diamond Eyes*, and synthesized them into something that felt simultaneously like a culmination and a beginning.

The album debuted at number 11 on the Billboard 200, the highest chart position in Deftones' career at that point. Critically, it received some of the best reviews of the band's history, with many publications describing it as their greatest achievement. Songs like "Swerve City," "Tempest," "Entombed," and "Leathers" entered the permanent canon of alternative metal — tracks that demonstrated what the genre could be when executed at the highest level.

For Cunningham, *Koi No Yokan* represented the full maturation of an approach he had been developing since *White Pony*: dynamic restraint in service of emotional impact, groove over complexity, feel over technical display. His work here is perhaps the most complete expression of what makes him one of the most influential drummers in heavy music — present in every song without dominating any of them.

Producer Nick Raskulinecz again captured the band's live energy at Studio 606, preserving the natural dynamics that quantized digital production would have destroyed. The drum sound on *Koi No Yokan* is warm, dimensional, and real — you can feel the sticks hitting the heads, the shells resonating, the room breathing around the kit.

## Recording Context

Recorded at Studio 606 in Northridge, California with producer Nick Raskulinecz — the second consecutive Deftones album with this team following *Diamond Eyes* (2010). By 2012, the working relationship had deepened considerably. Raskulinecz understood Cunningham's approach: the deliberate imprecision that creates feel, the moments where slightly behind-the-beat serves the song better than metronomic precision. The drum sound on *Koi No Yokan* is considered some of Raskulinecz's finest production work.

## Signature Tracks

**"Swerve City"** — The album opener features a compressed hi-hat groove with silence used as a rhythmic element — the gaps between kick hits as important as the hits themselves.

**"Tempest"** — Demonstrates orchestral dynamic building: Abe adds elements incrementally from sparse atmospheric beginnings to the massive final chorus, each layer earning its place before the next arrives.

**"Entombed"** — Perhaps the finest drum performance on the album. Verses feature ghost notes so subtle they're felt rather than heard; choruses land with cathedral weight. The contrast creates one of the most emotionally resonant moments in Deftones' catalog.

**"Leathers"** — Opens with a half-open hi-hat groove creating washy atmospheric texture before transitioning seamlessly from textural to propulsive when the full band arrives.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Abe Cunningham use on Koi No Yokan?**
A: Abe Cunningham recorded Koi No Yokan using his Tama Starclassic Maple/Bubinga hybrid kit at Studio 606. The configuration included a 22x18" bass drum, 10 and 12" rack toms, and 14 and 16" floor toms. This was the same core setup used on Diamond Eyes (2010), refined through two years of touring and brought to its fullest expression on the 2012 album.

**Q: What makes Koi No Yokan significant in Deftones' discography?**
A: Koi No Yokan is widely considered Deftones' finest album since White Pony (2000) and by some measures their overall best work. It debuted at #11 on the Billboard 200 — the highest chart position of the band's career at the time — and received near-universal critical acclaim. Songs like "Swerve City," "Tempest," "Entombed," and "Leathers" represent the band operating at peak creative maturity.

**Q: Who produced Koi No Yokan?**
A: Koi No Yokan was produced by Nick Raskulinecz, marking the second consecutive Deftones album he helmed following Diamond Eyes (2010). The album was recorded at Studio 606 in Northridge, California. Raskulinecz's approach prioritized capturing live, dynamic performances with natural room ambience, complementing Abe Cunningham's feel-based drumming style.

**Q: How does Koi No Yokan compare to White Pony in terms of drum approach?**
A: Both albums feature Abe Cunningham at his most restrained and emotionally intelligent, but Koi No Yokan represents a more mature expression of the same philosophy. On White Pony, Cunningham was pioneering an approach — discovering how to use silence, ghost notes, and dynamic contrast as compositional tools. On Koi No Yokan, he executes that approach with 12 additional years of experience, resulting in performances that feel completely inevitable rather than experimental.
