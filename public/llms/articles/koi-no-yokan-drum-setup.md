## Overview

*Koi No Yokan* — released November 13, 2012 — is widely regarded as the finest album Deftones have made since *White Pony*. The Japanese phrase translates approximately to "the premonition of love": the feeling upon meeting someone that you will inevitably fall in love with them. That sense of yearning, inevitability, and emotional weight permeates every track.

Following the critical success of *Diamond Eyes* (2010), Deftones returned to Studio 606 in Northridge, California, with producer Nick Raskulinecz for their seventh studio album. The creative momentum built during the *Diamond Eyes* sessions carried forward, but *Koi No Yokan* pushed further — into denser textures, longer song structures, and a more expansive sonic palette that brought the band's shoegaze and dream-pop influences to the surface alongside their metal foundation.

The album debuted at #11 on the Billboard 200, the highest chart position of Deftones' career at that point, and was named album of the year by multiple publications. Songs like "Swerve City," "Tempest," "Leathers," and "Entombed" demonstrated a band operating at peak creative power, with each member contributing performances of genuine artistry.

Abe Cunningham's drumming on *Koi No Yokan* represents the culmination of his evolving approach. The grooves are more varied than on *Diamond Eyes*, the dynamics more nuanced, and the rhythmic language more sophisticated. On "Tempest" — the album's closest thing to a traditional metal track — he delivers powerful, driving beats that demonstrate his ability to operate across the full spectrum of heaviness. On "Rosemary" and "Gauze," his playing is so understated it borders on invisible, yet remove those drum parts and the songs collapse.

Working again with Raskulinecz allowed Abe to build on the established drum sound from *Diamond Eyes* while refining it further. The production is slightly warmer and more nuanced on *Koi No Yokan*, with the drum room ambience playing a more prominent role. The album's longer, more developed song structures also gave Abe space to build drum parts that evolve across five or six minutes rather than compressing everything into three-minute tracks.

Chi Cheng passed away in April 2013 — after the album's recording but before its anniversary. *Koi No Yokan* was, in some ways, a tribute to a band that had survived the worst and emerged capable of creating their most mature, emotionally complex work. Abe's drumming embodies that transformation: patient, purposeful, and profoundly musical.

### Drum Kit: Tama Starclassic Maple

By the time Deftones entered Studio 606 to record *Koi No Yokan* in 2012, Abe Cunningham's Tama Starclassic Maple setup had been refined over more than a decade of touring and recording. The configuration — unchanged in its fundamental architecture from the *White Pony* era — now reflected an intimate knowledge of how each piece responded in recording environments and live contexts.

Configuration:
- 22" x 18" Bass Drum
- 10" x 8" Rack Tom
- 12" x 9" Rack Tom
- 14" x 14" Floor Tom
- 16" x 16" Floor Tom

The room at Studio 606 played a larger role on *Koi No Yokan* than on *Diamond Eyes*. Raskulinecz's approach pushed more room ambience into the final mix, giving the drums a more open, spatial quality that suits the album's expanded sonic landscape.

### Snare: Controlled Authority

The snare sound on *Koi No Yokan* sits slightly warmer and more organic than on *Diamond Eyes* — reflecting Raskulinecz's refined production approach and the enhanced room presence in the final mix. Abe used a 14" drum tuned to medium-high pitch that provides authority on heavy passages while remaining musical and controlled on atmospheric sections.

The dynamic range Abe extracts from the snare is remarkable: on "Tempest" it drives with focused intensity; on "Rosemary" and "Gauze" it whispers with ghost notes barely above the threshold of audibility yet anchoring the entire groove. Raskulinecz's mic technique allowed some of Studio 606's ambient character to color the drum's natural tone — contributing to the album's organic, lived-in feel.

### Cymbals: Zildjian A / K Series

- **Hi-Hats:** Zildjian A 14" New Beat Hi-Hats
- **Crash (L):** Zildjian A Custom 16" Medium Crash
- **Crash (R):** Zildjian A Custom 18" Medium Crash
- **Ride:** Zildjian K Custom 20" Dark Ride
- **China:** Zildjian 18" Oriental China Trash

The K Custom Dark Ride plays a central role on *Koi No Yokan*. Tracks like "Entombed" and "Poltergeist" build extended sections where the ride drives the groove across five-to-seven-minute structures. The K Custom's complex, slightly dry character prevents monotony — each stroke sounds slightly different, maintaining musical interest.

The A New Beat hi-hats anchor the rhythmic foundation, with Abe varying open, half-open, and closed positions to sculpt dynamics within grooves. The crashes appear with calculated restraint — when a crash hits on *Koi No Yokan*, it means something because they're not overused.

## FAQ

**What drum kit did Abe Cunningham use on Koi No Yokan?**
Abe Cunningham recorded Koi No Yokan in 2012 using his Tama Starclassic Maple kit at Studio 606 in Northridge, California. The setup included a 22x18 inch bass drum, 10 and 12 inch rack toms, and 14 and 16 inch floor toms. Producer Nick Raskulinecz captured a warmer, more spatially open drum sound than on Diamond Eyes, with enhanced room ambience contributing to the album's organic character.

**What cymbals did Abe Cunningham use on Koi No Yokan?**
Abe used Zildjian A and K Series cymbals: 14 inch A New Beat hi-hats, A Custom crashes in 16 and 18 inch sizes, a 20 inch K Custom Dark Ride that plays a central role on the album's longer tracks, and an 18 inch Oriental China Trash for controlled aggressive accents. The K Custom Dark Ride's warm, complex character was especially suited to the album's extended song structures.

**How does Koi No Yokan compare to White Pony in terms of drum production?**
Koi No Yokan's drum production is warmer and more spatially open than White Pony's. Terry Date's approach on White Pony emphasized organic ambience at Village Recorder and Sunset Sound; Raskulinecz brought a slightly more present, forward drum sound on both Diamond Eyes and Koi No Yokan while preserving Abe's dynamic range. Koi No Yokan features Abe's most compositionally sophisticated and dynamically varied drumming across the Deftones catalog.
