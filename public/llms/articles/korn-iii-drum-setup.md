## Overview

When Korn released *Korn III: Remember Who You Are* on July 13, 2010, it answered the question the nu-metal world had been asking since founding drummer David Silveria's 2006 departure: could anyone fill that seat without losing Korn's identity? Ray Luzier's answer was emphatic. Debuting at #7 on the US Billboard 200 — Korn's highest chart position in years — the album proved the band could not only survive the transition but thrive with a new rhythmic foundation.

Ray Luzier (born February 11, 1970, in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania) arrived in Korn as one of the most experienced professional drummers in rock. His years as David Lee Roth's touring drummer and his stint with the Army of Anyone supergroup had built him into a session-caliber player who could lock in with any musical context. But Korn presented a specific challenge: a band with a deeply idiosyncratic groove identity, built around the locked interaction between Silveria's drumming and bassist Fieldy's percussive, syncopated low-end.

Luzier cleared the bar decisively. Producer Ross Robinson — the same producer who had helmed Korn's original debut in 1994 and Slipknot's landmark recordings — returned for *Korn III* with the explicit mission of stripping the band back to its raw, early-era roots. The album is aggressive, dense, and driven by the Luzier/Fieldy rhythm section's ability to anchor songs like "Oildale (Leave Me Alone)," "Let the Guilt Go," and "Pop a Pill" with the kind of dark, hypnotic groove that Korn had built their identity on.

For the *Korn III* sessions, Luzier was running a DW Collector's Series setup — DW's professional shell line, built for the demands of studio recording and world touring. This article breaks down every element of that rig: the DW Collector's drum kit, DW Collector's snare, Paiste 2002 and Meinl Byzance cymbals, and DW 9002 double bass pedal that powered one of nu-metal's most celebrated comeback records.

### DW Collector's Series: Professional-Grade Nu-Metal Foundation

For the *Korn III* sessions, Ray Luzier ran a DW Collector's Series kit — DW Drums' professional-tier shell line that sits between the USA Custom entry-level and the upper VLX/Exotic ranges. The Collector's Series delivers the consistent shell quality and tonal character that Ross Robinson's recording approach demanded: well-tuned, resonant drums that would lock into the dense *Korn III* production without requiring heavy treatment to sit correctly in the mix.

The DW Collector's construction uses American maple or maple/mahogany hybrid shells depending on the specific configuration. Maple provides bright attack and focused midrange — the tonal qualities needed to cut through Fieldy's distinctive percussive bass and Korn's multi-tracked seven-string guitar walls. The 22" bass drum configuration generates authoritative sub-frequency punch without excessive boom, providing a bass drum sound with clear attack transients that sit alongside Fieldy's low-end without masking it.

The four-piece tom setup (10", 12", 14" racks, 16" floor) gives Luzier the range for the melodic, moving fills that characterize his playing style — fills that travel across the kit in flowing arcs, musical rather than merely percussive. For *Korn III*'s return-to-roots production philosophy, the straightforward tom configuration was a deliberate choice: more kit is not always better, and Robinson's dense, aggressive production did not require a six- or eight-tom spread.

The DW Collector's hardware — DW's True-Pitch tension rods, MAG throw-off snare mechanism, and steel lug design — provides the mechanical consistency that professional recording sessions demand. Luzier's ability to tune each shell precisely to interact with Robinson's recording environment was as important as the shells themselves.

### DW Collector's Edge: The Backbeat Crack

Ray Luzier's snare for the *Korn III* sessions was a DW Collector's Series 14" × 5.5" — a snare matched to the DW shell line for consistent tonal character across the full kit. The 5.5" shell depth delivers a focused, punchy response that cuts through Korn's dense production without the excessive sustain of deeper shells.

Ross Robinson's production approach on *Korn III* was specifically designed to capture aggression and raw energy — the snare sound needed to project with authority rather than ring with complexity. Luzier's DW snare, tuned to medium-high tension with tight snare wires, produced the crisp backbeat crack that drives tracks like "Oildale (Leave Me Alone)" and "The Past" without smearing the rhythmic precision the songs demand.

The 14" diameter is a natural match for Korn's style: wide enough for full, projecting backbeats, focused enough for the ghost notes and ghost-note-to-accent transitions that Luzier incorporates in Korn's verse grooves. DW's steel lug design and True-Pitch tensioning give precise, symmetrical tuning across all tension points — important when Robinson's close-miking setup would expose any tuning inconsistency.

### Paiste 2002 and Meinl Byzance: Aggressive and Complex

Ray Luzier's cymbal setup for *Korn III* combined Paiste's 2002 series — the legendary aggressive cymbal line originally designed in collaboration with John Bonham — with Meinl Byzance pieces for tonal complexity. This combination provided both the raw, projecting aggression that Korn's live-driven production demanded and the harmonic depth that Ross Robinson's recording philosophy could exploit.

Paiste 2002 cymbals are hand-hammered Swiss bronze (CuSn8 alloy), delivering a bright, full-bodied sound with significant projection and controlled sustain. The 2002 Sound Edge Hi-Hats — with their wavy top cymbal for faster closing response and unique hi-hat chick character — provide the distinct, slightly raw hi-hat voice that Luzier uses to anchor Korn's groove patterns. The attack is immediate and the stick definition is clear, essential qualities when the hi-hat must project through Fieldy's bass and Korn's seven-string guitar layers.

The Meinl Byzance China represents the complementary texture in the setup. Where the 2002 series is bright and aggressive, the Byzance series — hand-hammered in Istanbul — delivers a darker, more complex tonal character with warm undertones and controlled wash. The combination of Paiste and Meinl cymbals gave Luzier distinct tonal zones across his setup, preventing the sonic uniformity that can occur when every cymbal comes from the same line and alloy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Who replaced David Silveria in Korn?**

A: Ray Luzier replaced David Silveria as Korn's drummer. Silveria, a founding member, departed in 2006 citing health issues and creative differences. Korn initially continued with temporary drummers before Luzier — a veteran touring drummer known for his work with David Lee Roth and Army of Anyone — joined as a touring member in 2007. Luzier made his full studio debut on Korn III: Remember Who You Are (2010), produced by Ross Robinson. The album debuted at #7 on the US Billboard 200, confirming the successful transition.

**Q: What drums did Ray Luzier use on Korn III: Remember Who You Are?**

A: Ray Luzier used a DW Collector's Series drum kit on Korn III: Remember Who You Are (2010). The setup included a 22" bass drum with DW 9002 double pedal, 10", 12", and 14" rack toms, and a 16" floor tom. His snare was a DW Collector's Series 14" × 5.5". The DW Collector's Series is DW's professional shell line, offering maple-forward construction that provided the bright attack and focused midrange needed to cut through Korn's dense production.

**Q: What cymbals did Ray Luzier use on Korn III?**

A: Ray Luzier used a combination of Paiste 2002 cymbals and Meinl Byzance pieces for Korn III (2010). His Paiste 2002 setup included 14" Sound Edge Hi-Hats, 18" and 20" Crashes, a 22" Ride, and a 10" Splash. He supplemented this with a Meinl Byzance 18" China for dark, complex accent tones. The combination of Paiste 2002's aggressive projection with Meinl Byzance's darker character gave Luzier distinct tonal zones across the kit.

**Q: What bass drum pedal did Ray Luzier use on Korn III?**

A: Ray Luzier used the DW 9002 double bass pedal on Korn III: Remember Who You Are (2010). The 9002 features DW's Accelerator cam design, which provides a lighter feel at the beginning of the stroke and more power at impact — a profile well-suited to Luzier's groove-oriented double-kick approach. Korn's double-bass patterns prioritize feel and consistency over burst speed, and the 9002's design matches that requirement.

**Q: Who produced Korn III: Remember Who You Are?**

A: Korn III: Remember Who You Are (2010) was produced by Ross Robinson — the same producer who helmed Korn's influential 1994 self-titled debut. Robinson was brought back explicitly to help Korn return to their raw, aggressive roots after a period of more polished productions. His approach on Korn III emphasized live performance capture, minimal processing, and the same emotional intensity he brought to the band's earliest recordings.
