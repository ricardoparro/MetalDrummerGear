## Overview

Released on June 4, 1996, Metallica's sixth album Load marked the most dramatic single-album stylistic pivot in the band's history. After five records that built the architecture of modern thrash metal, Metallica arrived with shorter hair, a Robert Mapplethorpe photograph on the cover, blues-inflected riffs, and a sprawling 79-minute running time. The metal press was divided; the commercial audience was not. Load debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 and went on to achieve 5× Platinum certification in the United States — making it one of the best-selling heavy metal records of 1996.

For Lars Ulrich, the Load sessions represented the third chapter in an ongoing evolution that producer Bob Rock had driven since the Black Album (1991). Where the Black Album had pushed Lars from thrash complexity toward groove and feel, Load took that direction further still. The drumming on Load is Lars's most blues-influenced and deliberately understated studio performance — sparse where the thrash era was dense, open where Justice was compressed, serving songs that breathe in ways "Blackened" or "Dyer's Eve" never did.

The gear shift that accompanied this evolution was equally significant. By 1995, when recording began at The Plant Studios in Sausalito, California, Lars had transitioned from the Tama Artist Maple kit used on the Black Album to the Tama Star Classic Maple — Tama's flagship series, built for greater projection, warmth, and resonance than the Artist line. Simultaneously, Lars's cymbal setup shifted from the Zildjian A series that had defined the Justice and Black Album eras to the newer A Custom series — a brighter, more articulate sound that matched the material's shift toward accessible hard rock and blues.

Bob Rock returned for his third consecutive Metallica album, working with the band across extended sessions at The Plant Studios in Sausalito and Prairie Sun Recording in Cotati, California. Bob Rock's production approach for Load differed meaningfully from the Black Album: reduced gated reverb on the snare, more natural room acoustics, and a philosophy that let the Star Classic Maple's natural voice breathe rather than processing it into the cavernous ambience of 1991.

This article covers the complete drum gear for Metallica's Load sessions: the Star Classic Maple kit that drove the recording, the Zildjian A Custom cymbal configuration that launched a new era in Lars's setup, the DW and Tama hardware choices, and Bob Rock's recording approach at The Plant Studios. For the full arc of Lars's development, the [Lars Ulrich drummer profile](/drummer/lars-ulrich) covers the complete career timeline. For the preceding record, the [Black Album drum setup article](/articles/black-album-drum-setup) details the transition from birch Artstar II to the Artist Maple that bridges into the Load era. Load was followed by Reload (1997), which continued the same Tama Star Classic Maple and A Custom era setup.

### Lars's Studio Kit: Tama Star Classic Maple

For Load, Lars Ulrich upgraded from the Tama Artist Maple used on the Black Album to the Tama Star Classic Maple — Tama's premier maple offering, positioned above the Artist line in construction quality, shell refinement, and acoustic performance. The Star Classic's enhanced maple shell construction delivered greater warmth, projection, and sustain than the Artist Maple, well-suited to Load's open, blues-influenced production aesthetic and the breathing space Bob Rock created at The Plant Studios.

The Star Classic's improved bearing edges and shell construction allowed the maple to resonate more freely than the Artist series — a property that Bob Rock exploited by placing room microphones to capture the kit's full acoustic voice alongside the standard close microphones. The Plant Studios' large recording room was a key asset: the Star Classic could develop its full resonance before the ambient mics caught the sound, resulting in drum tracks with depth and dimension that the more compressed Black Album approach did not pursue.

The kit configuration followed Lars's Black Album template closely: a 22" bass drum driven by a double pedal, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. This was Lars's standard configuration since 1991, and the continuity allowed him to focus on the gear's sonic qualities rather than relearning an unfamiliar setup. Tom tuning for Load was slightly lower and warmer than the Black Album — suiting the album's more laid-back, blues-influenced character — while retaining the presence the maple construction provided.

Load's recording process was notably extended even by Metallica's standards. Sessions at The Plant Studios and Prairie Sun Recording stretched from early 1995 through early 1996, giving Lars more time with the Star Classic than any previous Metallica studio project. This extended familiarity is audible in the recordings: the drum performances on Load, while less technically complex than earlier albums, are exceptionally settled and confident, exploiting the Star Classic's natural acoustic qualities with the assurance that comes from months of studio time together.

One historical note for researchers: the Tama Star Classic (mid-1990s era) predates the later product renamed Tama Starclassic (one word) — the naming conventions across the eras can cause confusion, but Load-era documentation consistently references the Star Classic Maple as Lars's primary recording kit during 1995–1996. The two lines share a philosophy of maple flagship construction but represent distinct product generations.

### The LU1465 Refined — Snare for the Load Era

The snare drum story that began with the Black Album prototype continued through Load. By 1996, the Tama LU1465 Lars Ulrich Signature snare had reached either full production release or a refined late-prototype stage — the instrument that emerged from the Black Album sessions had been developed further through intervening years of touring and refinement.

Where the Black Album snare prototype was tuned dramatically low under Bob Rock's direction — creating the boomy, Bonham-esque crack that defines "Sad But True" — the Load snare sits at a more moderate tension. The blues and rock influences shaping the album's songwriting also shaped Lars's snare tuning: tighter than the Black Album, brighter in character, but still warmer and lower than the dry, cracking Justice-era high-tension approach.

The 14" × 6.5" steel shell provided the brightness and cut that Lars needed for the album's groove-oriented patterns to register clearly in the mix. Bob Rock's less reverb-heavy approach on Load — less gated snare treatment compared to the Black Album — allowed the drum's natural character to speak more directly. On tracks like "King Nothing" and "Until It Sleeps," the snare sits in the mix with a naturalistic authority that reflects both the instrument's character and the more restrained production philosophy.

The LU1465 design features a steel shell that prevents the drum from sounding muddy at lower tunings while retaining warmth when tensioned moderately. For the Load sessions, Lars found a middle ground between the Black Album's extreme low tuning and the Justice era's high-tension attack — a balanced snare sound that suited the album's bluesy character without sacrificing clarity. This approach carried directly into the Reload sessions that followed, where the same kit and snare continued to serve the extended recording project.

### Zildjian A Custom — The New Era Begins

Load marked the beginning of Lars Ulrich's long-running relationship with Zildjian's A Custom series — a line that remains his primary cymbal choice to this day. The transition from the A series (used on the Black Album) to the A Custom represented more than a model change: A Custom cymbals have a distinctly brighter, more overtone-rich character than the warmer A series, producing a more contemporary, cutting sound that suited Load's shift toward accessible hard rock.

The A Custom Dyno Beat hi-hats replaced the 14" New Beat hi-hats that had been Lars's constant companion since the Justice era. The Dyno Beats have a more open, washy character with a brighter "chick" response — audible in the more relaxed, organic hi-hat patterns Lars played on Load compared to the tighter, more precise work of the thrash era. On tracks like "Hero of the Day" and "Mama Said," the Dyno Beats' open character contributes to the album's laid-back feel.

The A Custom crashes are faster and brighter than their A series counterparts, with a pronounced attack transient and quick decay that suited Load's more direct, less reverb-heavy production. Where the Black Album's crashes benefited from Bob Rock's roomy production to bloom and sustain, Load's A Custom crashes spoke directly and clearly in a slightly drier mix environment.

The 22" A Custom Ping Ride introduced a new ride character to Lars's setup. The Ping Ride's defined bell and clear bow articulation were ideal for the groove-riding patterns Load demanded — particularly on mid-paced tracks where the ride carries the rhythmic center. This is a significant departure from the Z series Power Ride used on Justice, chosen for its cutting power in a denser production.

The A Custom series' refined hammer pattern and lathing technique produces a warmer shimmer than the A series while retaining the brightness that distinguishes it from darker lines like the K series. For Load's production aesthetic — rock-inflected, melodic, commercially oriented — the A Custom's balance of brightness and musicality was precisely right. This cymbal transition, begun at Load, has proven to be one of the most durable equipment decisions Lars ever made.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Lars Ulrich use on Metallica's Load?**

A: Lars Ulrich recorded Metallica's Load (1996) on a Tama Star Classic Maple kit — Tama's flagship maple series and an upgrade from the Artist Maple used on the Black Album (1991). The configuration was a 22" bass drum driven by a double pedal, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. The Star Classic's enhanced maple shell construction delivered greater warmth and resonance than the preceding Artist Maple, suited to Load's open, blues-influenced production approach. Bob Rock recorded the kit at The Plant Studios in Sausalito, California, with room microphones capturing the natural acoustic character of the shell rather than relying on the heavy gated reverb that defined the Black Album drum sound.

**Q: What cymbals did Lars Ulrich use on Load?**

A: Load marked the beginning of Lars Ulrich's long-running relationship with Zildjian's A Custom series — a line that remains his primary cymbal choice today, making Load a pivotal moment in his equipment history. The A Custom setup for Load included 14" Dyno Beat Hi-Hats (replacing the A New Beats used on the Black Album), 16" and 17" Medium Thin Crashes, an 18" Medium Crash, a 22" Ping Ride, and an 18" China. The A Custom series has a brighter, more overtone-rich character than the A series, which suited Load's shift toward accessible hard rock. The Dyno Beat hi-hats in particular contribute to Load's more open, organic hi-hat character compared to the tighter Black Album patterns.

**Q: Why did Metallica change their sound so dramatically on Load?**

A: Load's stylistic shift — from thrash metal toward blues-influenced hard rock — was a deliberate artistic decision by the band. James Hetfield has cited influences including Bob Seger, ZZ Top, and Neil Young on the songwriting direction. For Lars's drumming specifically, producer Bob Rock continued pushing him in the direction begun on the Black Album: away from technical complexity and toward feel, groove, and serving the song above all else. Load's songs, with their pentatonic riffs and slower tempos, demanded different drumming than the thrash-era material — and Lars's adoption of the Tama Star Classic Maple and Zildjian A Custom cymbals supported a production approach oriented toward natural warmth and room ambience rather than the compressed, close-miked thrash sound. Commercially, the gamble paid off: Load debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 and went 5× Platinum in the United States.

**Q: How does the Load drum sound differ from the Black Album?**

A: The Load drum sound differs from the Black Album in three primary ways. First, reduced gated reverb on the snare: the Black Album's snare has a distinctive reverb bloom gated to a sharp decay; Load's snare has a more natural, less processed character. Second, more moderate room microphone placement: the Black Album used room mics at 15–20 feet for a cavernous theatrical effect; Load uses closer room placement for natural tone. Third, the kit itself: the Tama Star Classic Maple used on Load has a different character from the Artist Maple used on the Black Album — greater resonance and warmth, encouraging a more open recording approach with less internal muffling. The net result is that Load sounds more naturalistic and less architecturally engineered than the Black Album, reflecting Bob Rock's evolving production philosophy across the two records.

**Q: Where was Metallica's Load recorded?**

A: Load was recorded primarily at The Plant Studios in Sausalito, California, with additional sessions at Prairie Sun Recording in Cotati, California. The Plant Studios is a legendary Bay Area facility with a large tracking room that provided the natural acoustic environment Bob Rock wanted for Load's drum sound — spacious enough for genuine room ambience without uncontrolled reflections. Prairie Sun Recording, a residential-style studio north of Sausalito, offered a different acoustic character and contributed to the sonic variety across the album's 14 tracks. The recording sessions stretched from early 1995 through early 1996 — an extended process that gave Lars significant time to develop the Tama Star Classic Maple setup before the final tracking passes.

**Q: What happened to the Tama Artist Maple after the Black Album?**

A: Lars Ulrich used the Tama Artist Maple for the Black Album (1991) and the extended Metallica world tour through 1993 — one of the longest tours in rock history. By the time Load recording began in 1995, Lars had transitioned to the Tama Star Classic Maple, Tama's refined flagship series that succeeded the Artist line. The Artist Maple kits from the Black Album era were retired from primary studio and touring use. The Star Classic that replaced them continued into the Reload sessions (1997) and represented Lars's primary setup through the late 1990s, making it the cornerstone of the most artistically controversial — and commercially successful — period of Metallica's mid-career.
