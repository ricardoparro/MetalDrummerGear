# My Arms, Your Hearse Drum Setup: Martin Lopez's Opeth Debut (1998)

> Discover the drum kit, cymbals, and gear Martin Lopez used on his Opeth debut My Arms, Your Hearse (1998). Complete breakdown of the budget touring kit, Fredman Studio sessions, and the album that opened the Lopez/Opeth cluster.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Martin Lopez](/llms/drummers/martin-lopez.md)
**Band / Album:** Opeth — *My Arms, Your Hearse* (1998)
**Genre:** Progressive Death Metal

## Overview

Released on August 18, 1998, "My Arms, Your Hearse" is Opeth's third studio album and Martin Lopez's debut recording with the band. The album sits at the hinge between Opeth's atmospheric early period and the progressive-death-metal identity they would refine across the next decade. For Lopez, joining mid-album after original drummer Anders Nordin's departure, it was a baptism by fire — and the first document of a drumming voice that would define progressive metal for the next twenty years.

"My Arms, Your Hearse" is the only Opeth studio album without acoustic clean-vocal sections — a deliberate concession to Candlelight Records, who wanted a heavier, more single-minded death metal record. The constraint shaped the drumming. Lopez had no acoustic interludes to soften the album's edges, no brush-work breathing room, no jazz ride passages to lean on. What you hear instead is a Uruguayan-born drummer with jazz training delivering the most uncompromising death metal performance of his career, with the dynamic vocabulary he would later unleash still held in reserve.

The album was recorded at Fredman Studio in Gothenburg — the same room that would later host Still Life (1999) and Blackwater Park (2001) — with Mikael Åkerfeldt and the band producing. Without an outside producer's influence, the record carries the rawer, more direct production aesthetic typical of late-1990s Swedish death metal. Lopez's playing is technical and precise, but the production keeps everything tightly compressed and close to the foreground.

Gear-wise, this was Lopez's pre-endorsement era. Working with a budget touring kit assembled from whatever Opeth's 1998 finances allowed, Lopez delivered a performance that punches well above the equipment's price point. The contrast with the Sonor Designer Series he would play on Blackwater Park two years later illustrates how much of his sound was technique rather than gear — and how rapidly Opeth's commercial trajectory would change after this album.

This article breaks down the gear Martin Lopez used on My Arms, Your Hearse, places the performance in Opeth's career arc, and explains why this 1998 record is the essential starting point for understanding the Lopez/Opeth cluster that follows.

### Lopez's 1998 Kit: Budget Touring Gear, Pro Technique

For My Arms, Your Hearse, Martin Lopez worked with the budget touring kit he had assembled in his pre-endorsement years. Sources from the era and band interviews suggest a mix of Pearl Forum or Sonor Force-class shells — entry-level professional kits that Opeth's 1998 budget could realistically support. There were no premium-shell endorsements yet, no signature snares, no boutique cymbal deals. This was working-musician gear, used hard on the road and in the studio.

The 22" bass drum gave Lopez the deep foundation he would carry forward through every Opeth album. On My Arms, Your Hearse's heaviest passages — "April Ethereal," "Demon of the Fall," "Madrigal" — the kick punches hard against Fredman Studio's dense guitar tracking. The mid-range shell construction limited the resonance and decay compared to his later Sonor Designer Series, which gave the album a tighter, more clipped low-end than Blackwater Park's open warmth.

Tom configuration was already the Lopez standard: 10" and 12" racks, 14" and 16" floors. This four-tom layout would carry through every Opeth album he played on. The fills on "April Ethereal" and "Karma" already show his melodic-phrasing instinct — using toms to answer guitar lines rather than just fill space — even within the album's heavier framework.

The dynamic ceiling of budget shells saturated faster under hard playing than premium maple kits, but Lopez compensated through technique. His attack was already controlled, his velocity choices already deliberate. The gear constrained his sustain but not his vocabulary. What you hear is a drummer who would sound recognizably himself on any kit, with the touring-grade shells of 1998 providing the rawest, most punch-forward version of that voice.

### The Snare: Cutting Through the Wall of Guitars

Lopez used a generic steel snare on My Arms, Your Hearse — likely a Pearl or comparable budget steel shell consistent with the touring kit. The steel construction delivered the sharp, cutting crack the album's dense guitar arrangements demanded. Brightness and attack were the priorities; warmth and complexity would come later with the Sonor Designer snare on Blackwater Park.

At 14" x 5.5", the snare sat in standard territory: deep enough for body on heavy backbeats, shallow enough to remain crisp through the album's faster sections. The steel shell decayed quickly and projected hard, which suited Fredman Studio's close-microphone approach where excessive ring would have muddied the mix.

What's notable about Lopez's snare work on My Arms, Your Hearse is the restraint of his ghost note vocabulary compared to Still Life. The constraint isn't technical — Lopez was already capable of the full ghost note architecture he would deploy a year later. It's compositional: the album's no-clean-vocals mandate left less low-volume space where ghost notes could register. What ghost notes appear are tighter, more functional, less expressive.

The snare's tuning sat at medium-tight tension, suited to the album's brutal-but-melodic positioning. Lopez kept enough head resonance to land hard on backbeats without choking the drum's natural projection — already showing the touch that would mature on later records.

### Sabian: Entry-Level Brass, Workable Tone

Martin Lopez's cymbal setup on My Arms, Your Hearse was likely entry-level Sabian — B8 Pro or comparable budget-line brass. This was before he transitioned to the Sabian HH (Hand Hammered) series that would define his Opeth sound from Still Life onward. The B8 Pro range delivers bright, cutting attack — suited to death metal's aggression but lacking the dark complexity the HH series would bring.

This cymbal choice fits the album's production aesthetic. My Arms, Your Hearse needs cutting cymbals to slice through Fredman Studio's wall-of-guitar mix. The bright B8 Pro brass projects against the distorted guitar tracks where darker, washier cymbals would have muddied the upper midrange. It's a straightforward gear choice for an album that prioritizes impact over nuance.

The 18" China cymbal is the most prominent effect cymbal on the record. Lopez deploys it as punctuation — the brutal accent at section changes on "April Ethereal" and "Demon of the Fall." The B8 Pro Chinese's harsh, trashy character suits the album's no-compromise heaviness in a way that his later AAX Chinese (introduced by Still Life) would refine but not fundamentally change.

The cymbal upgrade between My Arms, Your Hearse and Still Life is one of the clearest gear-evolution markers in Lopez's career. Where the B8 Pro range here delivered bright, functional attack, the HH series on Still Life and beyond would bring the dark, complex, washy character that became inseparable from the Opeth drum sound. You can hear the difference clearly by A/B-ing the ride cymbal on "Demon of the Fall" against any ride passage on Blackwater Park.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: When did Martin Lopez join Opeth?**

A: Martin Lopez joined Opeth in 1997, partway through the recording of "My Arms, Your Hearse" (1998). He replaced original drummer Anders Nordin, who had left the band, and went on to record five consecutive studio albums with Opeth: My Arms, Your Hearse (1998), [Still Life (1999)](/articles/still-life-drum-setup), [Blackwater Park (2001)](/articles/blackwater-park-drum-setup), [Deliverance (2002)](/articles/deliverance-drum-setup), and Damnation (2003), plus Ghost Reveries (2005), before leaving the band in 2006. My Arms, Your Hearse is the document of his first year in Opeth and the foundation of what would become one of progressive death metal's most influential drumming careers.

**Q: What drum kit did Martin Lopez use on My Arms, Your Hearse?**

A: Martin Lopez used a budget touring kit on My Arms, Your Hearse (1998) — likely a mix of Pearl Forum or Sonor Force-class shells consistent with Opeth's pre-endorsement era finances. His configuration included a 22x18" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms — the same four-tom layout he would carry through every subsequent Opeth album. He paired the kit with Sabian B8 Pro entry-level cymbals (later upgraded to the Sabian HH series by [Still Life in 1999](/articles/still-life-drum-setup)) and a DW 5000 double pedal, which would remain his pedal of choice throughout his Opeth career. The gear was modest; the technique already wasn't.

**Q: Why does My Arms, Your Hearse sound different from other Opeth albums?**

A: My Arms, Your Hearse is the only Opeth studio album without acoustic clean vocal sections — a deliberate concession to Candlelight Records, who wanted a heavier, more single-minded death metal record. The constraint shaped the drumming. Without clean acoustic interludes to soften the album's edges, Lopez had no space for the brush work, ghost note architecture, or jazz ride passages that would dominate [Still Life (1999)](/articles/still-life-drum-setup) and [Blackwater Park (2001)](/articles/blackwater-park-drum-setup). The production also differs — Mikael Åkerfeldt and Opeth produced themselves on this record, applying standard late-1990s Swedish death metal compression and minimal room ambience. Without Steven Wilson's later production philosophy, the album's dynamic window is the narrowest of any Lopez Opeth record.

**Q: What cymbals did Martin Lopez use on My Arms, Your Hearse?**

A: Martin Lopez used Sabian B8 Pro entry-level cymbals on My Arms, Your Hearse — the budget-line brass appropriate to Opeth's 1998 pre-endorsement finances. His setup included Sabian B8 Pro 14" Hi-Hats, 16" and 18" Crashes, a 20" Ride, and an 18" Chinese cymbal used primarily as a punctuation effect. The B8 Pro range delivered bright, cutting attack — workable for death metal but lacking the dark complexity of the Sabian HH (Hand Hammered) series he would adopt by [Still Life (1999)](/articles/still-life-drum-setup). The cymbal upgrade between these two albums is one of the clearest gear-evolution markers in Lopez's career, and the difference is audible from the first ride passage of either record.

**Q: How does My Arms, Your Hearse fit into Martin Lopez's Opeth arc?**

A: My Arms, Your Hearse is the entry point for the five-album Lopez/Opeth cluster that defined progressive death metal from 1998 to 2005. It established his technical foundation — the four-tom layout, the architectural fills, the kick precision, the cymbal restraint — within the tightest compositional constraints he would face in the band. [Still Life (1999)](/articles/still-life-drum-setup) immediately unlocked his jazz vocabulary. [Blackwater Park (2001)](/articles/blackwater-park-drum-setup) brought Steven Wilson and the Sonor Designer Series, capturing his full dynamic range. [Deliverance (2002)](/articles/deliverance-drum-setup) pushed his most extreme performance. Damnation (2003) opened the brush and texture work. Ghost Reveries (2005) closed the arc. Every album that followed built on the foundation Lopez documented in 1998. To understand the Opeth drum sound, you start here.
