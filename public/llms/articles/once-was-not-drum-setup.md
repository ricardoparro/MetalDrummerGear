# Once Was Not Drum Setup: Flo Mounier's Fastest Recorded Performance

> Discover the exact drum kit, cymbals, and gear Flo Mounier used to record Cryptopsy's Once Was Not (2005). Complete breakdown of the Yamaha Recording Custom, Zildjian ZXT cymbals, and DW hardware behind the most technically demanding album in Cryptopsy's catalog.

## Overview

Released on November 1, 2005 on Century Media Records, "Once Was Not" marked both a return and an escalation. Five years after the compositional extremity of [And Then You'll Beg](/articles/and-then-youll-beg-drum-setup) (2000), Cryptopsy reconvened with Lord Worm back on vocals — his first appearance since [None So Vile](/articles/none-so-vile-drum-setup) in 1996 — and proceeded to deliver what many in the technical death metal community consider Flo Mounier's fastest and most physically punishing recorded performance.

The five-year gap between albums had not softened Flo's approach. If anything, the hiatus had sharpened it. Where "And Then You'll Beg" pursued odd-time complexity, "Once Was Not" pursued sheer velocity and technical density simultaneously. Tracks like "Praise the Lord (Opium of the Masses)" and "The Pestilence That Walketh in Darkness" set new benchmarks for sustained blast beat tempos within compositionally sophisticated song structures.

Equally significant was the gear shift. Flo moved from the Pearl Masters BRX of the 2000 era to a Yamaha Recording Custom kit, paired with Zildjian ZXT cymbals — a combination that produced a crisper, more articulate sonic character suited to the album's extreme tempo demands. Producer Chris Donaldson, Cryptopsy's guitarist, captured the sessions at Piccolo Studio in St-Hubert, Quebec with a direct, punishing clarity that lets every stroke speak individually even in the most extreme passages.

"Once Was Not" is the technical death metal community's primary argument for Flo Mounier as the genre's most complete drummer. Not merely fast — though he is — but precise at speeds where precision should be physically impossible. This article breaks down the gear configuration that made it possible.

### Flo's 2005 Arsenal: Yamaha Recording Custom

The Yamaha Recording Custom represented a fundamental sonic departure from the Pearl Masters BRX of the "And Then You'll Beg" era. Where Pearl's hybrid maple/birch construction offered warmth and sustain, the Yamaha Recording Custom's 7-ply birch shells produced a tighter, more controlled resonance with a faster attack transient — exactly what the extreme velocity demands of "Once Was Not" required.

Birch's natural acoustic properties produce a brighter, more focused tone than maple-dominant shells. At the tempos Flo executed on this album — pushing past 270 BPM on the album's most intense passages — each stroke's attack must resolve before the next arrives. The Yamaha Recording Custom's shorter decay and tighter shell construction delivered this separation where warmer shells would have blurred.

The 22-inch bass drum configuration reflected a return to the more responsive smaller shell after the 26-inch experiment of "And Then You'll Beg." The smaller diameter generates a faster initial transient and a more immediate low-frequency response — advantages at extreme double bass tempos where the longer settling time of a large shell becomes a liability. Chris Donaldson's production captured the birch kit with close-miking that preserved each shell's attack character without artificial brightening.

The compact rack tom configuration (10" and 12") remained consistent with Flo's established approach — fast-responding pitched accent instruments rather than rhythm anchors. The floor toms (14" and 16") provided the kit's lower register for the album's fill sequences and compositional transition moments.

### The Blast Beat Core: Yamaha Steel Snare

For the sustained blast beat demands of "Once Was Not," Flo required a snare with the fastest possible attack transient and minimal sustain at high tension. The Yamaha steel snare complementing the Recording Custom kit provided the metallic crack essential for blast beat articulation at the album's extreme tempos.

Steel shells generate a brighter, more cutting attack than wood or brass alternatives, with a higher fundamental pitch that sits above the dense guitar frequencies in Cryptopsy's mix. At the sustained 270+ BPM blast sections that define this album, snare clarity is not aesthetic preference — it is technical necessity. Each snare stroke must be audible as an individual event or the blast beat becomes a wash of undifferentiated noise.

Flo tuned the snare to very high tension, sacrificing sustain and body for maximum attack sharpness and minimum decay time. The snare wires were set tight, reducing sustain further and maximizing the sensitivity needed for the ghost note dynamics that appear in the album's less extreme passages. Producer Chris Donaldson's microphone placement prioritized the attack transient, capturing the snare's cutting character with a close-mic setup that defined the album's central rhythmic voice.

### The Zildjian ZXT Foundation

The shift from Paiste Alpha cymbals (used on "And Then You'll Beg") to Zildjian ZXT for "Once Was Not" brought a distinct sonic character to the album. The ZXT series delivers a bright, cutting attack profile that complements the Yamaha Recording Custom's birch shell clarity — both the kit and cymbals share the same emphasis on attack transient over sustained resonance.

The 14" ZXT Medium Hi-Hats formed the foundation of every blast beat on the album. The ZXT's focused, tight response at high velocities allowed Flo's hand speed to translate directly into precise articulation even in the sustained 270+ BPM passages. Where broader cymbal profiles wash at these velocities, the 14" ZXT's compact, focused response maintained individual stroke clarity.

The crash setup — 16" and 18" ZXT — provided the fast-decaying accent voices needed for blast beat section boundaries. In high-velocity blast playing, slow-decaying crashes build uncontrolled wash that obscures the rhythmic structure. The ZXT's relatively quick decay resolved this: each accent spoke cleanly and dissipated before the next structural moment arrived.

The 20" ZXT Ride and 18" ZXT China completed a setup oriented around bright, cutting character — cymbals that project through Cryptopsy's dense guitar walls without requiring high-volume playing. Producer Chris Donaldson's overhead and room miking captured the ZXT array's combined brightness with precision, creating the album's cymbal soundscape.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Flo Mounier use on Once Was Not?**

A: On Cryptopsy's Once Was Not (2005), Flo Mounier played a Yamaha Recording Custom kit — a significant departure from the Pearl Masters BRX of his 2000 album And Then You'll Beg. The Yamaha Recording Custom's 7-ply birch shell construction produces a faster attack transient and tighter resonance compared to Pearl's maple/birch hybrid, a sonic characteristic suited to the extreme velocities Once Was Not demanded. The configuration included a 22-inch bass drum (returning to a smaller shell after the 26-inch experiment of And Then You'll Beg), 10-inch and 12-inch rack toms, and 14-inch and 16-inch floor toms. The smaller bass drum delivered faster attack response for the album's 270+ BPM blast sequences.

**Q: What cymbals did Flo Mounier use on Once Was Not?**

A: On Once Was Not (2005), Flo Mounier used Zildjian ZXT cymbals — replacing the Paiste Alpha series he had used on And Then You'll Beg (2000). The ZXT series delivered a bright, cutting attack character that complemented the Yamaha Recording Custom's birch shell clarity. His setup included 14-inch ZXT Medium Hi-Hats as the blast beat foundation, 16-inch and 18-inch ZXT Crashes for accent work, a 20-inch ZXT Ride for ride pattern passages, and an 18-inch ZXT China for aggressive section-boundary accents. The ZXT's fast decay profile prevented cymbal wash buildup during the album's extended high-velocity blast sections.

**Q: Why is Once Was Not considered Flo Mounier's fastest recorded performance?**

A: Once Was Not (2005) is considered Flo Mounier's fastest recorded performance because it consistently operates at higher sustained blast beat tempos than any previous Cryptopsy album — reaching 270-290 BPM in its most extreme passages while maintaining stroke-level precision. Unlike And Then You'll Beg (2000), which pursued compositional complexity in odd time signatures, Once Was Not pursued maximum velocity in more direct song structures, allowing Flo to push the upper limit of his physical speed. The Yamaha Recording Custom's birch shell construction supported this approach with faster attack response than his previous Pearl setup. Lord Worm's return on vocals also removed some of the rhythmic accommodations the DiSalvo era had introduced.

**Q: Who produced Once Was Not, and where was it recorded?**

A: Once Was Not (2005) was produced by Chris Donaldson — Cryptopsy's guitarist — making it the first Cryptopsy album produced in-house by a band member rather than Pierre Rémillard, who had produced None So Vile, Whisper Supremacy, and And Then You'll Beg. The album was recorded at Piccolo Studio in St-Hubert, Quebec, Canada. Donaldson's production approach was more direct and punishing than Rémillard's clinical Studio Victor style, capturing more of the kit's physical presence while maintaining the individual stroke clarity essential for technical death metal drumming at Once Was Not's extreme tempos.

**Q: How does Once Was Not compare to And Then You'll Beg for Flo Mounier's drumming?**

A: Once Was Not (2005) and And Then You'll Beg (2000) represent different peaks of Flo Mounier's technical death metal drumming. And Then You'll Beg pursued compositional sophistication — blast beats in odd time signatures (11/8 and 13/8), extended progressive passages, and metrics complexity that required cognitive load management at high velocities. Once Was Not pursued pure velocity within more direct song structures — higher sustained BPM, more relentless blast sequences, and less compositional relief. The gear changes reflect this difference: the 26-inch Pearl bass drum of And Then You'll Beg provided low-frequency authority for progressive passages; the 22-inch Yamaha of Once Was Not provided fast attack response for sustained high-velocity work. Both are considered career peaks — the 2000 album for complexity, the 2005 album for speed.
