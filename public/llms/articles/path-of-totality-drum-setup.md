## Overview

Released on December 6, 2011, *The Path of Totality* remains one of the most divisive and fascinating albums in Korn's catalog — and one of the most audacious genre experiments in nu-metal history. Korn partnered with a roster of the era's leading electronic and dubstep producers — Skrillex, Excision, Feed Me, Noisia, Downlink, Kill the Noise, and 12th Planet — and let them build production frameworks around which the band's performances were recorded. The result was a record that polarized Korn's fanbase while demonstrating the band's willingness to destroy creative comfort zones.

For Ray Luzier, *The Path of Totality* presented a unique challenge: how does a live drummer coexist with electronic production built by producers who don't think in the language of live drumming? The answer called on exactly the skill set that had made Luzier's pre-Korn career exceptional. His years of session work — playing alongside sequencers, loops, and click tracks in professional studio contexts — prepared him to record in hybrid environments where his live kit would interact with programmed elements rather than replace them.

The album's most prominent single, "Get Up!" (produced by Skrillex), became one of Korn's biggest streaming-era tracks — a wubstep-drenched anthem that demonstrated how far the band could stretch without breaking. Luzier's drumming on the track, and across the album, is necessarily more restrained than the full-kit aggression of *Korn III* — but that restraint is itself a demonstration of his versatility. Knowing when to leave space is as technically demanding as filling it.

This article breaks down the gear Ray Luzier used for *The Path of Totality* sessions — the same DW Collector's Series rig from the *Korn III* era, refined for the specific challenge of recording live drums alongside electronic production.

### DW Collector's Series: Adapting Live Drums to Electronic Context

Ray Luzier continued with his DW Collector's Series setup for *The Path of Totality* — the same professional shell line he had used for *Korn III* the previous year. The kit's inherent qualities — bright maple attack, focused midrange, precise tuning response — remained exactly right for the new challenge: recording live drums that would sit alongside Skrillex's bass-heavy, transient-aggressive dubstep production without clashing with the electronic elements.

The challenge of hybrid recording — live kit in an electronic production context — is fundamentally about frequency management and transient control. Dubstep production operates heavily in the sub-bass frequencies (80–120Hz kick drums, heavy low-end bass wobble) and the high treble (harsh synth transients, dense reverb tails). Luzier's DW Collector's kit, tuned appropriately for each producer's sonic environment, needed to occupy the spaces that the electronic production left open rather than fighting it for frequency ownership.

The 22" bass drum presented the most delicate integration challenge: at frequencies that overlapped significantly with the electronic sub-bass kicks produced by Skrillex's production, Luzier's acoustic kick needed to be balanced — present enough to feel like a live performance, but not competing with the produced low-end that defined the album's sound. The DW Collector's maple shells, with their tight fundamental frequencies and relatively controlled overtones, were ideal for this precision tuning challenge.

For tracks like "Get Up!" — where Skrillex's production dominates the sonic landscape — Luzier's drumming provides groove and human feel rather than raw power. The DW kit's professional quality ensured that even in more supportive roles, the drum sound held up against the density of the electronic production.

### DW Snare: The Human Crack in the Machine

Luzier's DW Collector's snare provided the essential human element in *The Path of Totality*'s electronic landscape: the organic backbeat crack that distinguished his live performance from the machine-precise elements surrounding it. In a production context dominated by quantized, sample-based percussion elements, the natural variance and acoustic character of a live snare drum is an asset, not a liability — it gives the listener's ear an anchor in human performance within the electronic sea.

For the hybrid sessions, the DW snare was tuned to cut through the dense electronic production layers. Snare drums that compete with reverb-heavy synth elements and layered effects need specific tuning: tight enough to produce a fast, cutting transient, with enough snare wire tension to minimize the tail that would smear against the electronic reverb tails. Luzier's professional experience with session recording in varied contexts informed his approach.

On tracks like "Narcissistic Cannibal" (Skrillex) and "Chaos Lives in Everything" (Kill the Noise), the snare occupies a critical role: grounding the track's electronic chaos with a physical, acoustic presence that prevents the album from feeling purely programmed. The 14" × 5.5" DW snare's natural acoustic character was the sonic signature that identified Korn's drummer as a live player in a produced landscape.

### Paiste 2002 and Meinl Byzance: Acoustic Texture in the Electronic Mix

Luzier's Paiste 2002 and Meinl Byzance cymbal combination continued on *The Path of Totality*, providing acoustic texture that contrasted with the digital precision of the album's electronic production. In a recording environment built around samples and synthesized elements, the organic, hand-hammered character of Paiste 2002 and Meinl Byzance cymbals provided an essential sonic counterpoint.

The Paiste 2002 Sound Edge Hi-Hats were critical for groove definition. In tracks where the kick drum and snare must coexist with programmed bass and electronic percussion elements, the hi-hat becomes the most clearly identifiable 'live' groove element — its organic opening and closing motion and natural dynamic variation marking the track as performed rather than entirely produced. The 2002's bright, cutting character ensured the hi-hat remained audible even in Skrillex's dense mixes.

The Meinl Byzance China's dark, complex tonal character provided an interesting textural complement to *The Path of Totality*'s electronic sound design. Where electronic producers use reverb and effects to create decay and atmosphere, the Byzance China's natural wash and complex overtone structure offered an organic version of similar sonic qualities — accents that didn't sound mechanical even in a mechanically produced context.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Ray Luzier use on The Path of Totality?**

A: Ray Luzier used his DW Collector's Series drum kit on The Path of Totality (2011) — the same professional shell line he had used on Korn III the year before. The configuration included a 22" bass drum with DW 9002 double pedal, 10", 12", and 14" rack toms, and a 16" floor tom. The DW Collector's maple construction provided the controlled transient response needed for recording live drums alongside Skrillex's and other producers' dense electronic productions.

**Q: How did Ray Luzier record live drums alongside dubstep production on The Path of Totality?**

A: Ray Luzier's extensive session experience before joining Korn was the key enabling skill. Recording live drums in an electronic production context requires precise click track discipline, careful kit tuning to avoid frequency conflicts with electronic bass elements, and the restraint to leave space for produced elements rather than filling the sonic landscape with live performance. Luzier's DW Collector's Series kit was tuned specifically for each producer's sonic framework, and his drums were recorded to interact with rather than compete against Skrillex's, Excision's, and other producers' bass-heavy electronic production.

**Q: What song is Korn's biggest track from The Path of Totality?**

A: "Get Up!" (produced by Skrillex) is The Path of Totality's most prominent track and one of Korn's biggest streaming-era hits. The song's dubstep-metal fusion — Korn's aggressive nu-metal performance over Skrillex's characteristic bass-heavy production — made it both a commercial success and a frequently cited example of nu-metal and electronic music intersection. Ray Luzier's restrained, groove-supportive drumming on the track demonstrates his session-drummer ability to serve a production rather than dominate it.

**Q: Who produced The Path of Totality?**

A: The Path of Totality (2011) featured seven electronic producers: Skrillex (two tracks), Excision, Feed Me, Noisia, Downlink, Kill the Noise, and 12th Planet. Each producer brought their own production approach, requiring Korn — and Ray Luzier specifically — to adapt their performance style to different electronic production philosophies. The multi-producer format was unprecedented for Korn and resulted in one of nu-metal's most unique genre experiments.

**Q: What bass drum pedal did Ray Luzier use on The Path of Totality?**

A: Ray Luzier used the DW 9002 double bass pedal on The Path of Totality (2011), continuing his DW hardware setup from the Korn III era. The 9002's Accelerator cam design and consistent chain-drive action provided the mechanical consistency needed for recording in electronic production contexts — each bass drum stroke producing the same attack transient that could sit predictably alongside programmed production elements.
