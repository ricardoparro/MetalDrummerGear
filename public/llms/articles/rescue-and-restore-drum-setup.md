# Matt Greiner's Drum Setup on August Burns Red's Rescue & Restore (2013)

> Inside Matt Greiner's drum setup on August Burns Red's Rescue & Restore (2013) — the Mapex Saturn IV kit, Mapex Black Panther maple snare, Zildjian A Custom and Z Custom cymbals, Pearl Eliminator Demon Drive pedals, and the post-Leveler creative pivot that pushed ABR into more progressive song structures.

## Overview

Released on June 25, 2013 through Solid State Records, August Burns Red's *Rescue & Restore* arrived in the long shadow of *Leveler* (2011) — the Grammy-nominated album that had redefined what metalcore drumming could achieve. Rather than recycling the formula that earned them that recognition, the band — and Matt Greiner in particular — used *Rescue & Restore* to push further into experimental territory, embracing more progressive song structures, expanded rhythmic vocabulary, and a willingness to let individual tracks breathe in ways the previous album rarely did.

Greiner's drumming on *Rescue & Restore* reflects a drummer at the height of his technical powers and beginning to mature as a compositional voice within the band. The post-*Leveler* creative pivot is audible from the opening track onward: time signatures shift more freely, sections develop rather than simply alternating, and Greiner's fills increasingly function as melodic statements rather than transitions. Tracks like "Treatment" and "Sincerity" demonstrate a drummer thinking in terms of long-form musical arcs, not just measure-to-measure execution.

For *Rescue & Restore*, Greiner continued with the Mapex Saturn IV kit that had become his primary studio and touring instrument through the early-to-mid 2010s. Mapex's hybrid maple/walnut shells gave him a tonal character that split the difference between the warmth of his earlier Ludwig setup and the brighter attack of harder shells — articulate enough for the album's fastest passages but rich enough to support its more dynamic, breathing sections. Paired with his longstanding Zildjian cymbal endorsement (a mix of A Custom and Z Custom models) and the Pearl Eliminator Demon Drive double pedal, the rig was built for both the precision and the dynamic range *Rescue & Restore* demanded.

Reuniting with producer Jason Suecof at Audiohammer Studios in Sanford, Florida — the same team behind *Leveler* — gave the band a familiar production environment in which to take creative risks. This article examines the gear and technique behind one of ABR's most adventurous drum performances: every shell, cymbal, head, and pedal that Matt Greiner brought to *Rescue & Restore*.

### Matt's Rescue & Restore Kit: Mapex Saturn IV

Matt Greiner's Mapex Saturn IV kit defined his mid-2010s recorded sound, and *Rescue & Restore* is one of the cleanest documents of that instrument in a fully realized production. Mapex's Saturn series uses a hybrid maple/walnut shell construction — maple for the brightness and projection metalcore demands, walnut for the lower-mid warmth and complexity that keeps the kit from sounding one-dimensional.

The hybrid shell character is critical to understanding why this kit served Greiner so well during the *Rescue & Restore* sessions. Where a pure maple kit (like his earlier Ludwig Classic Maple) would have leaned warm and rounded, and a pure birch kit would have leaned bright and aggressive, the Saturn IV's hybrid gave him an articulate but full-bodied tone — exactly what an album exploring more dynamic, breathing song structures required.

The dual 22" bass drum configuration remained non-negotiable for Greiner's lever-driven double bass technique. Each kick delivers an independent fundamental, and the 18" depth gave him the low-end punch that *Rescue & Restore*'s heaviest passages — the breakdowns on "Treatment" and "Animals" especially — demanded. The four-tom configuration (two rack, two floor) expanded the melodic palette beyond the *Leveler* setup's three toms, giving Greiner more compositional options for the more arrangement-driven songwriting of the new album.

Mapex SONIClear bearing edges — a Saturn-series feature — improve head-to-shell contact, which translates into more consistent tuning across the kit and a fundamental tone that holds together at the high tensions metalcore demands. On a track like "Spirit Breaker," where the toms need to project clearly through dense guitar work, that bearing edge consistency is audible.

### The Rescue & Restore Snare: Mapex Black Panther Maple

Matt Greiner paired the Saturn IV kit with a Mapex Black Panther 14" x 6.5" maple snare for *Rescue & Restore* — a snare choice that aligned with his Mapex endorsement while delivering the specific tonal character the album required. Mapex's Black Panther line represents the company's premium snare offerings, and the 6.5" depth maple model gave Greiner a balance of crack, body, and tuning range.

The 6.5" depth is meaningful. A shallower snare (like the 5" Ludwig aluminum he used on *Leveler*) emphasizes sharp crack and projection at the expense of body. The deeper 6.5" maple shell adds resonant body to the sound — a fuller fundamental that supports the more dynamic, song-driven arrangements of *Rescue & Restore* without sacrificing the cutting attack metalcore demands.

On tracks like "Sincerity" and "Treatment," Greiner's snare work alternates between thunderous backbeat accents, dense ghost note passages, and rim-shot punctuations within single sections. The Black Panther maple's wide dynamic range handled all of it without changing instruments — the same snare delivering subtle ghost note texture and full-on backbeat crack.

Greiner tuned the snare toward medium-high tension — tight enough for projection through Brent Rambler and JB Brubaker's dense guitar work, but with enough head movement to preserve the ghost note responsiveness his playing demands. The maple shell's natural warmth kept the high tuning from sounding thin or brittle.

### Zildjian A Custom + Z Custom: The Rescue & Restore Cymbal Voice

Matt Greiner's cymbal setup on *Rescue & Restore* reflects his longtime Zildjian endorsement and the strategic blending of two distinct Zildjian lines: the A Custom and the Z Custom. The two series offer complementary tonal characters, and Greiner used them deliberately across the album.

The A Custom series — brilliant-finished B20 bronze with hand-hammered character — gave Greiner the bright, defined attack and musical sustain that the album's more dynamic, breathing sections required. The 14" A Custom hi-hats anchor the kit's rhythmic vocabulary; their clear stick attack remained articulate through the album's complex hi-hat patterns, including the dense 16th-note work on "Spirit Breaker" and the shifting accents on "Provision."

The Z Custom series brought a more aggressive, cutting voice to the setup. Z Custom cymbals are designed for high-volume metal and metalcore contexts — louder, brighter, with a more immediate attack than the A Custom line. The 19" Z Custom crash and 18" Z Custom China gave Greiner the trashy, punishing accent character that *Rescue & Restore*'s heaviest moments demand — particularly the breakdown sections on "Treatment" and "Beauty in Tragedy."

The strategic blending of the two lines reflects mature thinking about cymbal selection. Rather than picking one series and committing to its character, Greiner deployed each cymbal where its specific voice served the song best — A Customs for musical sustain and articulate ride work, Z Customs for aggressive section punctuation. The result is a cymbal voice that supports the album's full dynamic range without sacrificing definition at either extreme.
