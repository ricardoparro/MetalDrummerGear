# Ride the Lightning Drum Setup: Lars Ulrich's 1984 Gear Breakdown

> Discover the exact drum kit, cymbals, and gear Lars Ulrich used to record Metallica's Ride the Lightning in 1984. From 'For Whom the Bell Tolls' to 'Fade to Black' — the complete setup breakdown.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Lars Ulrich](/llms/drummers/lars-ulrich.md)
**Band / Album:** Metallica — *Ride the Lightning* (1984)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal

## Overview

Released on July 27, 1984, "Ride the Lightning" was a seismic leap forward from Metallica's debut. Where Kill 'Em All had detonated the genre, Ride the Lightning refined it — longer songs, darker themes, dynamic range that stretched from delicate acoustic passages to full-speed thrash assault. At the center of that evolution was Lars Ulrich, now recording in a world-class European studio with a professional setup to match his ambitions.

Lars tracked the drums at Sweet Silence Studios in Copenhagen, Denmark — the first Metallica album recorded with producer Flemming Rasmussen, who would become the architect of the classic Metallica sound through Master of Puppets (1986). Sweet Silence's controlled acoustic environment and superior outboard gear gave Lars the tools the Rochester budget session for Kill 'Em All simply couldn't offer.

The gear upgrade was substantial. Lars replaced the entry-level Tama Imperial Star with a professional Tama Artstar II, traded his mixed Paiste/Zildjian cymbal collection for an all-Zildjian A Series setup, and introduced the Ludwig Supraphonic LM402 snare that would become his studio benchmark through the Camco era. The result was a dramatically clearer, more powerful drum sound — one that could carry "For Whom the Bell Tolls," "Fade to Black," and "Creeping Death" with equal authority.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Artstar II 5-piece (birch shells, die-cast hoops, black finish)
- **Snare:** Ludwig Supraphonic LM402, 14" x 6.5" (seamless aluminum "Ludalloy" shell)
- **Cymbals:** Full Zildjian A Series (complete transition from the mixed Paiste/Zildjian KEA setup)
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Tama single bass drum pedal; Tama professional hardware package
- **Heads:** Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear (kick), Remo Ambassador Coated (snare batter and toms)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension for maximum cut and projection

### Lars's 1984 Professional Upgrade: The Tama Artstar II

The Tama Artstar II represented a massive step up from the budget Imperial Star Lars had used on Kill 'Em All. Where the Imperial Star's Philippine mahogany shells produced a warm, slightly muffled character suited to raw thrash recording, the Artstar II's birch construction delivered attack, clarity, and projection on a different level entirely.

Birch shells were the professional standard for aggressive music in the early-to-mid 1980s. The material's fast attack transient and punchy mid-range cut through dense guitar arrangements with precision — exactly what Metallica's increasingly complex song structures demanded. The die-cast hoops added sustain control and durability, critical for the demanding RTL material.

Lars retained his compact five-piece layout: 22-inch bass drum, 12" and 13" rack toms, one 16" floor tom. This configuration had been consistent since Kill 'Em All and would carry through the Camco era on Master of Puppets. Maximum power with minimal setup — a characteristic Lars Ulrich signature.

The Sweet Silence studio environment made an enormous difference. Flemming Rasmussen's drum room had controlled acoustics and the isolation Lars hadn't experienced in Rochester. The Artstar II's inherent sonic qualities could breathe fully, producing the clear, punchy sound that made Ride the Lightning such a dramatic production leap from the debut.

### The Snare Upgrade: Ludwig Supraphonic LM402

One of the defining upgrades between Kill 'Em All and Ride the Lightning was the introduction of the Ludwig Supraphonic LM402 — one of the most recorded snare drums in rock and metal history. After the warm but buried sound of the Tama wood snare on the debut, the LM402's seamless aluminum shell brought an entirely new character to Lars's backbeat.

The Supraphonic's aluminum construction produces a bright, sensitive crack with excellent projection. At 6.5" depth, the LM402 offers more body than the standard 5" model while maintaining the articulation that makes it a studio favorite. The result is a snare that cuts through dense guitar arrangements without sounding thin.

Flemming Rasmussen's microphone placement on the Supraphonic — top (Shure SM57) and bottom (AKG C451) mics blended carefully — produced the iconic cutting snap audible on "Creeping Death" and "Trapped Under Ice." Lars tuned it to medium-high tension for maximum cut.

This snare became a cornerstone of Lars's studio setup. The same LM402 appears on Master of Puppets two years later, delivering the instantly recognizable crack that defined thrash metal production. Ride the Lightning is where the partnership began.

### The Full Zildjian Transition

Ride the Lightning marked Lars's complete transition from the mixed Paiste 2002/Zildjian A setup of Kill 'Em All to an all-Zildjian A Series configuration. The cymbal setup:

- **Hi-Hats:** Zildjian A 14" New Beat Hi-Hats — clear articulation for fast 16th-note patterns
- **Crash (left):** Zildjian A 16" Medium Thin — fast, explosive decay for thrash accents
- **Crash (right):** Zildjian A 18" Medium — fuller crash for dynamic swells and section endings
- **Ride:** Zildjian A 20" Medium — versatile, musical ride for mid-tempo sections
- **China:** Zildjian A 18" China Boy — aggressive, trashy accents on riff transitions

The all-Zildjian setup provided tonal consistency across the album's wide dynamic range, from the delicate cymbal swells of "Fade to Black" to the full-assault crashes of "Fight Fire with Fire."

### For Whom the Bell Tolls: Single-Pedal Double-Bass

The thunderous bass drum pattern in "For Whom the Bell Tolls" is often cited as proto-double-bass drumming, but Lars played it on a single bass drum throughout the RTL sessions. No second pedal, no second bass drum — aggressive single-pedal technique at the main riff tempo, with his left foot controlling the hi-hat. The Artstar II's birch shells and the 22-inch bass drum's authority made the floor tom intro and kick drum patterns sound massive without any hardware shortcut.

## Key Facts

- Recorded at Sweet Silence Studios, Copenhagen, Denmark (1984) — first Metallica album with Flemming Rasmussen
- Tama Artstar II 5-piece: professional birch shells, major upgrade from the Kill 'Em All Imperial Star
- Ludwig Supraphonic LM402 snare introduced — the studio benchmark through the Camco era and MoP
- Full Zildjian A Series transition — no more mixed Paiste/Zildjian from the KEA budget setup
- Single bass drum pedal throughout — "For Whom the Bell Tolls" double-bass effect is single-pedal technique
- Dynamic range unprecedented for Metallica: acoustic intro ("Fade to Black") to full-speed thrash
- Lars would transition to vintage Camco Oaklawn Badge maple drums for Master of Puppets (1986)
- Estimated kit value: $1,200-1,800 (1984) / $3,000-5,000 (vintage today)
- Estimated cymbal value: $1,000-1,400 total (1984)

## Track Highlights

- **Fade to Black** — Metallica's first ballad; New Beat hi-hats and 18" crash handle delicate and explosive equally
