## Overview

Released on August 22, 2006, Lamb of God's "Sacrament" debuted at #8 on the Billboard 200 and earned a Grammy nomination for Best Metal Performance ("Redneck"). It remains the band's highest-charting album and represents Chris Adler at the peak of his commercial and artistic power — a more polished, arena-ready evolution of the groove metal template he established on Ashes of the Wake (2004).

Recorded at Capitol Studios with producer Machine (Mark Lewis), Sacrament captured a significant gear transition: Adler moved from Mapex Pro M drums and Meinl Byzance cymbals to a DW Collector's Series kit and Zildjian A Custom cymbals. The brighter, more polished tone of these new tools is embedded in the album's DNA.

## DW Collector's Series Kit

For Sacrament, Chris Adler transitioned from the Mapex Pro M to DW's Collector's Series. DW's VLT (Vertical Low Timbre) shell construction — where grain runs vertically rather than horizontally — produces warmer, lower fundamental pitches with more resonance. This gave Sacrament's drums a bigger, fuller tone than the more focused Mapex sound of 2004. Dual 22"×18" bass drums (maintained from the Ashes touring rig) retained the configuration Adler had locked in, while DW's True Pitch tuning system enabled Machine to achieve precise consistent tension across every lug.

## Snare: The 6.5" Upgrade

Moving to a 14"×6.5" DW maple snare gave Adler more body and longer decay than the 5.5" Black Panther from Ashes of the Wake. The deeper shell produced the fuller crack that defines "Redneck" and "Walk with Me in Hell" — immediate and powerful, with just enough room to breathe in Capitol's spacious acoustic environment. Die-cast hoops ensured rimshot consistency essential to Adler's precise playing style.

## Zildjian A Custom Cymbals: The Brightness Shift

The move from Meinl Byzance to Zildjian A Custom is the most audible tonal difference between Ashes of the Wake and Sacrament. Byzance cymbals are dark, complex, and multi-overtone; A Customs are bright, cutting, and defined. Machine's Sacrament production leaned into this brightness — cymbals sit higher in the mix with more shimmer and immediacy, giving the album an energetic, arena-ready quality. The 13" A Custom hi-hats maintained Adler's preference for smaller hats that respond quickly to his pivot technique footwork. The 18" A Custom China delivers an explosive, slashing accent character — more cutting than the Byzance China's darker wash.

## Recording at Capitol Studios

Capitol Studios in Hollywood provided an acoustic environment substantially different from Audiohammer Studios (where Ashes was recorded). Machine blended room microphones capturing Capitol's natural ambience with close-mic capture and selective sample reinforcement — more integrated than on Ashes, particularly on kick drums during fast passages. The result is a drum sound that's bigger, more spacious, and more polished than any prior Lamb of God record.

## Key Technique: "Walk with Me in Hell" Groove

"Walk with Me in Hell" (136 BPM) contains one of modern metal's most-studied drum grooves. Adler locks in a syncopated right-hand/kick interplay — kick punctuating on the "e" and "and" of beat 2 — while ghost notes on the snare weave texture through the pattern. Left-foot hi-hat work (Adler's "pivot" technique) adds dynamic variation without disrupting the locked groove. It's technically demanding but sounds inevitable when played correctly.

## FAQ

**What kit does Chris Adler use on Sacrament?**
DW Collector's Series with dual 22"×18" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. VLT maple shells.

**What cymbals does Chris Adler use on Sacrament?**
Zildjian A Custom: 13" hi-hats, 16"/17"/18" crashes, 20" ride, 18" China, 10" splash.

**Where was Sacrament recorded?**
Capitol Studios, Hollywood, California. Produced by Machine (Mark Lewis).

**What Grammy nomination did Sacrament earn?**
"Redneck" was nominated for Best Metal Performance at the 49th Grammy Awards (2007).

**How does Sacrament differ from Ashes of the Wake sonically?**
Brighter cymbals (A Custom vs Byzance), deeper snare shell (6.5" vs 5.5"), warmer drum shells (DW VLT vs Mapex maple), more room ambience from Capitol Studios, and more integrated trigger reinforcement — producing a polished, arena-ready sound vs Ashes' focused, punchy attack.
