# Lamb of God 'Sacrament' Drum Setup: Chris Adler's 2006 Grammy-Era Kit

> Breakdown of Chris Adler's drum gear on Lamb of God's Grammy-nominated Sacrament (2006) — Mapex Black Panther kit, Meinl Byzance cymbals, and the groove metal technique behind a Billboard #8 debut.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Chris Adler](/llms/drummers/chris-adler.md)
**Band / Album:** Lamb of God — *Sacrament* (2006)
**Genre:** Groove Metal / New Wave of American Heavy Metal

## Overview

When Lamb of God released "Sacrament" on August 22, 2006, they were no longer underground favorites — they were mainstream heavy metal's new torchbearers. Debuting at #8 on the Billboard 200 and earning a Grammy nomination for Best Metal Performance ("Redneck"), Sacrament announced that groove metal had arrived at the highest levels of commercial and critical recognition.

At the center of this achievement was Chris Adler, whose drumming on Sacrament represented a refinement and maturation of the style he'd forged on "Ashes of the Wake" (2004). If Ashes was the blueprint, Sacrament was the polished structure built from it — the same groove-first DNA applied with two more years of confidence, major label resources, and the pressure of following one of American metal's most celebrated records.

Produced by Machine at Machine Shop Recording in Richmond, Virginia, with mixing at Capitol Studios in Hollywood, Sacrament had sonic depth and clarity that matched its commercial ambitions. The drums sit forward in the mix with more definition than on Ashes, each element of Adler's setup serving the songs' dynamic architecture.

### The Upgraded Arsenal: Mapex Black Panther Custom

By 2006, Adler had evolved beyond the Mapex Pro M Series used on Ashes of the Wake, moving into the Black Panther custom configuration. The Black Panther line represented Mapex's premium tier — maple shell construction with more refined hardware, tighter tolerances, and greater customization options for a now-major-label act.

The dual 22" x 18" bass drum configuration carried over deliberately. Adler's groove metal identity depends on two independent bass drums — each producing slightly different tone, with physical independent beater attacks giving the bottom-end pocket its signature texture. For Sacrament's polished production, the bass drums were tuned tighter than on Ashes, giving tracks like "Redneck" their punchy, almost snapping low-end character.

### The Sacrament Crack: Refined Snare Power

The snare is the same fundamental tool from Ashes — Mapex Black Panther maple at 14" x 5.5" — but tuned and captured differently for Sacrament's polished production. Where the Ashes snare had room breathe, the Sacrament snare is tighter and more defined, with immediate impact on every stroke.

On "Walk with Me in Hell," the half-time snare hits land with immense authority, commanding the space between guitar riffs. On "Redneck," faster snare work drives the thrash energy without losing the articulation that separates Adler from pure speed drummers. Ghost notes are even more prominent on Sacrament, with the tuning supporting dynamic range from barely audible brushes to explosive rimshots within the same measure.

### The Byzance Evolution: Expanded Three-China Setup

Sacrament's cymbal configuration built on Ashes while adding variety for the album's expanded dynamic range. Core architecture remained: 13" hi-hats, Traditional crashes in progression from 18" to 20", a 21" Medium Ride, and Brilliant China cymbals.

The key addition was a 16" Byzance Brilliant China positioned closer to the hi-hat zone, giving Adler a third China option for quicker accents. This expanded his textural palette without adding clutter — every piece serves a specific musical function.

The 13" hi-hats continued to anchor Adler's pivot technique, where his left foot operates the hi-hat as a semi-independent melodic element while his right foot drives kick patterns. On "Blacken the Cursed Sun," this creates a subtle counter-rhythm beneath the main groove that elevates the track from simply heavy to genuinely musical.

## Recording Context

Sacrament was tracked at Machine Shop Recording in Richmond, VA with producer Machine, then mixed at Capitol Studios in Hollywood. Capitol's legendary console and acoustic properties gave the drums weight and clarity that Audiohammer couldn't achieve. Machine retained his subtle trigger-blending approach from Ashes but with greater restraint — the Sacrament drums are primarily acoustic, with sample reinforcement only on fast double-kick passages where attack consistency demanded it.

Machine specifically avoided quantizing Adler's performances. The slight human timing variations — particularly in the hi-hat pivot work and syncopated kick patterns — are essential to Sacrament's feel.

## FAQ

**Q: What kit does Chris Adler use on Sacrament?**
A: Chris Adler used a Mapex Black Panther custom configuration on Sacrament (2006) — an upgrade from the Mapex Pro M Series used on Ashes of the Wake (2004). The setup features dual 22" x 18" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms, all with maple shells. The Black Panther line offers improved hardware and bearing edge precision compared to the Pro M, giving the Sacrament drums more definition and clarity.

**Q: What cymbals does Chris Adler play on Sacrament?**
A: Adler used Meinl Byzance cymbals on Sacrament, expanding on the setup from Ashes of the Wake. The configuration included 13" Byzance Traditional Hi-Hats, crashes in 18", 19", and 20" sizes, a 21" Medium Ride, and three China cymbals: 16", 18", and 20" Byzance Brilliant models. The 16" China was new for Sacrament, added for quicker accent options.

**Q: How was Lamb of God's Sacrament recorded?**
A: Sacrament was tracked at Machine Shop Recording in Richmond, Virginia with producer Machine. The album was then mixed at Capitol Studios in Hollywood. Machine used minimal sample triggering, preserving the organic feel of Adler's performances. No quantization was applied — groove variations were preserved intentionally.

**Q: What is Chris Adler's pivot technique?**
A: The pivot technique is Chris Adler's signature hi-hat approach, where his left foot operates the hi-hat pedal as a semi-independent rhythmic element while his right foot drives bass drum patterns. This creates a left-foot counter-rhythm that adds musical sophistication to his grooves. He uses 13" hi-hats (smaller than the standard 14") for faster response. Clearly audible on Sacrament tracks like "Blacken the Cursed Sun" and "Walk with Me in Hell."

**Q: Was Sacrament the album that got Lamb of God a Grammy nomination?**
A: Yes. "Redneck" from Sacrament earned Lamb of God their first Grammy nomination for Best Metal Performance at the 2007 Grammy Awards. The nomination confirmed Sacrament's mainstream breakthrough status. Chris Adler's drumming was specifically cited in music press coverage for elevating the track. Adler would later win a Grammy with Megadeth's "Dystopia" (Best Metal Performance, 2017).

**Q: What is the BPM of Redneck by Lamb of God?**
A: "Redneck" runs at approximately 184 BPM. The Grammy-nominated track alternates between thrash-speed verse double bass patterns and a powerful half-time chorus groove — dynamic contrast that showcases Adler's ability to serve the song across a wide tempo range.
