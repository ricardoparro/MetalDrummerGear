# Igor Cavalera's Drum Setup on Sepultura's Schizophrenia (1987)

> Drum gear breakdown for Sepultura's 1987 album Schizophrenia. A 17-year-old Igor Cavalera, pre-Roadrunner deal, pre-Pearl and Sabian endorsements — the honest origin story of Brazilian thrash drumming at JG Recording Studio in Belo Horizonte.

## Overview

Released on October 30, 1987 through São Paulo's Cogumelo Records, "Schizophrenia" is the album where Sepultura stopped being a curious Brazilian footnote and became a band that the international metal underground could no longer ignore. Recorded at JG Recording Studio in Belo Horizonte, mixed by engineer Belmiro Rangel, and produced by the band itself, "Schizophrenia" is the foundational document of Igor Cavalera's drumming — the moment a 17-year-old kid from Minas Gerais committed his first fully-formed extreme metal performance to tape.

This article exists because honesty is the editorial value here. There is no signature snare endorsement to break down. No Pearl artist roster catalogue page from 1987 to reference. No Sabian press release. No Modern Drummer cover story. Sepultura recorded "Schizophrenia" two years before they signed to Roadrunner Records and traveled to Tampa for "Beneath the Remains." In 1987, they were a regional Brazilian metal band working with whatever drums, cymbals, and hardware they could afford during a period of severe import restrictions, inflation, and economic hardship in Brazil. Period interviews and photographs from the era suggest a budget Pearl Export-style kit and unbranded or low-end cymbals, but the exact specifications were never formally documented.

What is well-documented is the music itself. Tracks like "From the Past Comes the Storms," "Inquisition Symphony," "Septic Schizo," and the title track captured Igor's developing speed, his growing command of double bass, and the raw aggression that would later be refined under Scott Burns at Morrisound. The drumming on "Schizophrenia" is not technically polished — and it does not need to be. The album is a snapshot of a 17-year-old drummer pushing past the limits of his equipment, his recording budget, and the boundaries of what Brazilian metal had attempted up to that point.

For context, when Igor recorded "Schizophrenia," he was younger than Dave Lombardo when he recorded "Show No Mercy," younger than Lars Ulrich when he recorded "Kill 'Em All," and younger than Charlie Benante when he recorded "Fistful of Metal." He was operating without a major label budget, without imported gear access, and without the production help that would later transform Sepultura's sound. Every gain in tightness, every increase in attack, every refinement that listeners hear on "Beneath the Remains," "Arise," "Chaos A.D.," and "Roots" begins with what Igor figured out on "Schizophrenia" in 1987.

This article is the honest origin story. We document what is known, hedge what is not, and treat the absence of definitive gear specifications as part of the historical record. Sepultura's first masterpiece was recorded by a teenager with limited resources and unlimited determination — and that is the story worth telling.

### Igor's 1987 Kit: What We Know, What We Hedge

Exact gear unconfirmed. That sentence has to come first, because every detail that follows is an inference drawn from contemporary band interviews, the limited photographic record of the Cogumelo Records era, and reasonable assumptions about what was available to a Brazilian metal band in 1987.

Period interviews and photographs from the era suggest that Igor Cavalera was playing a budget kit consistent with the Pearl Export line — the affordable workhorse that launched countless metal careers in South America during the late 1980s. The Export's poplar and basswood shells were the kind of accessible, mid-tier construction that made professional-sounding drumming possible for bands operating without import budgets or endorsement relationships. Whether the specific kit was a Pearl Export, a comparable Tama or Yamaha entry-level series, or a generic Brazilian-market kit, the underlying truth is the same: this was budget equipment played hard.

The bass drum configuration is one of the genuinely contested details. Brazilian thrash and early death metal of the era often used a single bass drum with rapid right-foot work — the influence of European bands like Kreator and Destruction, combined with the practical reality that two matching kick drums were a luxury. Some accounts and photographs suggest Igor used a single 22-inch kick on "Schizophrenia," developing the right-foot speed that would later translate to his iconic double-bass work. Other accounts mention dual 20-inch kicks. The truth may simply be that the kit Igor recorded with was whatever was available on the day, and the documentation never solidified.

The tom configuration was almost certainly minimal — two rack toms and a floor tom is the most common assumption, consistent with the cymbal-stand-and-tom-arm hardware budget of an independent band. Brazilian thrash of this era did not feature elaborate kit setups. The focus was on attack, speed, and aggression, not melodic tom work.

What "Schizophrenia" demonstrates is that none of this gear matters as much as the player. Igor was 17, he had whatever kit he could afford, and he played it with the conviction of someone who knew this was his only shot. The exact specifications were never formally documented because nobody — including the band — expected this album to become a foundational document of South American extreme metal. They were just trying to make a record.

### The Cutting Crack of a Budget Steel Snare

The snare sound on "Schizophrenia" is the cutting, mid-heavy crack of a budget steel-shell drum recorded in a small Brazilian studio with limited microphone options. Steel was almost certainly the shell material — wood snares of the late 1980s were typically more expensive, and the cutting attack required to compete with two guitars and a bass through cassette-era playback systems demanded the brightness that steel shells deliver naturally.

Whether the drum was 14" x 5.5" or 14" x 6.5" is unconfirmed. Both depths were common on budget steel snares of the era. The 5.5" depth would have given the recording a slightly tighter, more cutting character; the 6.5" depth would have added more body. Listening to the album, the snare sits in a high-mid pocket that suggests tight tuning regardless of depth — that focused, aggressive crack is the priority.

JG Recording Studio in Belo Horizonte was not Morrisound. The microphone selection, the room acoustics, the available outboard gear — all of it was modest. What you hear on "Schizophrenia" is largely what Igor's snare sounded like in the room: a tight, mid-forward attack with minimal processing. There is no parallel compression chain, no Solid State Logic console magic, no Scott Burns-level engineering refinement. The drum cracks because it was tuned to crack and recorded without much in the way of tone-shaping intervention.

Igor's tuning approach on "Schizophrenia" can be reconstructed by ear: high tension on the batter head, tight snare wires, minimal damping. This is the configuration that produces maximum cut in a budget recording environment. It is also the configuration that a 17-year-old self-taught drummer would naturally arrive at — crank everything tight, hit hard, let the drum scream.

What is striking about the "Schizophrenia" snare sound, in retrospect, is how much of the eventual Sepultura snare DNA is already present. The cutting attack, the high-mid emphasis, the willingness to let the drum sound aggressive rather than polished — all of it foreshadows the "Beneath the Remains" and "Arise" snare tones that Scott Burns would later refine at Morrisound.

### Budget Unbranded Cymbals: The Honest Truth

This is the section where honesty matters most. Sepultura could not afford pro-line Paiste 2002, Zildjian A, or Sabian AA cymbals in 1987. The band's entire recording budget for "Schizophrenia" was modest by any standard, and the cymbal selection reflected that reality. Period evidence points to budget unbranded cymbals, possibly some low-end Zildjian or Paiste student-line pieces, but no specific models can be reliably confirmed.

The cassette-budget recording of the era reflected the cymbal quality directly. Listen to "Schizophrenia" and the cymbal sound is dry, mid-heavy, and lacking the wash and sustain of pro-line bronze. This is not a criticism of Igor's playing — it is a documentation of what budget cymbals sound like when captured in a small Brazilian studio with limited microphone options. The hi-hats sit in a tight, choked-sounding pocket. The crashes feel short and abrupt rather than expansive. The ride, where you can hear it, is more a textural element than a bell-focused voice.

What this means for the listener is that the cymbal work on "Schizophrenia" reads as raw and aggressive almost by accident — the budget gear contributed to the album's aesthetic in ways that more expensive cymbals could not have replicated. When Sepultura later moved to Paiste 2002 series cymbals for "Beneath the Remains" and beyond, the contrast was immediate. Those cymbals open up, project, sing. The "Schizophrenia" cymbals do their job and step out of the way.

We are not going to list specific cymbal models here. To do so would be to fabricate detail that the historical record does not support. What we can say with confidence is this: the cymbals were budget, they were probably unbranded or low-line variants of established brands, and they sounded the way they sounded because that was what was available to a 17-year-old drummer in an independent Brazilian metal band in 1987.
