# Dave Lombardo's Drum Setup on Slayer's Seasons in the Abyss (1990)

> Dave Lombardo's full range on one album: blast-speed 'War Ensemble,' groove-metal 'Dead Skin Mask,' and the iconic mid-tempo title track. Complete gear breakdown of the Pearl Masters Custom kit and Zildjian A/K cymbals on Slayer's commercial peak.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Dave Lombardo](/llms/drummers/dave-lombardo.md)
**Band / Album:** Slayer — *Seasons in the Abyss* (1990)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal

## Overview

Released on October 9, 1990, Seasons in the Abyss was Slayer's most commercially successful album to that point — reaching #40 on the Billboard 200 and topping the Heatseekers chart. But the chart performance almost undersells what the album achieved artistically: on a single record, Dave Lombardo delivered the complete range of his drumming, from the relentless speed assault of "War Ensemble" to the half-time groove of "Dead Skin Mask" to the iconic mid-tempo swagger of the title track.

No other Slayer album documents Lombardo's versatility so completely. Reign in Blood (1986) showed what he could do at maximum speed. South of Heaven (1988) showed he could groove. Seasons in the Abyss showed he could do both on the same album, switching between registers without losing identity or intensity.

Rick Rubin produced for the third consecutive Slayer record, again at Eldorado Recording Studios in Hollywood. But Lombardo came to these sessions with a new kit: the switch from Tama to Pearl — Pearl Masters Custom shells, Zildjian A and K series cymbals — marked a significant gear transition at the peak of the Lombardo era. The Pearl's maple character brought a warmer, more open sound than the Tama Artstar II's punchy birch, while the Zildjian cymbals provided a drier, more controlled response than the Paiste RUDE series that had defined Reign in Blood and South of Heaven.

The result is a drum sound that sits between Slayer's two earlier Rick Rubin records: heavier and more sonically varied than Reign in Blood's deliberately monolithic approach, but with more attack and aggression than South of Heaven's groove-forward mix. "War Ensemble" opens the album at close to Reign in Blood tempos. The title track closes it with one of the most studied mid-tempo drum performances in metal history.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Pearl Masters Custom (Natural Maple finish)
- **Snare:** Pearl Pearl Free-Floating Steel, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian — Zildjian A Series / K Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW 5002 (two single pedals); DW 5500 Hi-Hat Stand; Pro-Mark 2B Wood Tip
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (batter), Remo Ambassador Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension — bright attack, minimal ring, controlled sustain

### The Pearl Transition: Masters Custom

For Seasons in the Abyss, Dave Lombardo made the most significant gear change of his early Slayer career: switching from the Tama Artstar II — used on Reign in Blood (1986) and South of Heaven (1988) — to Pearl Masters Custom with maple shells. This was not a minor upgrade; maple and birch produce fundamentally different sounds, and the Pearl's ISS mounting system changed the way the toms responded compared to the Tama's die-cast hardware configuration.

Maple shells are warmer and more open than birch. Where the Tama Artstar II delivered a punchy, focused attack optimized for extreme speeds, the Pearl Masters Custom produced a fuller, more sustaining tone that worked naturally in the groove contexts Lombardo was being asked to deliver more frequently. The "Seasons in the Abyss" title track — with its mid-tempo riff lock and open tom fills — showcases the Pearl's character in ways the Tama would not have delivered identically.

The double bass configuration remained: two separate 22-inch bass drums rather than a double pedal. This setup — two independently tuned and miked drums — was a Lombardo constant across all three Rick Rubin Slayer productions. Each drum gave the low end a slightly different character, and the dual-microphone setup gave the recording engineers complete control over the kick sound in the mix.

At the tempos of "War Ensemble" — approaching 200 BPM — the Pearl Masters Custom demonstrated that the maple shells could perform as a precision instrument at Reign in Blood-adjacent speeds. The warmth of maple doesn't cost attack when the drummer is striking at maximum velocity.

### Pearl Steel Custom: Attack at Any Tempo

The Pearl steel snare that Lombardo used on Seasons in the Abyss carries the same character priority as the Tama Superstar Steel it replaced: maximum attack, focused crack, controlled ring. Steel snares sit above maple kit shells in a way that birch-plus-steel never quite achieves — the warmth of the Pearl Masters Custom kit is balanced against the snare's cold, precise aggression.

On "War Ensemble," the snare functions as it does on Reign in Blood tracks: a punctuation mark at extreme speed, the crack appearing between double bass patterns with mechanical regularity. At "War Ensemble"'s tempo, the snare's note is almost too fast to perceive as texture — it becomes rhythm itself, the leading edge of patterns that only resolve at the bar level.

On "Dead Skin Mask," the same drum is a completely different instrument. The half-time groove slows the snare to a position where its individual character is fully exposed — the steel's brightness sitting above the Pearl kit's maple warmth, each backbeat a deliberate statement rather than a rapid-fire element. Rick Rubin's close-mic approach captured this contrast precisely: the snare sounds the same on both tracks, but its musical role is unrecognizable between contexts.

The title track "Seasons in the Abyss" showcases the snare's most nuanced use: mid-tempo, locked to a guitar riff that is itself one of metal's most iconic compositions, each stroke placed with an economy that demonstrates Lombardo's evolved musical sensibility from the pure-speed approach of six years earlier.

### Zildjian A and K: The New Palette

The switch from Paiste RUDE and 2002 series to Zildjian A and K series is the most audible gear change between South of Heaven and Seasons in the Abyss. Paiste RUDE cymbals are aggressive, unlathed, intentionally raw — built for thrash metal's abrasive aesthetic. Zildjian A series cymbals are fully lathed, with a brighter, more articulate response and faster decay characteristics. K series pieces bring darker, drier texture.

The combination shapes the Seasons in the Abyss drum sound in specific ways. The Zildjian A New Beat hi-hats produce a crisp, cutting articulation that's cleaner than the Paiste 2002 Sound Edge equivalent — at "War Ensemble"'s speed, this means each hi-hat stroke is individually defined rather than blurring into texture. The A Medium Thin Crashes respond faster and decay faster than the RUDE Wild Crashes, which suits the tempo demands of both the album's fastest and slowest tracks.

The K series China adds the production's most distinctive cymbal character. Where the Paiste RUDE 20" Wild China was broad and aggressive, the Zildjian K China is darker and more controlled — its "trashiness" comes from the K series' unlathed bell and hammered bow rather than from raw, un-processed metal. On "Seasons in the Abyss" and "Dead Skin Mask," this China sounds like a complement to the groove rather than a contrast.

The addition of an A Splash cymbal — absent from the Reign in Blood and South of Heaven setups — reflects Lombardo's expanded sonic vocabulary. Splash cymbals are fast-transient accent tools that only work when the drummer has enough space in the arrangement to use them meaningfully. Their presence on this album signals the increased dynamic range Slayer built into Seasons in the Abyss.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Dave Lombardo use on Seasons in the Abyss?**

A: Dave Lombardo used a Pearl Masters Custom kit for Seasons in the Abyss (1990), switching from the Tama Artstar II he had used on Reign in Blood (1986) and South of Heaven (1988). The Pearl Masters Custom featured maple shells — warmer and more open than the Tama's birch — with ISS tom mounting for improved resonance. The configuration included two 22-inch bass drums (not a double pedal), 10-inch and 12-inch rack toms, and 14-inch and 16-inch floor toms. This gear change is the most significant equipment transition between any two consecutive Rick Rubin Slayer productions.

**Q: How does Lombardo's double-bass technique work on "War Ensemble"?**

A: On "War Ensemble," Lombardo drives two separate 22-inch Pearl bass drums at approximately 200 BPM, producing continuous 16th-note patterns at the song's full tempo. The technique is not a double pedal on one drum — each foot operates an independent DW chain-drive pedal on its own drum, giving each bass drum a slightly different acoustic character and allowing independent tuning. The pattern locks with the guitar riff rather than functioning as a sustained blast: Lombardo places the double bass to reinforce the rhythmic structure of Kerry King and Jeff Hanneman's parts, creating the forward momentum that makes "War Ensemble" one of thrash metal's most studied tracks. This controlled, riff-locked approach distinguishes Lombardo's double bass from drummers who use continuous blast patterns as pure texture.

**Q: How does Seasons in the Abyss differ from Reign in Blood drumming?**

A: Reign in Blood (1986) is a 29-minute endurance test at 220-250+ BPM — blast beats, maximum speed, no variation in the album's core tempo approach. Every track runs at extreme velocity; groove is not part of the vocabulary. Seasons in the Abyss (1990) covers a range from 92 BPM ("Seasons in the Abyss" title track) to 200 BPM ("War Ensemble") on the same album, requiring Lombardo to switch between speed and groove registers track-to-track. The gear is also completely different: Tama Artstar II birch and Paiste RUDE cymbals on Reign in Blood; Pearl Masters Custom maple and Zildjian A/K on Seasons. The Pearl's warmer tone and the Zildjian's brighter, more articulate response are both audible in the wider dynamic range that Seasons in the Abyss was built to accommodate.

**Q: What cymbals did Dave Lombardo use on Seasons in the Abyss?**

A: Dave Lombardo switched from Paiste RUDE and 2002 series (used on Reign in Blood and South of Heaven) to Zildjian A and K series for Seasons in the Abyss. The setup included Zildjian A 14-inch New Beat Hi-Hats, Zildjian A 18-inch and 19-inch Medium Thin Crashes, a Zildjian A 20-inch Medium Ride, Zildjian K 18-inch China, and Zildjian A 10-inch Splash. The Zildjian A series is fully lathed, producing a brighter, more articulate response than the Paiste RUDE's unlathed, aggressive character. The K series China adds dark, dry texture that complements groove tracks like "Dead Skin Mask" differently than the RUDE Wild China's broad aggression would have.

**Q: Why is "Seasons in the Abyss" considered one of Lombardo's best performances?**

A: The title track "Seasons in the Abyss" is studied by drumming educators because it demonstrates controlled mid-tempo playing at its highest level. At 92 BPM, Lombardo locks precisely with Jeff Hanneman's iconic guitar riff — every kick and snare placement intentional, every tom fill timed to the phrase structure. There are no blast beats, no speed demonstrations: the track's power comes entirely from groove and precision. This is the hardest kind of drumming to teach: restraint that conveys intensity without relying on velocity. Combined with "War Ensemble's" 200-BPM double bass assault on the same album, it documents the full spectrum of Lombardo's ability in a single record — which is why Seasons in the Abyss remains the most-cited album in Lombardo's discography for drum education purposes.
