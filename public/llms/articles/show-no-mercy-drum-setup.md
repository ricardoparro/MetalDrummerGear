# Show No Mercy Drum Setup: Dave Lombardo's Gear on Slayer's 1983 Debut

> Discover the exact drum kit, cymbals, and gear Dave Lombardo used on Slayer's Show No Mercy (1983) — his debut recording at age 19, before any endorsement deals. Complete breakdown of the pre-endorsement Ludwig kit, Zildjian A cymbals, and the $8,000 self-produced sessions that launched thrash metal.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Dave Lombardo](/llms/drummers/dave-lombardo.md)
**Band / Album:** Slayer — *Show No Mercy* (1983)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Speed Metal

## Overview

Released on December 3, 1983 on Metal Blade Records, *Show No Mercy* was Slayer's debut album — and Dave Lombardo's first recorded performance. He was 19 years old. The album was self-produced by the band at Track Recording Studio in Torrance, California on a budget of approximately $8,000. No click track was used. What you hear is thrash metal being invented in real time.

The gear Lombardo used was entirely pre-endorsement: a Ludwig 5-piece double bass kit with Zildjian A Series cymbals, sourced independently without corporate sponsorship. This stands in complete contrast to the Tama Artstar II and Paiste RUDE/2002 setup he would use on *Reign in Blood* just three years later. The difference between those two setups — in gear quality, production budget, and playing precision — documents one of the most dramatic developmental arcs in metal drumming history.

*Show No Mercy* features tempos reaching 220–240 BPM with no click track, giving the album a surge-and-push energy that machine-locked precision could never replicate. Tracks like "Evil Has No Boundaries," "The Antichrist," and the title track established the speed ceiling for early thrash metal. Lombardo's double bass approach — informed by his Cuban-American musical background — provided a polyrhythmic foundation that distinguished Slayer from simpler punk-derived speed merchants.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Ludwig Standard / Club Date (poplar/maple shells, Black Wrap finish)
- **Bass Drums:** 22" x 14" (x2) — double bass configuration
- **Toms:** 12" x 8" and 13" x 9" rack toms; 16" x 16" floor tom
- **Snare:** Ludwig Acrolite 14" x 5" (aluminum alloy shell)
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A 14" New Beat Hi-Hats; A 16" Medium Thin Crash; A 18" Medium Crash; A 20" Medium Ride; A 18" China Boy High
- **Pedals:** Ludwig Speed King (x2)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B Wood Tip
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Clear (toms), Remo Ambassador Coated (snare), Remo Fiberskyn (kick front)

## Gear Details

### Ludwig Kit: Pre-Endorsement American Workhorse

In 1983, Lombardo played Ludwig — the American brand that had dominated professional drumming since the Beatles era. At 19, without endorsement support, Ludwig's affordable Club Date and Standard lines were what a serious young drummer could realistically acquire. The poplar/maple shells gave the kit a bright, somewhat thin tone compared to the pure birch Tama Artstar II he would later use. In the context of *Show No Mercy*'s raw budget production, that tonal character served the music: urgent, slightly aggressive, and unpolished in exactly the right way.

The double bass configuration was present from day one. Lombardo's Cuban-American background had given him a rhythmic foundation rooted in polyrhythmic thinking, and that translated directly into his double kick approach. Two 22-inch bass drums drove the engine of every track on the album.

### Ludwig Acrolite Snare: Bright and Cutting

The Ludwig Acrolite aluminum-shell snare was an industry standard from the 1960s through the 1980s, used across countless studio recordings for its consistent attack and affordable price point. On *Show No Mercy*, the 14" x 5" Acrolite provided a bright, cutting crack that penetrated the dense rhythm guitar wall. Its shallower depth compared to the 6.5" Tama steel snare of *Reign in Blood* means less body and more high-frequency attack — thinner but appropriately raw for the production aesthetic.

### Zildjian A Cymbals: Before the Paiste Era

Every Slayer fan associates Dave Lombardo with Paiste RUDE and 2002 cymbals. But *Show No Mercy* was recorded before those endorsement relationships existed. The Zildjian A Series setup Lombardo used has a warmer, more complex sound than the Paiste 2002's brighter cutting character. On *Show No Mercy*'s dense mix, the Zildjian warmth translates as slightly darker and washier than later Slayer recordings. The 18" China Boy High was already a fixture of Lombardo's vocabulary from the start — that trashy explosive accent above the floor tom remained a defining element of Slayer's sound across every era.

### Ludwig Speed King Pedals: Before the Iron Cobra

Lombardo's association with Tama Iron Cobra pedals — including prototype versions he tested before *Reign in Blood* — is well documented. But on *Show No Mercy*, he played Ludwig Speed King pedals: the classic American cam-driven single-chain pedal. The Speed King's relatively light spring tension and consistent cam action supported his fast double bass approach, even if they lacked the precision engineering of the Iron Cobra he would later adopt.

## Recording Context

*Show No Mercy* was self-produced by Slayer at Track Recording Studio in Torrance, California in 1983. Total budget: approximately $8,000. No click track was used. The band played live in the studio, and the performances were captured with minimal editing. The microphone approach was utilitarian — Shure SM57s and Sennheiser MD421s in practical positions — with no room mics or elaborate cymbal miking. The dense, guitar-forward mix reflects underground metal's production aesthetic of the era.

The absence of a click track is audible throughout. Tempos surge and breathe with human adrenaline rather than machine precision. On "The Antichrist," the BPM accelerates noticeably in the second half. On the title track, the tempo pushes and pulls with the band's collective energy. This imprecision is historically valuable: it documents what 220+ BPM playing sounded like before studio technology could lock it to a grid.

## Track Analysis

### Evil Has No Boundaries
Album opener at approximately 220 BPM. Hi-hat drive on eighth notes through the verses with double bass under the chorus riffs. Fills are short and efficient — no wasted motion at this tempo. The Ludwig kick drums provide the foundational attack while Zildjian A crashes punctuate riff changes.

### The Antichrist
One of the fastest tracks on the album, approaching 230 BPM. Blast beat sections presage the *Reign in Blood* approach. Lombardo's double bass stamina is remarkable for a 19-year-old. The tempo surges noticeably in the second half — no click track tension at its most audible and most authentic.

### Die by the Sword
More mid-paced at 180–200 BPM, demonstrating Lombardo's groove sensibility beyond pure speed. Latin-influenced fill patterns emerge in the bridge — the Cuban rhythmic heritage is audible beneath the metal aggression. Double bass used for emphasis rather than constant pedal. The ride cymbal gets more use here.

### Show No Mercy (title track)
The title track at approximately 225 BPM captures the album's full dynamic range. An extended drum intro section is rare for the genre at this point. Tempo changes handled without a click track. One of the strongest performances on the record. The Zildjian A crashes define the section transitions throughout.

## FAQs

**What drum kit did Dave Lombardo use on Show No Mercy?**
On *Show No Mercy* (1983), Dave Lombardo played a Ludwig 5-piece double bass kit — two 22-inch bass drums, 12" and 13" rack toms, and a 16" floor tom. This was pre-endorsement gear: no corporate deal, just the drums he could source independently as a 19-year-old. The Ludwig poplar/maple shells gave the kit a brighter, rawer tone than the Tama Artstar II birch setup he would use on *Reign in Blood* three years later.

**How old was Dave Lombardo when he recorded Show No Mercy?**
Dave Lombardo was 19 years old when Slayer recorded *Show No Mercy* in 1983. Born February 16, 1964, he was a teenager when he helped invent thrash metal on the band's debut. The raw, slightly imprecise quality of his performance — no click track, budget studio, pure adrenaline — is part of what makes the recording historically significant. By *Reign in Blood* in 1986, he had transformed into one of metal's most precise drummers.

**What cymbals did Dave Lombardo use on Show No Mercy?**
Dave Lombardo used Zildjian A Series cymbals on *Show No Mercy* — completely different from the Paiste setup he would later be associated with. His configuration included Zildjian A 14-inch New Beat Hi-Hats, a 16-inch Medium Thin Crash, an 18-inch Medium Crash, a 20-inch Medium Ride, and an 18-inch China Boy High. The China cymbal was already central to his playing from the very start.

**How was Show No Mercy recorded?**
*Show No Mercy* was self-produced by Slayer at Track Recording Studio in Torrance, California in 1983. Budget: approximately $8,000. No click track was used — Lombardo played to the band, which is why tempos breathe and surge throughout. The microphone approach was utilitarian given the budget, and the dense mix reflects the guitar-forward aesthetic common to underground metal of the era.

**How does Dave Lombardo's Show No Mercy setup compare to Reign in Blood?**
The contrast traces Lombardo's entire developmental arc. *Show No Mercy* (1983): budget Ludwig kit, Zildjian A cymbals, $8,000 self-produced. *Reign in Blood* (1986): Tama Artstar II, Paiste 2002 and RUDE cymbals, Rick Rubin producing at Eldorado Studios. Playing evolved too: *Show No Mercy* has human tempo surges and raw imprecision; *Reign in Blood* delivers the same speeds with machine-like consistency. Together they document one of metal's most dramatic three-year developments.
