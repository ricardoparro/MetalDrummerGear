# Significant Other Drum Setup — John Otto's Gear on Limp Bizkit's #1 Album (1999)

> Complete breakdown of John Otto's drum gear on Limp Bizkit's Significant Other (1999). Pearl kit, Zildjian cymbals, Gibraltar hardware — the nu-metal setup behind 'Nookie', 'Break Stuff', and 'N 2 Gether Now'.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [John Otto](/llms/drummers/john-otto.md)
**Band / Album:** Limp Bizkit — *Significant Other* (1999)
**Genre:** Nu-Metal

## Overview

Released on June 22, 1999, *Significant Other* is Limp Bizkit's commercial breakthrough and one of the defining albums of the nu-metal era. Debuting at #1 on the Billboard 200 and selling over seven million copies in the United States, the album established Limp Bizkit as one of the most commercially powerful rock acts of the late 1990s — and placed John Otto's drumming at the center of one of rock radio's most dominant sounds.

Otto's role on *Significant Other* is inseparable from the album's success. The rhythmic architecture he builds across "Nookie," "Break Stuff," "Re-Arranged," and "N 2 Gether Now" synthesizes three distinct musical traditions: the weight and power of heavy rock, the groove-forward pocket of hip-hop, and the dynamic sensitivity of the jazz vocabulary he absorbed at the Douglas Anderson School of the Arts in Jacksonville. The result is a drum performance that could support Fred Durst's rap-rock delivery, satisfy metal audiences' appetite for aggression, and cross over to mainstream pop-rock radio simultaneously.

The production — helmed by DJ Lethal, Limp Bizkit's own turntablist — kept Otto's drums front and center in the mix. Rather than the heavily compressed, tightly gated drum sounds typical of late-1990s rock production, *Significant Other* let Otto's natural groove breathe. The kick and snare sit in a hip-hop-influenced pocket, slightly behind the grid, giving the album its characteristic forward momentum.

For the *Significant Other* sessions, Otto played a Pearl kit paired with Zildjian cymbals and Gibraltar hardware — the same configuration he had used through the *Three Dollar Bill, Y'all$* era and Limp Bizkit's early touring cycle. This was the final extended Pearl era before Otto began his transition toward the OCDP custom configuration he would use throughout the 2000s.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Masters / Professional Series (double bass configuration)
- **Snare:** Pearl snare, 14" x 5.5" or 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series and A Custom Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Pearl double bass pedal; Gibraltar Professional Series hardware; Zildjian sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (tom batter), Remo Powerstroke 3 (kick batter), Remo Coated Ambassador (snare batter)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high for projection and crack, with ghost note sensitivity preserved

For the *Significant Other* sessions, John Otto played a Pearl kit — the brand he had used through Limp Bizkit's formative years. Pearl drums were among the most widely used professional kits in rock and metal throughout the late 1990s, delivering the punchy, attack-focused tone that nu-metal production required. The kick drum needed to compete with down-tuned guitars without losing rhythmic clarity; Pearl's shell design delivered the focused attack for exactly this context.

Otto's double bass configuration with a clean four-tom spread kept fills fast and accessible rather than requiring navigation across complex multi-tom spreads — consistent with his groove-first philosophy and DJ Lethal's hip-hop influenced production approach.

Zildjian A Series and A Custom cymbals provided the projecting, cutting tone that commercial nu-metal production and live performance required. The 14" New Beat hi-hats delivered dry, articulate response for Otto's 16th-note hip-hop influenced patterns. The A Custom crashes — lighter and faster than standard A Series — gave quick-responding accent cymbals suited to syncopated crash placement derived from hip-hop programming methodology.

## Key Tracks

**"Nookie"** — Primary nu-metal groove template. Hip-hop pocket meets hard rock riffing. Ghost note hi-hat patterns reflect Otto's jazz training. Kick sits slightly behind the grid for forward momentum. Became Limp Bizkit's breakout single.

**"Break Stuff"** — Heaviest track on the album. Full double bass aggression. China cymbal accents the transitions. Pre-chorus build demonstrates Otto's dynamic escalation control.

**"Re-Arranged"** — Atmospheric ballad. Sparse, careful playing. Subtle ghost notes from Otto's jazz background add texture without weight. One of the strongest arguments for his dynamic range.

**"N 2 Gether Now" (ft. Method Man)** — Most hip-hop oriented track on the album. Kick pattern reflects drum machine programming. Otto's jazz/hip-hop background most explicitly audible.

## Recording Context

*Significant Other* was produced primarily by DJ Lethal, with the production approach prioritizing groove over strict tempo grid. Otto's kick and snare often sit slightly behind the beat — a deliberate technique borrowed from hip-hop drum machine programming that creates forward momentum.

The drum sound is characterized by a dry, punchy snare with minimal reverb, forward kick drum presence, and natural cymbal bleed — closer to the hip-hop tradition of present, live drum sounds than the heavily processed production that characterized much of 1990s alternative rock.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did John Otto use on Significant Other?**
A: John Otto played a Pearl kit on Significant Other (1999) — his primary configuration through the Three Dollar Bill era and Limp Bizkit's early touring cycle. His double bass setup used 22" kick drums, two rack toms (10", 12"), and two floor toms (14", 16"). Pearl's punchy, attack-focused shells suited the album's hip-hop influenced production. This was the late stage of his Pearl era before transitioning to OCDP custom drums. Full profile: [John Otto at MetalForge](/drummer/john-otto).

**Q: What cymbals did John Otto use on Significant Other?**
A: John Otto used Zildjian A Series and A Custom cymbals — his longstanding endorsement configuration. Setup: A 14" New Beat hi-hats, A Custom 16" crash, A 18" Medium crash, A 20" Medium ride, A 18" China. The A Series' projecting, cutting character suited both the studio recording and the live performance demands that Significant Other's commercial success generated. Full gear overview: [John Otto drum kit guide](/articles/john-otto-drum-setup).

**Q: How did John Otto's drumming make Significant Other commercially successful?**
A: Otto's jazz training gave the album a rhythmic sophistication that pure metal or rap-rock drummers couldn't deliver. His groove pocket — kicks and snare slightly behind the beat in hip-hop tradition — created the forward momentum that made "Nookie" and "Break Stuff" work across rock radio and hip-hop influenced audiences simultaneously. The ghost notes and dynamic range from his Douglas Anderson education added texture that made the album feel human rather than mechanically aggressive. This dual-audience appeal was the rhythmic engine behind 7 million US sales.

**Q: What made Significant Other's drum sound distinctive?**
A: Producer DJ Lethal placed Otto's drums in a hip-hop influenced groove pocket, with the kick and snare slightly behind the beat. The dry, punchy snare and natural room character gave the album a feel closer to hip-hop production than typical rock recording — a key factor in the album's crossover appeal. The Pearl kit's punchy attack suited this production approach perfectly.

**Q: What was Significant Other's chart performance?**
A: Significant Other debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 on June 22, 1999, and sold over seven million copies in the US — one of the fastest-selling rock albums of that year. It produced three major singles: "Nookie," "Break Stuff," and "Re-Arranged." Its commercial performance set the stage for Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water's record-breaking 2000 debut.
