# Significant Other Drum Setup: John Otto's Limp Bizkit 1999 Gear Breakdown

> Complete breakdown of John Otto's drum setup on Limp Bizkit's Significant Other (1999). Pearl drums, Zildjian A Series cymbals, Gibraltar hardware — the nu-metal rig behind "Nookie," "Break Stuff," and Limp Bizkit's #1 Billboard album.

## Overview

Released on June 22, 1999, Significant Other launched Limp Bizkit from hard-working rap-rock act into one of the biggest bands on the planet. The album debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 and sold over seven million copies in the United States alone — eventually certified 15×Platinum — making it one of the fastest-selling albums of 1999. At the center of that commercial explosion was John Otto's drumming: a precise synthesis of hip-hop groove, jazz-trained sensitivity, and metal weight that gave Limp Bizkit's music its rhythmic identity.

Tracks like "Nookie," "Break Stuff," "Re-Arranged," and "N 2 Gether Now" showcased a drummer capable of operating across wildly different contexts within a single record: the locked hip-hop groove of "Nookie," the explosive fill-heavy aggression of "Break Stuff," the dynamic restraint of "Re-Arranged," and the genuinely funky pocket of "N 2 Gether Now" featuring Method Man.

What distinguished Otto from his nu-metal contemporaries was his formal jazz education at Douglas Anderson School of the Arts in Jacksonville, Florida — absorbing jazz vocabulary, Cuban and Brazilian rhythms, and Afro-Cuban percussion techniques before applying them to the heaviest commercial rock of his generation. That training is audible throughout Significant Other: ghost notes adding texture to hi-hat patterns, kick drum placement sitting slightly behind the grid for forward momentum, and dynamic range control that makes the album's explosive moments land by contrast.

Producer Terry Date, working at NRG Studios in North Hollywood and Indigo Ranch Studios in Malibu, captured the drum performances with the clarity and punch the album's commercial reach required. The album's success — 15× Platinum, #1 Billboard 200, one of 1999's fastest-selling records — validated Otto's approach and established Limp Bizkit's drum sound as a nu-metal template.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Export / Reference (double 22" bass drums, maple shells)
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating, 14" x 6.5" maple
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — A New Beat 14" Hi-Hats, A 18" Medium Crash, A Custom 16" Fast Crash, A 20" Medium Ride, A 18" China, A 10" Splash
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Gibraltar Double Bass Pedal; Gibraltar 9607 Hi-Hat Stand; Gibraltar SC-4422 Boom Stands; Zildjian 5A Wood Tip sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear (kick), Remo Emperor Clear (toms), Remo Coated Ambassador (snare)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension — crack and sensitivity balanced for ghost notes and hard backbeats

### The Recording Setup: Pearl Drums with Nu-Metal Configuration

For the Significant Other recording sessions, John Otto played a Pearl drum setup with the double-bass configuration that defined his Limp Bizkit sound. The Pearl maple-shell construction provided the warmth and projection needed for Terry Date's production approach — a drum sound that could anchor hip-hop groove passages and explode into full-volume metal intensity within the same track.

The double 22-inch bass drum configuration was essential to Otto's playing style. Limp Bizkit's arrangements relied on double-kick patterns as a rhythmic foundation even during relatively moderate-tempo sections. Otto's jazz training informed exactly how he placed those kicks: not mechanically on the beat, but with the slight behind-the-bar displacement that separates hip-hop drum programming from strictly metronomic rock playing.

The two-rack-tom and two-floor-tom configuration (10", 12", 14", 16") gave Otto sufficient melodic range for the fills on "Break Stuff" and "N 2 Gether Now," while keeping the overall setup practical for live reproduction.

### Zildjian A Series: Nu-Metal Projection

John Otto used Zildjian A Series cymbals for the Significant Other sessions — the same cymbal endorsement he maintained throughout Limp Bizkit's commercial peak. The A Series' B20 bronze construction delivers bright, projecting tones with complex overtones. For Significant Other's dense production, the A Series' projection characteristics were essential: the cymbals needed to cut through Wes Borland's layered guitar processing and Sam Rivers' heavily amplified bass.

The New Beat 14" hi-hats gave Otto the articulation his hip-hop-influenced patterns required. The crash array — combining the 18" Medium Crash with the faster 16" A Custom Fast Crash — gave Otto different accent qualities for the album's varied contexts. The China cymbal's trash-tone provides the section-change punctuation most audible in "Break Stuff."

### Terry Date's Nu-Metal Production

Terry Date produced Significant Other with a clarity-first philosophy that preserved the rhythmic detail in Otto's performances. Working at NRG Studios in North Hollywood and Indigo Ranch in Malibu, Date captured the drums with close-miking and room ambience that maintained the pocket feel of Otto's hip-hop-placed kick drum placement — the behind-the-grid displacement that gives "Nookie" and "N 2 Gether Now" their forward momentum.

**Q: What cymbals did John Otto use on Significant Other?**

**Q: Who produced the drums on Significant Other?**
