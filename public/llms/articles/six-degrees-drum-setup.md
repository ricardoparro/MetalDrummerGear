# Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence Drum Setup — Mike Portnoy's Double Album Masterwork (2002)

> Complete breakdown of Mike Portnoy's drum gear on Dream Theater's Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence (2002). Discover the Tama Starclassic Maple kit, Sabian cymbals, DW pedals, and the polyrhythmic techniques behind the 42-minute title suite and 'The Glass Prison'.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup

**Band / Album:** Dream Theater — *Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence* (2002)
**Genre:** Progressive Metal

## Overview

Released on January 29, 2002, *Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence* is Dream Theater's most ambitious studio statement — a double album spanning nearly 100 minutes of music, anchored by a 42-minute, 8-movement title suite that explores the inner lives of six characters living with mental illness. The album bridges the emotional depths of *Metropolis Pt. 2: Scenes from a Memory* (1999) and the crushing heaviness of *Train of Thought* (2003), occupying a unique position in Dream Theater's discography where prog complexity and heavy instinct coexist in equal measure.

For Mike Portnoy, *Six Degrees* was a pivotal creative milestone in two distinct ways. Musically, the double album format demanded an unprecedented range of drumming approaches within a single recording — from the relentless, autobiographical intensity of "The Glass Prison" (the first entry in Portnoy's career-long AA suite) to the delicate, narrative-sensitive passages of the title suite's quieter movements. Personally, "The Glass Prison" marked the first time Portnoy addressed his alcoholism recovery through Dream Theater's music, setting the template for a multi-album autobiographical thread that would define his legacy.

The album was recorded at BearTracks Studios in Suffern, New York — the same facility that had produced *Scenes from a Memory* three years earlier — with John Petrucci and Portnoy self-producing for the first time. The return to BearTracks gave the sessions a familiar acoustic environment, but everything around it had evolved. Portnoy's playing style had grown more aggressive since SFaM, and the expanded scope of a double album required him to sustain peak performance across two full discs worth of material.

Disc 1 opens with "The Glass Prison" — 13 minutes of autobiographical intensity drawing on Portnoy's AA experience — and moves through "Blind Faith," "Misunderstood," "The Great Debate," and "Disappear." Disc 2 is dedicated entirely to the 42-minute *Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence* suite, eight movements tracing six lives affected by mental illness. The drumming across both discs required a player capable of serving both a metal album and a prog symphony in the same week.

This article documents every piece of gear Mike Portnoy used to record this landmark double album.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Snare:** Tama Tama Starclassic Maple Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Sabian — Sabian Artisan / HH Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW Double Pedal; DW 5000 Hi-Hat Stand; Tama Power Tower Rack; Tama 1st Chair; Vic Firth Mike Portnoy Signature
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), Remo Ambassador Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium to medium-high tension — authority with dynamic sensitivity

### Mike's 2002 Studio Kit: The Tama Starclassic Maple Evolved

By 2002, Mike Portnoy had spent several years with the Tama Starclassic Maple platform, and the *Six Degrees* sessions found him deepening his understanding of what those shells could offer. The midnight blue sparkle finish distinguished the configuration from the cherry fade of the *Scenes from a Memory* era — a visual marker of an evolved setup while the maple shell architecture remained constant.

Maple shells were the correct choice for an album with the emotional range of *Six Degrees*. The warmth and sustain of maple served both extremes: the pounding double-bass patterns of "The Glass Prison" needed the shell's low-end mass and resonance, while the measured, narrative-driven passages of the title suite required the same warmth to give quiet moments genuine weight rather than thinness.

The double 22"x18" bass drum configuration carried over directly from *Scenes from a Memory*, providing the air movement and sub-bass depth that Dream Theater's tunings demanded. For a double album where Disc 1 contained one of the most intensely personal tracks Portnoy had ever recorded and Disc 2 demanded 42 consecutive minutes of focused musical storytelling, the bass drum foundation needed to be unmovable and consistent.

The six-tom layout — from 8" rack tom to 16" floor tom — gave Portnoy the melodic fill range necessary for the title suite's eight movements. Each movement introduced new characters and emotional contexts, and the tom work throughout the suite is compositional rather than connective — fills that don't just move between sections but actually say something about the characters they accompany.

The Star-Cast mounting system's isolation of each drum from the rack preserved shell resonance across the full kit, ensuring that even with complex mounting arrangements for the double bass and extended cymbal positions, every drum sounded like itself.

### Snare for a Double Album

The Starclassic Maple snare that had served the *Scenes from a Memory* sessions continued into the *Six Degrees* recording, matched tonally to the kit and calibrated for the album's dynamic range. At 6.5" depth, it provided the body to cut through dense arrangements while retaining the sensitivity needed for the ghost-note work that defines the title suite's quieter movements.

The snare on "The Glass Prison" is tuned for authority — driving the track's powerful, confessional intensity with each backbeat carrying personal weight. But the same drum, in the hands of a drummer thinking narratively, adapts throughout the double album. The ghost notes that appear in softer passages of the title suite require a snare that speaks at low velocities without forcing the player to overcompensate.

Engineer sound was captured at BearTracks with the same close-mic approach that had served SFaM well — batter head top mic for attack, resonant head mic for wire sensitivity, blended to create a presence that sits in the midfield of the mix. For a 100-minute double album, midfield snare placement was essential — too prominent and it fatigues across two discs, too recessed and the rhythmic backbone of songs like "The Great Debate" loses definition.

Tuned in the medium-to-medium-high range, the snare balanced the warmth the maple concept demanded with enough tension to deliver decisive attacks on the album's most aggressive passages.

### The Sabian Setup: Transitional Era

For the *Six Degrees* sessions, Mike Portnoy maintained the Sabian Artisan and HH series configuration that had defined the *Scenes from a Memory* era. The Artisan line's dark, hand-hammered character was still the right sonic palette for music with the emotional complexity of a concept double album — brighter cymbals would have fought against the title suite's more introspective movements.

The 14" Artisan hi-hats delivered the warm articulation that the title suite required. In movements like "About to Crash (Reprise)" and "Solitary Shell," Portnoy's hi-hat technique is as compositional as his tom work — half-open hi-hats creating texture rather than simply marking time, tight closed patterns providing pulse without aggression. The Artisan's complex, layered voice made this expressive variety possible in a way that brighter cymbals wouldn't have permitted.

For "The Glass Prison" and the Disc 1 heavy tracks, the same Artisan cymbals proved adaptable. The crashes respond differently at high-velocity impacts — opening up more, sustaining longer — and Portnoy used this characteristic to mark the emotional peaks of the personal material with crashes that bloom rather than cut.

The Artisan 20" ride remained central to the quiet passages. Its warm bell tone — used extensively across the title suite's gentler sections — provided the shimmering, sustained quality that served as sonic punctuation between characters and movements. The bell is never aggressive on this album; it functions as a musical element rather than a rhythmic tool.

The HH Chinese added aggressive punctuation capability without requiring a full cymbal change — a critical resource for an album that moves between heavy and quiet within single tracks, sometimes within single minutes.

## Key Facts

- Recorded at BearTracks Studios, Suffern NY — same facility as Scenes from a Memory

- Self-produced for the first time by John Petrucci and Mike Portnoy
- Tama Starclassic Maple platform continuing from the Scenes from a Memory era

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Mike Portnoy use on Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence?**

A: Mike Portnoy recorded Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence (2002) on a Tama Starclassic Maple kit — the same shell platform he had used on Scenes from a Memory (1999), now configured with a midnight blue sparkle finish. The setup featured double 22"x18" bass drums and six toms ranging from 8" to 16". The Starclassic Maple's warm, resonant character served both the aggressive Disc 1 material and the dynamic range required across the 42-minute title suite on Disc 2. For more on his gear history, see the [Mike Portnoy drummer profile](/drummer/mike-portnoy).

**Q: What cymbals did Mike Portnoy use on Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence?**

A: For the Six Degrees sessions, Portnoy used the Sabian Artisan and HH series — a continuation of the cymbal setup he had used on Scenes from a Memory. The Artisan line's dark, hand-hammered character suited the emotional complexity of a concept double album. His setup included Artisan 14" hi-hats, multiple Artisan crashes (16", 17", 18"), an Artisan 20" ride, a Sabian HH 19" crash, and a Sabian HH 18" Chinese cymbal. The transition to the brighter Sabian AAX series would come with Train of Thought the following year. See the [Scenes from a Memory drum setup article](/articles/scenes-from-a-memory-drum-setup) for how this setup compared to the 1999 configuration.

**Q: What is 'The Glass Prison' and why is it significant in Portnoy's drum career?**

A: 'The Glass Prison' is the 13-minute opening track of Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence, and it represents the first chapter of Mike Portnoy's career-long AA suite — a multi-album autobiographical narrative about his struggle with and recovery from alcoholism. The track references earlier Dream Theater material ('The Mirror' and 'Lie' from Awake) and would be continued across several subsequent albums. The drumming is among the most intense of the Starclassic Maple era: sustained double-bass patterns, high-velocity fills, and an emotional commitment that reflected the track's deeply personal subject matter. It established a template for the way Portnoy used Dream Theater's music to process his own story.

**Q: How does the Six Degrees drum setup differ from Scenes from a Memory?**

A: The two setups share the Tama Starclassic Maple shell platform but differ in details. Six Degrees featured a midnight blue sparkle finish vs. the cherry fade of SFaM, and Portnoy's playing approach had evolved toward heavier technique by 2002. The Sabian Artisan cymbal setup was maintained from SFaM, but the recording context shifted — Six Degrees was self-produced by Petrucci and Portnoy for the first time, giving them full control over the drum sound direction. The most significant musical difference is the scope: Six Degrees required Portnoy to sustain peak performance across a double album's worth of material, demanding greater stamina and a broader stylistic range than the single-disc SFaM. See the [Scenes from a Memory drum setup article](/articles/scenes-from-a-memory-drum-setup) for the 1999 setup details.

**Q: What makes the Six Degrees title suite challenging to drum?**

A: The 42-minute Six Degrees of Inner Turbulence suite presents several distinct challenges. First, stamina: sustaining peak focus and physical consistency across 42 minutes of compositionally dense music is a different demand than any single-track performance. Second, range: the eight movements span multiple emotional contexts, each requiring a different drumming approach within a single continuous performance. Third, character-driven technique: each movement follows a different character dealing with mental illness, and Portnoy's playing adapts to each character's emotional state — requiring him to think as much as a film composer as a drummer. The hi-hat work alone demonstrates five or six distinct approaches across the suite's movements. See the [Train of Thought drum setup article](/articles/train-of-thought-drum-setup) for how the heavy pivot of 2003 contrasted with this approach.
