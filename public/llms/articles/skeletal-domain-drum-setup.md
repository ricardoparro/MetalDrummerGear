# Paul Mazurkiewicz's Drum Setup on Cannibal Corpse's Skeletal Domain (2014)

> Complete drum gear breakdown for Cannibal Corpse's A Skeletal Domain (2014). Paul Mazurkiewicz's Pearl Reference and Meinl rig under Mark Lewis's Audiohammer Studios production — the band's highest Billboard 200 debut to that point.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup

**Band / Album:** Cannibal Corpse — *Skeletal Domain* (2014)
**Genre:** Death Metal

## Overview

Released September 16, 2014 through Metal Blade Records, "A Skeletal Domain" (commonly shortened to "Skeletal Domain") is Cannibal Corpse's thirteenth studio album and the record that followed 2012's "Torture." After three consecutive albums produced by Erik Rutan, the band moved to producer Mark Lewis at Audiohammer Studios in Sanford, Florida — a change in production environment that gave Paul Mazurkiewicz's drumming a different sonic frame while the underlying gear and technique carried over unchanged.

The album became Cannibal Corpse's best commercial debut to that point, entering the Billboard 200 at #32 — surpassing even Torture's #38 two years earlier. That upward chart trajectory came without a single lineup change: George "Corpsegrinder" Fisher, Pat O'Brien, Rob Barrett, Alex Webster, and Mazurkiewicz remained the same five-piece unit that had recorded Kill, Evisceration Plague, and Torture, giving Skeletal Domain the benefit of a band with nothing to prove about chemistry and everything to prove about pushing its established sound further.

Bassist Alex Webster described the record as darker and more eerie in character than the band's recent output, and that atmosphere gave Mazurkiewicz's arrangements — including standout track "Kill or Become" — a slightly different emotional register to serve, even as the underlying technical demands (extended blast passages, tight interplay with Webster's bass) remained squarely in Cannibal Corpse's wheelhouse.

Behind the kit, nothing about Mazurkiewicz's rig needed to change: the Pearl Reference configuration, Meinl cymbal setup, and Pearl Eliminator double bass pedal that had powered three Rutan-produced albums carried over intact into the Audiohammer sessions. This article breaks down that rig, Mark Lewis's production approach, and how Skeletal Domain extends the arc that began with Torture two years earlier.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Pearl Reference (mature endorsement configuration, 2014) (Custom finish (Pearl endorsement era) finish)
- **Snare:** Pearl Pearl Free-Floating Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl — Meinl Classics Custom / Byzance series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Pearl Eliminator Double Bass Pedal; ddrum Acoustic Pro Triggers (live context); Vic Firth Paul Mazurkiewicz Signature
- **Heads:** Remo Coated Ambassador (batter), Remo Diplomat Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-bright for cut through dense, down-tuned guitar frequencies

### Pearl Reference, Unchanged Into a New Studio

Skeletal Domain marked the first time in three albums that Paul Mazurkiewicz's Pearl Reference kit was captured by a producer other than Erik Rutan, and Mark Lewis's Audiohammer sessions kept the physical setup entirely intact: dual 22" x 18" bass drums, the same compact four-tom layout, and the same maple/African mahogany hybrid shell construction that had defined the sound since the early 2000s.

Audiohammer Studios — a Florida facility with an established death metal production pedigree — gave Lewis a well-equipped environment to capture the kit, and the resulting drum tone sits comfortably alongside the Rutan-era albums: familiar low-end weight from the deep kick drums, familiar attack definition from the Reference shells, adapted to a new studio's room and microphone choices rather than a fundamentally different drum sound.

### The Free-Floating Steel Voice, Consistent Across Studios

Mazurkiewicz's 14" x 6.5" Pearl Free-Floating steel snare made the transition to Audiohammer Studios unchanged, and Mark Lewis's mix preserved the same priorities that had defined its sound across the Rutan-produced albums: a bright, cutting tuning built to punch through Cannibal Corpse's dense, down-tuned guitar mix without additional processing.

On a track like "Kill or Become," where the album's slightly darker atmosphere calls for the snare to carry both aggressive backbeat duty and eerie tonal weight, the Free-Floating design's open resonance gives Lewis more natural harmonic content to work with than a conventionally lugged snare would offer — useful for a producer working to differentiate his mix from three albums' worth of Rutan's established approach.

### Meinl: The Rig That Didn't Need Reinventing

Mazurkiewicz's Meinl cymbal setup — the same 14" hi-hats, dual crashes, 20" ride, and 18" china that had anchored Torture and Evisceration Plague — carried over unchanged into the Audiohammer sessions. Mark Lewis's mix leaned on the same fast-attack, controlled-decay character that had made Meinl's hand-hammered bronze construction a fit for Cannibal Corpse's dense arrangements across the previous decade.

Given Skeletal Domain's slightly darker, more eerie character, the china cymbal's aggressive punctuation takes on added weight — a familiar sonic tool deployed within a marginally different atmospheric context, rather than a change to the cymbal setup itself.
