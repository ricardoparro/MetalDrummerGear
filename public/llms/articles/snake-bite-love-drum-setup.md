# Snake Bite Love Drum Setup: Mikkey Dee's Gear on Motörhead's 1998 Album

> Discover the exact drum kit, cymbals, and gear Mikkey Dee used on Motörhead's Snake Bite Love (1998) — the last album produced by Howard Benson and one of the band's most rushed studio experiences. Complete breakdown of the Tama kit, Paiste cymbals, and the sessions Dee called stressful but the drums never show it.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Mikkey Dee](/llms/drummers/mikkey-dee.md)
**Band / Album:** Motörhead — *Snake Bite Love* (1998)
**Genre:** Heavy Metal / Hard Rock

## Overview

Released on 10 March 1998 through SPV/Steamhammer in Europe and CMC International in North America, *Snake Bite Love* is Motörhead's fourteenth studio album and Mikkey Dee's fourth full studio recording with the band, following [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup) (1993), [*Sacrifice*](/articles/sacrifice-drum-setup) (1995), and [*Overnight Sensation*](/articles/overnight-sensation-drum-setup) (1996). It also closed out an era: this was the final Motörhead album produced by Howard Benson, who had helmed all four of the band's records since Dee joined in 1992.

The sessions were tracked at The Valley, Benson's own recording studio in Woodland Hills, California — familiar ground by this point, but the timeline was anything but comfortable. Dee later described the making of *Snake Bite Love* as rushed, saying the band "were stressed out of our minds" and needed more time than the schedule allowed. Eleven tracks in under 45 minutes came together under real time pressure, with Lemmy Kilmister, Phil Campbell, and Dee leaning on years of road-tested chemistry to get through it. Campbell, in a nod to the era's most famous symbol-based credit dispute, was jokingly billed on the sleeve as "The Artist Frequently Seen at the Liquor Store," a parody of Prince's unpronounceable stage name.

Despite — or perhaps because of — the compressed schedule, *Snake Bite Love* found a respectable audience, reaching #9 on the UK Rock & Metal Albums chart, #22 on the UK Independent Albums chart, and charting in Germany (#47) and Sweden (#49). Joe Petagno's Snaggletooth artwork, redesigned for the album's cover, became one of the more striking variations on Motörhead's mascot across the band's catalog.

For Dee, the rushed sessions were as much a test of consistency as of chops. With no time to second-guess takes or experiment with new gear, he leaned entirely on the same Tama kit and Paiste cymbals that had carried him through the previous three albums — proven, reliable, and fast to dial in under pressure. This article breaks down that gear and the drumming that held together one of Motörhead's most time-crunched studio experiences.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Tama Artstar II / Swingstar (Black finish)
- **Snare:** Tama Tama Artstar II Steel Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Paiste — Paiste Giant Beat / Paiste 2002
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Tama Iron Cobra; Tama Iron Cobra Hi-Hat Stand; Roc-N-Soc Nitro Throne; Vic Firth 5B
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (batter), Remo Ambassador Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension for crack and projection, consistent with prior albums

### The Same Trusted Tama Kit, Under a Tighter Clock

With *Snake Bite Love*'s sessions compressed into a tight production window, Mikkey Dee brought the same Tama Artstar II/Swingstar kit he had used since *Bastards* (1993) — no experimentation, no new gear to break in. The 22" bass drum and 10"/12"/13" rack tom configuration, unchanged across three previous Motörhead albums, meant Dee could walk into The Valley and start tracking immediately, without the setup and tuning time a new kit would have demanded.

Howard Benson's own studio was well-suited to capturing this exact kit; having recorded Dee on it for *Bastards*, *Sacrifice*, and *Overnight Sensation*, Benson's engineering team already knew how to mic and mix these shells efficiently — a crucial advantage when the band was working against the clock rather than exploring new sonic territory.

Dee's playing on tracks like the album opener "Love for Sale" shows no sign of the time pressure the band was under; if anything, the rushed sessions forced a leaner, more economical approach to fills, with Dee favoring tight, purposeful tom work over extended showcases. The 16"/18" dual floor toms gave him the low-end weight to drive the record's heavier cuts, like "Assassin" and "Dead and Gone," without needing elaborate arrangements to make an impact.

In hindsight, the reliability of this exact Tama configuration — the same kit, the same studio relationship, four albums running — is precisely what allowed Motörhead to deliver a finished record despite a production timeline Dee himself considered too short.

### The Steel Snare That Cut Through a Rushed Mix

Dee's 14" x 6.5" steel-shell Artstar II snare — the same drum heard on his three previous Motörhead albums — carried extra importance on *Snake Bite Love*, where a compressed mixing schedule left little room for extensive re-tracking or drum replacement. The snare's bright, cutting steel tone gave engineers a dependable foundation to build the rest of the mix around, exactly the kind of consistency a rushed production needs.

On "Take the Blame" and the title track "Snake Bite Love," the snare's crack sits high and aggressive in the mix, tuned on the higher side of medium as Dee had done since *Bastards*. With Howard Benson's team already intimately familiar with this specific drum's tone from three prior albums, dialing in the snare sound took a fraction of the time it might have on a fresh production, freeing up studio hours for the record's more experimental cuts, like the funk-tinged "Joy of Labour."

The steel shell's cutting projection also meant Dee's ghost notes and backbeat accents remained audible even in a mix that, by Dee's own account, didn't get the polish time the band wanted. A less dependable snare might have gotten lost in that compression; this one didn't.

### Paiste Giant Beat and 2002: One Last Album Together

*Snake Bite Love* marks the last Motörhead album to feature Mikkey Dee's original Paiste Giant Beat/2002 pairing in its familiar early-career form — the same 15" Giant Beat hi-hats he had used since his first-ever Motörhead session on "Hellraiser" in 1992. Within two years, on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup), Dee's cymbal setup would begin to shift, but on this record the combination remained exactly what it had been for three prior albums: bright, cutting, and completely dialed in.

Under the rushed production schedule, that familiarity mattered. Dee's crash placement on tracks like "Dead and Gone" and "Better Off Dead" shows no hesitation — every cymbal choice lands with the confidence of a setup played hundreds of times on stage and in the studio. The 2002 series' CuSn8 bronze alloy continued to cut through Motörhead's dense guitar-and-bass wall without requiring additional EQ work, a small but real time-saver during sessions where time was the scarcest resource.

The China cymbal's trashy accents, used more sparingly here than on *Overnight Sensation*, punctuate the album's heaviest moments — "Assassin" in particular — without overloading a mix that Benson's team had precious little time to refine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Mikkey Dee use on Snake Bite Love?**

A: On *Snake Bite Love* (1998), Mikkey Dee used the same Tama Artstar II/Swingstar kit he had played since [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup) (1993) — a 22" bass drum, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, and 16"/18" floor toms, paired with a 14" x 6.5" Tama Artstar II steel snare. Under the album's compressed production schedule, Dee stuck with proven, dialed-in gear rather than experimenting with anything new. See the [Mikkey Dee drummer profile](/drummer/mikkey-dee) for his complete gear evolution.

**Q: Why did Mikkey Dee call the Snake Bite Love sessions rushed?**

A: Mikkey Dee has said the band was "stressed out of our minds" during the making of *Snake Bite Love*, describing a production schedule that didn't allow the time the band felt the record needed. Despite the compressed timeline, the album was completed and released on 10 March 1998, reaching #9 on the UK Rock & Metal Albums chart. It was also the final Motörhead album produced by Howard Benson, who had worked with the band since Dee's debut on [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup).

**Q: Was Snake Bite Love the last album Howard Benson produced for Motörhead?**

A: Yes. Howard Benson produced four consecutive Motörhead albums featuring Mikkey Dee — [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup) (1993), [*Sacrifice*](/articles/sacrifice-drum-setup) (1995), [*Overnight Sensation*](/articles/overnight-sensation-drum-setup) (1996), and *Snake Bite Love* (1998) — before the band moved to a new production team for [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup) (2000).

**Q: What cymbals did Mikkey Dee use on Snake Bite Love?**

A: Mikkey Dee used Paiste Giant Beat 15" hi-hats alongside Paiste 2002 crashes (18" and 20"), a 22" ride, and an 18" China cymbal on *Snake Bite Love*. This was the last Motörhead album to feature this exact Giant Beat/2002 pairing before his cymbal setup began to shift on [*We Are Motörhead*](/articles/we-are-motorhead-drum-setup) (2000).
