# Somewhere in Time Drum Setup: Nicko McBrain's Electronics-Augmented Kit on Iron Maiden's 1986 Synth-Era Masterpiece

> The complete gear breakdown for Iron Maiden's Somewhere in Time (1986). Discover the Premier Resonator kit, Roland electronic drum pads, and dry snare tuning that defined Nicko McBrain's performance on Iron Maiden's most electronically ambitious album.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Nicko McBrain](/llms/drummers/nicko-mcbrain.md)
**Band / Album:** Iron Maiden — *Somewhere in Time* (1986)
**Genre:** Heavy Metal / NWOBHM

## Overview

Released on September 29, 1986, Somewhere in Time marked a bold departure for Iron Maiden — and a unique chapter in Nicko McBrain's gear history. Following the mammoth World Slavery Tour behind Powerslave, the band embraced Roland guitar synthesizers for the first time, giving the album a futuristic texture unlike any prior Maiden record. To complement this electronic dimension, Nicko added Roland electronic drum pads alongside his Premier Resonator kit — using them primarily for kick drum sample reinforcement and percussion accents.

Produced by Martin Birch at Wisseloord Studios in Hilversum, Netherlands, the album's drum sound is distinctly different from Powerslave. Where Powerslave's drums had natural warmth from Compass Point's live room, Somewhere in Time's drums are tighter, drier, more controlled — shaped to sit alongside synthesized guitar textures without competing with them. Nicko tuned his snare notably flat and dry, producing a short, punchy crack that cut through the dense electronic production.

This is the one Iron Maiden album where electronics are genuinely audible in the drum mix — the one where Nicko's setup tells a different gear story from every other era.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Premier Resonator (Black Lacquer finish)
- **Configuration:** Single 22"x18" bass drum, 12" and 13" rack toms, 16" and 18" floor toms
- **Electronics:** Roland electronic drum pads (kick reinforcement and accent triggers) — first appearance in Nicko's Iron Maiden setup
- **Snare:** Premier 2000 Snare, 14" x 6.5" (steel shell) — flat, dry tuning
- **Cymbals:** Paiste 2002 — 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 16" Medium Crash, 18" Medium Crash, 20" Medium Ride, 18" China Type
- **Bass Drum Pedal:** Premier 252 single pedal — no double bass
- **Sticks:** Pro-Mark 5B
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), Remo Ambassador Clear (kick)
- **Snare tuning:** Low to medium tension, muffled — dry, flat, minimal sustain

### Premier Resonator Kit

Nicko recorded Somewhere in Time on a Premier Resonator kit — the professional British drumset that was his primary setup through the mid-1980s. Premier's birch shells delivered a punchy, focused attack suited to the tighter production Martin Birch was building around the album's synthesized textures. The shell character differed meaningfully from warmer maple alternatives — birch's quicker decay was exactly what Birch needed to seat the drums inside a dense arrangement of guitar synthesizers without muddiness.

### Roland Electronic Drum Pads

Somewhere in Time was the first Iron Maiden album where Nicko integrated electronic drum pads into his setup. Positioned alongside the acoustic kit, the Roland pads triggered sampled kick sounds for reinforcement — a technique increasingly common in mid-1980s professional studios. The electronic kick was blended subtly with the acoustic bass drum signal, adding punch and articulation without replacing the acoustic character.

### Flat, Dry Snare Tuning

The snare sound on Somewhere in Time is one of the album's most distinctive drum production choices. Tuned lower and drier than prior Maiden albums, the snare produces a flat, punchy hit with minimal sustain — cutting through synthesized guitars without adding ring that would muddy the dense arrangement. The steel Premier shell contributed to this character: cutting, midrange-heavy, with less harmonic spread than aluminum or brass.

### Paiste 2002 Cymbals

The full Paiste 2002 setup continued from Powerslave and Piece of Mind — the same professional series, different deployment. On "Wasted Years," the 20" ride bell's clear, singing tone cuts above the guitar synthesizer layers. "Heaven Can Wait" used Paiste crashes to build anthemic swells. The 14" hi-hats delivered consistent articulation across the album's range of tempos.

### Single Bass Drum

Nicko McBrain maintained his single-bass-drum philosophy on Somewhere in Time — as on every Iron Maiden album before and since. The Premier 252 single pedal drove the galloping rhythm that defined the band's identity, proving the technique remained fully expressive without a second pedal.

## Key Tracks

- **Caught Somewhere in Time** (160 BPM): Driving opener. Fast gallop with Roland kick reinforcement most audible. Cascading tom fills between guitar synthesizer runs.
- **Wasted Years** (126 BPM): Most enduring Iron Maiden 1980s single. Mid-paced, melodic — Nicko plays with restraint and purpose. Dry snare tuning most notable here.
- **Heaven Can Wait** (115 BPM): Anthemic, crowd-participation track. Crash cymbals build swells. Controlled, steady groove over feel and dynamics.
- **Alexander the Great** (130–155 BPM): 8-minute historical epic closing the album. Multiple sections from march rhythms to full metal. Floor toms carry the mid-section climax.

## FAQ

**What drum kit did Nicko McBrain use on Somewhere in Time?**
Premier Resonator kit — single 22"x18" bass drum, two rack toms (12", 13"), two floor toms (16", 18"), plus Roland electronic drum pads integrated for the first time in his Iron Maiden career. See [Nicko McBrain's full profile](/drummer/nicko-mcbrain) for career-wide gear history.

**Did Nicko McBrain use electronic drums on Somewhere in Time?**
Yes — Somewhere in Time (1986) is the first Iron Maiden album where Nicko integrated electronic drum pads. Roland pads were used for kick drum sample reinforcement in the album's synth-heavy production. No prior Iron Maiden album used electronic drum augmentation in this way. The [Powerslave drum setup article](/articles/powerslave-drum-setup) covers the preceding purely acoustic era.

**How does the drum sound on Somewhere in Time differ from Powerslave?**
Drier, tighter, more controlled. Powerslave was recorded at Compass Point Studios (Nassau) with natural room warmth; Somewhere in Time used Wisseloord Studios (Netherlands) with a closer-miking approach. The key change: Nicko tuned the snare flat and dry — short attack, minimal sustain — to cut through guitar synthesizers without ring. Roland kick reinforcement added punch that compensated for the reduced room ambience. Full comparison at [Powerslave drum setup](/articles/powerslave-drum-setup).

**What cymbals did Nicko McBrain use on Somewhere in Time?**
Paiste 2002 series throughout: 14" Medium Hi-Hats, 16" and 18" Medium Crashes, 20" Medium Ride, 18" China Type. Same series as Powerslave and Piece of Mind — the consistent backbone of his 1980s sound. The 20" ride bell's clarity on "Wasted Years" is a standout production moment. See the [Seventh Son drum setup article](/articles/seventh-son-drum-setup) for how this cymbal setup carried into the next album.

## Recording Notes

Martin Birch recorded Somewhere in Time at Wisseloord Studios, Hilversum, Netherlands. Drum recording used AKG D12 on kick (blended with Roland pad DI signal), Shure SM57 on snare (capturing the flat, dry tuning), Sennheiser MD421s on toms, and Neumann U87 overheads. Closer-miking than Powerslave reduced room ambience to suit the denser electronic arrangement. The Roland pad signal was blended subtly into the kick bus — enhancing punch without replacing acoustic character.
