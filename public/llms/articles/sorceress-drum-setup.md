## Overview

Released on September 30, 2016 through Nuclear Blast — Opeth's first album away from Roadrunner Records — *Sorceress* debuted at #1 in Sweden and Norway and reached #25 in the UK. It was produced by Tom Dalgety and Mikael Åkerfeldt at Rockfield Studios in Wales, and it is the prog-era record that brought the widest dynamic range back into Opeth's palette.

For Martin Axenrot, Sorceress was the culmination of the progression that [Heritage (2011)](/articles/heritage-drum-setup) had started and [Pale Communion (2014)](/articles/pale-communion-drum-setup) had deepened. He arrived at Rockfield in 2016 with five years of Heritage material internalized and three years of Pale Communion's confident prog-rock vocabulary behind him. Sorceress asked for something different from both predecessors: not restraint alone, but dynamic range — the ability to move between the softest jazz passages and significantly heavier sections without losing the prog-rock fluency the previous two albums had required.

New producer Tom Dalgety brought a different perspective than Steven Wilson or Jens Bogren had. His approach — shaped by work with Killing Joke, Pixies, and Royal Blood — gave Sorceress more presence and directness than Heritage or Pale Communion had carried. The guitar tones have slightly more edge, the arrangements more rhythmic assertiveness, and Axenrot's drums more dynamic contrast between quiet passages and heavier sections. The Pearl Reference Series kit and Meinl Byzance cymbals remained consistent, but Dalgety's production gave them more presence in the mix.

Sorceress is Opeth's most dynamically varied prog-era album — a record where Axenrot demonstrates the full range from Heritage's whisper-quiet brush passages to something approaching the assertive drumming of [Watershed (2008)](/articles/watershed-drum-setup), all within a single album framework.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Series (Natural / Gloss Lacquer, 6-ply maple/mahogany hybrid shells)
- **Configuration:** Single 22"x18" bass drum; 10" and 12" rack toms; 14" and 16" floor toms
- **Snare:** Pearl Reference Maple Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Traditional / Jazz series (expanded from Pale Communion)
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Pearl Demon Drive single pedal; Vic Firth 5A sticks; Vic Firth Heritage brushes
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), Remo Diplomat Snare Side (resonant)

### Pearl Reference Series: Third Consecutive Opeth Album

Sorceress continues Axenrot's Pearl Reference Series setup for the third consecutive Opeth album — a consistent platform that now carries five years of prog-rock context and a new producer's perspective. Tom Dalgety's approach at Rockfield Studios emphasized presence and clarity over the ambient warmth that had characterized Pale Communion's drum sound. The Pearl Reference shells' maple/mahogany hybrid construction delivered both the warmth appropriate to prog-rock passages and enough attack for the heavier sections Sorceress reintroduced.

The single 22" bass drum configuration remained unchanged. Sorceress occasionally asks more of the kick drum than its predecessors — the heavier sections that Dalgety's production encouraged need a kick with presence — and the Pearl Reference delivers. Familiarity with Rockfield's rooms from Pale Communion helped: Axenrot knew the space and how the kit responded in the live room.

### Meinl Byzance: Expanded Role in Heavier Sections

Sorceress marks a small but meaningful expansion of Axenrot's Byzance setup. The 19" Dark Crash — present on [Watershed](/articles/watershed-drum-setup) but absent from Heritage and Pale Communion — returns for Sorceress's heavier sections. This cymbal's trashy, aggressive voice had been unnecessary on Heritage and only occasional on Pale Communion, but Sorceress's more dynamic arrangement called for it.

Full setup: 14" Jazz Medium Hi-Hats, 16" Traditional Thin Crash, 18" Traditional Medium Thin Crash, 19" Dark Crash (reintroduced), 20" Jazz Thin Ride (primary), 22" Traditional Medium Ride (secondary). The result covers the full Axenrot spectrum: Heritage's atmospheric jazz-ride passages through something approaching the Watershed-era accent vocabulary.

### Tom Dalgety at Rockfield: More Presence, More Directness

Dalgety's production philosophy on Sorceress emphasized presence and directness over Wilson's ambient warmth. Drums sit slightly forward in the mix with more attack on snare and kicks and less reliance on Rockfield's natural room reverb. The result suits Sorceress's more dynamic arrangement — present enough for heavier sections, while maintaining the warmth required for quieter passages. Second consecutive album at Rockfield meant more efficient tracking and confidence in the environment.

## Five Years of Prog-Rock Development on Display

Sorceress is where the full arc of Axenrot's prog-era evolution becomes audible in a single album. Heritage asked him to learn something new. Pale Communion let him prove he had learned it. Sorceress shows how far the development had taken him.

The dynamic range across Sorceress — from "Will O the Wisp's" acoustic restraint to the title track's assertive groove — is the widest of the prog-era trilogy, navigated with the confidence of a drummer who has thoroughly internalized a vocabulary that was foreign to him five years earlier.

**Key tracks:**
- **Sorceress** (title track) — Most assertive prog-era groove; heavier riffs, Dalgety's more present production
- **The Ward** — Most dynamically varied track; full range from quiet to assertive within one arrangement; most sophisticated fill work of the trilogy
- **Will O the Wisp** — Acoustic restraint; brush work demonstrating Heritage vocabulary within Sorceress context
- **Era** — Prog-rock confidence at its most sustained; ride cymbal lead voice throughout

The return of slightly heavier riffs gave Axenrot permission to reach for the parts of his vocabulary Heritage and Pale Communion had rarely required. The assertive snare backbeat on "Sorceress," the 19" Dark Crash reappearing for accent moments, the kick carrying more weight in Dalgety's more present production — these are small returns of the Watershed vocabulary, applied with five years of prog-rock context behind them. Sorceress proves the restraint of Heritage was always a choice, not a limitation.

## Sorceress in Context

Sorceress consolidates Opeth's progressive identity and completes the prog-era arc that Heritage and Pale Communion traced. As Opeth's first Nuclear Blast album, it also marks a label transition that signaled the band's long-term commitment to the direction: Nuclear Blast had no expectation of death metal returning. ## FAQ

**Q: What drum setup did Martin Axenrot use on Sorceress?**
Martin Axenrot used a Pearl Reference Series kit for Opeth's Sorceress (2016), recorded at Rockfield Studios in Monmouth, Wales. Configuration: single 22"x18" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms. Cymbals were Meinl Byzance series with a 20" Jazz Thin Ride as primary cymbal, 14" Jazz Medium Hi-Hats, 16" and 18" crashes, and a reintroduced 19" Dark Crash for heavier accents (absent from Heritage and Pale Communion). Produced by Tom Dalgety and Mikael Åkerfeldt.

**Q: How does Sorceress differ from Heritage and Pale Communion?**
Sorceress (2016) has greater dynamic range than [Heritage (2011)](/articles/heritage-drum-setup) and [Pale Communion (2014)](/articles/pale-communion-drum-setup). Producer Tom Dalgety's more direct approach gave drums and guitars more presence in the mix. Slightly heavier riffs return in sections, and Axenrot's drumming reflects this: more assertive backbeats on the title track and "The Ward," the reintroduction of the 19" Byzance Dark Crash, and more dynamic contrast between quiet and loud sections within arrangements. The Pearl Reference Series kit and core Byzance setup remained consistent across all three albums; the change was in production approach and musical demands.

**Q: Why did Opeth move to Nuclear Blast for Sorceress?**
Opeth's move to Nuclear Blast for Sorceress (2016) followed the end of their Roadrunner Records relationship after eleven years and five studio albums (Ghost Reveries through Pale Communion). Nuclear Blast — known for Nightwish, Dimmu Borgir, Amon Amarth, and Meshuggah — specializes in heavy music while being comfortable with Opeth's identity as a band no longer making death metal. The partnership continued with In Cauda Venenum (2019) and Last Will and Testament (2024). For Martin Axenrot's full career across all these albums, see his [drummer profile](/drummer/martin-axenrot).

**Q: What makes Martin Axenrot's drumming on Sorceress distinctive?**
Sorceress is distinctive for demonstrating the full dynamic range Axenrot developed across the prog-era trilogy. After Heritage's sustained restraint and Pale Communion's growing confidence, Sorceress allowed him to reach further in both directions simultaneously — quieter brush passages and more assertive heavier sections within the same album. "The Ward" shows the most sophisticated fill work of the trilogy, while "Will O the Wisp" proves the Heritage sensitivity remained intact alongside the more dynamic sections. Tom Dalgety's production gave both the presence in the mix that made the dynamic contrast audible.
