# Lars Ulrich's Drum Setup on Metallica's St. Anger (2003)

> Complete breakdown of Lars Ulrich's drum setup on Metallica's St. Anger (2003) — including the most controversial snare in metal history: a DW Collector's Series maple snare tuned ultra-tight with the snare wires intentionally disengaged, producing the polarising metallic 'clang' that defined the album.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Lars Ulrich](/llms/drummers/lars-ulrich.md)
**Band / Album:** Metallica — *St. Anger* (2003)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Nu Metal

## Overview

Released on June 5, 2003, on Elektra Records (Vertigo internationally), Metallica's eighth studio album St. Anger debuted at #1 in 30 countries and arrived as one of the most context-dependent records in heavy metal history. The sessions that produced it were documented extensively in the feature documentary Some Kind of Monster — and that documentary, more than any prior Metallica release, framed how listeners heard the album. James Hetfield entered rehab partway through the writing period, founding bassist Jason Newsted had left the band in January 2001, and producer Bob Rock — who had produced every Metallica album since 1991's Black Album — stepped in to play bass on the record itself. Robert Trujillo joined the band only after recording was complete. This was the last Metallica album Bob Rock would produce.

The drum setup at the centre of those sessions belonged to Lars Ulrich, and the snare sound he committed to became the most polarising single percussive choice in modern metal. For St. Anger, Lars used a custom DW Collector's Series Maple kit assembled at the band's San Francisco facility — HQ, located inside the Presidio — and tuned his snare drum ultra-tight with the snare wires intentionally disengaged. The result is a dry, metallic "clang" — frequently described as a trashcan lid — that runs through every track on the record. It is not a recording mistake, not the product of broken hardware, and not the result of a bad room: it is a deliberate aesthetic decision, made by Lars and Bob Rock, that the band has defended in interviews ever since. The intent was an industrial, raw, almost garage-band character — a deliberate rejection of the polished groove tones the band had cultivated on the Black Album, Load, ReLoad, and Garage Inc.

The custom DW Collector's Series Maple kit itself was a substantial rig: dual 22"x18" bass drums, a full rack and floor tom configuration, a black finish, and DW's flagship North American Hard Rock Maple shell construction. Cymbals came from Lars's longstanding Zildjian endorsement — a Z Custom and A Custom hybrid setup built for cut and aggression. Pedals were Tama Speed Cobra HP910s — Lars's signature pedal era. Sticks were the aluminium-core Ahead Lars Ulrich signatures he has used for decades, and heads were a full Evans pack: EC2 on toms, Heavyweight on the snare batter, EMAD on the kicks.

This article documents the kit, the snare configuration, and the production decisions that produced the St. Anger drum sound — including a direct answer to the question that has been asked about this record for more than twenty years: yes, the snare really does sound like that on purpose.

### Custom DW Collector's Series Maple: The Presidio Kit

For the St. Anger sessions, Lars Ulrich moved away from the Tama Artist Maple and Starclassic kits he had used through the Black Album, Load, and ReLoad eras and worked with DW to assemble a custom Collector's Series Maple kit at HQ — the Metallica-owned facility located inside the Presidio in San Francisco. The kit was finished in black with chrome hardware, matching the visual aesthetic the band had established for the record.

The configuration was a true double bass setup — two 22" x 18" kick drums rather than a single kick with double pedal. This was a deliberate choice for St. Anger's relentless, often surf-tempo-meets-thrash kick patterns: two independent drums project two independent attacks, with the physical mass of dual shells producing a heavier low-end footprint in the room than a single kick with double pedal can replicate. Tracks like "Frantic," "St. Anger," and "Some Kind of Monster" depend on this kind of sustained dual-kick weight.

The tom configuration was deliberately spartan compared to Lars's Black Album and Load-era spreads. A single 12" rack tom over the hi-hat side, with two floor toms (14" and 16") on the right, reduced the kit to its functional core for a record whose drum sound philosophy was about raw impact rather than melodic tom orchestration. DW's Hard Rock Maple shell construction provided the attack and projection necessary for the dry, close-miked production approach the album required.

### The Most Controversial Snare Sound in Metal History

The snare drum on St. Anger is the single most discussed piece of metal percussion of the 2000s, and the conversation has not stopped in twenty-plus years. The instrument itself is a DW Collector's Series 14" maple snare — quality gear by any standard, the kind of drum that produces a textbook fat, articulate crack in normal use. On St. Anger it does not sound like that, and the reason is straightforward: Lars and Bob Rock tuned the batter head extremely tight and then deliberately disengaged the snare wires for the recording.

A standard snare drum sound — the crack, the buzz, the "snare-ness" of a snare — comes from the wires stretched across the underside of the bottom head vibrating sympathetically when the batter head is struck. Remove that wire interaction and the drum no longer behaves as a snare: it behaves as a small, tight tom-tom. Combine that with an ultra-high batter head tension and the result is exactly what is heard on St. Anger: a metallic, ringing "clang" with a sharp, almost percussive-industrial character — frequently and not unfairly compared by listeners to a trashcan lid or a steel pipe being struck.

This was a deliberate aesthetic choice. The St. Anger production brief, as documented in Some Kind of Monster and in Bob Rock and Lars Ulrich's later interviews, was for a raw, industrial, almost garage-band drum sound — a hard rejection of the polished, room-mic-driven Black Album and Load tones. Disengaging the snare wires was the most direct way to deliver that aesthetic. It is also the choice that polarised the metal audience more than any other single production decision in the band's history. Some fans hear it as a bold, brave commitment to a raw sonic identity. Others consider it the band's worst single creative decision. Both reactions are valid responses to a sound that was, by design, supposed to provoke.

The snare appears in this configuration on every track of the record. There was no "corrected" version mixed for radio singles. What was committed to tape was what shipped.

### Zildjian Z Custom + A Custom: Cut Over Subtlety

Lars's St. Anger-era cymbal setup was built around his longstanding Zildjian endorsement, blending the heavier, brighter Z Custom series with the more musical A Custom line. The Z Custom B20 alloy is computer-lathed for a consistently bright, projecting voice — well-suited to the close-miked, dry, frequency-dense St. Anger mix where cymbal frequencies needed to cut without the help of room ambience.

The 14" A Custom hi-hats were a continuation of the hi-hat size Lars had used for years — the bright, defined articulation he relies on for fast pattern work and accent transitions. The crash array mixed Z Custom and A Custom voices: heavier Z Custom crashes for sharp, aggressive sectional accents matching the album's raw character, with A Custom 18" providing a more musical crash voice for transitions that needed slightly more body.

The Z Custom 20" ride and A Custom 18" China rounded out the array. The Z Custom ride delivers the bright, cutting bell definition needed for the album's aggressive ride passages, while the A Custom China provides the trashy accent voice that punctuates St. Anger's frequent riff transitions and breakdown moments.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does St. Anger snare sound different?**

A: The St. Anger snare sounds different because Lars Ulrich and producer Bob Rock intentionally disengaged the snare wires on the bottom head and tuned the batter head extremely tight for the entire recording. With the wires off, the drum no longer produces the buzzing crack that defines a normal snare — it produces a dry, metallic "clang" that listeners frequently compare to a trashcan lid. This was a deliberate aesthetic decision aligned with the album's raw, industrial production brief, not a recording mistake or a hardware problem. The sound has been one of the most polarising production choices in modern metal since the album's 2003 release.

**Q: Did Lars use snare wires on St. Anger?**

A: No. The snare wires were intentionally disengaged for the entire St. Anger recording. The drum used was a DW Collector's Series 14" maple snare — a quality professional instrument that produces a textbook fat snare crack in normal use. For St. Anger, the wires were turned off and the batter head was tuned to extremely high tension, producing the metallic "clang" sound heard across every track on the album. This was confirmed by Lars Ulrich and Bob Rock in interviews and documented visually in the Some Kind of Monster film. It was not a recording mistake — it was an explicit aesthetic choice the band has consistently defended for more than twenty years.

**Q: What drum kit did Lars Ulrich use on St. Anger?**

A: Lars Ulrich used a custom DW Collector's Series Maple kit on St. Anger — a departure from the Tama Artist Maple and Starclassic kits he had used through the Black Album, Load, and ReLoad eras. The kit was finished in black with chrome hardware and configured with two 22"x18" bass drums (a true double bass setup, not a single kick with double pedal), a 12" rack tom, and 14" and 16" floor toms. Shells were DW's flagship North American Hard Rock Maple construction. Cymbals were a Zildjian Z Custom and A Custom hybrid, pedals were Tama Speed Cobra HP910, sticks were Ahead aluminium-core signatures, and heads were Evans throughout (EMAD on kicks, EC2 on toms, Heavyweight on the snare batter). The kit was assembled at HQ — Metallica's San Francisco facility inside the Presidio — for the album sessions.

**Q: Was the St. Anger snare sound intentional?**

A: Yes. The St. Anger snare sound was an entirely intentional production decision made by Lars Ulrich and producer Bob Rock. The brief for the album was a raw, industrial, garage-band drum sound that rejected the polished, room-mic-heavy approach of the Black Album, Load, and ReLoad. Disengaging the snare wires and tuning the batter head ultra-tight was the most direct way to deliver that aesthetic. The decision is documented extensively in the Some Kind of Monster film, has been defended in numerous interviews by both Lars and Bob Rock, and was committed to across the entire record — there is no "corrected" version of any track. Listener reception has been deeply polarised since release, but the band has never described the sound as a mistake; it was the sound they meant to make.
