## Overview

When Testament recorded "The Gathering" in 1999, they made a decision that raised eyebrows across the thrash metal world: hire Dave Lombardo — Slayer's iconic drummer — as their session player. The result was Testament's most critically acclaimed album and one of the most significant drummer crossover moments in extreme metal history.

Dave Lombardo, best known as Slayer's drummer, recorded The Gathering with Testament in 1999 after the band found themselves between drummers. Chuck Billy, Eric Peterson, and Alex Skolnick wanted someone who could match the album's increasingly technical, death-metal-influenced direction — and Lombardo, fresh from his first reunion with Slayer and well into his Tama Starclassic Bubinga era, delivered a performance that pushed Testament into territory they had never fully explored.

Produced by James Murphy — himself a former Testament guitarist with deep roots in technical death metal — and recorded at Morrisound Recording in Tampa, Florida (the same studio responsible for Death's definitive albums), "The Gathering" sounds unlike any Testament record before it. The death metal production environment shaped every aspect of the drum sound: tight, punchy, technically precise, with Lombardo's characteristic intensity applied to riffs that demanded it.

This article breaks down the gear Dave Lombardo used on "The Gathering," contextualizes the crossover event within both his career and Testament's discography, and explains why this album remains the definitive answer to "Did Dave Lombardo ever record with a band other than Slayer?" ### Tama Starclassic Bubinga: Lombardo's 1999 Flagship

By 1999, Dave Lombardo had moved from the Pearl Masters Custom that defined his final Slayer recordings into Tama's Starclassic Bubinga series — a professional flagship built around bubinga wood shells that delivered exceptional density, projection, and a distinctive fundamental tone unlike anything in maple or birch.

Bubinga is a dense African hardwood that produces drums with pronounced low-end punch, fast attack, and exceptional sustain control. Where maple shells resonate warmly and birch focuses aggressively, bubinga sits in its own category: authoritative, immediate, and darkly powerful. On "The Gathering" — an album pushing Testament toward death metal's technical density — the bubinga shells' naturally compressed character proved ideal. The kick drums hit with the kind of focused, chest-thumping authority that James Murphy's Morrisound production could capture and emphasize.

Lombardo's twin bass drum configuration remained constant throughout his career. Since his earliest Slayer work, he has favored two independent bass drums over a double pedal — a choice that gives each kick drum its own resonant chamber and character, rather than a single drum hit from alternating sides. At "The Gathering" tempos, which frequently push into blast-adjacent territory on tracks like "3 Days in Darkness," this setup provided the physical independence and endurance that the performances demanded.

The four-tom spread — 10", 12", 14", 16" — gave Lombardo a melodic fill vocabulary suited to Testament's compositionally complex arrangements. Unlike the more straightforward thrash structures of early Slayer records, "The Gathering" features riff architecture that required drums to navigate through technical guitar passages, and the tom tuning was adjusted accordingly: tighter and more attack-focused than his Slayer setup to cut through James Murphy's dense production.

### Pearl Sensitone: Precision for Technical Death Territory

For the precise, cutting snare sound "The Gathering" required, Dave Lombardo paired his Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit with a Pearl Sensitone Steel snare — a drum engineered for maximum sensitivity, articulation, and cutting power across high-tempo passages.

The steel shell delivers a bright, aggressive crack with a tight, focused fundamental — exactly what a death-influenced thrash metal production demands when the snare needs to cut through down-tuned guitar walls and dense double-bass patterns. At Morrisound Recording, James Murphy's production approach captured the snare with precision: prominent in the mix, with enough transient information to communicate every dynamic nuance in Lombardo's playing.

At 14" x 6.5", the drum offered the depth needed for crushing backbeats without sacrificing the sensitivity required for the ghost notes and articulations that define Lombardo's playing even at extreme tempos. On tracks like "D.N.R. (Do Not Resuscitate)" and "The Gathering," the snare sits at the center of mixes that are simultaneously brutal and technically detailed — a balance that reflects both Lombardo's metronomic consistency and Murphy's production philosophy.

The Sensitone's character was deliberately distinct from the Zildjian-focused cymbal palette surrounding it: where the cymbals provided complexity and shimmer, the snare delivered authority. Every backbeat on "The Gathering" sounds like a statement.

### Zildjian A Series: Dark Authority for the Morrisound Environment

Dave Lombardo's cymbal choice for "The Gathering" sessions reflected a shift from the Paiste RUDE/2002 series that had defined his early Slayer recordings toward Zildjian's A series — hand-hammered, warm, complex cymbals with more overtone complexity and musical character than Paiste's deliberately aggressive RUDE series.

The Zildjian A series suited Morrisound Recording's sonic environment. Where Paiste RUDE cymbals cut with raw, almost harsh aggression — perfect for Reign in Blood's monolithic sonic assault — the A series delivered a more nuanced attack with complex decay. In the context of "The Gathering's" death-influenced arrangements, that complexity worked with the music rather than against it.

The A 14" hi-hats provided warm, articulate patterns across tempos that ranged from mid-paced thrash to near-blast territory. Testament's guitarists were executing technically demanding riff patterns, and Lombardo's hi-hat work needed to complement rather than compete with that texture. The A series' hand-hammered variation produced a more human, responsive sound than computer-assisted designs — each stroke slightly different from the last, maintaining musical interest across extended passages.

The three-crash configuration — 16", 18", 19" — offered Lombardo graduated options for the album's dynamic range. The 18" China added the cutting, aggressive accent that the album's most aggressive passages demanded: "3 Days in Darkness" in particular features China usage that mirrors the guitar's percussive, low-register attack patterns.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Did Dave Lombardo play on a Testament album?**

A: Yes — Dave Lombardo, best known as Slayer's drummer, recorded The Gathering with Testament in 1999 as a guest/session drummer. The Gathering is Testament's most critically acclaimed album and represents Lombardo's most prominent non-Slayer studio recording. He used his Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit, Pearl Sensitone snare, and Zildjian A series cymbals for the sessions at Morrisound Recording in Tampa, Florida, produced by James Murphy.

**Q: What gear did Dave Lombardo use on The Gathering?**

A: Dave Lombardo recorded The Gathering (1999) using a Tama Starclassic Bubinga drum kit — a professional flagship built around dense African bubinga wood shells. His configuration included twin 22" x 18" bass drums (his signature independent setup), 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. His snare was a Pearl Sensitone Steel at 14" x 6.5". Cymbals were Zildjian A Series — hand-hammered for complex, warm character distinct from the Paiste RUDE cymbals of his early Slayer recordings. He used Tama Iron Cobra single pedals on each bass drum.

**Q: How does Lombardo's work on The Gathering differ from his Slayer recordings?**

A: The Gathering (1999) captures Lombardo in a fundamentally different sonic context from his Slayer work. His Slayer albums — especially Reign in Blood (1986) and Seasons in the Abyss (1990) — were built around velocity, impact, and Rick Rubin's monolithic production approach. The Gathering, produced by James Murphy at Morrisound Recording, prioritized articulation within intensity: every note audible, every pattern detailed. His gear also differed — Tama Starclassic Bubinga and Zildjian A series rather than the Paiste RUDE cymbals of Reign in Blood. The result is a Lombardo performance that demonstrates his technical range beyond Slayer's specific sonic demands.

**Q: Why did Testament choose Dave Lombardo to drum on The Gathering?**

A: Testament chose Dave Lombardo for The Gathering (1999) because the album's increasingly technical, death-metal-influenced direction required a drummer who could match that intensity while bringing credibility to the crossover moment. Lombardo was between Slayer obligations and had demonstrated his technical range with Grip Inc. (with Gus G.) during his 1990s Slayer hiatus. Producer James Murphy — himself a former Testament guitarist with deep technical death metal roots — understood that Lombardo's velocity, precision, and reputation would elevate what Testament was attempting. The combination worked: The Gathering became the band's most critically acclaimed album.
