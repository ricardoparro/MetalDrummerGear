## Overview

Released on September 27, 2011, Mastodon's *The Hunter* marked a deliberate commercial and stylistic pivot following the dense, conceptually demanding *Crack the Skye* (2009). Produced by Mike Elizondo — best known for his work in hip-hop production with Dr. Dre and Eminem — *The Hunter* embraced shorter, more direct song structures while retaining the band's technical mastery. The result was Mastodon's most radio-friendly album to date: accessible hooks, compressed arrangements, and a production sheen that widened the band's audience significantly.

For [Brann Dailor](/drummer/brann-dailor), *The Hunter* represented a creative reorientation. After the sprawling double-kick polyrhythmic architecture of [Crack the Skye](/articles/crack-the-skye-drum-setup), the new album demanded a different kind of drumming — groove-centred, song-serving, and melodically direct. Dailor's fills remained characteristically flowing and melodic, but the context shifted from concept-album complexity to radio-ready momentum. The album opened the chapter that would reach its fullest expression on [Emperor of Sand](/articles/emperor-of-sand-drum-setup) six years later.

*The Hunter* also earned Mastodon their first Grammy Award: **"Black Tongue" won Best Metal Performance at the 55th Grammy Awards (2013)**, confirming that the commercial pivot had not come at the cost of critical credibility. The win also demonstrated that Dailor's groove-forward approach — less technically ostentatious than *Crack the Skye* but equally sophisticated in its musical intelligence — resonated with the wider music community.

Mike Elizondo's production brought a tighter, punchier drum sound to *The Hunter* compared to Brendan O'Brien's more open, atmospheric work on *Crack the Skye*. The kit sits in the mix with more definition and less ambient bloom — a choice that suited the album's shorter, punchier songs and its commercial ambitions. This article breaks down every piece of gear Dailor used during *The Hunter* sessions: the Tama Starclassic Maple kit that became his defining instrument, the Meinl Byzance cymbals that bridged the *Crack the Skye* and *Emperor of Sand* eras, and the hardware that drove one of Mastodon's most commercially significant performances.

### The Tama Starclassic Maple: Brann's Groove-Era Foundation

For *The Hunter* sessions, Brann Dailor used his Tama Starclassic Maple kit — the warm, resonant platform he had been developing across the *Crack the Skye* touring cycle. The Starclassic Maple is Tama's flagship maple-shell series, featuring 100% pure maple shells with the Star-Cast mounting system that allows shells to vibrate freely without the restriction of traditional lug mounting. This free resonance is precisely what Dailor's melodic drumming philosophy requires: shells that sing with musical pitches, not just percussive noise.

*The Hunter* required a different expressive mode from the kit than *Crack the Skye* had demanded. The shorter, more direct song structures called for a setup that could groove with conviction and serve the songs' commercial hooks without sacrificing the melodic tom work that defines Dailor's style. The Starclassic Maple's warm fundamental tone was ideal — it provided the resonance for melodic fills to register musically while also producing the punch and definition that a more groove-forward production style needed.

The expanded tom configuration on *The Hunter* — adding an additional floor tom compared to the *Crack the Skye* setup — gave Dailor a wider melodic palette for his characteristic cascading patterns. The additional floor tom extended the lower register of his melodic range, enabling tom sequences that traversed a broader pitch span. This expansion reflected the album's sonic ambition: bigger, bolder arrangements that could carry FM radio and reach audiences beyond the progressive metal underground.

The single bass drum configuration continued from the transition Dailor was making away from the double-kick architecture of *Crack the Skye*. On *The Hunter*, the single 22" kick, tuned for a round, full fundamental, anchors the album's groove-oriented playing with a warmth and directness that double kick would have complicated. For the technical comparison with the Crack the Skye setup, see the [Brann Dailor drummer profile](/drummer/brann-dailor).

### The Snare: Punch and Projection for Radio-Ready Metal

On *The Hunter*, the snare drum had to fulfil a more commercially direct brief than on the previous two Mastodon albums. Mike Elizondo's production style — shaped by years of crafting punchy, well-defined drum sounds in hip-hop contexts — demanded a snare with sharp attack and clean definition. Dailor's Tama snare on this album delivers: full crack with enough body to sit in the mix assertively, enough sensitivity for the ghost note work that runs beneath even *The Hunter*'s most direct songs.

The 14" x 6.5" configuration provides the depth for a full, authoritative backbeat — important when the album's songs were being designed with FM radio in mind. On tracks like "Curl of the Burl" and "Black Tongue" (the Grammy-winning Best Metal Performance), the snare needed to lock in the groove with the same conviction it provided on the more complex passages of *Crack the Skye*, but in a more direct rhythmic context.

Ghost notes remain a central element of Dailor's playing even on *The Hunter*'s more straightforward material. The snare's medium-high tension tuning allows those ghost notes to register as intentional rhythmic texture rather than disappearing into the mix. The combination of Elizondo's punch-forward production philosophy and Dailor's sensitivity-aware tuning approach produced a snare sound that is both commercially assertive and musically nuanced. For the full evolution of Dailor's snare approach across Mastodon's catalogue, see the [Brann Dailor drummer profile](/drummer/brann-dailor).

### Meinl Byzance: Carrying the Dark Palette Forward from Crack the Skye

Brann Dailor's Meinl Byzance cymbal palette on *The Hunter* carried forward the dark, complex B20 bronze setup he had established on [Crack the Skye](/articles/crack-the-skye-drum-setup). The Byzance series — hand-hammered and lathed using traditional Turkish techniques — produces cymbals with warm, complex overtone development and a dark, blooming character that sits far from the bright, cutting sound of more common metal cymbal choices.

For *The Hunter*'s commercial context, the Byzance palette served a particularly interesting function. Elizondo's production approach brought a more defined, punchy overall drum sound, but Dailor's Byzance cymbals provided the musical warmth and overtone complexity that prevented the drum sound from becoming purely mechanical. The crashes bloom rather than slash — important when songs are shorter and each cymbal accent carries more structural weight. A bright, aggressive cymbal in this context would have dominated the mix; the Byzance's dark, blooming character adds emotional colour without imposing aggression.

The 14" Traditional hi-hats — Dailor's long-time choice — produce the intricate open-close variations that his groove-oriented playing demands. Even on *The Hunter*'s most direct songs, Dailor's hi-hat work retains a musicality and complexity that distinguishes his playing from purely functional groove drumming. The warm, complex sound of the Traditional hi-hats means that even quick, articulate patterns retain musical quality rather than becoming mechanical noise.

The ride cymbal's washy body and complex bell worked well for the album's more expansive passages — moments where Dailor moved from groove-oriented playing to the melodic, atmospheric drumming that had defined *Crack the Skye*. Even on a more commercially focused album, those passages benefit from a ride that adds textural colour rather than simply marking time. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums does Brann Dailor use on The Hunter?**

A: Brann Dailor recorded Mastodon's The Hunter (2011) using a Tama Starclassic Maple drum kit. The configuration featured a single 22 inch bass drum with three rack toms at 10, 12, and 13 inches, plus an expanded floor tom setup including both a 14 inch and 16 inch floor tom. The Tama Starclassic Maple's 100% maple shells produce the warm, resonant tone that gives Dailor's melodic tom patterns their musical character. The expanded tom configuration on The Hunter gave Dailor a wider melodic palette compared to the Crack the Skye setup — an expansion that suited the album's broader arrangements.

**Q: What kit did Brann Dailor use in 2011?**

A: In 2011, Brann Dailor used a Tama Starclassic Maple kit for The Hunter sessions and subsequent world tour. The setup included a single 22 inch bass drum driven by a Tama Iron Cobra single pedal, an expanded tom configuration with 10, 12, and 13 inch rack toms and floor toms at 14 and 16 inches. His cymbal setup was Meinl Byzance across the Traditional series — the same dark, complex B20 bronze palette he had established on Crack the Skye. He played Meinl sticks and used Evans UV1 Coated heads on batter surfaces.

**Q: What cymbals does Brann Dailor use on The Hunter?**

A: On The Hunter (2011), Brann Dailor used Meinl Byzance cymbals, carrying forward the dark, complex B20 bronze palette he had established on Crack the Skye (2009). His setup included 14 inch Traditional hi-hats, an 18 inch Traditional Thin Crash, a 20 inch Traditional Medium Crash, a 22 inch Traditional Ride, an 18 inch China, and a 10 inch splash. The Meinl Byzance series uses hand-hammered B20 bronze with Turkish craftsmanship that produces dark, warm, complex tones — suited to The Hunter's musically nuanced production even within its more commercial context.

**Q: Did Mastodon win a Grammy for The Hunter?**

A: Yes. Mastodon won the Grammy Award for Best Metal Performance at the 55th Grammy Awards (2013) for "Black Tongue" from The Hunter. This was Mastodon's first Grammy win. The award confirmed the commercial and critical success of The Hunter's more accessible direction and recognised Brann Dailor's groove-forward drumming on "Black Tongue" as among the year's best metal performances.

**Q: How does The Hunter differ from Crack the Skye in terms of drumming?**

A: The Hunter (2011) marks a significant transition in Brann Dailor's drumming compared to Crack the Skye (2009). The most fundamental change is the move to single bass drum — Crack the Skye featured extensive double-kick polyrhythmic complexity, while The Hunter commits to a single Iron Cobra pedal throughout. The song structures are shorter and more direct, requiring groove discipline over extended polyrhythmic development. Producer Mike Elizondo's hip-hop-influenced production approach also shaped a punchier, more defined drum sound compared to Brendan O'Brien's more atmospheric work on Crack the Skye. The Hunter represents the transitional album between Crack the Skye's conceptual ambition and Emperor of Sand's emotionally mature groove-forward approach.

**Q: Who produced The Hunter by Mastodon?**

A: The Hunter (2011) was produced by Mike Elizondo, a departure from Brendan O'Brien who had produced Crack the Skye (2009). Elizondo is primarily known for his hip-hop production work with Dr. Dre and Eminem, bringing a different sonic sensibility to Mastodon — punchier, more defined drum sounds, tighter commercial arrangements, and a production polish that suited the album's more accessible direction. The collaboration resulted in Mastodon's most commercially successful album to that point, and the Grammy-winning 'Black Tongue.'
