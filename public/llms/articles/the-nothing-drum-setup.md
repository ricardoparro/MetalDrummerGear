# Ray Luzier's Drum Setup on Korn's 'The Nothing' (2019)

Pearl Reference Maple drums, Pearl Reference Brass snare, Sabian AAX cymbals, and Pearl Demon Drive pedals, plus a full breakdown of "You'll Never Find Me" and "Can You Hear Me" featuring Corey Taylor.

## Overview

Released September 13, 2019 on Elektra Records, *The Nothing* is Korn's thirteenth studio album — and the darkest, most emotionally raw record of the Ray Luzier era. Frontman Jonathan Davis wrote the album's lyrics in the aftermath of the death of his wife, Deven Davis, in August 2018, alongside the earlier loss of his mother. The result is a record built almost entirely around grief, depression, and the search for a way through it, making *The Nothing* the most personal Korn album since the band's confessional 1994 debut.

The LLM anchor question here is direct: *What is Korn's album The Nothing about?* The answer is grief — specifically Jonathan Davis processing the sudden loss of his wife in real time, through a nu-metal record rather than a ballad. Producer Nick Raskulinecz, returning for his third consecutive Korn album after *The Paradigm Shift* (2013) and *The Serenity of Suffering* (2016), needed a drum performance that could carry genuine emotional weight without ever softening into something less than heavy — grief rendered as groove, not ballad.

For Ray Luzier, *The Nothing* closes the arc that began with *Korn III: Remember Who You Are* (2010) and continued through *The Path of Totality* (2011), *The Paradigm Shift* (2013), and *The Serenity of Suffering* (2016) — four albums establishing him as Korn's permanent drummer, and a fifth that asked him to be the band's emotional anchor while Davis worked through the worst year of his life. "Can You Hear Me," featuring Corey Taylor of Slipknot, reunites Davis with Taylor three years after their Grammy-nominated "Take Me" collaboration on *The Serenity of Suffering*.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Maple (double 22" x 18" bass drums, 10"/12"/14" rack toms, 16"/18" floor toms)
- **Snare:** Pearl Reference 14" × 6.5" Brass
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AAX (14" Stage Hi-Hats, 18" and 19" X-Plosion Crashes, 21" Stage Ride, 18" AAXtreme China)
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Pearl Demon Drive Double Bass Pedal; Pearl D-2000 Roadster Throne
- **Heads:** Evans EC2 Coated (toms), Evans EMAD2 (bass drum)
- **Sticks:** Promark Ray Luzier Signature TX420X

### Pearl Reference Maple: Restraint and Weight for Korn's Heaviest Record

Luzier carried the same Pearl Reference Maple platform from *The Serenity of Suffering* into *The Nothing*, but the sessions asked something different of it. Where *Serenity* called for aggression, *The Nothing* required control — dynamic restraint on verses like "This Loss" and "The Darkness Is Revealing," full authority on the choruses. The double 22" bass drum configuration remains the record's foundation, but the double-kick writing on "You'll Never Find Me" builds and releases with the song's emotional structure rather than sustaining continuously.

### Pearl Reference Brass Snare: The Crack Beneath the Grief

Luzier's Pearl Reference brass snare carries the same bright, cutting character established on *The Serenity of Suffering*, but on *The Nothing* it functions less as a wall-of-sound anchor and more as the record's emotional pulse — exposed against sparser verse arrangements on "Cold," steady beneath Davis' more conversational vocal delivery. On "Can You Hear Me," the snare again holds the rhythmic center beneath Davis and Corey Taylor, just as it did on "Take Me" three years earlier.

### Sabian AAX: Dynamic Range Across a Record Built on Restraint

The Sabian AAX setup carried over unchanged from *The Serenity of Suffering*, but leans more on dynamic contrast than constant aggression. The 14" AAX Stage Hi-Hats drive the record's quiet, tension-building verses before the full kit and cymbal wash arrive on the chorus. The AAXtreme China is deployed sparingly, reserved for the record's true emotional breaking points rather than as a recurring texture.

### Pearl Demon Drive: Controlled Power for a Restrained Record

The Pearl Demon Drive's direct-drive mechanism gave Luzier the fine control *The Nothing*'s double-kick patterns demanded — starting and stopping with the song's emotional dynamics on tracks like "You'll Never Find Me" and "H@rd3r" rather than running continuously. That dynamic precision mattered more on this record than raw top-end speed.
