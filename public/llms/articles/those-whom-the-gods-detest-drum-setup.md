## Overview

Released on October 27, 2009, on Nuclear Blast Records, Nile's sixth studio album "Those Whom the Gods Detest" is the band's best-selling and most critically acclaimed release. It marks the completion of George Kollias's transition from the DW/Tama gear era into full Pearl endorsement — Pearl Reference Maple kit, Pearl Free-Floating snare, and Pearl Demon Drive pedals — paired with his established Meinl Byzance cymbal setup.

Tracks like "Those Whom the Gods Detest," "Kafir!," "The Eye of Ra," and "Permitting the Noble Dead" are now canonical death metal recordings. Allmusic awarded the album 4/5 stars; the Metal Music Archives consensus rates it 9/10. The album charted in Germany, the UK, and reached the US Billboard Heatseekers chart — Nile's widest commercial reach.

### George's 2009 Kit: Pearl Reference Maple

For "Those Whom the Gods Detest," George Kollias played a Pearl Reference Maple kit — Pearl's professional-grade flagship featuring all-maple shells with OptiMount suspension for free-resonating toms.

The shift from Tama Starclassic Maple (used on "Annihilation of the Wicked") to the Pearl Reference Maple delivered a fuller fundamental tone and longer sustain. In Nile's densely layered production, this distinction mattered: each drum stroke had to project through guitars, bass, and production without sounding brittle.

The double 22" kick setup remained non-negotiable. Two separate bass drums gave Kollias independent tactile response per foot that a double pedal on a single kick cannot replicate at 240+ BPM. The compact tom configuration — 10", 12", 14", 16" — matched his "Annihilation" layout, preserving the reach efficiency built through four years of touring that material.

### The Pearl Free-Floating Snare

Kollias used a Pearl Free-Floating snare on these sessions. In a Free-Floating drum, the shell resonates independently of the lug casings — the hoops apply head tension while the shell is free. The result is exceptional sustain, cleaner fundamental pitch, and even response across the entire head.

For extreme metal recording, the Free-Floating design ensured consistent snare response whether blast beat strokes landed at center or toward the edge — critical at 240+ BPM. The tone is distinctly fuller than the steel-focused "Annihilation" snare sound, reflecting the maturing Pearl endorsement relationship.

### The Meinl Byzance Arsenal

George Kollias's Meinl Byzance relationship was already deep by 2009. The B20 bronze Byzance series delivered the dark, complex overtones that Nile's Egyptian-themed compositions require without the brightness that would clash with dense guitar frequencies.

Setup:
- **14" Byzance Medium Hi-Hats** — clear articulation for fast hi-hat patterns and blast beat work
- **17" Byzance Medium Thin Crash** — fast response for quick accents between blast sections
- **18" Byzance Medium Crash** — primary crash for riff section accents
- **19" Byzance Medium Heavy Crash** — full crash for major dynamic peaks
- **20" Byzance Medium Ride** — clear stick definition in mid-tempo compositional sections
- **18" Byzance China** — aggressive trashy accent for the heaviest transitions

### Pearl Demon Drive: Direct-Drive Foundation

The Pearl Demon Drive replaced the DW 9002 as Kollias's speed foundation. The Demon Drive uses a direct-drive mechanism — the footboard connects to the cam and beater shaft through rigid linkage rather than chain or belt — giving immediate, linear response with no chain slack at extreme tempos.

At 240+ BPM, the direct-drive's elimination of chain lag translates to more consistent stroke timing and cleaner recorded attack. This pedal laid the mechanical groundwork for the co-designed Pearl Demon XR (2015), which incorporated insights from touring "Those Whom the Gods Detest" material worldwide.

## Technique Highlights

**Heel-Toe at Mature Execution:** Four years of touring "Annihilation" and "Ithyphallic" material had refined Kollias's heel-toe technique to its most efficient form. "Kafir!" demonstrates this: sustained blast beats at 240–260 BPM with absolutely even dynamics and timing throughout.

**Gravity Blast with Compositional Intent:** The title track deploys gravity blasts with structural purpose — blast sections emerge from and return to measured passages in a way that shapes the listening experience rather than simply maximizing aggression.

**Polyrhythmic Density:** "The Eye of Ra" and "Permitting the Noble Dead" contain the album's most rhythmically complex passages — bass drum patterns suggesting one metric layer while hands operate in another, with cymbal accents creating a third implied grid.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit does George Kollias use on Those Whom the Gods Detest?**
A: Pearl Reference Maple — double 22" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms. Transition from the Tama Starclassic Maple used on "Annihilation of the Wicked" (2005). See the [Annihilation of the Wicked drum setup](/llms/articles/george-kollias-nile-annihilation-drum-setup.md) for comparison.

**Q: How fast is George Kollias on Those Whom the Gods Detest?**
A: Sustained blast beats at 230–260 BPM on tracks like "Kafir!" and the title track — consistent with his Guinness World Record-certified peak of 280 BPM single strokes. The Pearl Demon Drive's direct-drive mechanism contributed to the precise, consistent stroke timing audible throughout the most extreme passages.

**Q: What cymbals does George Kollias use on Those Whom the Gods Detest?**
A: Meinl Byzance series — 14" Medium hi-hats, 17"/18"/19" crashes, 20" Medium ride, 18" China. B20 bronze construction for dark, complex overtones and controlled response at extreme speeds.

**Q: What pedals does George Kollias use on Those Whom the Gods Detest?**
A: Pearl Demon Drive double bass drum pedal — direct-drive mechanism with linear, immediate response. Predecessor to the co-designed Pearl Demon XR (2015).

**Q: Why is Those Whom the Gods Detest Nile's most successful album?**
A: It balances Nile's extreme technical demands with their most compositionally accessible songwriting. Nuclear Blast's distribution gave it wider reach than earlier Relapse releases. Allmusic 4/5, Metal Music Archives 9/10. Charted in Germany, UK, and US Billboard Heatseekers.
