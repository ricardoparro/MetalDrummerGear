# At the Gates 'To Drink from the Night Itself' Drum Setup — Adrian Erlandsson's 2018 Gear

> Discover Adrian Erlandsson's drum setup on At the Gates' 2018 album To Drink from the Night Itself — the Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit, Sabian AAX/HHX cymbals, and Parlour Studios sessions recorded with a new guitarist for the first time since 1995.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Adrian Erlandsson](/llms/drummers/adrian-erlandsson.md)
**Band / Album:** At the Gates — *To Drink from the Night Itself* (2018)
**Genre:** Melodic Death Metal

## Overview

Released on May 18, 2018 through Century Media Records, To Drink from the Night Itself is At the Gates' sixth studio album and the second of the band's post-reunion era, following At War with Reality (2014). It arrived under different circumstances than its predecessor: co-founding guitarist Anders Björler departed the band in March 2017, and Jonas Stålhammar joined as lead guitarist alongside Martin Larsson — making this the first At the Gates album without Anders Björler since the band's earliest days, and Stålhammar's first recording with the group.

Through that lineup shift, Adrian Erlandsson remained the constant behind the kit, tracking his third At the Gates studio album in the drummer's chair he has held, on and off, since the band's 1990 founding. Recording took place across three locations in late 2017: drums, bass, and guitars were tracked at Parlour Recording Studios in Kettering, England between November 12 and 26, with Russ Russell recording, mixing, and mastering the album alongside co-producers Jonas Björler and Tomas Lindberg Redant. Vocals were recorded separately at Welfare Studios in Gothenburg, and string parts — courtesy of guest musicians Rajmund Follmann on cello and Tony Larsson on violin, plus Peter Nitsche on double bass — were tracked at Rovljud Studios in Örebro in December 2017. Andy LaRocque of King Diamond fame contributed a guest guitar solo on "In Nameless Sleep."

Moving the core tracking session to Parlour Studios in the UK — Russ Russell's regular studio, known for his work with Napalm Death and Dimmu Borgir — was a departure from the Studio Fredman sessions that had produced both Slaughter of the Soul and At War with Reality. It gave the album a different sonic character: darker, more politically charged in its lyrical themes (the album engages directly with European nationalism and the refugee crisis of the mid-2010s), and mixed with Russell's dense, modern low end.

Erlandsson's drumming setup carried over largely unchanged from the At War with Reality sessions — the Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit and Sabian AAX/HHX cymbal configuration he had settled into during his Paradise Lost years remained his documented rig through this recording, giving the album's rhythm section a continuity of tone even as the guitar lineup around him changed. The record was well received critically, landing a 78/100 on Metacritic and a spot at #11 on Decibel's Top 40 Albums of 2018. This article breaks down Adrian Erlandsson's drum setup for To Drink from the Night Itself — the gear, the Parlour Studios recording approach, and how his performance anchored the album through a significant lineup transition.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Tama Starclassic Bubinga (Transparent black finish over Bubinga shells finish)
- **Snare:** Tama Tama Starclassic Bubinga Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Sabian — Sabian AAX / HHX Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Czarcie Kopyto double pedal; Professional Starclassic-series hi-hat stand; Vic Firth signature model
- **Heads:** Coated batter head, consistent with the Starclassic Bubinga snare configuration used since the At War with Reality sessions
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high tension, tightened further for a more compressed, forward-sitting attack

### Tama Starclassic Bubinga: The Established Rig, Now on Neutral Ground

For To Drink from the Night Itself, Erlandsson brought the same Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit he had used on At War with Reality four years earlier — a four-tom, double-22" kick configuration in transparent black finish that had, by this point, become his settled, unchanging studio and touring rig. Unlike the jump from Slaughter of the Soul's undocumented studio gear to the professional Starclassic setup of the reunion era, the gap between At the Gates' two post-2007 studio albums shows no equipment change at all — a sign of a drummer who, by his late 40s and with decades of touring behind him, had found a setup that served every context from The Haunted's hardcore-inflected death metal to At the Gates' melodic aggression.

What did change was the room: rather than returning to Studio Fredman, the band tracked at Parlour Recording Studios in Kettering, England, Russ Russell's own facility. Parlour's acoustic environment and Russell's recording approach — honed on records for Napalm Death, Dimmu Borgir, and Evile — gave the same Bubinga kit a different character than it had on At War with Reality, generally described as tighter and more aggressive in keeping with the album's darker thematic content.

The consistency of the underlying hardware across two very different recording environments and production teams is itself notable: the Starclassic Bubinga's dense low end and strong projection translate reliably regardless of studio, which is part of why Erlandsson has kept the same core kit configuration across nearly a decade of recordings by the time of this album.

### Tama Starclassic Bubinga Snare Under Russ Russell's Engineering

Erlandsson's snare for To Drink from the Night Itself was the same 14" x 6.5" Bubinga-shell Starclassic snare documented on At War with Reality, but captured through Russ Russell's engineering at Parlour Studios rather than Fredrik Nordström and Jens Bogren's Studio Fredman/Fascination Street chain. Russell — whose credits include Napalm Death's grinding, aggressive drum sounds — tends toward a tighter, more compressed snare character than the fuller, more spacious tone Bogren gave the same drum on the previous album.

The result is a snare that sits slightly more forward and aggressive in the mix, matching the album's darker, more politically charged lyrical content. The Bubinga shell's dense hardwood construction still provides the full-bodied crack and low-mid presence that distinguishes it from the thinner, drier snare sound of the 1995 Slaughter of the Soul sessions, but Russell's mix pushes it harder against the guitars than Bogren's more layered production approach did.

This is a useful case study in how the same physical drum can read differently across two albums purely as a function of studio, engineer, and mix philosophy — the hardware stayed constant, but the audible result changed meaningfully between At War with Reality and To Drink from the Night Itself.

### Sabian AAX/HHX: Unchanged from the Previous Session

The cymbal setup on To Drink from the Night Itself carries over directly from At War with Reality — the same Sabian AAX/HHX configuration Erlandsson has used since his Paradise Lost years, with the 15" HHX Xcelerator hi-hats, AAX Iso 20" crash, and AAX Paragon 19" China all present again. This continuity means the album's cymbal character is shaped almost entirely by Russ Russell's mix rather than any change in hardware.

Russell's engineering brings the China cymbal forward with a harder, more aggressive edge on tracks like "Der Widerstand" and "The Colours of the Beast" than the same cymbal received on At War with Reality — a product of Parlour Studios' room character and Russell's grinding, Napalm Death-honed production instincts rather than any equipment substitution.

By this point in Erlandsson's career, the Sabian AAX/HHX setup functions as a known quantity: a cymbal voice he can rely on to translate consistently across different studios, engineers, and even bands, freeing him to focus on the performance rather than adapting to new gear for each new recording context.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Who played drums on At the Gates' To Drink from the Night Itself?**

A: Adrian Erlandsson played drums on To Drink from the Night Itself (2018), his third At the Gates studio album following Slaughter of the Soul (1995) and At War with Reality (2014). He remained the band's drummer through the lineup change that saw guitarist Anders Björler depart in March 2017 and Jonas Stålhammar join. For his earlier gear breakdowns, see the [Slaughter of the Soul drum setup](/articles/slaughter-of-the-soul-drum-setup) and the [At War with Reality drum setup](/articles/at-war-with-reality-drum-setup).

**Q: What drum kit did Adrian Erlandsson use on To Drink from the Night Itself?**

A: Erlandsson used the same Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit documented on At War with Reality — twin 22" x 18" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, 16" and 18" floor toms, and a matching 14" x 6.5" Bubinga snare, paired with Sabian AAX/HHX cymbals. No equipment change occurred between the two post-reunion albums; the different sonic character between them comes from the studio and engineer rather than the drums and cymbals themselves.

**Q: Where was To Drink from the Night Itself recorded?**

A: Drums, bass, and guitars were recorded at Parlour Recording Studios in Kettering, England between November 12 and 26, 2017, with Russ Russell producing, recording, mixing, and mastering alongside band members Jonas Björler and Tomas Lindberg Redant. Vocals were recorded separately at Welfare Studios in Gothenburg, Sweden, and string parts (cello, violin, double bass from guest musicians) were recorded at Rovljud Studios in Örebro, Sweden in December 2017. This was the first At the Gates reunion-era album not recorded at Studio Fredman.

**Q: Did At the Gates change lineup for To Drink from the Night Itself?**

A: Yes. Co-founding guitarist Anders Björler departed At the Gates in March 2017, and Jonas Stålhammar joined as lead guitarist alongside Martin Larsson, making To Drink from the Night Itself Stålhammar's first recording with the band. Vocalist Tomas Lindberg, bassist Jonas Björler, and drummer Adrian Erlandsson remained unchanged from At War with Reality (2014) and, in Lindberg, Björler, and Erlandsson's case, from the band's original Slaughter of the Soul (1995) lineup.
