## Overview

Released on November 22, 1992 on Metal Blade Records, *Tomb of the Mutilated* is Cannibal Corpse's third studio album and arguably the band's most culturally significant record — banned in several countries, featured in *Ace Ventura: Pet Detective* (1994), and one of the best-selling death metal albums of all time. Paul Mazurkiewicz's 220–240 BPM blast beats with alternating-leg double kick delivered the rhythmic foundation for tracks like "Hammer Smashed Face," "Stripped, Raped and Strangled," and "I Cum Blood."

Recorded at Morrisound Recording in Tampa, Florida — the studio Scott Burns had transformed into death metal's Ground Zero — *Tomb of the Mutilated* documents the peak of the "Tampa sound" era. Paul's Pearl Export kit, a touring-budget workhorse, was transformed by Scott Burns's close-miking and surgical EQ into one of death metal's most recognizable drum sounds.

Paul Mazurkiewicz's technique is distinct from Pete Sandoval of Morbid Angel. Where Sandoval used twin bass drums (single-kick blasting), Paul deployed alternating-leg double kick on a single 22" bass drum via a Pearl P-100P double pedal — a biomechanically different approach that produced its own rhythmic character at extreme tempos and gave *Tomb of the Mutilated* its particular sonic fingerprint.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Export — 22" bass drum, 10"/12" rack toms, 16" floor tom (first endorsement era)
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating 14" x 5.5" Maple
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — 14" A New Beat hi-hats, 16" A Crash, 18" A Crash, 20" A Ride, 18" A China
- **Pedals:** Pearl P-100P Double Pedal (single bass drum, alternating-leg technique)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5A
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated (batter toms/snare), Remo Powerstroke 3 (bass drum batter), Remo Ambassador Snare Side (resonant)

### The Pearl Export at Morrisound

Paul's Pearl Export poplar/Asian mahogany shells produced a brighter, more forward-sounding tone than the birch Tama kits Pete Sandoval used on Morbid Angel records at the same studio. This tonal distinction, preserved by Scott Burns's engineering approach, gives *Tomb of the Mutilated* its own character within the Tampa sound family.

The Pearl Free-Floating 14" x 5.5" maple snare used Pearl's isolation mounting system — shell vibrates free of hardware contact — producing a more open, resonant tone that Burns shaped with targeted EQ rather than muffling. The result is the crack on "Hammer Smashed Face" that remains one of death metal's most recognizable snare sounds.

### The Tampa Sound

Scott Burns's recording philosophy at Morrisound was built around close-miking and surgical separation rather than reverb-based ambience. Every drum stroke was captured as a distinct event, remaining intelligible at 220–240 BPM. Kick EQ focused on the upper attack transient rather than sub-bass, maintaining double-kick clarity throughout sustained blast beat passages.

### Paul vs. Pete: The Technique Distinction

Pete Sandoval used two separate 22" bass drums; Paul Mazurkiewicz used one 22" drum with a Pearl P-100P double pedal. The secondary beater travels a slightly longer mechanical path than the primary, and both beaters impact the same resonating shell. At extreme tempos the rhythmic character of alternating-leg single-drum double kick has a subtly different groove quality than twin-bass blasting — a distinction audible when comparing *Tomb of the Mutilated* directly against *Altars of Madness* (1989).

## Key Facts

- Recorded at Morrisound Recording, Tampa, FL — Scott Burns engineering, 1992
- Pearl Export kit — first Cannibal Corpse endorsement era
- Single 22" bass drum with Pearl P-100P double pedal (not two separate drums)
- 220–240 BPM sustained blast beats; alternating-leg double kick technique
- Pearl Free-Floating 14" x 5.5" maple snare
- Zildjian A Series cymbals throughout — pre-Sabian era
- Banned in several countries; featured in *Ace Ventura: Pet Detective* (1994)
- One of the best-selling death metal albums of all time
- Estimated Pearl Export kit value: $600–900 (1992)
- Estimated Pearl Free-Floating snare value: $400–700 (1992)
