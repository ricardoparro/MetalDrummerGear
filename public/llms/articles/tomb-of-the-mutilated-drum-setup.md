# Paul Mazurkiewicz's Drum Setup on Cannibal Corpse's Tomb of the Mutilated (1992)

> Complete drum gear breakdown for Cannibal Corpse's Tomb of the Mutilated (1992). Paul Mazurkiewicz's pre-endorsement Pearl kit, Morrisound recording with Scott Burns, the 'Hammer Smashed Face' anchor track, and the German ban that defined a death metal landmark.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup

**Band / Album:** Cannibal Corpse — *Tomb of the Mutilated* (1992)
**Genre:** Death Metal

## Overview

Released on September 17, 1992 through Metal Blade Records, Cannibal Corpse's third album "Tomb of the Mutilated" arrived at the precise moment when Florida death metal had become a fully formed genre — and immediately became its most notorious flashpoint. The album was banned in Germany alongside its predecessor "Butchered at Birth" (1991), pulled from shelves under youth protection laws over its cover art and song titles. That ban only amplified its legend. Today, "Tomb of the Mutilated" is considered a foundational text of death metal, with "Hammer Smashed Face" functioning as the genre's most universally recognized anthem.

Behind the kit was Paul Mazurkiewicz, co-founder of Cannibal Corpse and the band's only drummer since their formation in Buffalo, New York in 1988. By 1992 he was 24 years old, three albums into a career that would eventually run for more than 35 years and counting. "Tomb of the Mutilated" represents Mazurkiewicz at the moment his approach to death metal drumming crystallized into the style that would define the band: brutal consistency over flashy technical excess, blast beats as compositional architecture rather than speed exhibitions, and a percussive identity built on conventional grip and a relentless locomotive double-bass attack.

The sessions took place at Morrisound Studios in Tampa, Florida — by 1992 already the indisputable epicenter of death metal production. Producer Scott Burns had engineered or produced virtually every important death metal record of the era: Death's "Human" (1991), Morbid Angel's "Altars of Madness" (1989), Obituary's "Cause of Death" (1990), Deicide's self-titled debut (1990). When Cannibal Corpse rolled into Morrisound to track "Tomb of the Mutilated," they were stepping into a studio that had already invented the sound they needed. Burns close-miked everything, treated the room for minimal ambience, and dialed in the dry, attack-forward drum mix that became the Tampa template. For Mazurkiewicz, this meant his playing had to deliver clarity at speed — every kick, every snare, every cymbal had to articulate distinctly inside a dense wall of down-tuned guitars and Chris Barnes's guttural vocals.

The gear was pre-endorsement and unglamorous. In 1992, Mazurkiewicz was playing what he could afford and what worked — Pearl drums from the Export or Masters MX range, a wood-shell snare in the 14x5.5 inch size, Paiste or Zildjian budget-line cymbals, and Vic Firth 5B sticks. There was no signature stick yet, no Pearl Reference kit, no Meinl endorsement deal. What "Tomb of the Mutilated" captures is the sound of a working death metal drummer at the moment his band was becoming the genre's commercial leader. The kit was the bare professional minimum required to execute the material; what made the record sound the way it does was Mazurkiewicz's playing, Scott Burns's recording technique, and the Morrisound room.

This article breaks down every component of Paul Mazurkiewicz's 1992 setup, examines how Scott Burns captured it, and traces the technical approach — conventional grip blast beats, locomotive double-bass, and serve-the-riff phrasing — that "Tomb of the Mutilated" cemented as the Cannibal Corpse template for the next three decades.

### Pearl Export / Masters MX: The Working Drummer's Pre-Endorsement Kit

In 1992, Paul Mazurkiewicz was not yet the Pearl endorsee he is today. The kit he brought into Morrisound for "Tomb of the Mutilated" was working-class professional gear from Pearl's Export or Masters MX series — the same drums thousands of touring metal drummers were using at the time because they could be bought, transported, and beaten on without bankrupting a young band that was still building its commercial base.

Pearl's Export was the entry-pro tier — mahogany or basswood shells with a hardware package designed for reliability over refinement. The Masters MX (Maple/Mahogany hybrid) was a step up in shell quality without crossing into the boutique price range. Either kit gave Mazurkiewicz what he needed: punchy attack on the toms, solid low-end on the kicks, and shells durable enough for the punishment of constant double-bass abuse.

The dual 22" x 16" bass drum configuration is the defining feature of the setup. Cannibal Corpse's material on "Tomb of the Mutilated" — particularly the blast sections of "Hammer Smashed Face," "I Cum Blood," and "Necropedophile" — demanded continuous double-kick patterns at tempos that would later be associated with Mazurkiewicz's "locomotive" identity. Two independent kicks (rather than a single drum with a double pedal) gave him the symmetric foot independence required to sustain those passages across a 38-minute album.

The tom configuration was minimal by later death metal standards. Two rack toms (10" and 12") and a single 16" floor tom kept the kit fast to move around and focused — Mazurkiewicz wasn't designing fills that required four floor toms and a sextet of racks. The tom vocabulary on "Tomb of the Mutilated" is functional: short fills marking section transitions, occasional rolls into chorus drops, and supporting punctuation behind Bob Rusay and Jack Owen's tremolo riffing.

Scott Burns tuned the drums medium-tight for maximum attack and minimal sustain — the Morrisound approach he had refined across the Death and Morbid Angel sessions. The shells were close-miked with the bleed-control philosophy that defined the Tampa sound: every drum captured directly, with the room contributing only as much ambience as a treated tracking space allowed.

### The 14x5.5 Wood-Shell Crack: Cutting Through the Tampa Wall

The snare voice on "Tomb of the Mutilated" is the percussive signature of the record. In a mix that pushes the down-tuned guitars and bass into the entire low-mid range, the snare had to claim its space through sheer brightness and cut — and a 14" x 5.5" wood-shell snare delivers exactly that profile: enough body to feel substantial, but enough top-end attack to slice through the wall of Tampa guitar tone.

Wood shells (maple, birch, or hybrid constructions) give the snare a warmer, more musical voice than the bright, almost metallic crack of steel shells. For Mazurkiewicz's playing on "Tomb of the Mutilated," that wood character serves the album well — the snare punches without becoming so brittle that it competes with the guitars in the high-mids. Across "Hammer Smashed Face," the snare's backbeat anchors the iconic mid-tempo groove that made the song so recognizable; across the blast passages of "I Cum Blood" and "Beyond the Cemetery," the snare articulates each rapid single-stroke distinctly even at speeds that would blur lesser instruments together.

The 5.5" depth keeps the response fast. Deeper snares (6.5" and up) sustain longer and feel heavier, but they give up some of the rapid-response speed that blast beat playing demands. The 5.5" dimension is the death metal sweet spot: enough body to project, fast enough to articulate at velocity.

Scott Burns close-miked the snare with the Shure SM57 he used on virtually every Morrisound session, positioned tight to the head for maximum direct sound and minimal cymbal bleed. Burns kept the snare prominent in the final mix — a calculated choice that lets the listener track Mazurkiewicz's playing through the dense guitar arrangements that drove the record.

### Budget Paiste and Zildjian: The Pre-Endorsement Cymbal Reality

In 1992, Paul Mazurkiewicz had no cymbal endorsement deal. The Meinl partnership that defines his modern setup was years in the future. What he played on "Tomb of the Mutilated" was the era-typical working-band cymbal arsenal: a mix of Paiste 2002s, Paiste 502s, Zildjian A, or Zildjian ZBT cymbals — whatever combination of professional, semi-pro, and budget cymbals he could assemble and replace as cracks and bell separations took their toll across constant touring.

The 14" hi-hats are the rhythmic anchor of the record. On the iconic groove of "Hammer Smashed Face," the hi-hats sit just under the snare backbeat, driving the song's mid-tempo pulse with the kind of locked, unwavering eighth-note pattern that became the Cannibal Corpse rhythmic signature. In the blast passages, the hi-hats define the fast subdivision over the alternating kick-snare pattern — at extreme tempos, that articulation matters more than tonal sophistication.

The 16" and 18" crashes give Mazurkiewicz two accent voices for riff transitions. Death metal compositions live and die on tight, precise transitions between sections, and the crashes punctuate those changes without cluttering the mix.

The China cymbal is increasingly central across "Tomb of the Mutilated." Mazurkiewicz uses it as a trashy accent for major riff transitions — its aggressive, fast-decay voice became a signature element of the Cannibal Corpse sound, and Burns mixed it forward enough to make those accents readable through the guitar density.

Scott Burns close-miked the cymbals individually rather than relying purely on overheads — the bleed-control approach that kept cymbal wash from blurring blast beat passages at extreme tempo. The overheads were positioned tight in an XY configuration to capture spatial information without dragging the room sound into the mix.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Paul Mazurkiewicz use on Tomb of the Mutilated?**

A: Paul Mazurkiewicz recorded Cannibal Corpse's Tomb of the Mutilated (1992) on a Pearl drum kit from the pre-endorsement era — most likely a Pearl Export or Pearl Masters MX configuration. The setup featured dual 22-inch x 16-inch bass drums for double-kick independence, 10-inch and 12-inch rack toms, and a single 16-inch floor tom. His snare was a 14-inch x 5.5-inch wood-shell drum (maple or birch construction), tuned medium-high for cutting attack. The Pearl Reference kit that Mazurkiewicz uses today was years in the future — in 1992 he was playing the working-band professional gear available to a band that had not yet broken through commercially.

**Q: Who produced Tomb of the Mutilated and where was it recorded?**

A: Tomb of the Mutilated was produced by Scott Burns at Morrisound Studios in Tampa, Florida. By 1992, Burns had already established Morrisound as the indisputable epicenter of death metal production through his work on Death's Human (1991), Morbid Angel's Altars of Madness (1989), Obituary's Cause of Death (1990), and Deicide's self-titled debut (1990). The Tampa drum sound he developed — close-miked, dry, attack-forward, minimal room ambience — was the template he applied to Mazurkiewicz's kit on Tomb of the Mutilated. The album was tracked with pure acoustic drums; no triggers reinforced the kicks and no samples blended into the snare, an approach Burns relied on Mazurkiewicz's stroke consistency to make possible.

**Q: Why was Tomb of the Mutilated banned in Germany?**

A: Tomb of the Mutilated was banned in Germany shortly after its 1992 release under the country's youth protection laws (the Bundesprüfstelle für jugendgefährdende Medien indexing system), alongside its predecessor Butchered at Birth (1991). The ban was driven by the album's cover art and the graphic content of songs like 'I Cum Blood' and 'Necropedophile.' Indexed albums in Germany could not be advertised, displayed openly in stores, or sold to minors. The ban remained in place for many years, with the band even prohibited from performing songs from the album during German tour dates. The controversy amplified the album's underground notoriety internationally and helped cement Cannibal Corpse's position as the most commercially significant death metal band of the era.

**Q: What is special about Paul Mazurkiewicz's drumming style on this album?**

A: Mazurkiewicz's playing on Tomb of the Mutilated showcases several elements that became his signature identity: traditional (conventional) grip on the snare hand, a relentless 'locomotive' double-bass approach prioritizing consistency over peak velocity, standard alternating blast beats rather than gravity-blast technique, and a serve-the-riff phrasing philosophy that subordinates drum-centric showcasing to making the songs hit harder. The album's iconic anchor track, 'Hammer Smashed Face,' became the most recognizable single in death metal history because of Mazurkiewicz's disciplined restraint — the mid-tempo groove leaves space between the snare hits empty rather than filling them with showy flurries, and that compositional discipline is the most important thing on the record.

**Q: Were drum triggers used on Tomb of the Mutilated?**

A: No. Tomb of the Mutilated was recorded with pure acoustic drums — no triggers reinforced the kick drums, no samples blended into the snare. Scott Burns at Morrisound Studios used close-miking on every drum (AKG D112 inside each kick, Shure SM57 on snare, Sennheiser MD421 on toms) and a treated tracking room to capture clarity at extreme tempos without electronic reinforcement. The pure acoustic capture was possible because Mazurkiewicz's stroke consistency was already strong enough by 1992 that triggers were not needed for articulation. The ddrum live triggers Mazurkiewicz uses today on tour are a touring-context decision for high-SPL live consistency, not a studio practice that applies to the 1992 album.

**Q: What cymbals and sticks did Paul Mazurkiewicz use on Tomb of the Mutilated?**

A: In 1992, Mazurkiewicz had no cymbal endorsement deal — the Meinl partnership that defines his modern setup was years in the future. On Tomb of the Mutilated he played an era-typical working-band cymbal arsenal: a mix of Paiste 2002, Paiste 502, Zildjian A, or Zildjian ZBT cymbals, including 14-inch hi-hats, 16-inch and 18-inch medium crashes, a 20-inch medium ride used sparingly, and an 18-inch China cymbal for trashy riff accents. His sticks were standard Vic Firth 5B wood-tip — the signature Vic Firth Paul Mazurkiewicz model he plays today did not exist yet. Heads were Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear on the kicks, Remo Emperor Clear on the toms, and Remo Ambassador or Emperor Coated on the snare batter.
