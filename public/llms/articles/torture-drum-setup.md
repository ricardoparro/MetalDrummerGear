# Paul Mazurkiewicz's Drum Setup on Cannibal Corpse's Torture (2012)

> Complete drum gear breakdown for Cannibal Corpse's Torture (2012). Paul Mazurkiewicz's fully matured Pearl Reference and Meinl rig, Erik Rutan's third consecutive production, and the band's highest-charting album to date.

**Genre:** Death Metal

## Overview

Released March 13, 2012 through Metal Blade Records, "Torture" is Cannibal Corpse's twelfth studio album and the record that followed 2009's "Evisceration Plague" — the album that, alongside 2006's "Kill," is widely regarded as a high point of the Fisher-era catalog. Torture didn't chase reinvention; it doubled down on the density and technicality the band had spent the two prior albums building, and the results landed as the band's best commercial opening to that point: a #38 debut on the Billboard 200, with top-10 placements on the US Independent Albums and Top Hard Rock Albums charts.

Producer Erik Rutan — the Hate Eternal guitarist who had also helmed Kill and Evisceration Plague — returned for a third consecutive album, tracking sessions across Sonic Ranch in Tornillo, Texas and his own Mana Recording Studios in St. Petersburg, Florida. That continuity mattered: by Torture, Rutan understood exactly how to capture Paul Mazurkiewicz's kit for maximum low-end weight without sacrificing the attack clarity that death metal blast sections demand, and the album's drum sound reflects three albums' worth of refined studio chemistry between drummer and producer.

The same five-piece lineup that recorded Kill and Evisceration Plague — vocalist George "Corpsegrinder" Fisher, guitarists Pat O'Brien and Rob Barrett, bassist Alex Webster, and Mazurkiewicz — remained fully intact, giving Torture the stability of a band operating at the peak of its collective chemistry. Tracks like "Scourge of Iron" and "Encased in Concrete" showcase Mazurkiewicz's playing at its most physically demanding: sustained blast sections that never lose definition, interlocking with Webster's bass lines and the twin-guitar attack with mechanical precision.

Behind the kit, Mazurkiewicz's gear had reached full maturity by this point: the Pearl Reference configuration, Meinl cymbal setup, and Pearl Eliminator double bass pedal that define his modern rig were now completely settled, tested across three consecutive Rutan-produced albums. This article breaks down that mature 2012-era configuration and the production approach that captured it.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Pearl Pearl Reference (mature endorsement configuration, 2012) (Custom finish (Pearl endorsement era) finish)
- **Snare:** Pearl Pearl Free-Floating Snare, 14" x 6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl — Meinl Classics Custom / Byzance series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Pearl Eliminator Double Bass Pedal; ddrum Acoustic Pro Triggers (live context); Vic Firth Paul Mazurkiewicz Signature
- **Heads:** Remo Coated Ambassador (batter), Remo Diplomat Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-bright for cut through dense, down-tuned guitar frequencies

### Pearl Reference at Full Maturity

By Torture, Paul Mazurkiewicz's Pearl Reference setup had been running unchanged through three consecutive Erik Rutan productions — Kill (2006), Evisceration Plague (2009), and now Torture. The double 22" x 18" bass drums carried the sub-bass weight that Rutan's mixes had come to lean on, and the compact four-tom layout (two rack, two floor) stayed focused on the interplay between kick, snare, and Cannibal Corpse's dense twin-guitar riffing rather than extended melodic fills.

Rutan's familiarity with the kit by this point meant less time spent chasing tone in the studio and more time refining performance — a luxury the band's earlier producers, working with a less settled rig, didn't have. The maple/African mahogany hybrid shells delivered the same focused attack and warm low-end that had defined Mazurkiewicz's sound since the Pearl endorsement solidified in the early 2000s, now captured by a producer who had three albums of prior experience dialing in exactly how those shells should sit in a mix built for maximum density.

### The Free-Floating Steel Crack

Mazurkiewicz's 14" x 6.5" Pearl Free-Floating steel snare was, by 2012, a fully settled part of his rig — the same drum documented across Kill and Evisceration Plague. The Free-Floating design's decoupled shell delivers an open, explosive crack that cuts through Torture's dense, down-tuned guitar mix without additional EQ trickery, giving Rutan a snare sound that needed minimal shaping to sit forward and legible.

Tuned bright for cut rather than body, the snare has to articulate cleanly against the alternating hi-speed blast and mid-tempo passages that define tracks like "Sarcophagic Frenzy" and "As Deep As the Knife Will Go." At the tempos Torture demands, any ambiguity in the snare's attack would blur into the surrounding wall of guitars — the Free-Floating design's fast response prevents that.

### Meinl: The Established Arsenal

By Torture, Mazurkiewicz's Meinl cymbal setup was a fully mature, established part of his rig — the same 14" hi-hats, dual crashes, 20" ride, and 18" china documented across the prior two Rutan-produced albums. Meinl's hand-hammered bronze construction gave Rutan's mix cymbals that spoke clearly and decayed quickly, essential for keeping the sixteenth-note pulse of tracks like "Scourge of Iron" legible against Torture's dense low end.

The china cymbal remains Mazurkiewicz's signature punctuation tool, deployed at the album's most brutal transitions — a role it has played consistently since the earliest Cannibal Corpse records, now voiced through a cymbal setup Rutan had three albums of experience mixing.
