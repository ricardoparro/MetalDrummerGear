## Overview

Released on May 13, 2008 on Century Media Records, "The Unspoken King" remains the most debated entry in Cryptopsy's catalog. The album introduced Matt McGachy as vocalist — replacing Lord Worm, who had anchored [Once Was Not](/articles/once-was-not-drum-setup) (2005) — and brought clean vocals into Cryptopsy's sound for the first time in the band's history. The fan reaction was immediate and largely hostile: a band defined by uncompromising technical brutality had introduced melody.

What receives less discussion is Flo Mounier's specific contribution to the album — and what it reveals about his adaptability. The Yamaha Recording Custom kit that had powered the velocity peak of "Once Was Not" was retained, but the tuning approach and head selection were revised to serve a fundamentally different musical context. Where "Once Was Not" demanded maximum attack sharpness at extreme velocities, "The Unspoken King" required a drum sound that could sit beneath melodic vocal lines without overwhelming them — a significantly different engineering challenge.

The result is Flo's most dynamically versatile studio performance. Tracks like "Silence the Tyrants" demonstrate that he could deploy blast beat intensity within a framework that also accommodated groove-based passages and dynamic space. "The Plagued" shows the clean vocal/extreme drumming juxtaposition at its most direct. Whether the album works as a whole remains contested; what Flo achieves on it individually does not.

For drummers asking 'what happened to Cryptopsy in 2008,' the drum performance offers the clearest answer: the band attempted a pivot, Flo executed it with complete technical command, and the controversy was about the direction, not the execution.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Yamaha Recording Custom Series (Natural Wood / Solid Black finish)
- **Snare:** Yamaha Recording Custom Steel Snare, 14" x 5.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian — ZXT Series
- **Hardware / Pedals:** DW 9002 Double Pedal; DW 9000 Hi-Hat Stand; Roc-n-Soc Nitro Throne; Vic Firth 5A American Classic
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador Coated (batter), Remo Diplomat Snare Side (resonant)
- **Snare tuning:** Moderate-high tension — reduced from Once Was Not for dynamic versatility while maintaining blast beat clarity

### The Same Kit, Different Context: Yamaha Recording Custom Revisited

The Yamaha Recording Custom kit on "The Unspoken King" is physically the same instrument Flo played on "Once Was Not" — same shells, same configuration, same fundamental hardware. The difference is in the tuning approach and head selection, which were revised to serve the album's different musical demands.

Where "Once Was Not" required maximum attack and minimum sustain for extreme velocity blast passages, "The Unspoken King" introduced passages where the kit needed to provide rhythmic support under melodic vocal lines. A kit tuned for blast beat extremity — very high tension, very fast decay — does not naturally support melodic contexts where some resonance and body are musically appropriate.

The revision for this album involved loosening the overall tuning approach on the toms and adjusting the bass drum muffling to allow slightly more resonance. This did not compromise the kit's extreme metal capability — the blast beat passages on the album are executed with the same articulation precision as "Once Was Not" — but gave the drum sound more body for the album's more dynamic sections.

The birch shell construction retained its attack advantage: even at lower tension, Yamaha Recording Custom birch shells produce a clear, defined attack that prevents muddiness in dense mix environments. The decision to retain the same shells rather than changing kits was itself a statement: Flo could get what the album needed from the instrument he knew without starting over.

### Adapted for Dynamic Range: Yamaha Steel Snare with Revised Tuning

The snare tuning revision for "The Unspoken King" was the most significant equipment adjustment between albums. On "Once Was Not," the snare was tuned to extreme tension — maximum attack, minimum sustain — to serve relentless blast beat passages. "The Unspoken King" required the snare to function in both contexts: the extreme blast passages that remained core to Cryptopsy's identity and the more dynamic passages that the album's new direction introduced.

The solution was a moderate tension reduction combined with a snare wire tension adjustment that preserved attack clarity while allowing slightly more sustain on the fuller strokes of groove-oriented sections. The steel shell retained its brightness and cutting attack — essential for the blast sections — but the moderate reduction in head tension added enough body for the snare to function musically in dynamic contexts without the choked, overly dry character that would have sounded incongruous beneath melodic vocal lines.

This was not a softening of Flo's approach — it was a calibration. The snare passages in "Silence the Tyrants" demonstrate that he could move between the tightly-tensioned blast beat mode and the slightly more open groove mode within the same track. The adjustment reveals sophisticated understanding of how head tension affects the snare's role in different musical contexts.

### Zildjian ZXT: The Continuing Foundation

The Zildjian ZXT cymbal setup carried over from "Once Was Not" with minimal changes — the same models in the same configuration. What changed was the proportional deployment of each element in the mix. On "The Unspoken King," the ride cymbal saw significantly more use in groove-oriented passages, functioning as a primary rhythmic voice in contexts where "Once Was Not" would have defaulted to hi-hat-driven blast patterns.

The 14" ZXT Hi-Hats remained the blast beat foundation for the album's most extreme passages. Their focused, tight response continued to serve the extreme sections with the same articulation clarity as the previous album. In the dynamic passages unique to "The Unspoken King"'s more melodic approach, the hi-hats were opened more frequently — a subtly different technique than the tight, fast-chicking of pure blast playing.

The 20" ZXT Ride's expanded role was the most significant cymbal usage change between albums. Matt McGachy's melodic vocal lines required rhythmic support that a ride cymbal provides more naturally than hi-hat/blast patterns. Flo's use of the ride in these sections demonstrates his ability to switch between technical death metal drummer and rhythmic accompanist within the same recording session.

The 18" ZXT China maintained its role as an aggressive section-boundary marker, functioning consistently across both the album's extreme and melodic sections as the most reliable accent voice for large-scale dynamic shifts.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drum kit did Flo Mounier use on The Unspoken King?**

A: On Cryptopsy's The Unspoken King (2008), Flo Mounier used the same Yamaha Recording Custom kit as the previous album Once Was Not (2005) — but with different tuning and head configuration to serve the album's more dynamic musical approach. The shells are the same 7-ply birch Recording Custom drums in the same configuration (22" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms), but the tuning was adjusted to provide more body and resonance for the album's melodic passages while still accommodating the blast beat sections. This demonstrates that the same physical kit can serve very different musical contexts through thoughtful tuning rather than equipment replacement.

**Q: What happened to Cryptopsy in 2008 and how did it affect the drums?**

A: In 2008, Cryptopsy released The Unspoken King with new vocalist Matt McGachy, who introduced clean vocals into the band's sound for the first time. This caused significant controversy among fans who expected the band to continue in the extreme technical death metal direction of Once Was Not (2005). For Flo Mounier's drumming specifically, the change meant adapting his blast beat-centered approach to accommodate melodic vocal passages — deploying dynamic range he had always possessed but rarely needed to deploy in Cryptopsy's previous extreme context. His ride cymbal saw significantly more use, his ghost note technique became more prominent, and snare tuning was calibrated for both extreme and dynamic contexts. The controversy was about Cryptopsy's musical direction, not Flo's performance — his execution remained technically impeccable throughout.

**Q: How does The Unspoken King drum setup differ from Once Was Not?**

A: The Unspoken King (2008) uses the same Yamaha Recording Custom shells and Zildjian ZXT cymbals as Once Was Not (2005), but with key configuration differences. The primary changes: (1) Head tension was reduced slightly on toms and snare to add body for melodic passages; (2) Bass drum muffling was adjusted for more resonance in dynamic contexts; (3) The ride cymbal was more prominently featured — serving a rhythmic accompaniment role in clean vocal sections that was absent on Once Was Not. These are tuning and technique adaptations rather than equipment changes, reflecting Flo's approach of configuring existing tools for new musical demands rather than changing gear for each album.

**Q: Is The Unspoken King considered a good drum performance despite the album controversy?**

A: Yes. While The Unspoken King remains Cryptopsy's most controversial album due to its clean vocal direction and departure from extreme technical death metal, Flo Mounier's drum performance is consistently recognized as technically strong. The album required him to expand his dynamic range beyond the blast beat-dominant approach of previous Cryptopsy records, deploying groove playing, ghost note technique, and ride cymbal-based rhythmic accompaniment alongside the extreme passages that remained in the material. His ability to adapt to the melodic context without compromising technical precision — moving between blast intensity and musical accompaniment within the same track — demonstrates a broader command of the instrument than any previous Cryptopsy album had required. The controversy about the album is about direction; the execution is not in question.

**Q: What cymbals did Flo Mounier use on The Unspoken King?**

A: On The Unspoken King (2008), Flo Mounier continued using the Zildjian ZXT series he had introduced on Once Was Not (2005). The cymbal selection remained the same — 14" ZXT Hi-Hats, 16" and 18" ZXT Crashes, 20" ZXT Ride, 18" ZXT China — but the proportional deployment changed significantly from the previous album. The 20" ZXT Ride played a much more prominent role, functioning as a primary rhythmic voice in the groove and melodic passages that The Unspoken King introduced. The hi-hats also alternated more frequently between blast-mode tight chicking and open groove articulation, reflecting the album's wider dynamic context.
