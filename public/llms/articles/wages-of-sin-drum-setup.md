## Overview

*Wages of Sin*, released on March 19, 2001, marks the defining turning point in Arch Enemy's career. The album introduced vocalist Angela Gossow — replacing original singer Johan Liiva — and the combination of Gossow's ferocious delivery with Daniel Erlandsson's most technically refined drumming to date produced Arch Enemy's commercial breakthrough and the album that defined melodic death metal drumming for a generation.

Daniel Erlandsson had co-founded Arch Enemy in 1996 alongside the Amott brothers. By 2001, five years of relentless development had transformed him into one of the most technically complete players in extreme metal. *Wages of Sin* is the document of that peak: his blast beats are among the fastest and cleanest of any Gothenburg-era recording, his groove integration is more sophisticated than any previous Arch Enemy album, and his stamina through the album's most demanding sections is remarkable.

For the *Wages of Sin* sessions at Fredman Studios in Gothenburg, Sweden, Erlandsson played a Tama Starclassic Maple kit — a professional-grade shell series that he used through several subsequent Arch Enemy albums. Producer Fredrik Nordström captured the drum sound with a clarity and punch that became the sonic template for countless melodic death metal productions in the years that followed.

### Daniel Erlandsson's Tama Starclassic Maple

For *Wages of Sin*, Daniel Erlandsson played a Tama Starclassic Maple kit — one of the most respected professional drum series of the late 1990s and early 2000s. The Starclassic Maple's all-maple shell construction delivered a warm, full-bodied tone with focused attack: characteristics that set the album's drum sound apart from the brighter, more clinical production of some contemporary melodic death metal releases.

The twin 22" x 18" bass drum configuration reflected Erlandsson's commitment to independent double-kick from two separate instruments. Tama's Star-Cast mounting system allowed the shells to vibrate freely without hardware contact dampening the resonance — important for a drummer who tuned his toms to specific pitches and used melodic descending fills throughout the album. The 10", 12", 14", and 16" tom configuration gave Erlandsson options for the elaborate fill passages throughout *Wages of Sin*.

The Starclassic Maple's warm character created a contrast with the tight, bright production Fredrik Nordström was known for — the maple depth in the shells gave the drum sound a natural warmth that Nordström preserved in the recording, making *Wages of Sin's* drums feel more three-dimensional than many productions from this era.

### The Warm Crack: Maple Snare in a Gothenburg Production

The snare sound on *Wages of Sin* is one of the most distinctive in melodic death metal. Warm, fat, and punchy — different from the bright, cutting steel snares typical of Swedish death metal — the maple shell produced a tone that complemented the melodic sensibility of the Amott brothers' guitar arrangements.

At 14" x 6.5", the depth gave the drum sufficient body for high-tempo playing without the snare becoming overwhelmed by the bass drums' low-end presence. The maple shell's warmth meant that even at the fast snare patterns throughout "Ravenous" and "Dead Inside," every hit retained tonal character rather than becoming a dry, undifferentiated crack.

Fredrik Nordström's production preserved the snare's full character while adding enough top-end presence for it to cut through two guitars and a prominent bass. The resulting snare sound influenced a generation of Scandinavian melodic death metal productions.

### Paiste Signature Cymbals: Warmth Over Aggression

Erlandsson's choice of Paiste Signature cymbals for *Wages of Sin* was unconventional for the genre. Where many Scandinavian death metal drummers favored bright, aggressive alternatives, the Paiste Signature's warmer, more complex tonality gave the album's cymbal sound a distinct musicality. The Signature series' hand-hammered B20 bronze construction produces overtones that are richer and more sustained than machine-lathed alternatives.

The 14" Traditional Hi-Hats were Erlandsson's rhythmic foundation through the album's blast sections and groove patterns. Their warm, medium-weight character provided clear stick definition without the harsh brittleness that thin, bright hi-hats can produce at extreme speeds. The three-crash configuration (17", 18", 19") matched his dynamic range: Fast Crashes for rapid re-strike patterns, the Power Crash for major section boundaries. The Tama Iron Cobra — the industry standard for melodic death metal double-kick in 2001 — gave him the mechanical consistency his blast beats demanded through the album's most demanding sections.

## FAQ

**What drums did Daniel Erlandsson use on Wages of Sin?**
Daniel Erlandsson recorded Wages of Sin (2001) on a Tama Starclassic Maple kit — an all-maple professional shell series known for warm, full-bodied tone. His configuration included twin 22" x 18" bass drums, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. His snare was a Tama Starclassic Maple 14" x 6.5". Cymbals were Paiste Signature series, and bass drum pedals were Tama Iron Cobra singles (one per bass drum). The album was produced by Fredrik Nordström at Fredman Studios in Gothenburg. For his complete modern gear profile, see [Daniel Erlandsson's drum setup](/articles/daniel-erlandsson-drum-setup).

**Why is Wages of Sin significant for Arch Enemy?**
Wages of Sin (2001) is Arch Enemy's commercial breakthrough and first album with vocalist Angela Gossow, who replaced Johan Liiva. The combination of Gossow's ferocious vocal delivery with Daniel Erlandsson's most technically refined early-career drumming produced the album that defined the band's signature sound and established them as one of the leading melodic death metal bands globally. It remains one of the genre's defining releases.

**How does Wages of Sin differ from Daniel Erlandsson's later Arch Enemy gear?**
On Wages of Sin (2001), Erlandsson played a Tama Starclassic Maple kit with Paiste Signature cymbals and Tama Iron Cobra pedals. His current setup uses a Pearl Reference Pure kit in custom black finish, a Pearl Daniel Erlandsson Signature snare (14" x 5.5"), Sabian AAX and HHX cymbals, and Pearl Eliminator double pedal. The transition from Tama to Pearl reflects his evolution as an endorsed artist, while the shift from Paiste Signature to Sabian provides a brighter, more aggressive cymbal character suited to modern Arch Enemy productions.
