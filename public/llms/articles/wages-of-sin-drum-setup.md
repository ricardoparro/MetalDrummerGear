## Overview

Released in March 2002 on Century Media Records, Arch Enemy's fourth studio album Wages of Sin is the most consequential record in the band's history. It was the debut of vocalist Angela Gossow, who replaced Johan Liiva and immediately transformed Arch Enemy from a respected Gothenburg underground act into one of the most widely heard melodic death metal bands on earth. The album reached mainstream rock charts across Europe, launched an international touring campaign of unprecedented scale, and remains the primary entry point for the vast majority of Arch Enemy's global fanbase more than two decades later.

Daniel Erlandsson's drumming on this record is simultaneously the most technically demanding and the most compositionally sophisticated he had delivered to that point: blast beats deployed with precision, double-kick patterns that lock with the Amott brothers' guitar lines, and a dynamic range that moves from full-velocity aggression to mid-paced groove without losing intensity.

### Drum Kit: Tama Starclassic Maple

By 2002, Arch Enemy's Century Media signing and the momentum behind the Gossow lineup change came with increased resources. Daniel Erlandsson's move to Tama Starclassic Maple shells represented a significant upgrade from earlier Arch Enemy recording setups. The Starclassic Maple's all-maple construction provides the combination of warm fundamental tone and defined attack that Studio Fredman's direct recording philosophy required: clean articulation for double-kick patterns, controlled sustain for tom fills, and enough natural resonance to carry in large live venues.

The double bass drum configuration — two 22" x 18" kick drums — is the structural foundation of Arch Enemy's recorded sound on Wages of Sin. Tracks like "Enemy Within," "Ravenous," and "Savage Messiah" are built on sustained double-bass foundations running at 180–210 BPM. Erlandsson's compact tom spread (10", 12", 14", 16") keeps tom fills fast and articulate — melodic punctuation rather than technical display.

### Snare: Tama Bell Brass 14" x 6.5"

Bell brass shells produce a snare sound that is distinctly brighter and more cutting than maple or birch alternatives — essential for cutting through Michael and Christopher Amott's dense dual-guitar arrangements without smearing at high velocity. The 6.5" depth provides enough body for weight and presence while maintaining the fast response that blast sections require. Tuned medium-high, it delivers the dry, defined crack that is one of the sonic signatures of Wages of Sin's drum production.

### Cymbals: Paiste 2002 Series

The Paiste 2002 Series — a B8 alloy formula developed in the 1970s — delivers bright, clean, cutting character that became the preferred choice of countless extreme metal drummers. On Wages of Sin, the 2002 hi-hats maintain consistent articulation at 16th-note patterns even at 200+ BPM. The 2002 China cymbal marks every major riff change and structural transition with the aggressive, flanged character that defines Gothenburg melodic death metal production. The 2002 crashes provide fast attack for verse-to-chorus transitions; the 20" ride appears in the album's comparative mid-tempo passages.

### Pedals: Axis A-21 Long-Board

The Axis A-21 long-board is the key hardware decision of the Wages of Sin setup. Its extended footboard reduces physical strain during sustained double-kick passages and improves stroke consistency at extreme tempos. The Axis cam system is lighter and more responsive than heavy-duty competitor mechanisms, prioritising speed and endurance over maximum power — the correct choice for an album where double-kick sustains across five-minute songs at 200 BPM. At the velocities that "Enemy Within" and "Savage Messiah" demand, pedal quality directly affects both accuracy and endurance.

## Recording Context: Studio Fredman 2001

Wages of Sin was recorded at Studio Fredman with Fredrik Nordström applying the same close-miked, direct production philosophy he developed across the Gothenburg melodic death metal scene throughout the 1990s. No room microphones. Aggressive gating on all drum channels. Kick drums tracked individually with large-diaphragm dynamics (AKG D112 style). Snare captured with a single Shure SM57 — letting the bell brass character speak directly. The result is the clean, defined Arch Enemy drum sound: each instrument audible and independent even in the densest blast passages.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum setup did Daniel Erlandsson use on Arch Enemy's Wages of Sin?**
A: Tama Starclassic Maple kit (natural finish, double 22" x 18" bass drums), Tama Bell Brass snare (14" x 6.5"), Paiste 2002 Series cymbals (14" hi-hats, 16" and 18" crashes, 20" ride, 18" China), Axis A-21 Long-Board double pedal, Vic Firth 5B sticks. Recorded at Studio Fredman with producer Fredrik Nordström.

**Q: Why is Wages of Sin Daniel Erlandsson's most important album?**
A: Wages of Sin (2002) is the Angela Gossow debut that transformed Arch Enemy from a Gothenburg underground act into an internationally recognised melodic death metal band. It charted across Europe and launched a world touring campaign of unprecedented scale. Erlandsson's drumming on the record — blast beat precision, sustained double-kick at extreme tempos, integrated melodic fill vocabulary — is the most accomplished he had delivered to that point, and the tracks remain Arch Enemy's most-performed live songs.

**Q: What pedals did Daniel Erlandsson use on Wages of Sin?**
A: Axis A-21 Long-Board double bass pedals. The long-board design reduces physical strain during sustained double-kick passages and improves stroke consistency at 200+ BPM. The Axis cam system is lighter and more responsive than heavier mechanisms, prioritising speed and endurance — critical for an album where double-kick sustains across full-length songs at extreme tempos. In subsequent years, Erlandsson transitioned to the Pearl Eliminator for his current setup.

**Q: How does the 2002 Wages of Sin setup compare to Daniel Erlandsson's current gear?**
A: His current setup uses Pearl Reference Pure kit (custom black finish), Pearl Daniel Erlandsson Signature snare (14" x 5.5"), Sabian AAX/HHX cymbals, and Pearl Eliminator double pedal — a shift from Tama to Pearl and Paiste to Sabian reflecting two decades of touring development and endorsement relationships. Both configurations are top-tier professional gear; the Wages of Sin setup is the earlier, rawer version of the same perfectionist approach. Full comparison at [Daniel Erlandsson drum setup](https://metalforge.io/articles/daniel-erlandsson-drum-setup).

**Q: Who produced Wages of Sin and how was the drum sound recorded?**
A: Produced by Fredrik Nordström at Studio Fredman, Gothenburg, 2001. Close-miked throughout with no room microphones, aggressive gating for definition, and both 22" kick drums tracked individually. The Tama Bell Brass snare was captured with a single Shure SM57. The result is Wages of Sin's clean, powerful drum sound: precise and aggressive without reverb bloom. The same approach Nordström used for At the Gates' Slaughter of the Soul — see [Slaughter of the Soul drum setup](https://metalforge.io/articles/slaughter-of-the-soul-drum-setup) for the Studio Fredman production comparison.
