Complete breakdown of the gear behind the Sex Pistols cover and the millennium-era Motörhead sound.

## Overview

Released on 15 May 2000 through SPV/Steamhammer (with CMC International distributing in North America), *We Are Motörhead* is the band's fifteenth studio album and Mikkey Dee's fifth full studio record with the band. It marked a clean break from the four-album run producer Howard Benson had overseen since Dee's arrival: this time, the band produced the record themselves alongside Bob Kulick, Bruce Bouillet, and Duane Baron, with Bill Cooper engineering.

The sessions themselves were unusually far-flung for a Motörhead record. Principal tracking took place at Karo Studios in Brackel, Germany, between June and August 1999, with overdubs completed months later at American Recorders in Calabasas, California, between December 1999 and March 2000 — a transatlantic production timeline that gave the album nearly a year of gestation, a sharp contrast to the compressed schedule behind [*Snake Bite Love*](/articles/snake-bite-love-drum-setup) two years earlier. The ten-track album blends the band's usual hard-driving originals with a cover of the Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen," filmed for a promo video atop an open-top London double-decker bus — one of the more memorable visual stunts of the band's later career.

Commercially, *We Are Motörhead* charted at #91 on the UK Albums chart and #5 on the UK Rock & Metal Albums chart, with additional placements in Germany (#21), Sweden (#24), and Finland (#39). Lemmy Kilmister later reflected that he considered it "a much better album than it got credit for," noting that its commercial performance suffered partly because it arrived at the same time as a wave of Motörhead compilation releases competing for the same audience.

For Mikkey Dee, the extended, multi-studio production gave him more room than usual to refine his sound, and this is the album where his kit began a gradual evolution — the first hints of the Tama Starclassic tone that would define his later 2000s work, alongside a shift in his cymbal setup that moved him away from the Giant Beat hi-hats he'd used since 1992. This article breaks down that transition in full.

### The Starclassic Era Begins: A New Tama Kit for a New Decade

*We Are Motörhead* is where Mikkey Dee's drum kit began its quiet evolution away from the Tama Artstar II/Swingstar setup he had used on every Motörhead album since *Bastards* (1993). With a new production team and an unusually long recording schedule spanning two studios and two countries, Dee moved to an early configuration of the Tama Starclassic line — the maple/birch hybrid shells that would fully define his sound by [*Inferno*](/articles/inferno-drum-setup) (2004).

The core dimensions stayed familiar: a 22" bass drum, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, and 16"/18" floor toms, matching the configuration he'd used for seven years. What changed was tone — the Starclassic's hybrid shell construction gave the drums a punchier, more contemporary attack, better suited to the more polished, multi-studio production Kulick, Bouillet, and Baron brought to the sessions compared to Howard Benson's leaner, faster approach on the band's 1990s records.

Recording at Karo Studios in Germany first, then finishing overdubs at American Recorders in California months later, meant Dee's kit had to translate across two very different rooms and, in effect, two separate recording periods. The newer Starclassic shells proved more consistent across that gap than the older Artstar II kit might have been, giving engineer Bill Cooper a more stable tonal target to match between the German and Californian sessions.

On tracks like the driving opener "See Me Burning" and the title track "We Are Motörhead," the kit's newer voicing is audible in the tighter low-end punch and slightly more defined tom attack compared to *Snake Bite Love* — a genuinely different drum character, even with the song structures and Dee's playing style remaining unmistakably his own.

### Steel Snare, Now with a Two-Studio Test

Dee's snare on *We Are Motörhead* kept the same 14" x 6.5" steel-shell dimensions he'd used since *Bastards*, but the drum itself was the newer Starclassic-series steel snare, matched to his evolving kit. The bright, cutting tone remained the constant through-line of his sound, even as the shell construction underneath it modernized.

Recording the snare across two separate sessions — Karo Studios in Germany and American Recorders months later in California — required unusually careful attention to tuning consistency. Engineer Bill Cooper had to match the drum's tone across an ocean and several months' gap, a genuine technical challenge that the Starclassic's more consistent manufacturing tolerances helped make achievable.

On "Stay Out of Jail" and the Sex Pistols cover "God Save the Queen," the snare's crack drives the tracks with the same aggressive backbeat Dee had used throughout the 1990s, but with a marginally tighter, more controlled decay — a subtle signature of the newer shell and the more polished production this album received compared to *Snake Bite Love*'s rushed sessions.

### Retiring the Giant Beat: A New Hi-Hat Voice

*We Are Motörhead* marks the moment Mikkey Dee retired the Paiste Giant Beat 15" hi-hats he had used on every Motörhead recording since his very first session, 1992's "Hellraiser." In their place came Paiste 2002 Sound Edge hi-hats — same 15" size, but with a serrated bottom cymbal that produces a tighter, more controlled chick sound better suited to the more polished, multi-producer approach this album received.

The rest of the cymbal setup — the 22" 2002 ride, 18"/20" crashes, and 18" China — carried over unchanged from *Snake Bite Love*, meaning the Sound Edge hi-hat swap was a deliberate, isolated update rather than a wholesale cymbal overhaul. It's a subtle but real evolution: the same bright, cutting Paiste 2002 alloy Dee had relied on since 1993, refined at exactly one point in the setup to match a more contemporary production standard.

On the Sex Pistols cover "God Save the Queen," the tighter Sound Edge hi-hats give Dee's driving eighth-note patterns a crisper, more controlled edge than the looser Giant Beat sound would have produced — a small technical choice that suited the track's punk-adjacent urgency particularly well.

## Key Facts

- *We Are Motörhead*, released 15 May 2000, is Motörhead's fifteenth studio album and Dee's fifth full studio record with the band
- First album without producer Howard Benson — self-produced by Motörhead with Bob Kulick, Bruce Bouillet, and Duane Baron
- Recorded across two continents: Karo Studios in Brackel, Germany (1999) and American Recorders in Calabasas, California (1999–2000)
- Features a cover of the Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen," with a video shot atop a London double-decker bus
- The album where Dee's gear began transitioning from Tama Artstar II toward the Starclassic line he'd use on [*Inferno*](/articles/inferno-drum-setup)
- First Motörhead album to feature Tama's newer Starclassic shell construction rather than the Artstar II/Swingstar Dee had used since 1993
- 22" bass drum and core tom configuration unchanged, but tonal character noticeably punchier
- Tracked across two studios on two continents — Karo Studios (Germany) and American Recorders (California)
- A transitional kit bridging Dee's 1990s Tama sound and the fuller Starclassic voicing of Inferno (2004)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What drums did Mikkey Dee use on We Are Motörhead?**

A: On *We Are Motörhead* (2000), Mikkey Dee transitioned to an early configuration of the Tama Starclassic line, moving away from the Artstar II/Swingstar kit he'd used since [*Bastards*](/articles/bastards-drum-setup) (1993). The core dimensions stayed the same — a 22" bass drum, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, and 16"/18" floor toms — but the Starclassic's maple/birch hybrid shells gave the kit a punchier, more contemporary tone that would fully define his sound by [*Inferno*](/articles/inferno-drum-setup) (2004). See the [Mikkey Dee drummer profile](/drummer/mikkey-dee) for his complete gear evolution.

**Q: Why did Motörhead record We Are Motörhead in two different countries?**

A: *We Are Motörhead* was tracked at Karo Studios in Brackel, Germany, during 1999, with overdubs completed months later at American Recorders in Calabasas, California, in early 2000. This was the band's first self-produced album since Mikkey Dee joined, made alongside Bob Kulick, Bruce Bouillet, and Duane Baron, and the extended, multi-location schedule reflected the more collaborative production approach they were taking after four straight albums with Howard Benson.

**Q: Did Mikkey Dee change his cymbal setup on We Are Motörhead?**

A: Yes. *We Are Motörhead* is the album where Dee retired the Paiste Giant Beat 15" hi-hats he had used since his first-ever Motörhead session, 1992's "Hellraiser," replacing them with Paiste 2002 Sound Edge 15" hi-hats. The rest of his cymbal setup — the 22" ride, 18"/20" crashes, and 18" China — carried over unchanged from [*Snake Bite Love*](/articles/snake-bite-love-drum-setup).

**Q: What is the Sex Pistols cover on We Are Motörhead?**

A: *We Are Motörhead* includes a cover of the Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen," for which the band filmed a promo video atop an open-top double-decker bus in London. Mikkey Dee's newly adopted Paiste 2002 Sound Edge hi-hats gave the track's driving pattern a tighter, more controlled edge than his earlier Giant Beat setup.
