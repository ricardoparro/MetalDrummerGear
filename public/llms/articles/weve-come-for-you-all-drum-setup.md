# We've Come for You All Drum Setup: Charlie Benante's Anthrax Gear Breakdown

> Complete breakdown of Charlie Benante's drum setup on Anthrax's We've Come for You All (2003) — the final John Bush-era studio album, the post-9/11 anthem "What Doesn't Die," and the Tama Starclassic/Paiste configuration that closed the DW Collector's/Sabian chapter opened on Sound of White Noise.

**Type:** Album Drum Setup
**Drummer(s):** [Charlie Benante](/llms/drummers/charlie-benante.md)
**Band / Album:** Anthrax — *We've Come for You All* (2003)
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Groove Metal

## Overview

Released on May 6, 2003 through Nuclear Blast in Europe and Sanctuary Records in North America, We've Come for You All closed out a five-year gap since Volume 8 (1998) and stands as the final Anthrax studio album to feature John Bush on vocals before Joey Belladonna's 2005 return. It was also the first Anthrax record to feature Rob Caggiano on lead guitar. Where Volume 8 had struggled commercially against the nu-metal tide of the late 1990s, We've Come for You All found Anthrax sharpening its attack and reconnecting with the aggression that had defined the band's thrash years, while still carrying the groove sensibility John Bush had brought to the lineup a decade earlier.

The album was recorded under the Scrap 60 Productions banner — guitarist Rob Caggiano, engineer Eddie Wohl, and Steve Regina sharing production duties. Drums were tracked at BearTracks Recording Studio in Suffern, New York, with the rest of the album split between Red Clay Studios in Suffern and Avatar Studios in Manhattan for guitar overdubs.

We've Come for You All carries unmistakable post-9/11 weight. "Superhero" was written as a tribute to New York City's firefighters and first responders, and "What Doesn't Die" became one of the era's defining metal anthems of resilience. The album's cover art was illustrated by comic artist Alex Ross, with guest contributions from Roger Daltrey of The Who and the late Dimebag Darrell of Pantera. Commercially, it debuted at #122 on the Billboard 200 and reached #10 on the UK Rock & Metal Albums chart, and it earned a nomination for Outstanding Hard Rock Album at the 2004 California Music Awards.

For Charlie Benante, the album documents a quiet but important gear transition: after a decade built around the DW Collector's Series kit and Sabian cymbals he adopted for Sound of White Noise (1993), Benante is fully back on Tama Starclassic drums and Paiste cymbals by 2003 — a configuration that would carry forward, largely unchanged, through Worship Music (2011) and beyond.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple, all-maple shells with Star-Cast mounting — dual 22" x 18" bass drums, 10"/12"/13" rack toms, 14" and 16" floor toms
- **Snare:** Tama Artstar Custom steel shell, 14" x 6.5", die-cast hoops
- **Cymbals:** Paiste 2002 / Signature series — 14" Sound Edge Hi-Hats, 16" Signature Fast Crash, 18" 2002 Medium Crash, 19" RUDE Crash/Ride, 22" 2002 Heavy Ride, 18" 2002 China
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Tama HP35 Camco double pedal configuration (two single pedals, 1984-2010 era); Tama Titan Hi-Hat Stand; Tama 1st Chair throne; Pro-Mark Hickory 2B sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Coated Ambassador (batter, snare), Remo Ambassador Snare Side (resonant), Remo Powerstroke 3 Clear (kick), Remo Emperor Coated (toms)
- **Snare tuning:** Medium-high for cut and projection

### Benante's 2003 Tama Starclassic Configuration

By the time Anthrax tracked We've Come for You All, Charlie Benante had moved off the DW Collector's Series kit documented on Sound of White Noise (1993) and settled back into Tama's Starclassic Maple line — a configuration that Stomp 442 (1995) and Volume 8 (1998) had already begun reintroducing. The dual 22" x 18" bass drums kept the deeper shell depth of the DW era rather than reverting to the 16"-deep Artstar II shells of the Among the Living years, giving Benante a kick tone with more low-end body than his 1980s sound while regaining Tama's brighter, more articulate attack.

The tom configuration expanded back to two floor toms (14" and 16") alongside the 10"/12"/13" rack tom progression — a fuller layout than the single-floor-tom Sound of White Noise setup, reflecting the album's heavier, more thrash-adjacent arrangements.

### Back to Paiste: The 2003 Cymbal Lineup

We've Come for You All documents Charlie Benante fully back on Paiste after the Sabian chapter that began with Sound of White Noise in 1993. The 2002 and Signature series mix gave him the bright, cutting cymbal voice that had defined Anthrax's thrash sound since Among the Living. The 14" 2002 Sound Edge hi-hats anchor the album's groove on tracks like "Taking the Music Back," while the crash configuration — 16" Signature Fast Crash, 18" 2002 Medium Crash, 19" RUDE Crash/Ride — gives Benante a dynamic range from quick accents to sustained, explosive hits.

### Tama HP35 Camco: Still Seven Years from Retirement

We've Come for You All was recorded squarely within Charlie Benante's 26-year relationship with the Tama HP35 Camco chain-drive pedal — a run that began in 1984 and wouldn't end until the Speed Cobra switch in 2010, seven years after this album. Because Benante played two separate 22" bass drums rather than a single drum with a double pedal, his setup used two single HP35 units rather than a combined double-pedal mechanism.

## Key Facts

- Recorded at BearTracks Recording Studio, Red Clay Studios (Suffern, NY), and Avatar Studios (New York, NY)
- Produced by Scrap 60 Productions: Rob Caggiano, Eddie Wohl, and Steve Regina
- Final Anthrax studio album with John Bush on vocals before Joey Belladonna rejoined in 2005
- First Anthrax album to feature Rob Caggiano on lead guitar
- Debuted at #122 on the Billboard 200; reached #10 on the UK Rock & Metal Albums chart
- Nominated for Outstanding Hard Rock Album at the 2004 California Music Awards
- "What Doesn't Die" became a widely cited post-9/11 metal anthem
- Cover art by comic artist Alex Ross; guest appearances from Roger Daltrey (The Who) and Dimebag Darrell (Pantera)
- Charlie Benante's gear fully returns to Tama Starclassic Maple and Paiste 2002/Signature cymbals, closing the DW Collector's Series / Sabian chapter opened on Sound of White Noise (1993)
- Tama HP35 Camco pedals continue uninterrupted — used from 1984 until the Speed Cobra switch in 2010
