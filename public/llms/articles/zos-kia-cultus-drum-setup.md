# Zos Kia Cultus Drum Setup: Inferno's Transitional Arsenal for Behemoth's 2002 Symphonic-Death Bridge Album

> Complete breakdown of Inferno's drum setup for Behemoth's Zos Kia Cultus (Here and Beyond) (2002). Discover the Tama Starclassic Maple kit, first Meinl Byzance integration, and gear that bridged Thelema.6 and Demigod.

**Genre:** Blackened Death Metal

## Overview

*Zos Kia Cultus (Here and Beyond)* (2002) is Behemoth's fifth studio album and the final record before *Demigod* (2004) launched them into international metal consciousness. Released on Avantgarde Music and Metal Blade Records, it stands at the hinge point between the band's black metal-influenced earlier work and the fully realized blackened death metal assault that would define their mature era. Recorded at Hertz Studios in Poland — the same facility that would host *Demigod*, *Evangelion*, and *The Satanist* — the album documents Behemoth at their most transitional.

Inferno — Zbigniew Robert Promiński — navigated a setup on *Zos Kia Cultus* that reflected the band's own transition. His Tama Starclassic Maple configuration continued from the *Thelema.6* (2000) era, but this album marked a critical shift in his cymbal approach: it was the first album to feature a notable Meinl Byzance integration, establishing the relationship with Meinl that would define his sound through *Demigod*, *Evangelion*, and *The Satanist*. The Hertz Studios recording environment also produced a meaningfully different drum sound compared to earlier Behemoth albums — denser, more orchestrated, with a kick attack profile that distinguished *Zos Kia Cultus* from the rawer productions of *Satanica* (1999) and *Thelema.6*.

## Gear Breakdown

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple (studio configuration, double 22" kick)
- **Snare:** Tama Steel Snare, 14" x 6"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance (early integration) — 14" hi-hats, 16" and 18" crashes, 20" ride, 18" China
- **Hardware / Pedals:** Tama Iron Cobra Double Pedal; Vic Firth 5B sticks
- **Heads:** Remo Coated Ambassador (batter), Remo Ambassador (resonant), Remo Powerstroke P3 (kick batter)
- **Snare tuning:** High tension for cut and definition through dense symphonic arrangements

### Inferno's Tama Starclassic Maple: Zos Kia Cultus Configuration

The Tama Starclassic Maple configuration Inferno used on *Zos Kia Cultus* carried forward from his *Thelema.6*-era setup while beginning expansions that would characterize his more complex later work. The all-maple shell construction delivered a warm fundamental with clearly defined attack — qualities that suited Hertz Studios' capturing approach and the album's denser, more orchestrated arrangements.

The double 22" bass drum configuration was consistent with Inferno's approach across this period. Two independent kick drums gave him mechanical independence on each foot — essential for the sustained double-bass passages that drive tracks like 'Horns ov Baphomet'. The kick attack, shaped by the Hertz Studios recording environment, carried a more deliberate, punishing quality compared to the rawer kick tone on *Satanica* and *Thelema.6*.

### Meinl Byzance: The First Integration

*Zos Kia Cultus* represents the beginning of one of extreme metal drumming's most identifiable gear relationships: Inferno and Meinl Byzance cymbals. This first notable Byzance integration established the sonic direction that would carry forward through Inferno's entire Behemoth career.

The Byzance series' hand-hammered construction and complex lathing produce a sound that sits between the brightness of traditional rock cymbals and the controlled darkness of purpose-built extreme metal options. For *Zos Kia Cultus*'s orchestrated arrangements, this tonal complexity served a specific function: the cymbals added harmonic richness to the sonic picture without the wash that would blur the rhythmic framework during sustained blast passages.

The 18" China cymbal integration was particularly significant. Behemoth's compositional language from this period made heavy use of accent punctuation — transitions between blast sections and more arranged passages. The Byzance China's aggressive, trashy character provided exactly the kinetic accents these moments required, prefiguring the prominent China usage that would characterize Inferno's setup through *Demigod* and beyond.

### Tama Steel Snare: Cutting Through Orchestration

The *Zos Kia Cultus* production demanded a snare that could cut through layers of symphonic arrangement. Inferno's steel snare delivered a focused, bright crack with fast decay — essential for maintaining rhythmic clarity during blast passages within the album's dense arrangements. Tuned to high tension, it spoke with authority through the orchestrated guitar and keyboard layers that characterized Behemoth's evolving compositional approach.

## FAQ

**What drums did Inferno use on Zos Kia Cultus?**
On Zos Kia Cultus (2002), Inferno used a Tama Starclassic Maple kit configured with double 22" kick drums, rack toms, and floor toms. The all-maple shell construction delivered warmth and tonal definition suited to the Hertz Studios recording environment and the album's denser arrangements. This Tama Starclassic Maple configuration carried forward from the Thelema.6 (2000) era and continued through the Demigod (2004) sessions.

**What cymbals did Inferno use on Zos Kia Cultus?**
Zos Kia Cultus (2002) marked the first notable Meinl Byzance cymbal integration in Inferno's Behemoth studio work. The Byzance setup included 14" hi-hats, 16" and 18" crashes, a 20" ride, and an 18" China cymbal. The Byzance series' hand-hammered construction produces a complex, dark tonal character that suits orchestrated extreme metal arrangements without excessive cymbal wash — establishing the sonic direction Inferno would maintain through Demigod and Evangelion.

**How does the Zos Kia Cultus drum setup differ from Thelema.6 and Demigod?**
Zos Kia Cultus introduced both new cymbal choices (early Meinl Byzance) and a new studio environment (Hertz Studios, Poland) compared to Thelema.6 (2000). The Hertz Studios recording produced a denser, more orchestrated drum sound than the rawer Thelema.6 production. Compared to Demigod (2004), Zos Kia Cultus's drum sound is slightly less refined — this was among Behemoth's earliest Hertz Studios sessions, and the production approach would develop further before Demigod was recorded.

**Why is Zos Kia Cultus significant in Inferno's gear history?**
Zos Kia Cultus (2002) is significant in Inferno's gear history primarily because it documents the first notable integration of Meinl Byzance cymbals into his Behemoth studio setup — the beginning of a relationship with Meinl that would define his sound through Demigod (2004), Evangelion (2009), The Satanist (2014), and beyond. The album also marks the last Behemoth record before the Demigod breakthrough, placing it at a pivotal point in the band's and Inferno's career trajectory.
