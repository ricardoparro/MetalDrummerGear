# Pete Sandoval Drum Kit Evolution — Complete Timeline

Pete Sandoval's evolution is essentially the history of extreme metal drumming speed. Joining Morbid Angel in 1988, he introduced sustained blast beats at tempos no drummer in the genre had previously achieved on record, culminating in Covenant (1993) — still cited as a benchmark for extreme drumming velocity. His gear progression from a stripped-down Pearl Export kit to a professional Pearl Reference platform tracks the growth of a physical instrument pushed to its biological limits. His story ends with human cost: multiple back surgeries forced his retirement in 2011, making his arc one of the most significant — and poignant — in the history of extreme music. This timeline documents his complete gear evolution across his 23-year career.

## Early Morbid Angel / Altars of Madness Era (1988–1991)

**Albums:** Altars of Madness (1989), Abominations of Desolation (recorded 1986, released 1991)
**Tours:** Earache Records European tour 1989, North American death metal circuit

Pete Sandoval joined Morbid Angel in 1988, replacing Mike Browning ahead of what would become one of the most important debut albums in death metal history: Altars of Madness (1989, Earache). Sandoval arrived with a natural gift for extreme speed — blast beats at tempos that no drummer in the genre had previously achieved on record. His early setup was functional rather than refined, prioritising raw power and the ability to sustain extreme tempos over tonal nuance.

- **Drums:** Pearl Export / Masters Series — maple shells: 22" kick, 10"/12"/14" toms (the standard for Florida death metal drummers of the era; stripped to essential drums for maximum speed)
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×5.5" (steel shell for explosive, cutting attack through the wall of distortion)
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — 13" hi-hats, 16"/18" crashes, 20" ride (durable and available; tonal nuance secondary to durability at these tempos)
- **Hardware:** Pearl P-201 Double Pedal (chain-drive; Sandoval's natural speed often exceeded what the hardware was designed for)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B (heavier sticks for power and durability at extreme tempos)
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador single-ply (replaced frequently due to breakage at extreme blast beat velocities)
- **Estimated kit cost (original):** ~$3,000

**Key developments:**
- Joined Morbid Angel in 1988, replacing Mike Browning
- Altars of Madness (1989) — one of the most important debut albums in death metal history
- Introduced sustained extreme blast beats to death metal recording
- Pearl kit provided a durable platform for Florida death metal's most extreme drummer
- Established blast beat as a compositional tool rather than a transitional effect

> "When I joined Morbid Angel, I wanted to play faster than anyone had ever played. I didn't think about technique — I just played as fast as I could and figured out the rest later." — *Metal Maniacs Interview, 1990*

## Covenant / Speed Peak Era (1992–2000)

**Albums:** Blessed Are the Sick (1991), Covenant (1993), Domination (1995), Formulas Fatal to the Flesh (1998)
**Tours:** Morbid Angel world tours 1992–1999, Monsters of Death tour 1995, various European festival dates

The peak of Pete Sandoval's physical capabilities. Covenant (1993, Giant/Earache) is widely considered the apex of his blast beat speed — recorded at tempos most drummers considered physically impossible, it is still cited as a benchmark for extreme drumming velocity. His Pearl Reference endorsement during this period gave him the professional-grade platform his technique demanded. Formulas Fatal to the Flesh (1998) showed the first signs of the physical toll his extreme playing was beginning to take.

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Series — maple/birch hybrid: 22" kick, 10"/12"/14"/16" toms *(upgrade — brighter, more projecting tone suited to increasingly high production values)*
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5" *(upgrade — deeper shell became Sandoval's signature snare sound on Covenant and Domination)*
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Custom Series — 13"/14" hi-hats, 16"/17"/18" crashes, 20" ride *(upgrade — crisper transient for the increasingly precise studio recordings)*
- **Hardware:** Pearl Eliminator Double Pedal — cam-driven *(switch — adjustable cam allowed Sandoval to fine-tune the speed/power ratio at peak tempos)*
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B (maintained heavier hickory preference at peak career velocity)
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor / Powerstroke 3 — Emperor on toms, Powerstroke 3 on kick *(upgrade — double-ply durability at extreme sustained velocities)*
- **Electronics:** Drum triggers (kick) *(new — basic kick triggers for live performance consistency)*
- **Estimated kit cost (original):** ~$8,000

**Key developments:**
- Pearl Reference Series endorsement — professional-grade platform
- Pearl Eliminator double pedal adopted — key tool for extreme blast beat control
- Covenant (1993) — widely cited as the apex of death metal blast beat speed
- Kick triggers integrated for live consistency
- Formulas Fatal to the Flesh (1998) — first signs of physical strain from extreme playing demands

> "The Eliminator gave me control I never had before. I could feel every beat, every subdivision. Covenant was us going to the absolute limit of what was possible." — *Modern Drummer, 1994*

## Health Decline / Final Era (2000–2011)

**Albums:** Gateways to Annihilation (2000), Heretic (2003, partial — Derek Roddy recorded), Illud Divinum Insanus (2011)
**Tours:** Final Morbid Angel touring dates 2000–2010, selective festival appearances only as health permitted

The final chapter of Pete Sandoval's career as a working drummer — and its most humanly significant. Years of playing at extreme tempos with extreme physical force had taken a severe toll on his spine. He missed recording sessions for Heretic (2003), replaced by Derek Roddy, before returning to document his final period with Morbid Angel. Illud Divinum Insanus (2011) was his last album; major back surgery that same year forced his retirement. The Pearl Reference setup that had served him through the Covenant peak remained consistent through the end, as the focus shifted from optimisation to survival.

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Series (continued) — maple/birch hybrid, same configuration as peak era (stability and familiarity prioritised as health constraints limited adaptation)
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5" (consistent snare, no change from peak era)
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Custom Series (same configuration through the final era, no significant changes)
- **Hardware:** Pearl Eliminator Double Pedal (continued; tension and cam settings modified to reduce physical strain)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B (maintained heavier hickory through retirement)
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor / Powerstroke 3 (same durable configuration maintained through the final albums)
- **Electronics:** ddrum Acoustic Pro Triggers *(upgrade — more sophisticated triggers for the final touring period at reduced physical output)*
- **Estimated kit cost (original):** ~$9,000

**Key developments:**
- Back injuries begin limiting live appearances and recording sessions
- Derek Roddy replaced Sandoval on Heretic (2003) recording
- Gateways to Annihilation (2000) — final complete Sandoval-era studio album before health decline
- Illud Divinum Insanus (2011) — final Morbid Angel album; final recording
- Major back surgery (2011) — retired from drumming
- Legacy: established blast beat as death metal's primary rhythmic language; influenced every extreme metal drummer who followed

> "The body gives out before the mind does. I still hear the music perfectly. But after the surgery, I knew that part of my life was over. I have no regrets — I played as hard as any human being could." — *Terrorizer Magazine, 2012*

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Pete Sandoval use on Covenant?**
A: Pete Sandoval recorded Covenant (1993) — still cited as a benchmark for extreme drumming velocity — with a Pearl Reference Series kit featuring maple/birch hybrid shells. His snare was a deep Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5", cymbals were Zildjian A Custom, and the Pearl Eliminator double pedal gave him the fine-tuned speed/power control the album's tempos demanded.

**Q: Why did Pete Sandoval retire from drumming?**
A: Pete Sandoval retired in 2011 after multiple back surgeries. Years of playing extreme blast beats at physically extreme tempos took a severe cumulative toll on his spine — he had already missed recording sessions for Heretic (2003), replaced by Derek Roddy, before major back surgery in 2011 forced his full retirement from Morbid Angel.

**Q: What double bass pedal did Pete Sandoval use?**
A: Pete Sandoval played the Pearl Eliminator double pedal for the majority of his career, from the Covenant era (1992) through his 2011 retirement. Its adjustable cam system let him fine-tune the speed and power ratio that defined his sound. Earlier, during the Altars of Madness era, he used the more basic Pearl P-201 chain-drive double pedal.

**Q: Is Pete Sandoval considered the fastest death metal drummer?**
A: Pete Sandoval is widely regarded as one of the fastest and most influential blast beat drummers in death metal history. His performance on Covenant (1993) is still cited as a benchmark for extreme drumming velocity, and he is credited with establishing the blast beat as death metal's primary rhythmic language.

**Q: Who replaced Pete Sandoval in Morbid Angel?**
A: Derek Roddy recorded Heretic (2003) in Sandoval's place after back injuries prevented Sandoval from completing the sessions. Sandoval returned afterward and remained Morbid Angel's drummer through Illud Divinum Insanus (2011), his final album before retiring due to major back surgery.
