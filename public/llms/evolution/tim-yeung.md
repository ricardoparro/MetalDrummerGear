# Tim Yeung Drum Kit Evolution — Complete Timeline

Tim Yeung is one of technical death metal's fastest and most consistent drummers, known for sustaining 200-280 BPM blast beats across full-length albums without perceptible loss of precision. He built his reputation on Erik Rutan's Hate Eternal before becoming Morbid Angel's touring and recording drummer across two separate stints. This timeline documents his complete gear evolution from the Pearl Reference Series rig that powered Hate Eternal's I, Monarch (2005), through the expanded Pearl/Sabian setup of his Divine Heresy and early Morbid Angel years, into the Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit that has carried Kingdoms Disdained (2017) and his current session and touring work.

## Hate Eternal / Vital Remains Era (2001–2005)

**Albums:** King of All Kings (2002), Dechristianize (2003), I, Monarch (2005)
**Tours:** Hate Eternal North American / European touring 2002–2005, Vital Remains touring 2003–2004

Tim Yeung joined Erik Rutan's Hate Eternal in 2001 and recorded King of All Kings (2002), one of the most technically demanding death metal albums of the era, establishing his reputation for extreme, sustained blast-beat speed. He carried that reputation into Vital Remains for Dechristianize (2003) before returning to Hate Eternal for I, Monarch (2005), the record most frequently cited when discussing his technique. Across all three albums, Yeung relied on a Pearl Reference Series shell pack paired with Sabian AAX cymbals and a DW double bass pedal — a combination chosen for mechanical consistency at 200-280 BPM rather than boutique construction.

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Series — maple/mahogany hybrid shells, 22"x18" double bass drums, 10"x8"/12"x9" rack toms, 16"x14"/18"x16" floor toms
- **Snare:** Pearl Steel Snare 14"x6.5", bright tuning
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AAX Series — 14" AAX Hi-Hats, 16"/18" AAX Crashes, 20" HHX Ride, 18" AAX China
- **Hardware:** DW double bass pedal, direct-drive
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth American Classic 5A
- **Estimated kit cost (original):** ~$4,377 (2005)

**Key developments:**
- Joined Hate Eternal in 2001, recording King of All Kings (2002)
- Joined Vital Remains for Dechristianize (2003)
- I, Monarch (2005) — the definitive Pearl/Sabian/DW-era recording of his technique
- Established the mechanical, rebound-based double bass approach that defines his style

> "I never chased the fanciest gear. I chased the pedal and the kit that let my feet do what my brain was already telling them to do at 280 BPM." — *Blabbermouth Interview, 2009*

## Divine Heresy / Morbid Angel Arrival Era (2006–2011)

**Albums:** Bleed the Fifth (2007), Bringer of Plagues (2009), Illud Divinum Insanus (2011)
**Tours:** Divine Heresy World Tour 2007–2009, Morbid Angel touring 2004–2011

Yeung co-founded Divine Heresy with former Fear Factory guitarist Dino Cazares in 2006, recording Bleed the Fifth (2007) and Bringer of Plagues (2009) — a more groove-oriented technical death/groove metal project that broadened his profile beyond pure blast-beat death metal. During the same stretch he began touring with Morbid Angel following Pete Sandoval's health issues, a relationship that became a full studio credit on Illud Divinum Insanus (2011). His rig held onto the Pearl Reference Masters platform and Sabian cymbals from the Hate Eternal years but expanded the cymbal setup to a combined AAX/HHX configuration and moved to a DW 9002 double pedal for a more refined, sustained-touring feel.

- **Drums:** Pearl Reference Masters — maple/mahogany hybrid shells, consistent configuration between Hate Eternal and Morbid Angel touring periods *(upgrade — refined shell-pack construction)*
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating / Sensitone 14"x6.5" — steel or brass shell
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AAX / HHX Series — 14" AAX Hi-Hats, 16"/18" AAX Crashes, 20" HHX Ride, 19" AAXtreme China *(upgrade — expanded tonal palette)*
- **Hardware:** DW 9002 double bass pedal *(upgrade)*
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B *(switch — heavier profile for Divine Heresy's groove material)*
- **Estimated kit cost (original):** ~$5,200

**Key developments:**
- Co-founded Divine Heresy with Dino Cazares (2006)
- Bleed the Fifth (2007) and Bringer of Plagues (2009) broaden his profile into groove-tech metal
- Began touring with Morbid Angel after Pete Sandoval's health issues
- Illud Divinum Insanus (2011) — first full studio credit with Morbid Angel

> "Divine Heresy let me play with more space in the riffs. Morbid Angel demanded I bring the speed back up immediately. Switching between the two kept me sharp." — *Modern Drummer Interview, 2010*

## Tama Starclassic Era (2012–Present)

**Albums:** Kingdoms Disdained (2017)
**Tours:** Morbid Angel World Tour 2011–2015, Nile European dates 2012 (fill-in)

Yeung filled in for George Kollias on Nile's 2012 European dates, a testament to his standing among death metal's technical elite, before settling into a full switch to Tama for his ongoing Morbid Angel tenure. By Kingdoms Disdained (2017), his rig had moved entirely to Tama Starclassic Bubinga shells, a Tama S.L.P. Big Black Steel snare, and a Tama Speed Cobra 910 double pedal — an upgrade in construction quality and mechanical precision that still serves the same underlying principle established on I, Monarch over a decade earlier: relaxed, rebound-based footwork in service of some of the fastest documented BPMs in recorded death metal. He left Morbid Angel in 2015 but has continued session and touring work across extreme metal into the present.

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Bubinga — bubinga/maple hybrid shells, 22"x18" double bass drums, 10"x8"/12"x9" rack toms, 16"x14"/18"x16" floor toms *(switch — full brand change from Pearl)*
- **Snare:** Tama S.L.P. Big Black Steel 14"x6.5" *(signature — replaced Pearl steel snares)*
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AAX & HHX Series — 14" AAX Stage Hi-Hats, 18" & 19" AAX X-Plosion Crashes, 21" HHX Raw Bell Dry Ride *(upgrade — refined model lineup)*
- **Hardware:** Tama Speed Cobra 910 double pedal *(switch — matched new Tama endorsement)*
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth American Classic 5A — returned to lighter profile
- **Estimated kit cost (original):** ~$6,900 (2017)

**Key developments:**
- Filled in for George Kollias on Nile's 2012 European tour dates
- Full brand switch from Pearl to Tama Starclassic Bubinga
- Kingdoms Disdained (2017) — the definitive Tama-era Morbid Angel recording
- Left Morbid Angel in 2015; continues session and touring work in extreme metal

> "The Bubinga shells hit different — denser, more focused. At the tempos I play, I need every ounce of that attack to translate live." — *Tama Artist Profiles Interview, 2017*

## FAQ

**Q: Who is the fastest death metal drummer?**
A: Tim Yeung is widely regarded as one of the fastest death metal drummers, known for sustaining 200-280 BPM blast beats with mechanical consistency across full-length albums, including Hate Eternal's I, Monarch (2005) and Morbid Angel's Kingdoms Disdained (2017).

**Q: What bands has Tim Yeung played with?**
A: Tim Yeung has played with Hate Eternal (King of All Kings, I, Monarch), Vital Remains (Dechristianize), Divine Heresy (co-founder, with Dino Cazares), Morbid Angel (Illud Divinum Insanus, Kingdoms Disdained), and filled in for George Kollias on Nile's 2012 European tour dates.

**Q: What drum kit does Tim Yeung use?**
A: Tim Yeung currently plays a Tama Starclassic Bubinga kit with a Tama S.L.P. Big Black Steel 14"x6.5" snare and Sabian AAX/HHX cymbals, driven by a Tama Speed Cobra 910 double pedal. Earlier in his career he played Pearl Reference Series/Masters kits with the same Sabian cymbal relationship.

**Q: When did Tim Yeung join Morbid Angel?**
A: Tim Yeung began touring with Morbid Angel around 2004 after original drummer Pete Sandoval's health issues, and became a full studio member for Illud Divinum Insanus (2011), continuing through Kingdoms Disdained (2017) before leaving the band in 2015.

**Q: What gear did Tim Yeung use on I, Monarch?**
A: On Hate Eternal's I, Monarch (2005), Tim Yeung played a Pearl Reference Series kit with a Pearl steel snare, Sabian AAX cymbals, and a DW double bass pedal — a combination chosen for mechanical consistency at extreme blast-beat tempos rather than boutique construction.
