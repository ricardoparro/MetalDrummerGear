# Pete Sandoval Drum Kit Gear History — Morbid Angel

## Gear Timeline

### Altars of Madness Era (1989)

- **Drums:** Rogers Drums (later Pearl Export) — 5-piece; 22"×16" kick (×2), 10"×10" rack, 12"×12" rack, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Pearl Export Steel 14"×5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — 14" hi-hats, 16" crash, 18" crash, 20" ride
- **Pedal:** Camco/DW double pedal (budget option available at the time)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B Hickory
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador clear
- **Original setup cost (1989):** ~$1,800
- **Inflation-adjusted to 2026:** ~$4,740
- **Notable:** Altars of Madness defined the death metal genre's sonic template. Sandoval's blast beats on this album — recorded with relatively modest gear — became the foundational reference for extreme drumming. The double kick configuration was improvised from what was accessible to the band at the time.

### Blessed Are the Sick Era (1991)

- **Drums:** Pearl Export — upgraded hardware; 22"×16" kick (×2), 10"×10" rack, 12"×12" rack, 16"×16" floor, 18"×16" floor (added)
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6" — first upgrade to professional-tier snare
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series (expanded — added second crash for fills)
- **Pedal:** Pearl P-100 double pedal
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B
- **Notable:** Slightly expanded configuration as Morbid Angel secured a deal with Giant Records. The production budget increase allowed minor gear upgrades, though the kit remained mid-range.

### Covenant Era (1993)

- **Drums:** Tama Rockstar Series — 5-piece; 22"×16" kick (×2), 10"×10" rack, 12"×12" rack, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A and Z Custom Series — 14" Z Custom hi-hats, 17" A crash, 19" A crash, 20" Z Custom ride, 18" A China
- **Pedal:** DW 5000 Turbo double pedal
- **Sticks:** Pro-Mark 747 Oak
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor clear toms, Powerstroke 3 kick, CS Black Dot snare
- **Original setup cost (1993):** ~$3,717
- **Inflation-adjusted to 2026:** ~$8,900
- **Notable:** Covenant became Morbid Angel's most commercially successful album, debuting at #59 on the Billboard 200 — remarkable for a death metal record. The Tama Rockstar was a reliable workhorse for the extensive touring cycle that followed. Sandoval's blast beats on "Rapture" and "Invocation of the Continual One" remain benchmark recordings.

### Domination Era (1995)

- **Drums:** Tama Rockstar (same configuration, updated hardware for road wear replacement)
- **Snare:** Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5" (same model; Sandoval replaced snares frequently)
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian Z Custom (expanded — additional China for faster choke-and-crash patterns)
- **Pedal:** DW 5000 Turbo double pedal
- **Notable:** Domination was recorded during a period of lineup flux; the gear configuration was largely unchanged from Covenant but better dialled in for the album's slightly slower, more doom-influenced riff structures.

### Formulas Fatal to the Flesh Era (1998)

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple — upgrade to professional shell pack; 22"×18" kick (×2), 10"×9" rack, 12"×10" rack, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Tama Bell Brass 14"×6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian Z Custom (retained) + Meinl China (added)
- **Pedal:** DW 5000 Turbo double pedal
- **Sticks:** Pro-Mark 747 Oak
- **Notable:** First step up to professional-tier Tama shells. The Starclassic Maple offered significantly more projection and sustain compared to the Rockstar, important for the album's denser, more atmospheric arrangements.

### Post-Back Surgery / Return Era (2010s)

- **Context:** Sandoval underwent multiple spinal surgeries between 2009 and 2013, eventually leaving Morbid Angel and later Terrorizer due to health complications
- **Drums:** Reduced kit configuration for rehabilitation and limited appearances
- **Notable:** One of extreme metal's most consequential health-related career interruptions. Sandoval's surgeries represented a cautionary story about the long-term physical toll of blast beat drumming at professional volume and velocity over two decades.

## Key Gear Changes

- **1989 → 1991:** Rogers/Pearl Export (improvised double kick) to Pearl Export with Pearl Free-Floating snare
- **1993:** First Tama endorsement (Rockstar) — mid-range upgrade; DW 5000 Turbo pedal adopted
- **1998:** Upgraded to Tama Starclassic Maple — first professional-tier shell pack
- **2009–2013:** Back surgeries forced progressively reduced activity; left Morbid Angel by 2013
- Sandoval's accessible gear choices across 1989–1995 are a key part of the story: death metal's first wave was built on mid-range equipment, not endorsement-level rigs

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Pete Sandoval use on Altars of Madness?**  
A: For Altars of Madness (1989), Pete Sandoval used a Rogers drum kit (some sources cite a Pearl Export) with a true double kick configuration — two 22"×16" kick drums. Cymbals were Zildjian A Series. The full setup cost approximately $1,800 in 1989, equivalent to around $4,740 in 2026.

**Q: What drums did Pete Sandoval use on Covenant?**  
A: On Covenant (1993), Sandoval used a Tama Rockstar Series with two 22"×16" kick drums. Cymbals were a mix of Zildjian A and Z Custom. Pedals were DW 5000 Turbo double pedals. His snare was a Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5". The setup cost approximately $3,717 in 1993, or ~$8,900 adjusted for 2026 inflation.

**Q: What pedals did Pete Sandoval use?**  
A: Sandoval used DW 5000 Turbo double pedals from approximately the Covenant era (1993) onward. Earlier — on Altars of Madness (1989) — he used a budget Camco or early DW double pedal configuration. His blast beat technique was notable for being executed on relatively standard chain-drive pedals rather than the high-end direct-drive mechanisms associated with later extreme metal drummers.

**Q: Why did Pete Sandoval stop drumming?**  
A: Pete Sandoval underwent multiple spinal surgeries beginning around 2009 due to complications that accumulated over decades of blast beat drumming at extreme volume and velocity. He was unable to continue playing at his previous level and departed Morbid Angel by 2013. His case is one of the most discussed examples of long-term physical injury risk in extreme metal drumming.

**Q: What was Pete Sandoval's snare setup?**  
A: Sandoval used budget steel snares (Pearl Export steel) in the early years before upgrading to Pearl Free-Floating Steel 14"×6.5" from around the Blessed Are the Sick era (1991) onward. The Free-Floating system's unrestricted resonance produced the cracking backbeat audible on Covenant recordings. He later adopted Tama Bell Brass snares with the Starclassic upgrade (1998).

**Q: How much would Pete Sandoval's Covenant-era kit cost today?**  
A: The Covenant-era (1993) setup cost approximately $3,717 at the time. Adjusted for 2026 inflation, that equates to approximately $8,900 in equivalent purchasing power — underlining how death metal's genre-defining drumming was achieved with comparatively modest gear investments.
