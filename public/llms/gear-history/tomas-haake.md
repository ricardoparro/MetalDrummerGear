## Gear Timeline

### DEI Era (1995)

- **Drums:** Tama Swingstar — entry-level 5-piece; 22"×16" kick, 10"×10" rack, 12"×12" rack, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Tama Steel 14"×5" (budget steel)
- **Cymbals:** Sabian B8 Series — 14" hi-hats, 16" crash, 18" crash, 20" ride
- **Pedal:** Tama HP30 single converted to double (budget option)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5A Hickory
- **Heads:** Remo Ambassador clear
- **Original setup cost (1995):** ~$1,200
- **Inflation-adjusted to 2026:** ~$2,570
- **Notable:** DEI introduced Western audiences to Meshuggah's off-grid polyrhythms. The budget Tama gear was pushed far beyond its design parameters; breakages were common on early tours.

### Chaosphere Era (1998)

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple — 6-piece; 22"×18" kick (×2), 10"×9" rack, 12"×10" rack, 14"×14" floor, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Tama Bell Brass 14"×6.5" — 10-lug
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Traditional — 14" hi-hats, 17" crash, 19" crash, 21" ride, 18" China
- **Pedal:** Tama Speed Cobra HP910LW double pedal
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5A Hickory (pre-signature model)
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor clear toms, Powerstroke 3 kick, Controlled Sound snare
- **Original setup cost (1998):** ~$6,479
- **Inflation-adjusted to 2026:** ~$13,000
- **Notable:** Chaosphere is regarded as the technical apex of extreme polyrhythmic metal production. The Starclassic's maple shells and the Meinl Byzance wash created a dense, complex sonic palette suited to the album's overlapping rhythmic grids.

### Nothing / I Era (2002–2004)

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple Bubinga — bubinga/maple hybrid shells for added low-end density
- **Snare:** Tama Bell Brass 14"×6.5" (same model; replaced regularly due to touring wear)
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Traditional (expanded — additional 20" crash added for the Nothing tour)
- **Pedal:** Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal (switched from Speed Cobra for heavier spring tension)
- **Notable:** Nothing stripped back sonic complexity somewhat; Haake's setup evolved toward more sustained, open cymbal choices to complement the album's longer riff cycles.

### obZen Era (2008)

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple Bubinga — custom Meshuggah livery; 22"×20" kick (×2), 10"×9" rack, 12"×10" rack, 14"×14" floor, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Tama S.L.P. Bell Brass 14"×6" — custom 10-lug
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Dark — 14" Dark hi-hats, 17" Dark Crash, 19" Dark Crash, 22" Dark Ride, 18" China
- **Pedal:** Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth Tomas Haake Signature (released in conjunction with obZen)
- **Electronics:** Roland SPD integrated for layered programmed samples on selected tracks
- **Original setup cost (2008):** ~$9,800
- **Inflation-adjusted to 2026:** ~$14,900
- **Notable:** obZen is widely considered the high-water mark of Meshuggah's critical reception. The Byzance Dark cymbals gave the kit a darker, less bright tone, complementing the album's heavier, more doom-influenced sections.

### Current Setup (2020s — Immutable / touring era)

- **Drums:** Tama Starclassic Maple — 7-piece custom configuration; 22"×20" kick (×2), 8"×7" rack, 10"×8" rack, 12"×9" rack, 14"×14" floor, 16"×16" floor
- **Snare:** Tama S.L.P. Bell Brass 14"×6.5"
- **Cymbals:** Meinl Byzance Dark — 14" Dark hi-hats, 17" Dark Crash, 19" Dark Crash, 22" Dark Ride, 20" Dark Crash, 18" China
- **Pedal:** Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth Tomas Haake Signature
- **Heads:** Remo Emperor Coated toms, Powerstroke 3 kick, Controlled Sound Black Dot snare

## Key Gear Changes

- **1995 → 1998:** Tama Swingstar to Tama Starclassic Maple — full endorsement as Meshuggah's profile grew following Destroy Erase Improve
- **1998:** Switched cymbals from Sabian B8 to Meinl Byzance — moved from entry-level to professional line
- **2002:** Adopted Tama Starclassic Bubinga shells for denser low-end response
- **2004:** Switched pedals from Speed Cobra to Iron Cobra for heavier spring tension
- **2008:** Vic Firth Tomas Haake Signature sticks released; Byzance Dark cymbals adopted for obZen tour
- **2008:** Roland SPD electronic pads integrated for hybrid setup on selected live tracks

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Tomas Haake use on Chaosphere?**  
A: For Chaosphere (1998), Tomas Haake used a Tama Starclassic Maple kit with a double kick configuration — two 22"×18" kick drums. Cymbals were Meinl Byzance Traditional, pedals were Tama Speed Cobra HP910LW double pedals, and his snare was a Tama Bell Brass 14"×6.5". The full setup cost approximately $6,479 in 1998, equivalent to around $13,000 inflation-adjusted to 2026.

**Q: What cymbals does Tomas Haake use?**  
A: Tomas Haake has been a Meinl Byzance endorser since approximately 1998. He used Byzance Traditional throughout the Chaosphere and Nothing eras before switching to Byzance Dark for the obZen period (2008) onward. His current configuration features 14" Dark hi-hats, 17" and 19" Dark crashes, a 22" Dark ride, and an 18" China.

**Q: What pedals does Tomas Haake use?**  
A: Haake has used Tama double pedals throughout his career. He began with the Speed Cobra HP910LW and switched to the Iron Cobra 900 around 2002–2004 for heavier spring tension during the Nothing touring cycle. He continues to use the Iron Cobra 900 on current tours.

**Q: What snare does Tomas Haake play?**  
A: Tomas Haake's primary snare is a Tama Bell Brass model, typically in a 14"×6.5" configuration with 10 lugs. He switched to the Tama S.L.P. Bell Brass as that line was introduced, retaining the same fundamental bell brass shell material for its bright, cutting crack above densely layered 8-string guitar tones.

**Q: What was Tomas Haake's first professional drum kit?**  
A: Haake's first full professional setup came with the Tama Starclassic Maple endorsement around the Chaosphere era (1998). Prior to that, the DEI-era setup (1995) was based on a Tama Swingstar with Sabian B8 cymbals — an entry-level configuration that was quickly pushed beyond its limits by the demands of early Meshuggah touring.

**Q: How much would Tomas Haake's obZen-era kit cost today?**  
A: The obZen-era (2008) kit cost approximately $9,800 at the time. Adjusted for 2026 inflation, that equates to approximately $14,900 in equivalent purchasing power — before accounting for any collector premium on used Tama Starclassic Bubinga shells of that vintage.
