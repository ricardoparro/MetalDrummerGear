## What Bass Drum Setup Do Post-Metal Drummers Actually Use?

Post-metal's bass drum has to cover more dynamic ground than almost any other subgenre's kick. A single song might open on a barely-audible mallet roll and build, over ten minutes, into a crushing wall of down-tuned distortion — and the bass drum needs to sound intentional at every point along that arc, from a whispered pulse to a bone-rattling low end. That's a different brief than genres built around one consistent intensity: post-metal rewards a shell that can be played softly with real tone, not just loudly with real weight.

Isis and Neurosis, the genre's foundational bands, don't currently have dedicated MetalForge gear profiles, so this guide draws on the closest working analogues from progressive and atmospheric-leaning metal lineages. Danny Carey's Sonor SQ2 Heavy Beech shell covers cinematic, ambient-to-crushing range with room for extended percussion. Mario Duplantier's dual-kick Tama Starclassic Bubinga setup builds atmospheric, environmentally-themed dynamic swells. Abe Cunningham's SJC Custom kit is bespoke-voiced for both crushing heaviness and ambient restraint in Deftones' genre-adjacent material.

This guide breaks down what makes a bass drum work for post-metal — dynamic range, shell tone, and low-end character — and which specific shells the genre's closest analogues actually play, from budget to professional touring rigs.

## What Makes a Great Post-Metal Bass Drum?

### 🌗 Dynamic Range Across Extremes

A post-metal bass drum has to sound intentional whether it's being played with a soft mallet at low volume or driving a crushing full-band climax. Brann Dailor's Tama Starclassic Performer B/B and Danny Carey's Sonor SQ2 Heavy Beech both deliver that range without sounding thin at low volume or muddy at high volume.

**Recommendation:** A warm, resonant shell that stays articulate at both quiet and crushing dynamics

### 🌳 Warm, Resonant Shell Tone

Post-metal's slow-building arrangements reward a musical, resonant tone over a dry, tightly-controlled thud — the shell needs to breathe during quiet passages, not just punch during loud ones.

**Recommendation:** Maple or beech shells tuned for warmth and natural resonance

### 🦵 Bass-Heavy Low End for Crushing Passages

When a post-metal song finally hits its crescendo, the bass drum needs real reserve low-end weight in the tank. Mario Duplantier's dual-kick Tama Starclassic Bubinga setup builds that reserve so the climax actually lands with tectonic force.

**Recommendation:** A dual-kick setup or dense-shell single kick for maximum reserve weight at the climax

### 🪶 Soft-Touch Sensitivity

Post-metal's quiet passages often use mallets, brushes, or restrained stick technique — the shell and head need to respond cleanly at low volume, not just when driven hard.

**Recommendation:** A responsive batter head that tracks light touch as cleanly as full-force strokes

### 🌫️ Atmospheric/Ambient Compatibility

Abe Cunningham's bespoke SJC Custom kit is explicitly voiced for both crushing heaviness and ambient restraint — proof that a single, well-designed shell can genuinely serve both ends of post-metal's dynamic spectrum.

**Recommendation:** A custom or well-matched shell voiced deliberately for both extremes rather than one compromise tone

### 🔗 Suspension Mounting for Sustain Control

Suspended mounting hardware reduces unwanted shell choke, letting the bass drum ring out more naturally during quiet, atmospheric passages while still allowing full control when the arrangement demands it.

**Recommendation:** Suspension-mounted hardware where possible, to preserve natural sustain during quiet passages

## Single Warm Shell vs Dual-Kick Setup for Post-Metal

Post-metal drummers split their bass drum choice across two proven approaches:

**Single Warm Shell (Brann Dailor, Danny Carey, Abe Cunningham):**
- One shell voiced deliberately for both quiet and crushing dynamics
- Simpler to tune and maintain across a dynamically demanding set
- Real low-end reserve depends entirely on shell size and material

**Dual-Kick (Mario Duplantier):**
- Extra low-end reserve for a crushing crescendo payoff
- Two shells locked in tune deliver more tectonic mass at the climax
- More hardware and tuning complexity across quiet-to-loud transitions

**Verdict:** Start with a single, warm-toned shell like Brann Dailor's Tama Starclassic Performer B/B or Danny Carey's Sonor SQ2 Heavy Beech — it's the proven foundation for covering post-metal's full dynamic range from one shell. Consider a dual-kick setup like Mario Duplantier's if your crescendos need maximum reserve low-end weight at the payoff.

## FAQ

**What bass drums do post-metal drummers use?**
Genre founders Isis and Neurosis don't have dedicated MetalForge gear profiles, so this guide draws on the closest working analogues. Brann Dailor of Mastodon plays a Tama Starclassic Performer B/B, Danny Carey of Tool plays a Sonor SQ2 Heavy Beech, Mario Duplantier of Gojira runs a dual-kick Tama Starclassic Bubinga setup, and Abe Cunningham of Deftones plays a bespoke SJC Custom kit.

**What makes a bass drum good for post-metal specifically, versus other metal genres?**
Post-metal songs often build from a whispered, barely-audible passage into a crushing full-band climax over several minutes, so the bass drum needs to sound intentional across an unusually wide dynamic range — most other subgenres only ask for one consistent intensity level.

**Single kick or dual kick for post-metal?**
Either works — Mario Duplantier's dual-kick Tama Starclassic Bubinga setup builds extra low-end reserve for a crushing crescendo payoff, while Brann Dailor's and Danny Carey's single-shell setups prove one well-voiced shell can cover the genre's full dynamic range.

**What shell material is best for post-metal bass drums?**
Warm, resonant shells — birch/bubinga hybrid (Brann Dailor) or heavy beech (Danny Carey) — stay articulate at both quiet and crushing dynamics. Dense bubinga (Mario Duplantier) adds extra reserve low-end weight for crescendo payoffs.

**How should I tune a post-metal bass drum for both quiet and loud passages?**
Aim for moderate, resonant tuning rather than either extreme — too dry and quiet passages sound lifeless, too open and crushing passages turn boomy. Danny Carey's Sonor SQ2 Heavy Beech and Abe Cunningham's SJC Custom are both deliberately voiced to avoid compromising toward either end.
