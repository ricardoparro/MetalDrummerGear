## Why Death Metal Needs a China That Cuts Through the Blast

A crash cymbal rings out. A china explodes and gets out of the way — exactly why death metal drummers reach for one whenever a blast-beat section needs a hard, dirty punctuation mark instead of a clean accent. Down-tuned, distorted guitar walls swallow bright, musical cymbals whole, so death metal's china choices lean dark, trashy, and fast-decaying.

George Kollias of Nile runs an 18" Zildjian China alongside his K Custom Dark crashes. Derek Roddy pairs his Mb20 Heavy crashes with an 18" Meinl Byzance China for a darker, drier trash built to survive his one-footed blast technique on tour. Flo Mounier and Paul Mazurkiewicz both rely on the 18" Sabian AAXtreme China, and Gene Hoglan runs a 20" Sabian china across his work in Death, Testament, and Dethklok.

## FAQ

**What china cymbal size is best for death metal?**
18" is the death metal china standard, used by George Kollias, Derek Roddy, Flo Mounier, and Paul Mazurkiewicz. Gene Hoglan's 20" is the outlier, chosen for extra low-end weight on heavier breakdowns.

**Do death metal drummers use china cymbals live?**
Yes, extensively. George Kollias, Derek Roddy, Flo Mounier, and Gene Hoglan all run a china as a standard part of their live and touring setups.

**China vs crash cymbal — what's the difference for death metal?**
A crash rings out cleanly with a longer decay. A china produces a faster, dirtier, more explosive attack with a much shorter decay, better suited to punctuating breakdowns and blast-beat transitions.

**What's the best budget china cymbal for death metal?**
The Zildjian ZBT China (€70–90) delivers a genuinely trashy B8 bronze sound at a true beginner price. The Sabian XSR Fast China (€100–130) brings B20 bronze to a mid-range price.
