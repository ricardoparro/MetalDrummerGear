## Why Post-Metal Needs Heads That Breathe and Crush

Post-metal lives on contrast. A song can spend four minutes building a hushed, cymbal-washed crescendo before detonating into a wall of down-tuned, distorted weight — and the drum heads underneath have to serve both halves of that arc without compromise. A head that's tuned purely for crushing low end goes dead and unresponsive during the quiet build; a head chasing maximum sensitivity can't carry the tectonic low-tuned tom hits that define the genre's climaxes.

Brann Dailor of Mastodon built his sound on exactly this tension — warm, open Remo heads that respond to brushed, jazz-informed dynamics one moment and slam into crushing sludge weight the next. Danny Carey's massive Tool kit uses the same open, resonant philosophy for the band's more atmospheric, post-metal-adjacent material, while Mario Duplantier of Gojira pairs thicker Remo Emperor and EMAD-family heads for a heavier, more controlled version of the same idea across his dual-kick setup. Abe Cunningham of Deftones rounds out the picture with a bespoke SJC kit voiced specifically to serve both ambient restraint and explosive heaviness from one build.

This guide breaks down the drum heads that deliver post-metal's full dynamic range — why moderate-ply, resonant constructions beat both fully open and heavily muffled extremes here, and which specific models let a kit whisper and crush within the same song.

**Key Points:**

- Post-metal needs heads that stay sensitive at low volume and controlled at maximum weight
- Remo Coated Ambassador and Emperor cover the genre's dynamic range from quiet builds to crushing climaxes
- Thicker, dampened heads matter for the down-tuned toms that anchor post-metal's heaviest passages
- Kick drum head choice matters for dual-kick, dynamically extreme setups like Mario Duplantier's

## What Makes a Great Post-Metal Drum Head?

### 🎚️ Dynamic Sensitivity

Post-metal's quiet builds need a head that responds honestly to brushed hits and light stick work, not just full-force blows. Single-ply and lightly built double-ply heads translate dynamics far more accurately than heavily muffled constructions.

**Recommendation:** Single-ply or light double-ply heads for accurate dynamic response

### 🔇 Thick, Dampened Construction for Climaxes

When a post-metal build finally detonates, down-tuned toms need to carry real weight without flopping or overtoning into mush. Thicker double-ply heads with light built-in dampening give the low-tuned sustain that anchors a crushing climax.

**Recommendation:** Double-ply, moderately dampened heads for down-tuned climax weight

### 🎯 Low Tuning Behavior

Post-metal toms are frequently tuned very low to maximize the contrast against ringing, ambient guitar chords. Heads engineered to stay full and defined at low pitches — rather than going slack — are essential to the genre's crushing half.

**Recommendation:** Heads that hold character at very low tunings

### 🦶 Kick Drum Flexibility

A post-metal set spans quiet, brushed kick patterns and full-weight double-kick climaxes in the same song. Removable-ring kick heads like the Evans EMAD2 let a drummer dial in exactly how open or controlled the low end is for each section.

**Recommendation:** Adjustable-muffle kick heads for songs that span quiet and crushing extremes

### 🎨 Coating and Warmth

Coated heads dominate post-metal for the warmth they bring to both the ambient and crushing halves of the genre — a coated batter softens attack during quiet passages and rounds out the low end during climaxes.

**Recommendation:** Coated batters across the kit for consistent warmth

### 🔊 Resonant Head Openness

An open, single-ply clear resonant head lets low-tuned toms sing and sustain during ambient passages, then still carries real weight when a song finally opens up into its heaviest section.

**Recommendation:** Open, clear single-ply resonant heads for maximum dynamic range

## FAQ

**What drum heads do post-metal drummers use?**
Post-metal drummers overwhelmingly favor warm, dynamically responsive heads. Brann Dailor and Danny Carey use Remo, particularly the Coated Ambassador, while Mario Duplantier steps up to the thicker Remo Emperor and EMAD-family heads for Gojira's heavier, more crushing material. Abe Cunningham's bespoke Deftones kit uses Evans-family heads voiced to serve both restraint and heaviness.

**Why do post-metal drummers need heads that work at both extremes?**
Post-metal songs frequently build from a hushed, ambient passage into a crushing, distorted climax within the same track. A drum head has to stay dynamically sensitive during the quiet build while still carrying real low-end weight when the song finally opens up — which is why single-ply and moderate double-ply Remo constructions are the genre standard over heavily muffled heads.

**What's the best kick drum head for post-metal?**
Evans EMAD2 is the most flexible choice — its removable internal muffle ring lets a drummer dial in exactly how open or controlled the low end is. Mario Duplantier's dual-kick Gojira setup relies on this adjustability to move between quieter passages and full-weight climaxes within a single song.

**Should post-metal toms use single-ply or double-ply heads?**
Both have a role. Single-ply heads like Remo Coated Ambassador give the most honest dynamic response for ambient, brushed passages, while double-ply heads like Remo Emperor add the durability and low-end control needed for a song's heaviest, most crushing sections. Many post-metal drummers use both across different drums on the same kit.

**Do post-metal drum heads need to be tuned very low?**
Frequently, yes — low-tuned toms provide the crushing contrast against ringing, ambient guitar chords that defines much of post-metal's sound. Heads engineered to stay full and defined at low pitches, like Remo Emperor or Aquarian Studio-X, hold their character where a thinner head would go slack or lose definition.
