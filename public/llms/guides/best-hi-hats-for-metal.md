## Why Hi-Hat Selection Defines Your Metal Groove

Your hi-hats are the rhythmic backbone of every metal groove. While crashes and rides handle accents and dramatic moments, the hi-hat drives the pattern — the constant pulse that locks your playing into the band's foundation. Metal places unique demands on hi-hats: they must cut through dense, distorted guitar walls at high tempo, stay articulate during fast 16th-note patterns, and handle explosive open accents without washing out.

Lars Ulrich's signature hi-hat tone on Metallica's "Master of Puppets" and "...And Justice for All" — that bright, cutting shimmer that slices through the wall of sound — comes directly from his Zildjian A Custom Mastersound hi-hats. Joey Jordison of Slipknot built his frantic nu-metal grooves on Zildjian hi-hats that could handle brutal tempos and physical playing. Mike Mangini of Dream Theater uses Meinl hi-hats to navigate complex odd-meter patterns where clarity at any tempo is essential. Matt Greiner of August Burns Red relies on hi-hats that stay tight and controlled during syncopated metalcore patterns.

## What Makes Great Metal Hi-Hats

**Size:** 14" is the standard for most metal — it balances volume, speed, and articulation. 13" for faster, tighter patterns; 15" for heavier projection in doom and power metal.

**Weight:** Medium top over medium-heavy bottom is the most common metal configuration — a cutting open sound with a defined, controlled chick.

**Alloy:** B20 bronze (80% copper, 20% tin) provides the complex frequency range needed to cut through dense guitar sounds. Budget options in B12 or B8 still work, but B20 is the pro standard.

**The Mastersound Advantage:** The Zildjian Mastersound design features unique bell profile and sound holes in the bottom cymbal that produce an ultra-tight, cutting chick — arguably the defining hi-hat sound in metal.

## Key Buying Decision: Size and Weight

The most important specifications for metal hi-hats:

**14" medium-thin top + medium-heavy bottom** is the most common professional metal configuration. This produces:
- Cutting, bright open sound that projects through heavy guitars
- Defined, clean chick on the closed stroke
- Fast response for high-tempo 16th note patterns

**13" hi-hats:** Faster and tighter — ideal for very fast technical playing in death metal and progressive styles. Slightly less volume and presence than 14".

**15" hi-hats:** More volume and projection — used by some classic heavy metal and doom players who want the hi-hat to lead the groove with full presence.

## FAQ

**What hi-hats does Lars Ulrich use?**
Lars Ulrich uses Zildjian A Custom hi-hats, which have defined Metallica's hi-hat sound across their career. The bright, cutting shimmer of the A Custom Mastersound design is audible throughout albums from "Master of Puppets" to the Black Album. His tight, precise, cutting hi-hat sound comes directly from this cymbal choice.

**What size hi-hats are best for metal?**
14" is the standard for most metal — it balances volume, speed, and articulation. Lars Ulrich, Joey Jordison, Mike Mangini, and most pro metal drummers use 14" hi-hats. 13" are faster and tighter for very technical styles; 15" are heavier and louder for doom and power metal.

**What are Mastersound hi-hats and why are they popular in metal?**
The Zildjian Mastersound design features a unique bell profile and specially shaped sound holes in the bottom cymbal. These eliminate air lock during closing and produce an extremely tight, cutting chick sound — the most precise closed-hi-hat sound in metal drumming. This is why Lars Ulrich, Joey Jordison, and Charlie Benante all rely on the Mastersound design.

**Should I use heavy or medium hi-hats for metal?**
Medium-thin top over medium-heavy bottom is the most common professional configuration. Heavier tops control wash better in extremely loud live contexts. Lighter tops produce more open, complex sound. The Zildjian A Custom Mastersound configuration is medium-thin top + medium-heavy bottom — the pro metal standard.

**How do I make my hi-hats cut through distorted guitars?**
Choose brighter, higher-quality hi-hats (Zildjian A Custom, Paiste 2002). Position them slightly higher to allow full open movement. In the mix, hi-hats naturally sit in upper midrange frequencies — a tight, clean chick helps define their presence even when not playing open patterns.
