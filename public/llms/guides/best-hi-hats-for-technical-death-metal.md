## What Hi-Hats Are Used in Technical Death Metal?

Technical death metal hi-hat setups have to solve a problem most metal drummers never face: staying dry and articulate through 240+ BPM blast beats while still clearly separating fast, odd-meter fills from the wall of downtuned guitars underneath. A hi-hat that washes out or blurs together at speed makes complex, rhythmically dense writing unreadable — which is exactly why the genre's leading drummers gravitate toward dry, controlled, cutting hi-hats over wide-open, washy ones.

George Kollias of Nile plays 14" Zildjian K Mastersound Hi-Hats, built for dry articulation at his 240+ BPM ceiling. Hannes Grossmann of Obscura, ex-Necrophagist, and Alkaloid plays 14" Meinl Byzance Traditional Hi-Hats, chosen for tightly articulated technical passages that suit his classically-informed, odd-subdivision writing. Flo Mounier of Cryptopsy relies on 14" Sabian HHX Stage Hi-Hats for rapid, controlled response during gravity-blast passages, while Derek Roddy pairs 14" Meinl Byzance Heavy Hi-Hats for tight, controlled chops at extreme tempo under his signature one-footed blast technique.

This guide breaks down what makes a great technical death metal hi-hat — dry voicing, fast response, and controlled articulation at extreme speed — and which specific models these four influential drummers rely on, from budget starter pairs to the professional setups behind the genre's most demanding recordings.

**Key Points:**

- Dry, controlled voicing dominates technical death metal — hi-hats need to stay articulate through 240+ BPM blast beats
- George Kollias's 14" Zildjian K Mastersound Hi-Hats are built specifically for dry, defined articulation at extreme speed
- Hannes Grossmann's Meinl Byzance Traditional Hi-Hats favor tight articulation for classically-informed, odd-meter phrasing
- Flo Mounier's Sabian HHX Stage Hi-Hats prove rapid open/close response is essential for gravity-blast technique

## What Makes Great Technical Death Metal Hi-Hats?

### 🎯 Dry, Controlled Voicing

Kollias's K Mastersound Hi-Hats and Mounier's HHX Stage Hi-Hats both favor a dry, defined chick that stays articulate rather than washing out at extreme blast-beat speed.

**Recommendation:** Dry or dark-leaning hi-hats for maximum articulation under sustained speed

### ⏱️ Fast Open/Close Response

Sustained 240+ BPM blast beats stress hi-hats harder than almost any other genre. Flo Mounier's HHX Stage pair is built for exactly this — a responsive, medium-weight pair that closes tightly and reopens instantly to keep gravity-blast patterns locked in.

**Recommendation:** Medium-weight hi-hats built for rapid open/close cycling

### 🧮 Articulation Through Odd-Meter Phrasing

Hannes Grossmann's Byzance Traditional Hi-Hats deliver crisp, tight articulation that reflects his dual identity as a technical death metal drummer and classically-trained composer — favoring control over sheer volume for odd-subdivision, ghost-note-heavy writing.

**Recommendation:** A tightly articulated pair for complex, ghost-note-heavy patterns

### 🦿 Control Under Extreme-Tempo Technique

Derek Roddy's Byzance Heavy Hi-Hats stay tight and controlled under his signature one-footed blast technique, proving that heavier-gauge construction can still deliver fast response at extreme, sustained tempo.

**Recommendation:** Heavier-gauge hi-hats for drummers who favor a harder, more forceful attack

### 🛡️ Weight for Touring Durability

Technical death metal's demanding, high-tempo touring schedules mean hi-hats need to survive years of repeated heavy impact. Mid-to-heavy B20 bronze construction holds up better than thin, delicate alternatives.

**Recommendation:** Mid-to-heavy gauge B20 bronze for serious touring durability

## Dry vs Heavy Hi-Hats for Technical Death Metal

Technical death metal's hi-hat choices split into two complementary camps, defined by the demands of extreme-tempo, odd-meter writing:

**Dry & Fast-Cycling (Zildjian K Mastersound — George Kollias's choice; Sabian HHX Stage — Flo Mounier's choice):**
- Dry, defined chick with minimal wash at extreme speed
- Built for rapid open/close cycling during blast beats and gravity-blast technique
- Suits drummers chasing maximum tempo and articulation
- The precision end of the genre's hi-hat spectrum

**Tight & Heavy-Gauge (Meinl Byzance Traditional — Hannes Grossmann's choice; Meinl Byzance Heavy — Derek Roddy's choice):**
- Tightly articulated, controlled response for odd-subdivision, ghost-note-heavy writing
- Heavier gauge stands up to a harder, more forceful attack
- Suits classically-informed composition and one-footed blast technique alike
- The controlled, touring-durable end of the genre's hi-hat spectrum

**Technical Death Metal Verdict:** Choose dry and fast-cycling (Zildjian K Mastersound or Sabian HHX Stage) if you're chasing maximum blast-beat speed in the Nile or Cryptopsy tradition. Choose tight and heavy-gauge (Meinl Byzance Traditional or Byzance Heavy) if your playing leans toward odd-meter composition or extreme-tempo touring durability in the Obscura or Hate Eternal tradition.

## FAQ

**What hi-hats are used in technical death metal?**
George Kollias of Nile plays 14" Zildjian K Mastersound Hi-Hats for dry, defined articulation at extreme speed. Hannes Grossmann of Obscura plays 14" Meinl Byzance Traditional Hi-Hats for tightly articulated technical passages. Flo Mounier of Cryptopsy plays 14" Sabian HHX Stage Hi-Hats for rapid gravity-blast response. Derek Roddy plays 14" Meinl Byzance Heavy Hi-Hats for tight, controlled chops under his one-footed blast technique.

**Why do technical death metal drummers use dry hi-hats?**
Sustained 240+ BPM blast beats and dense, odd-meter arrangements demand a hi-hat that stays articulate rather than washing into open sustain. A dry, controlled chick keeps every stroke legible against a wall of downtuned guitars, which is exactly why George Kollias's K Mastersound and Flo Mounier's HHX Stage both favor a dry, fast-cycling voice over a washier, more open one.

**What size hi-hats for technical death metal?**
14" is the standard size used by all four featured drummers — George Kollias, Hannes Grossmann, Flo Mounier, and Derek Roddy. The consistent 14" diameter across such different setups shows that technical death metal's hi-hat demands are driven more by voicing and response speed than by unusually large or small diameters.

**What hi-hats work best for gravity blast technique?**
Flo Mounier's 14" Sabian HHX Stage Hi-Hats are built specifically for gravity-blast technique, offering rapid open/close cycling and dry, controlled response that stays legible even at Cryptopsy's fastest tempos. A medium-weight, responsive pair is essential — heavier or washier hi-hats can lag behind the pattern at gravity-blast speed.

**Dry or heavy-gauge hi-hats for technical death metal?**
Both work at the highest level. Dry, fast-cycling hi-hats (Zildjian K Mastersound, Kollias's choice; Sabian HHX Stage, Mounier's choice) suit drummers chasing maximum blast-beat speed. Tighter, heavier-gauge hi-hats (Meinl Byzance Traditional, Grossmann's choice; Byzance Heavy, Roddy's choice) suit odd-meter composition and extreme-tempo touring durability. Choose based on which technique and touring demands you're prioritizing.

## Find Your Technical Death Metal Hi-Hat Voice

Technical death metal hi-hats split into two proven traditions: the dry, fast-cycling character that George Kollias and Flo Mounier both build their setups around, and the tightly articulated, heavier-gauge sound that Hannes Grossmann and Derek Roddy favor for odd-meter composition and extreme-tempo touring durability.

If you're chasing maximum blast-beat speed, start with a 14" Zildjian K Mastersound pair or the budget ZBT — both stay dry and legible at extreme tempo. If your playing leans toward odd-meter composition or gravity-blast technique, Meinl Byzance Traditional or Sabian HHX Stage will give you the controlled articulation your writing demands.

Whatever you choose, prioritize dry voicing and fast open/close response over flashy openness — the hi-hat carries the pattern in technical death metal drumming, and it needs to stay legible through every blast beat and odd-meter shift.
