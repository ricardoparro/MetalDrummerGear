# How to Sound Like Abe Cunningham — Ambient-Heavy Deftones Groove Technique Guide

**Drummer:** Abe Cunningham  
**Band:** Deftones  
**Genre:** Alternative Metal / Nu Metal  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-abe-cunningham

## Overview

Abe Cunningham has been Deftones' drummer since co-founding the band in Sacramento in 1988, making him one of alternative metal's longest-tenured rhythmic voices. Across nine studio albums — including the Grammy-winning "White Pony" (2000) and platinum-certified "Diamond Eyes" (2010) — Cunningham built a style defined less by speed or complexity than by taste: knowing exactly when to hit hard and when to disappear into the mix entirely.

What sets Cunningham apart from most metal drummers is his willingness to serve the song over showing off. On tracks like "Digital Bath," his playing builds from a near-whisper into an explosive chorus, sculpting the emotional arc rather than just keeping time.

## Tuning & Setup

Cunningham tunes for a balance of body and response, giving him room to whisper on quiet passages and detonate on heavy hits without changing drums:

- **Kick:** Medium tension, moderate/controlled sustain. A standard 22" kick tuned to lock with the bass guitar's low end rather than fight it for space.
- **Snare:** Medium tension, light muffling. The Big Black Steel's extra depth lets it sing on soft passages while still cracking hard on rimshots.
- **Toms:** Medium tension, minimal muffling. Kept resonant for purposeful, infrequent fills rather than constant motion.

## Technique Tips

Cunningham's playing prioritizes taste and dynamic range over technical flash. Rather than filling every bar, he leaves space — using unconventional snare accents, whisper-to-explosion dynamic builds, and atmospheric cymbal textures to serve Deftones' shift between crushing heaviness and shoegaze-influenced ambience. This restraint-first approach is a distinct technique niche from pure metal blast-beat drumming.

**Signature patterns:**

- **Whisper-to-Explosion Dynamic Build (80–110 BPM, Intermediate):** A groove that starts at near-silent volume and gradually intensifies over a verse until it detonates into a full-force chorus.
- **Unconventional Snare Placement (90–130 BPM, Intermediate-Advanced):** Snare hits placed off the expected beat to mirror vocal phrasing, creating tension and release a straight backbeat can't provide.
- **Ambient Cymbal Texture (Variable, Intermediate):** Dark, washy cymbals favoring texture and sustain over cutting articulation so the drums blend into the soundscape rather than driving it.

**Key songs to study:** *Digital Bath* (White Pony, 2000) · *Change (In the House of Flies)* (White Pony, 2000) · *My Own Summer (Shove It)* (Around the Fur, 1997) · *Elite* (White Pony, 2000, Grammy winner)

## Practice Routine

1. **Whisper-to-Explosion Drill (15 min daily):** Play a simple groove starting at ghost-note volume, gradually increasing intensity over 16 bars without rushing the tempo.
2. **Vocal-Phrasing Snare Drill (10 min daily):** Sing a vocal melody over a basic groove, then place snare accents only where the phrasing naturally pauses or lifts.
3. **Ambient Ride Wash Drill (10 min daily):** Play a slow ride pattern using only the bow, letting each note ring fully before the next, focusing on blend rather than cut.

**Common mistakes:** Filling every bar instead of leaving space for the song to breathe; starting dynamic builds too loud, leaving no room to grow into the chorus; playing a straight backbeat instead of mirroring vocal phrasing; over-tightening the snare and losing the Big Black Steel's warm tone.
