# How to Sound Like Abe Cunningham — Deftones Alternative Metal Drumming Guide

**Drummer:** Abe Cunningham  
**Band:** Deftones  
**Genre:** Alternative Metal / Nu-Metal  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-abe-cunningham

## Overview

Abe Cunningham has been the drummer behind Deftones since co-founding the band as a teenager in Sacramento, California in 1988. Across nine studio albums — from the raw aggression of "Adrenaline" (1995) through the shoegaze-tinged extremity of "Ohms" (2020) — he has become one of alternative metal's most quietly influential players, prized not for speed or technical flash but for restraint, dynamics, and an almost telepathic sense of when not to play.

What sets Cunningham apart from most metal drummers is that his signature moments are often his quietest ones. "Digital Bath" builds from a near-silent hi-hat pulse into a single cathartic cymbal-swell release; "Passenger" barely touches the kit for entire verses. Then, on songs like "My Own Summer (Shove It)," he flips into relentless, high-velocity power-groove drumming without ever sounding like a different player — the same musical intelligence just pointed at a different dynamic extreme.

## Tuning & Setup

Abe tunes for sensitivity across the widest possible dynamic range — every drum needs to register a barely-there ghost note as clearly as a full-force hit:

- **Kick:** Medium tension with moderate Evans EMAD muffling. Punchy and focused so it sits with the bass guitar rather than dominating it.
- **Snare:** Medium tension, minimal muffling. The deep steel S.L.P. shell needs less tension than a shallow snare to find its sweet spot — tight enough for a cutting crack, loose enough for ghost notes to speak.
- **Toms:** Medium-low tension with little to no muffling. Open and resonant to support melodic, atmospheric fills without choking their natural sustain.

## Technique Tips

Abe plays matched grip with a relaxed touch built around dynamic range rather than raw speed. His vocabulary centers on ghost notes, cymbal swells, and unconventional snare placement — tools borrowed more from groove and atmosphere than from technical metal drumming.

**Signature patterns:**

- **The Cymbal-Swell Build (70–90 BPM, Intermediate):** Rolling on a ride or crash cymbal to build orchestral tension before releasing into a full groove, as heard on "Digital Bath." Practice a slow roll gradually increasing in speed and pressure over 8–16 bars, then resolve into a full backbeat on beat one.
- **Ghost-Note Atmosphere Groove (60–80 BPM, Intermediate-Advanced):** Keeping a groove alive with barely-there ghost notes and a minimal hi-hat pattern, as on "Passenger" — the drums function as a heartbeat rather than a rhythm section.
- **High-Velocity Power Groove (120–140 BPM, Advanced):** A relentless, open hi-hat groove with punishing backbeats sustained for a full song, as on "My Own Summer (Shove It)," without ever losing the pocket.

**Key songs to study:** *Digital Bath* (White Pony, 2000) · *My Own Summer (Shove It)* (Around the Fur, 1997) · *Passenger* (White Pony, 2000) · *Diamond Eyes* (Diamond Eyes, 2010) · *Change (In the House of Flies)* (White Pony, 2000)

## Practice Routine

1. **Whisper-to-Roar Dynamic Drill (15 min daily):** Play a simple groove as quietly as possible for 8 bars, then build volume gradually over the next 8 bars to full intensity, focusing on a smooth crescendo.
2. **Cymbal Swell Practice (10 min daily):** Start a slow roll on a crash or ride at very low volume, increase speed and pressure over 8–16 bars, and release directly into a full groove on beat one.
3. **Power Groove Endurance (10 min, 3x per week):** Play a driving open hi-hat groove with backbeats for a full 4-minute song length without letting the tempo drift or energy sag.

**Common mistakes:** Filling every space instead of trusting silence as a rhythmic tool; rushing dynamic builds instead of letting tension develop gradually; losing tempo consistency during long high-velocity grooves; playing ghost notes too loud when they should be felt, not heard.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit does Abe Cunningham play?**  
A: Abe Cunningham plays a Tama Starclassic Maple/Bubinga kit with a compact configuration: a single 22" x 18" bass drum, 10" and 12" rack toms, and 14" and 16" floor toms. He's been a long-time Tama endorser, valuing the kit's punchy attack and durability for touring.

**Q: What snare does Abe Cunningham use?**  
A: Abe uses a Tama S.L.P. Big Black Steel snare at 14" x 8" — a deep steel-shell drum chosen for its powerful crack and cut through Deftones' heavier sections, while still responding sensitively to his quiet ghost notes.

**Q: What is the cymbal-swell technique Abe Cunningham is known for?**  
A: It's a build technique where Abe rolls on a ride or crash cymbal, gradually increasing speed and pressure to add orchestral tension before releasing into a full groove — most famously heard in the build sections of "Digital Bath" from White Pony.

**Q: What cymbals does Abe Cunningham use?**  
A: Abe plays a hybrid Zildjian setup: 14" A New Beat hi-hats, 16" and 18" A Custom crashes, an 18" A Custom China, and a 21" K Custom ride. The bright A Custom cymbals handle attack while the darker K Custom ride provides atmospheric wash.

**Q: How does Abe Cunningham build dynamics in Deftones songs?**  
A: By treating restraint as a compositional tool — playing near-silent verses that gradually build tension, then releasing into full power at the precise moment a song demands it, as heard on tracks like "Digital Bath" and "Passenger." He rarely plays at one consistent volume throughout a song.
