# How to Sound Like Igor Cavalera — Sepultura Drum Sound Guide

**Drummer:** Igor Cavalera  
**Band:** Sepultura  
**Genre:** Thrash Metal / Groove Metal  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-igor-cavalera

## Overview

Igor Cavalera (born September 4, 1970, Belo Horizonte, Brazil) co-founded Sepultura with his brother Max in 1984 at just 13 years old, and spent two decades building one of metal's most unique and influential rhythmic vocabularies. His work on Beneath the Remains (1989), Arise (1991), Chaos A.D. (1993), and Roots (1996) defined South American metal and introduced the world to a fusion of thrash aggression and Brazilian tribal percussion that no other drummer had attempted at that scale. Igor's defining quality is the intersection of groove and power — his mid-tempo patterns on Roots Bloody Roots and Refuse/Resist hit with physical force rooted in deep groove feel rather than raw speed. The contrast between his locked, heavy groove sections and his extreme thrash and blast beat passages gives Sepultura's music its unique dynamism. Post-Sepultura, Igor reunited with Max in Cavalera Conspiracy and explored electronic music with his DJ project Mixhell.

## Key Techniques

**The Roots Bloody Roots Mid-Tempo Stomp** — Igor's signature groove is a mid-tempo, anchored pattern built on the bass drum and floor tom relationship. The kick provides a heavy locked downbeat; snare and floor tom interplay creates tribal momentum. The pattern breathes with intentional space for an organic, human feel. Practice at 80 BPM focusing on the physical weight of each hit before increasing tempo.

**Tribal Beat Patterns** — Igor translates Brazilian percussion idioms (maracatu, baião, forró rhythms) onto the rock kit — rolling, forward-moving subdivisions that imply but don't always state the 4/4 downbeat, creating displaced, hypnotic grooves. Listen to Brazilian percussion recordings, identify repeating patterns, and transfer them to hi-hat and snare over a steady kick.

**Blast Beat Integration with Groove** — Igor's blast beats appear in extreme contrast to groove sections, placed for maximum impact rather than as a default. He treats blast beats as a textural device — the explosive contrast with groove sections makes both feel more extreme. Practice transitions between groove and blast separately, focusing on the instantaneous moment of change.

**Double Bass Hybrid Groove** — On Beneath the Remains and Arise, Igor uses double bass in a hybrid approach combining thrash runs with groove-oriented kick patterns. His double bass is more feel-driven than metronomic — it locks to the bass guitar line rather than running independently. Integrate double bass runs into mid-tempo groove patterns to make the feet feel like groove extensions.

## Gear

Igor Cavalera used a **Tama Artstar II** kit during the Roots era (1996) — Tama's flagship birch shell series of the 1990s:

- **Kick Drums:** 22" x 18" (x2) with Tama Iron Cobra Double Pedal
- **Snare:** 14" x 6.5" Tama Steel Snare — high tuning for crack and projection
- **Rack Toms:** 10" x 9", 12" x 10"
- **Floor Toms:** 16" x 16", 18" x 16" — large floor toms support his groove foundation
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AAX Series — 14" AAX Stage Hi-Hats, 18" and 19" AAX Stage Crashes, 21" AAX Stage Ride, 18" AAX Chinese
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B — mass for physical authority in groove patterns and loud crash accents
- **Heads:** Evans G2 Coated (toms and snare), Evans EQ3 Clear (kick), Evans EC Resonant

## Tuning & Setup

Igor tuned for maximum physical impact — heavy, earthy, and direct. The priority was a thick, low-end sound with good projection alongside Sepultura's downtuned guitars:

- **Kick:** Medium tension. Half-pillow touching batter head, light muffling on resonant. Keep the resonant head relatively open — you want the kick to have body, not just click. The Roots kick sound is fat and present, not thin and mechanical.
- **Snare:** High tension — 2-3 turns past finger-tight. One small Moongel maximum. Bright enough to cut through low-tuned guitar arrangements without becoming shrill.
- **Toms:** Medium tension. Light muffling or none — allow sustained warmth that contributes to the tribal, physical feel. Resonant heads slightly lower than batter. The open tone is part of the groove character.

## Practice Tips

1. **The Roots Groove Isolation (15 min daily):** At 100 BPM: kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4. Add floor tom hits between kick and snare for tribal displacement. Focus on physical weight — every stroke anchored and deliberate. Practice in 5-minute continuous sets.
2. **Brazilian Rhythm Vocabulary Study (10 min daily):** Listen to baião and maracatu recordings. Clap the patterns. Transfer to hi-hat and snare over a steady kick. Build source vocabulary — not copies of Igor's beats, but the rhythmic language he drew from.
3. **Groove-to-Blast Transition Drill (10 min daily):** 4-bar Roots groove at 110 BPM. Shift instantly to single-foot blast on downbeat of bar 5 for 2 bars. Return to groove on bar 7. Record yourself — transitions must be instantaneous and clean.
4. **Sepultura Song Deconstruction (20 min daily):** Slow Roots Bloody Roots to 70% speed. Identify verse groove, pre-chorus variations, fill patterns. Learn each separately, then connect in sequence.

**Common mistakes:** Playing groove patterns too mechanically (tribal influence means organic feel, not machine precision); over-muffling toms (sustained warm tone is part of the character); using double bass where single bass would create more impact; rushing blast beat sections (speed without lock is noise).

## Key Songs to Study

- *Roots Bloody Roots* — Roots (1996): the definitive Igor Cavalera groove — mid-tempo stomp with tribal feel and maximum physical impact
- *Refuse/Resist* — Chaos A.D. (1993): groove metal at its most powerful — locked, heavy, and relentless
- *Ratamahatta* — Roots (1996): direct incorporation of Brazilian tribal percussion alongside Igor's kit work
- *Attitude* — Roots (1996): mid-tempo groove sustaining through a full song — endurance and feel over fills
- *Beneath the Remains* — Beneath the Remains (1989): the thrash foundation his later groove work built upon

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit did Igor Cavalera use on Roots?**  
A: Igor Cavalera used Tama Artstar II kits during the Roots era (1996) — Tama's flagship birch shell series of the 1990s. The focused, heavy birch attack defines the Roots drum sound. He used a double bass configuration with 22" kick drums and large floor toms.

**Q: How does Igor Cavalera create his tribal groove feel?**  
A: Igor's tribal groove comes from his Brazilian heritage in music including baião and maracatu rhythmic patterns, and a physical, anchored playing approach that prioritizes the bass drum and floor tom relationship. His grooves breathe with intentional space that gives them an organic, human feel.

**Q: What cymbals did Igor Cavalera use with Sepultura?**  
A: Igor used Sabian cymbals during the Sepultura era, primarily the AAX Stage series. The AAX's fast attack and bright wash gave his accent work the cutting projection needed above Sepultura's heavily downtuned guitars.

**Q: What makes Igor Cavalera different from other metal drummers?**  
A: Igor's Brazilian heritage and deep connection to South American percussion traditions gave him a rhythmic vocabulary no other metal drummer of his era possessed. His tribal groove patterns — drawing from maracatu, baião, and Brazilian folk rhythms — created a sound completely distinct from American or European thrash conventions. He also had an exceptional sense for when restraint created more power than speed.

**Q: What songs are best for learning Igor Cavalera's style?**  
A: Start with Roots Bloody Roots (Roots, 1996) for his signature mid-tempo tribal stomp. Refuse/Resist (Chaos A.D., 1993) is essential for groove metal feel. Ratamahatta (Roots, 1996) shows direct Brazilian percussion integration. For the thrash foundation, Beneath the Remains (1989) is the starting point.
