# How to Sound Like Igor Cavalera — Sepultura Drum Sound Guide

**Drummer:** Igor Cavalera  
**Band:** Sepultura  
**Genre:** Groove Metal / Thrash Metal  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-igor-cavalera

## Overview

Igor Cavalera (born 1970) co-founded Sepultura with his brother Max in São Paulo in 1984 and spent twelve years crafting one of metal's most distinctive rhythmic voices. His drumming arc — from pure Brazilian thrash on "Beneath the Remains" (1989) to tribal-infused groove metal on "Roots" (1996) — represents one of the most compelling evolutions in extreme music drumming. What makes Igor remarkable isn't just that he incorporated tribal and world music elements into metal: it's that he did so without losing the power and intensity that made Sepultura dangerous. The mid-tempo stomp of "Roots Bloody Roots," the percussive chaos of "Ratamahatta," the blast-beat-to-tribal transitions in "Attitude" — these created a new vocabulary for how metal drumming could feel both savage and deeply grooved.

## Kit Setup

Igor used **Tama Artstar II** drums during Sepultura's classic era (Chaos A.D. through Roots):

- **Kick Drums:** 22" x 18" (x2) — double bass throughout
- **Snare:** 14" x 6.5" Tama SLP Steel (high-tuned for crack and projection)
- **Rack Toms:** 10" x 8", 12" x 9"
- **Floor Toms:** 14" x 14", 16" x 16"
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AAX — 14" AAX Stage Hi-Hats, 17" & 19" AAX Crashes, 21" AAX Ride, 18" AAX Chinese
- **Pedals:** Tama Iron Cobra Double Pedal (Power Glide cam — consistent feel from tribal grooves to blast beats)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5A (standard taper, round tip)
- **Heads:** Evans EMAD Clear (kick), Evans G1 Coated (snare), Evans G2 Batter (toms)

## Tuning & Setup

Igor tunes for power and resonance — the Roots era sound is warmer and more musical than his early thrash days:

- **Kick:** Medium tension with light muffling (pillow barely touching batter). Musical note, not just a click — the groove requires kick resonance.
- **Snare:** Medium-high tension, minimal muffling. Loud crack with body — the snare acts as a 'response' drum in tribal patterns and needs a voice.
- **Toms:** Medium tension, tuned lower than typical metal. Tune to musical intervals (e.g., G, D, A, E from high to low). In tribal patterns, toms carry melodic weight and need to ring — Moongel for control without killing resonance.

## Technique Tips

Igor plays **matched grip** with a powerful, full-stroke physical approach — big strokes, deep sounds, rhythmic commitment. For tribal patterns he uses a rebounding technique that lets sticks bounce in circular motions, mimicking hand percussion. He positions low to the kit with a wide stance for maximum power on bass drum.

**Signature patterns:**

- **The Roots Bloody Roots Mid-Tempo Stomp (90–100 BPM, Intermediate):** Powerful 4/4 at ~95 BPM with kick on 1&3, snare on 2&4, and tribal tom accents on 8th-note subdivisions. The groove's power comes from intensity and commitment, not speed. Treat every tom as a melodic percussion instrument.
- **Tribal Beat Double-Bass Hybrid (120–150 BPM, Advanced):** 3+3+2 clave patterns on toms while double bass drives below. The kick and hi-hat maintain metal framework while hands play batucada-derived patterns. Learn the clave at 60 BPM before adding kick.
- **Blast Beat to Tribal Transition (Variable — blasts at 180+ BPM, Advanced):** Seamless movement between full blast beats and mid-tempo tribal grooves. Keep quarter note pulse constant through the transition — this is what makes it musical. "Attitude" and "Ratamahatta" are the key study tracks.

**Key songs to study:** *Roots Bloody Roots* (Roots, 1996) · *Refuse/Resist* (Chaos A.D., 1993) · *Ratamahatta* (Roots, 1996) · *Attitude* (Roots, 1996) · *Beneath the Remains* (Beneath the Remains, 1989)

## Practice Routine

1. **Brazilian Clave Pattern on Kit (20 min daily, first month):** Play the 3+3+2 clave on hi-tom (3 hits, 3 hits, 2 hits = 8 total): R L R, R L R, R L. Start at 60 BPM. When comfortable, add kick on beats 1 and 3. Add open hi-hat on the "8". Goal: comfortable polyrhythmic independence between 3+3+2 tom pattern and steady kick.
2. **Tribal to Blast Transition (15 min daily):** Metronome at 100 BPM. Play 8 bars of Roots Bloody Roots groove. On bar 9, accelerate to blast tempo while keeping quarter note pulse = 100. Hold 4 bars. Drop back to groove. Goal: smooth musical transitions between tribal groove and blast contexts.
3. **Full Song Performance Study (30 min daily):** Learn "Roots Bloody Roots" from start to finish — every fill, every transition, every dynamic change. After mastering it, move to "Refuse/Resist." Goal: complete performance of 3 Sepultura songs from memory with full dynamics.

**Common mistakes:** Playing tribal patterns mechanically (they should breathe and bounce); over-muffling (Roots-era sound has resonance, don't kill it); ignoring early thrash material (understanding "Beneath the Remains" makes the Roots evolution meaningful); treating blast beats and tribal grooves as separate — they exist in the same musical context.

## FAQ

**Q: What drums did Igor Cavalera use on Roots?**  
A: Tama Artstar II drums with maple shells on Roots (1996). Double bass setup with 22" kicks, Sabian AAX cymbals, Tama Iron Cobra pedals. The Artstar II's warmer character suited the tribal groove approach — more resonant than the attack-focused thrash recordings.

**Q: How did Igor Cavalera incorporate tribal music into metal drumming?**  
A: Through polyrhythmic tom patterns derived from Brazilian batucada rhythms — particularly the 3+3+2 clave structure — combined with metal's double-bass drive. On Roots (1996), Sepultura collaborated with the Xavantes indigenous people. Igor absorbed those rhythmic concepts and integrated them into drum kit vocabulary without losing metal's intensity.

**Q: What is the drum pattern on 'Roots Bloody Roots'?**  
A: Mid-tempo stomp at ~95 BPM. Main groove: kick on 1&3, snare on 2&4, with tribal tom accents on 8th-note subdivisions creating a forward-marching feel. Igor adds open hi-hat accents and varies which toms he accents — treating every tom as melodic percussion.

**Q: What's the best Sepultura album to study Igor Cavalera?**  
A: Roots (1996) — every track offers study material. Chaos A.D. (1993) shows the transition from thrash to groove metal. Beneath the Remains (1989) for his thrash foundation. All three together trace the complete evolution.

**Q: Did Igor Cavalera use triggers on Sepultura recordings?**  
A: No triggers on classic Sepultura recordings through Roots. The tribal, organic sound is partly defined by acoustic drum character — particularly the resonance of toms and natural kick note. Some live enhancement occurred, but studio recordings are acoustic.
