## Overview

John Otto has been Limp Bizkit's drummer since the band's 1994 formation — a founding member whose playing is inseparable from the band's identity as one of nu-metal's defining commercial forces. Unlike most of his nu-metal peers, Otto studied jazz at the Douglas Anderson School of the Arts in Jacksonville, Florida, absorbing jazz vocabulary, Brazilian rhythms, and Afro-Cuban percussion before applying that training to the heaviest commercial rock of his generation.

That jazz background is the reason Limp Bizkit's rhythm section swings rather than just pounds. On *Significant Other* (1999, 7M+ US sales) and *Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water* (2000, #1 Billboard debut), Otto's kick and snare placement sits in a hip-hop-informed pocket — often just behind the strict grid — while ghost notes and dynamic control give quieter verses genuine musical depth before the choruses detonate at full nu-metal weight.

- **Floor Toms:** 14", 16"

- **Pedals/Hardware:** Gibraltar Professional Series
- **Sticks:** Zildjian 5A Wood Tip
- **Heads:** Remo Powerstroke 3 (kick), Remo Coated Ambassador (snare), Remo Emperor Coated (toms)

## Tuning & Setup

Otto tunes for punch and articulation that still leaves room for ghost notes to register:

- **Kick:** Medium-firm tension with ported resonant head and light dampening. Focused attack keeps kick placement clear across single-kick pocket and double-kick accent bursts.
- **Snare:** Medium-high tension, minimal muffling. Bright tuning lets both full-force backbeats and quiet ghost notes register clearly.
- **Toms:** Medium tension, light muffling. Emperor Coated heads add durability while keeping toms sensitive for melodic, jazz-informed fill phrasing.

## Technique Tips

Otto's playing works because he treats the backbeat as a groove decision, not just a metal accent. His kick and snare often land slightly behind the strict grid — a hip-hop production trait — while ghost notes borrowed from jazz training add texture beneath the main pattern.

**Signature patterns:**

- **Fat Backbeat Pocket (85–100 BPM, Intermediate):** A heavy, slightly-behind-the-grid backbeat that locks with the bass and rhythm guitar. Practice delaying the snare hit by a hair against a click track.
- **Ghost-Note Groove Texture (80–95 BPM, Intermediate):** Low-volume snare hits between backbeats add rhythmic texture and swing to verses. Add quiet ghost notes on the 'e' and 'a' of each beat.
- **Double-Kick Accent Bursts (100–130 BPM, Intermediate):** Short double-kick bursts on heavier sections rather than sustained runs — precision over duration.

**Key songs to study:** *Nookie* (Significant Other, 1999) · *Break Stuff* (Significant Other, 1999) · *N 2 Gether Now* (Significant Other, 1999) · *My Way* (Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water, 2000)

## Practice Routine

1. **Pocket-Delay Drill (15 min daily):** Play a basic backbeat against a click, placing the snare a hair behind the click without dragging tempo.
2. **Ghost-Note Layering Drill (15 min daily):** Add quiet ghost notes between backbeats, keeping them clearly softer than the main hit.
3. **Double-Kick Burst Precision (10 min daily):** Practice 2–4 note double-kick bursts landing precisely on a target accent.

**Common mistakes:** Playing every backbeat strictly on the grid instead of exploring pocket placement; skipping ghost notes entirely; overusing sustained double-kick runs instead of controlled bursts; over-muffling the kick drum.

## FAQ

**Q: What drum kit does John Otto use?**  
A: John Otto plays a custom Orange County Drum and Percussion (OCDP) kit — California-built drums with maple shells across a compact four-tom, single-kick (double-kick configurable) setup. Earlier in his career he used Pearl drums during the Three Dollar Bill, Y'all$ era before transitioning to OCDP.

**Q: What makes John Otto's drumming style unique in nu-metal?**  
A: Otto studied jazz at the Douglas Anderson School of the Arts, giving him ghost-note vocabulary and hip-hop-informed pocket placement most nu-metal drummers lack. His kick and snare frequently sit a fraction behind the grid, and his fills phrase more like a jazz drummer's — a synthesis Modern Drummer described as "grounded in metal, jazz, and hip-hop."

**Q: What cymbals and sticks does John Otto play?**  
A: Otto plays Zildjian cymbals — A Series for hi-hats, crashes, and ride, with lighter A Custom crashes for quick-responding accents — alongside Zildjian 5A Wood Tip sticks. Gibraltar hardware and Remo drumheads round out the setup.

**Q: How do I play a hip-hop-informed backbeat like John Otto?**  
A: Start with the Pocket-Delay Drill — play a basic backbeat against a click and place the snare a fraction behind the beat without dragging tempo. That micro-delay gives Otto's grooves their forward-pulling feel instead of a rigid metronomic backbeat.

**Q: What should I practice first to sound like John Otto?**  
A: Build the Ghost-Note Layering Drill first — quiet snare hits between backbeats at a volume clearly softer than the groove. That jazz-derived texture is the foundation of his dynamic approach.
