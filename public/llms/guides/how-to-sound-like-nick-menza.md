# How to Sound Like Nick Menza — Megadeth Thrash Drum Sound Guide

**Drummer:** Nick Menza  
**Band:** Megadeth  
**Genre:** Thrash Metal

## Overview

Nick Menza (1964–2016) was the heartbeat of Megadeth's most celebrated era. From 1989 to 1998 he played on six studio albums, including the genre-defining "Rust in Peace" (1990) and the commercially groundbreaking "Countdown to Extinction" (1992). His drumming combined thrash metal's relentless aggression with musical precision — double-bass patterns locked tightly to Dave Mustaine and Marty Friedman's guitar work, a signature snare crack that cut through even the densest arrangements, and polyrhythmic fills that punctuated Megadeth's complex song structures. Rust in Peace is widely regarded as one of the finest thrash drum performances ever recorded.

## Kit Setup

Nick used **Sonor Performer Series** drums — beech shells known for focused midrange attack and excellent projection:

- **Kick Drums:** 22" x 18" (x2) — double bass throughout his Megadeth tenure
- **Snare:** 14" x 6.5" Sonor Steel Snare (high-tuned for maximum crack)
- **Rack Toms:** 10" x 8", 12" x 9"
- **Floor Toms:** 14" x 14", 16" x 16"
- **Cymbals:** Zildjian A Series — 14" New Beat Hi-Hats, 17" & 19" A Medium Crashes, 20" A Medium Ride, 18" A China
- **Pedals:** DW 5000 Double Pedal (chain-drive for controlled power at high tempos)
- **Sticks:** Vic Firth 5B (oval tip)
- **Heads:** Evans EMAD Clear (kick), Remo Coated Ambassador (snare), Evans G2 Coated (toms)

## Tuning & Setup

Nick tuned for attack and projection — every drum needed to cut through Megadeth's layered guitar arrangements:

- **Kick:** Medium-tight with moderate muffling (pillow or foam strip). Every double bass stroke must register distinctly at 180–200 BPM — excessive sustain creates mud at thrash tempos.
- **Snare:** Very high tension — significantly above standard pitch. The rimshot technique does the rest. Minimal muffling (one small tape piece maximum). High tuning produces the crack, not the pop.
- **Toms:** Medium tension with one Moongel each. Quick decay helps fills stay defined in fast polyrhythmic passages. Tune in descending intervals for melodic fill work.

## Technique Tips

Nick played **matched grip** with strong wrist articulation and a heel-up double-bass approach. His snare stroke sat slightly behind the main beat, creating a subtle push that gave Megadeth its forward momentum.

**Signature patterns:**

- **Megadeth Thrash Double-Bass Drive (180–200 BPM, Advanced):** Nick's kick patterns mirror the guitar riffs exactly — learn the riff, then play it on kick. Start at 120 BPM and build. The riff-lock is the technique.
- **The Megadeth Snare Crack (Variable, Intermediate):** Tight tuning plus consistent rimshot contact at the same spot every time. Practice consistency before speed — the crack comes from precision, not force.
- **Polyrhythmic Fill Cascades (Variable, Advanced):** Triplets over 4/4, 5-note groupings in thrash contexts, cross-stick patterns that shift perceived meter. Practice triplet groupings (3 notes per beat) against a 4/4 metronome, then extend to 5-note groupings.

**Key songs to study:** *Holy Wars...The Punishment Due* (Rust in Peace, 1990) · *Tornado of Souls* (Rust in Peace, 1990) · *Symphony of Destruction* (Countdown to Extinction, 1992) · *Hangar 18* (Rust in Peace, 1990) · *Sweating Bullets* (Countdown to Extinction, 1992)

## Practice Routine

1. **Double-Bass Riff Lock (20 min daily):** Learn the guitar riff to "Symphony of Destruction." Play the exact riff rhythm on kick drum while maintaining snare backbeat. Start at 60% speed. Increase weekly. Goal: full-tempo riff-kick synchronization.
2. **Rimshot Consistency Training (10 min daily):** Mark your snare head at the rimshot contact point. Practice hitting exactly that spot every stroke, from slow quarter notes to fast fills. Goal: automatic consistent rimshot at any tempo.
3. **Polyrhythmic Fill Development (15 min daily):** Metronome at 100 BPM. Play triplet groupings around the kit for 8 bars. Then 5-note groupings for 8 bars. Combine. Goal: fluid movement between triplet, 4-note, and 5-note groupings over steady pulse.

**Common mistakes:** Ignoring riff-lock (kicks follow the guitar, not just the backbeat); inconsistent rimshot placement; playing too loud without dynamics; rushing double bass (precision beats speed in thrash).

## FAQ

**Q: What drums did Nick Menza use on Rust in Peace?**  
A: Nick used Sonor Performer series drums with beech shells on Rust in Peace (1990). Double bass setup with 22" kicks, Zildjian A cymbals, and DW 5000 double pedal. The Sonor Performer's focused midrange attack is essential to that album's sound.

**Q: How fast does Nick Menza play double bass?**  
A: Nick sustains double bass at 180–200 BPM through the main thrash sections of Rust in Peace. "Holy Wars...The Punishment Due" runs around 180 BPM with faster bridge passages pushing toward 200 BPM — all locked precisely to the guitar riffs.

**Q: What makes Nick Menza's snare sound different?**  
A: High tuning (above standard pitch), consistent rimshot technique, and a steel-shell snare. These combine to produce a sharp, penetrating crack rather than a loose thwack. His snare cuts through even Megadeth's densest arrangements.

**Q: What's the best Megadeth album to study Nick Menza?**  
A: Rust in Peace (1990) — every track is essential. "Holy Wars" for double-bass endurance, "Tornado of Souls" for musical groove within thrash, "Hangar 18" for technical fills and time changes. Countdown to Extinction (1992) shows his groove side at more accessible tempos.
