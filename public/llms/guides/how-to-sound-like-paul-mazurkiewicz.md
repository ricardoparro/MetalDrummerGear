# How to Sound Like Paul Mazurkiewicz — Cannibal Corpse Drum Sound Guide

## Overview

Paul Mazurkiewicz has been driving the world's best-selling death metal band for over 35 years. As Cannibal Corpse's co-founder and drummer since 1988, he has appeared on every studio album from "Eaten Back to Life" (1990) through the present — an unbroken career spanning 30+ albums and relentless international touring that makes him one of the most consistently active drummers in extreme metal history. Cannibal Corpse has sold over 2 million albums worldwide, making them the best-selling death metal band of all time. Paul's approach to extreme drumming is the most commercially proven in the genre.

What distinguishes Paul from many brutal death metal drummers is his groove-forward orientation. His signature two-kick shuffle groove — heard on tracks like "Hammer Smashed Face" and "Stripped, Raped and Strangled" — creates a bouncing, headbang-inducing rhythmic foundation that makes brutal death metal genuinely propulsive rather than purely chaotic. Paul serves the song first, and the result is death metal that is both technically demanding and endlessly listenable.

## Tuning & Setup

Paul tunes for attack and definition. In Cannibal Corpse's extremely dense sonic environment, every drum must punch through — boomy tuning disappears completely:

- **Kick:** Tight tension, heavy muffling (pillow or blanket contacting both heads). Port hole in resonant head essential. The bass drum should click and thump distinctly — every kick in the two-kick shuffle must be individually heard.
- **Snare:** High tension for crack and projection. Must cut through Cannibal Corpse's dense guitar wall. Minimal muffling — one Moongel maximum.
- **Toms:** Medium-high tension, one Moongel per tom. Fast attack, controlled decay for articulate death metal fills.

## Technique Tips

Paul uses **matched grip** with emphasis on power and consistency. His technique prioritizes locking in with the guitar riffs and vocals over technical showmanship — every drum decision serves Cannibal Corpse's brutal but groove-oriented compositions.

**Mazurkiewicz's Brutal-Yet-Groovy Toolkit:**

- **The Two-Kick Death Metal Shuffle (160–200 BPM, Intermediate-Advanced):** Paul's most distinctive pattern — a double kick shuffle that drives against the guitar riff rhythm rather than simply underlining it. Heard prominently on "Hammer Smashed Face" and "Stripped, Raped and Strangled," this pattern creates the bounce that makes classic Cannibal Corpse headbang-inducing. Learn the guitar riff first; Paul's kick patterns are written in conversation with the guitar. A Tama Iron Cobra or Pearl Eliminator helps with consistent double bass timing.
- **Brutal Death Metal Blast (180–220 BPM, Advanced):** Paul's blast beats are powerful but controlled — he transitions in and out of blast sections at specific compositional moments rather than sustaining them as constant texture. Start at 160 BPM and build consistency before adding speed. Every note should sound intentional.
- **Syncopated Snare Against Kick Riffs (Variable, Intermediate):** Many of Cannibal Corpse's heaviest passages feature snare accents that directly mirror the guitar riff rhythm. Transcribe the guitar riff rhythmically, then play those rhythms on snare against a steady kick pattern. This riff-mirroring approach is the core of Paul's compositional lock.

## Practice Routine

1. **Two-Kick Shuffle Builder (20 min daily):** Play a simple rock groove at 120 BPM. Replace the steady hi-hat with a shuffle pattern (long-short 8th notes). Add syncopated double bass that mirrors this shuffle feel. Lock to a metronome. Increase 10 BPM per week when the pattern feels effortless. Goal: natural two-kick shuffle at 180+ BPM.
2. **Riff-Mirror Exercise (15 min daily):** Record a simple 4-bar riff (or use a metronome pattern as proxy). Play that exact rhythm on bass drum while keeping snare on 2 and 4. Then put the riff on snare against steady kick 8ths. Practice both — Paul moves the riff between drum voices. Goal: instinctive riff-to-drum-pattern translation.
3. **Cannibal Corpse Song Study (30 min, 2x/week):** Take *Hammer Smashed Face* and learn it section by section at half speed. Identify how Paul transitions between groove and blast. Note where kick patterns follow guitar versus where they are independent. Goal: complete performance of one classic Cannibal Corpse track.

**Common mistakes:** Playing every section at maximum volume (Paul uses dynamics even in brutal contexts); focusing on blast speed before groove consistency (the shuffle is Paul's signature, not the blast); disconnecting kick from guitar riffs (Paul's kick patterns dialogue with the guitar); ignoring endurance training (35+ years of touring demands sustainable technique).

## FAQ

**Q: Why has Paul Mazurkiewicz stayed with Cannibal Corpse for 35+ years?**  
A: Paul has credited longevity to treating the band professionally, maintaining physical condition, and genuine enthusiasm for the music. As a co-founder, he has creative investment and financial stake in the band's identity. His sustainable, efficient technique also contributes — he plays without injury-inducing tension.

**Q: What makes Cannibal Corpse's drums different from other death metal bands?**  
A: Paul's groove orientation distinguishes Cannibal Corpse. While the band plays at extreme tempos with heavy blast work, Mazurkiewicz prioritizes headbang-able patterns over unbroken chaos. The two-kick shuffle and riff-mirroring kick patterns give the rhythmic foundation a bounce and momentum that purely blast-focused bands lack.

**Q: Does Paul Mazurkiewicz use triggers?**  
A: Paul uses kick drum triggers for live performances to maintain kick definition in loud stage environments. Studio recordings typically favor acoustic sounds.

**Q: What is the best Cannibal Corpse album to study Paul Mazurkiewicz?**  
A: *Tomb of the Mutilated* (1992) is the essential entry — it contains "Hammer Smashed Face" and captures Paul at his most distinctive. *The Bleeding* (1994) shows development. *Evisceration Plague* (2009) represents his modern era at peak form.
