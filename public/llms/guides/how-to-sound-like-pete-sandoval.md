# How to Sound Like Pete Sandoval — Morbid Angel Drum Sound Guide

**Drummer:** Pete Sandoval  
**Band:** Morbid Angel  
**Genre:** Death Metal  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-pete-sandoval

## Overview

Pete Sandoval is the blast beat godfather — the drummer who codified extreme speed and blast beat technique as compositional tools in death metal. As Morbid Angel's drummer from 1984 through 2013, he drove albums including "Altars of Madness" (1989), "Blessed Are the Sick" (1991), "Covenant" (1993), and "Domination" (1995) at tempos that set the genre's standard. His 1989 performance on "Altars of Madness" — recorded when blast beats were still novel — remains a benchmark more than three decades later. George Kollias, Flo Mounier, and virtually every death metal drummer who followed cite Pete as a primary influence. What distinguishes Pete from pure speed players is his internal blast beat structure: he accents beat 1 of each bar even at 200 BPM, giving his patterns a cyclical musical logic rather than undifferentiated aggression. His Tama Iron Cobra 900 double pedal and ankle-driven technique allow sustained blast beats across full-length songs — an endurance achievement as significant as the speed itself.

## Tuning & Setup

Pete tunes for maximum articulation at extreme tempos. At 200 BPM, sustain is the enemy — every note must speak and decay before the next arrives:

- **Kick:** Tight tension, heavy muffling (Aquarian Super-Kick II head plus internal pillow). Port hole in resonant head essential. Zero sustain — each double bass note must be individually audible.
- **Snare:** Very high tension, minimal muffling (bare or one Moongel strip). Must crack through 200 BPM blast sections without disappearing in the mix.
- **Toms:** Medium-high tension with one Moongel each. Fast attack and fast decay for brief fills between blast sections.

## Technique Tips

Pete uses **matched grip** with a highly refined ankle-driven foot technique. His approach eliminates unnecessary muscle groups to enable sustained extreme tempos.

**Pete's Core Techniques:**

- **Morbid Angel Blast (180–210 BPM, Advanced-Expert):** Hand-to-hand alternating snare with kick on every other 16th note. Critically, Pete accents beat 1 of each bar even within the blast — this musical anchor is what makes Morbid Angel's blasts feel structured. Start at 140 BPM and develop the accent before adding speed.
- **Ankle-Driven Double Bass:** Heel-up technique using ankle snap motion exclusively — no calf, no thigh. Isolate one foot at 160 BPM for 2 minutes. If the calf burns, you're using the wrong muscle group. This is the key to Pete's endurance over full-song blast passages.
- **Kick-Riff Lock (160–190 BPM, Advanced):** Pete doubles guitar riff rhythms on kick while the snare holds a blast pattern overhead. The result is two coexisting patterns — blast gives atmosphere, kick gives the riff's pulse. Transcribe the guitar riff first, then build the kick pattern from it.
- **Death Metal Groove Break (120–150 BPM, Intermediate):** Pete punctuates blasts with heavy half-time grooves — snare on beat 3 with double kick filling spaces. These breaks make blasts hit harder by contrast. Practice the blast-to-groove transition specifically.

## Practice Routine

1. **Ankle Isolation Drill (20 min daily):** Isolate one foot. Play 16th notes at 160 BPM using only ankle motion. Hold 2 minutes per foot. Rest 1 minute. 3 rounds. Goal: 200 BPM foot endurance for 2+ minutes per foot.
2. **Hand-to-Hand Blast Builder (15 min daily):** Alternating single strokes on snare at 160 BPM in 16th notes. Add kick on beats 1 and 3. Once solid, add kick on every other 16th. Increase 5 BPM weekly.
3. **Blast-to-Groove Transition Drill (10 min daily):** Play 8 bars of blast at comfortable tempo. Drop to half-time groove for 4 bars. Return to blast. Repeat 10 times — transition must feel instant.

**Common mistakes:** Using calf muscles for double bass (ankle isolation is the key to endurance); gripping sticks too tight during blasts (tension prevents speed and causes injury); ignoring groove breaks (Pete's pocket is as important as his blast speed); chasing tempo before consistency (sloppy fast blasts are useless in a band context).

## FAQ

**Q: What makes Pete Sandoval's blast beats different from other death metal drummers?**  
A: Pete accents beat 1 of each bar even within continuous blast sections, giving his patterns a cyclical musical structure. His kick also follows guitar riff rhythms rather than running parallel eighth notes, which locks Morbid Angel's arrangements together rhythmically. These musical decisions within extreme speed are what define his approach.

**Q: How fast does Pete Sandoval blast?**  
A: Pete sustains blast beats at 180-210 BPM across full Morbid Angel songs. On Altars of Madness (1989), the blasts sit around 185-195 BPM maintained for extended passages — extraordinary for the era.

**Q: Why does Pete use the Tama Iron Cobra specifically?**  
A: The Iron Cobra's Power Glide cam provides a linear, predictable feel that suits ankle-driven technique. The consistent cam curve allows Pete to develop precise muscle memory for 200 BPM sustained passages. Other cam curves can feel inconsistent at extreme tempos.

**Q: Can I learn Pete's style without playing at 200 BPM?**  
A: Yes. Pete's musical approach — groove contrast, fill placement, riff-locking kicks — applies at any tempo. Learn Covenant-era Morbid Angel's mid-tempo grooves before chasing Altars of Madness blast speeds.
