# How to Sound Like Pete Sandoval — Morbid Angel Drum Sound Guide

**Drummer:** Pete Sandoval  
**Band:** Morbid Angel / Terrorizer  
**Genre:** Death Metal / Grindcore  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-pete-sandoval

## Overview

Pete Sandoval is the blast beat godfather. His work on Morbid Angel's "Altars of Madness" (1989) arrived as a thunderclap — blast beats sustained at tempos no one had imagined possible, executed with supernatural consistency. AllMusic's Jason Birchmeier wrote that Sandoval "challenges one's perception of how fast a drummer can possibly drum." What makes Pete's story remarkable is that he had never played double bass before joining Morbid Angel in 1988. He built his speed from zero through obsessive practice, reportedly passing out from exhaustion mid-session and resuming immediately upon waking. This dedication produced the gravity blast — a one-handed blast technique that became foundational vocabulary for death metal drumming worldwide. Every extreme metal drummer who plays a blast beat owes a debt to Pete Sandoval. He is the most imitated death metal drummer alive.

## Kit Setup

Pete plays **Pearl drums** (Masters and Export series) with a setup engineered for extreme performance:

- **Kick Drums:** 22" x 18" (x2) with Pearl Demon Drive Double Pedal
- **Snare:** 14" x 6.5" Pearl Sensitone Steel or Brass Snare
- **Rack Toms:** 10" x 8", 12" x 9"
- **Floor Tom:** 16" x 16"
- **Cymbals:** Sabian AA / AAX Series — fast attack and bright cutting projection
- **Pedals:** Pearl Demon Drive Double Pedal (direct-drive for zero-lag precision at extreme tempos)
- **Sticks:** Promark 5B or Vic Firth 5B (heavier weight for death metal power and impact)
- **Heads:** Remo Pinstripe Clear or Emperor Clear (kick), Remo Ambassador X Coated (snare), Remo Emperor Clear (toms)

## Tuning & Setup

Pete tunes for maximum attack and minimum sustain — at 200+ BPM blast tempos, any drum sustain creates muddiness:

- **Kick:** Tight tension, heavy muffling (full pillow contact with both heads). Port hole in resonant head essential. The bass drum should thud and click, not boom — at blast speeds, any sustain creates a mud wall.
- **Snare:** High tension, no muffling or one thin tape strip. Must cut through blast components simultaneously without help from muffling.
- **Toms:** Medium-high tension, one Moongel per tom. Fast attack, quick decay for articulate tom fills in death metal's sonic environment.

## Technique Tips

Pete uses **matched grip** with a loose, whip-driven wrist stroke. His right hand anchors the hi-hat with steady 16th-note bursts while his left hand drives the snare, together creating the wall of sound that defined Morbid Angel's identity.

**Sandoval's Extreme Technical Toolkit:**

- **The Gravity Blast (180–240 BPM, Expert):** One-handed blast technique where the snare hand falls and rebounds under gravity rather than being forced down by muscle. This dramatically reduces fatigue and enables sustained extreme speeds. Practice the gravity motion slowly — let the stick fall, catch it just above the head, repeat. Speed follows naturally. A Pearl Demon Drive or Tama Iron Cobra provides the foot consistency to match this technique.
- **Double Bass Blast Foundation (160–210 BPM, Advanced):** Traditional alternating hand blast over continuous double bass 16th notes. Pete locks kick patterns to Trey Azagthoth's guitar riff rhythms — the kick isn't independent, it mirrors the riffs. Start at 120 BPM and build foot consistency before adding hands at speed.
- **Groove-to-Blast Transitions (120–200 BPM, Advanced):** Morbid Angel songs frequently shift from mid-tempo riff grooves into full blasts and back. Pete's ability to switch modes instantly while maintaining exact tempo makes the transitions feel crushing. Practice: 4 bars groove, snap to 4 bars blast, return. Keep hi-hat anchor consistent through both modes.

## Practice Routine

1. **Blast Beat Tempo Ladder (30 min, 4x/week):** Set metronome to 160 BPM. Blast for 60 seconds straight. Rest 2 minutes. Move to 170 BPM. Repeat. Add 5 BPM per week only when the lower tempo feels effortless. Log every session. Goal: sustained blast at 200+ BPM for 60 seconds.
2. **Gravity Blast Introduction (15 min daily):** Hold stick loosely in dominant hand. Let it fall onto snare from 4 inches — let it rebound fully. Catch it. Repeat continuously, adding speed only when the rebound feels natural. Never force downstrokes. Goal: clean gravity motion at 180+ BPM.
3. **Double Bass Endurance Build (20 min daily):** Continuous 16th notes on double bass at 140 BPM for 2 minutes without stopping. Rest 1 minute. Repeat 4 times. Increase 5 BPM per week. Goal: continuous double bass at 190+ BPM for 2 minutes.
4. **Morbid Angel Transcription (45 min, 2x/week):** Take *Immortal Rites* or *Chapel of Ghouls* at reduced speed. Identify where blasts start and stop, what triggers each transition, how groove sections relate to guitar riffs. Learn structure before speed.

**Common mistakes:** Gripping sticks tightly for power (tension kills speed and causes injury); adding tempo before mastering endurance at lower speeds; treating blast beats as background texture (Pete's blasts have internal dynamics); neglecting double bass endurance (blast speed lives as much in the feet as the hands).

## FAQ

**Q: What is the gravity blast and who invented it?**  
A: The gravity blast is a one-handed blast beat technique where the stick falls and rebounds under gravity rather than being forced down by muscle. Pete Sandoval is widely credited with popularizing this technique, which reduces fatigue and enables extreme sustained speeds. George Kollias has also developed and taught variations.

**Q: How did Pete Sandoval learn double bass so fast?**  
A: Pete had never played double bass before joining Morbid Angel. He built his speed through obsessive, hours-long practice sessions — reportedly passing out from exhaustion and resuming immediately upon waking. There is no shortcut; his technique was built through extreme dedication over months.

**Q: What drums did Pete use on Altars of Madness?**  
A: Pete used Pearl drums for the 1989 sessions, with Pearl hardware and Sabian cymbals. Pearl Export and early Masters series drums were part of his setup during this era.

**Q: Does Pete Sandoval use triggers?**  
A: Pete used kick triggers in live settings to maintain definition at extreme tempos. Classic Morbid Angel studio recordings relied primarily on acoustic sounds, which makes those performances all the more remarkable.

**Q: What is the best Morbid Angel album to study Pete Sandoval?**  
A: *Altars of Madness* (1989) is the essential reference — it captures Sandoval at maximum intensity and defined death metal drumming. *Covenant* (1993) shows groove integration. Together they give a complete picture.
