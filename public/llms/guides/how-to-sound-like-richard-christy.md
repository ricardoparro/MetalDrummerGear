# How to Sound Like Richard Christy — Death / Iced Earth Drum Sound Guide

**Drummer:** Richard Christy  
**Band:** Death / Iced Earth / Control Denied  
**Genre:** Death Metal / Progressive Metal / Power Metal  
**Guide URL:** https://metalforge.io/guides/how-to-sound-like-richard-christy

## Overview

Richard Christy occupies a unique position in metal drumming: simultaneously one of the most technically accomplished death metal drummers alive and one of the genre's most publicly recognizable personalities through his work with The Howard Stern Show. This crossover appeal — bridging the extreme metal underground and mainstream entertainment — makes Richard a gateway figure for audiences discovering technical death metal for the first time.

His drumming credentials are impeccable. From 1996 to 2001, Christy drummed for Death under Chuck Schuldiner, appearing on "The Sound of Perseverance" (1998) — the final Death album and widely considered the apex of progressive death metal. He also played for Control Denied, Schuldiner's progressive metal project, and later joined Iced Earth (2003–2007), bringing extreme metal credentials to a more commercially accessible power metal context. "Gettysburg (1863)," Iced Earth's 32-minute epic from "The Glorious Burden" (2004), stands as evidence of his versatility.

What distinguishes Richard's playing is the combination of extreme speed with genuine musical sensitivity. His drum parts on "The Sound of Perseverance" function as melodic counterpoints to guitar and bass lines — they are compositionally aware, not just technically impressive.

## Tuning & Setup

Richard tunes with more musical sensitivity than pure brutal death metal contexts require. Death's progressive compositions need drums that speak distinctly while supporting melodic and harmonic elements:

- **Kick:** Medium-tight tension, moderate muffling. More tone than pure brutal death metal setups — Death's production requires a kick that speaks within complex arrangements without excessive boom.
- **Snare:** Medium-high tension, minimal muffling (one Moongel for recording). Must handle both brutal blast passages and sensitive ghost note work within the same songs.
- **Toms:** Medium tension, one Moongel per tom. Richard's toms support Death's melodic approach — they need tone to function musically alongside guitar melody, not just as percussive punctuation.

## Technique Tips

Richard uses **matched grip** with emphasis on dynamic range and control. Unlike pure brutality-focused drummers, his work under Chuck Schuldiner required serving progressive compositional structures — drum parts on Death's later material function as melodic counterpoints to the other instruments.

**Christy's Technical-Musical Toolkit:**

- **Progressive Death Metal Groove (100–160 BPM, Advanced):** Richard navigates 7/8, 5/4, and mixed time signatures while maintaining momentum — the listener feels propulsion, not calculation. Practice 7/8 by playing a simple groove in that meter for 5 minutes until it feels natural rather than counted. Repeat with 5/4 and 9/8. A DW 9000 double pedal provides the precise timing Death's prog structures demand.
- **Technical Death Metal Blast (180–220 BPM, Advanced-Expert):** Richard's blast beats transition in and out at specific compositional moments rather than running as constant texture. Footwork maintains blast consistency while hands navigate complex patterns. Isolate blast-to-groove transitions from *Scavenger of Human Sorrow* and *Flesh and the Power It Holds* — the transitions are the lesson.
- **Power Metal Drive / Iced Earth Style (160–220 BPM, Intermediate-Advanced):** For Iced Earth, Richard adapted his extreme metal technique to galloping power metal — double bass 16th notes under melodic guitar lines, straight-ahead groove with maximum power, dramatic fills for cinematic arrangements. Iced Earth's gallop requires endurance rather than pure speed. Pearl Demon Drive or Tama Iron Cobra for foot consistency.

**Key songs to study:** *Scavenger of Human Sorrow* (The Sound of Perseverance, 1998) · *Flesh and the Power It Holds* (The Sound of Perseverance, 1998) · *Spirit Crusher* (The Sound of Perseverance, 1998) · *Declaration Day* (The Glorious Burden / Iced Earth, 2004) · *Gettysburg (1863)* (The Glorious Burden / Iced Earth, 2004)

## Practice Routine

1. **Odd Time Groove Development (20 min daily):** Take 7/8. Play simple groove: kick on beat 1, snare on beat 3, hi-hat on every beat. Loop for 5 minutes until 7/8 feels natural rather than counted. Repeat with 5/4 and 9/8. Then add double bass. Richard's playing proves odd meters become as natural as 4/4 with sufficient practice. Goal: uncontrived feel in 5/4, 7/8, and 9/8.
2. **Ghost Note Integration (15 min daily):** Play a groove with prominent ghost notes (accented snare on 2 and 4, ghost notes on every 16th). Ghost notes should be barely audible while accents project clearly. A Pearl Free-Floating snare maximizes sensitivity. Vary ghost note volume — some passages need slightly louder ghosts. Goal: ghost notes at 20% volume of accented strokes while maintaining tempo.
3. **Death Album Transcription (45 min, 2x/week):** Take *Scavenger of Human Sorrow* and work through it section by section at 60% speed using audio software. Mark every time signature change, fill start point, and blast entry point. Richard's genius is in navigating these changes — study the transitions specifically. Goal: complete structural analysis and partial performance.
4. **Power Metal Stamina Builder (20 min daily):** Continuous double bass 16th notes at 160 BPM for 3 minutes without stopping. Rest 90 seconds. Repeat 4 times. Increase 5 BPM per week. Iced Earth's approach requires endurance rather than extreme speed. Goal: continuous double bass at 200 BPM for 3 minutes.

**Common mistakes:** Focusing on Death's extremity without studying its melodic sensitivity (the musicality is the point); neglecting odd meter work (progressive death metal requires fluency, not tolerance, of odd time); playing too loudly throughout (Richard's dynamic range is wide and intentional); treating ghost notes as optional (they are structural in Death's approach).

## FAQ

**Q: What is Richard Christy's most important recording?**  
A: *The Sound of Perseverance* (Death, 1998) is Richard's definitive statement — the final Death album, Chuck Schuldiner's compositional peak, and progressive death metal at its most sophisticated. Every serious Death fan has heard Richard's drumming on this record.

**Q: How did Richard Christy get the Death drumming gig?**  
A: Richard joined Death in 1996. Schuldiner specifically sought drummers capable of handling Death's increasingly progressive compositions. Richard's reputation in Florida's death metal scene and his technical ability led to the audition.

**Q: What was Richard Christy's Iced Earth period like?**  
A: Richard was Iced Earth's drummer from 2003 to 2007, appearing on *The Glorious Burden* (2004). His extreme metal background gave Iced Earth's power metal arrangements extra aggression and precision. The "Gettysburg (1863)" trilogy — a 32-minute epic — showcases his versatility across multiple metal styles.

**Q: Does Richard Christy still play drums?**  
A: Yes, Richard continues to play drums recreationally and performs occasional metal events. Since 2004 his primary career has been with The Howard Stern Show, where he has frequently referenced his metal background and brought multiple extreme metal acts to mainstream audiences.

**Q: What is the best way to study Richard Christy's technique?**  
A: *The Sound of Perseverance* at reduced speed is the definitive study method. Focus on how Richard navigates time signature changes and how his fills respond to Chuck Schuldiner's guitar phrases. His playing is compositionally aware — every drum decision serves the song.
