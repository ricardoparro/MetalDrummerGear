## Nile Gravity Blast Pattern

**Song:** Papyrus Containing the Spell to Preserve Its Possessor | **Album:** In Their Darkened Shrines (2002) | **BPM:** 280+ BPM | **Technique:** blast-technique | **Difficulty:** expert

George Kollias is widely regarded as one of the fastest and most technically sophisticated drummers in extreme metal, and his gravity blast technique is the defining example of why. The gravity blast — also called the one-handed roll or rebound blast — involves using a single stick's natural rebound off the snare or hi-hat rim to generate a rapid-fire burst of strokes that would be impossible to match with traditional alternating strokes. Where a conventional blast beat interleaves left and right hands to produce a continuous stream of notes, the gravity blast allows a single hand to produce the same density at even higher speed, while the other hand maintains a ride or hi-hat pattern on top. At 280+ BPM this becomes one of the most demanding techniques in all of drumming: the motion has to be completely relaxed, letting gravity and the stick's own bounce do the work, because any muscular tension will kill both speed and consistency. Kollias has demonstrated this technique in hundreds of live performances and clinic settings, showing how the physics of the bounce — not raw arm strength — is what makes the extreme velocity possible. "Papyrus Containing the Spell to Preserve Its Possessor" from Nile's 2002 album In Their Darkened Shrines is one of the earliest showcases of this approach, and the live footage of Kollias performing it remains essential viewing for any serious student of extreme metal drumming. For drummers approaching this technique, the first priority is internalising the rebound mechanics away from the kit entirely, feeling how gravity returns the stick before any muscle pull is needed. The technique rewards patience: rushing the speed before the mechanics are solid produces a messy, uneven result, while trusting the rebound produces something genuinely remarkable.

### How to Play

- Use the stick's natural rebound off the snare or hi-hat rim — let gravity return the stick, do not pull it back with muscle
- One hand creates the rapid-fire snare stream; the other hand maintains the ride or hi-hat pattern independently
- Keep the wrist and forearm completely relaxed so the bounce can generate the extreme velocity
- Feet continue a standard double-bass blast pattern underneath, independent of the hand motion
- Practise the motion away from the kit first to understand the physics before applying it at speed

### Key Elements

- Begin without the kit: bounce a stick off your thigh or a practice pad and feel the rebound return naturally
- Isolate the one-handed roll at a slow, controlled speed before touching the kit; the motion must be automatic before adding complexity
- Watch slow-motion video of Kollias performing to study the wrist angle and forearm position before attempting at speed
- Treat the gravity blast as a long-term project: it may take months to internalise the mechanics, but once it clicks it becomes reliable

## Polyrhythmic Death Metal Pattern

**Song:** Annihilation of the Wicked | **Album:** Annihilation of the Wicked (2005) | **BPM:** 220 BPM | **Technique:** polyrhythmic | **Difficulty:** expert

"Annihilation of the Wicked" is the title track of Nile's 2005 record and represents one of George Kollias's most demanding compositional statements: blast-beat intensity combined with sophisticated polyrhythmic layering that draws on his classical and jazz influences as much as on death metal tradition. What separates Kollias from drummers who merely play fast is his ability to impose polyrhythmic structures over a brutal extreme-metal foundation — in "Annihilation of the Wicked" he layers groupings of five against the underlying pulse of four, creating a sense of rhythmic displacement and forward momentum that gives the song its restless, lurching energy. The feet maintain a relentless death metal pulse at around 220 BPM, while the hands operate on a different metric grid, and the challenge is maintaining both simultaneously without either collapsing into the other. This kind of advanced limb independence — the ability to count and feel two different rhythmic cycles at the same time — is normally associated with jazz drumming, and Kollias has spoken in interviews about how studying jazz drumming and classical percussion influenced his approach to writing and performing extreme metal. For drummers, this track is a gateway into polyrhythmic thinking as applied to death metal: it teaches you to internalise a 5-over-4 pattern, to feel both pulses simultaneously rather than counting them cerebrally, and to do all of this at a tempo that would be demanding even for straight 4/4 playing. The approach is to practise the polyrhythmic relationship in isolation — hands alone, feet alone — at a slow, controllable tempo, then to combine them gradually, only pushing the tempo once each element is stable.

### How to Play

- Feet maintain a constant death metal pulse at ~220 BPM, independent of whatever the hands are doing
- Hands play groupings of five notes against the underlying pulse of four, creating the polyrhythmic displacement
- Feel both metric cycles simultaneously rather than counting them separately — internalise each pulse as a physical sensation
- Practise the 5-over-4 relationship with hands alone, then feet alone, before combining them
- Each limb operates independently — the coordination comes from understanding the relationship, not from mechanical drilling alone

### Key Elements

- Study the 5-over-4 polyrhythm conceptually before touching the kit — draw it out or use a polyrhythm calculator until you can hear both pulses
- Practise 5-over-4 with hands alone at 60-70 BPM until the groupings feel automatic and you stop counting
- Add the foot pattern only once the hands are comfortable — isolate the combination of one hand plus one foot before adding the full kit
- Use a polyrhythmic metronome app to hear both pulses simultaneously while you practise

## Sustained 250+ BPM Blast

**Song:** Sacrifice Unto Sebek | **Album:** Those Whom the Gods Detest (2009) | **BPM:** 255 BPM | **Technique:** blast-section | **Difficulty:** expert

"Sacrifice Unto Sebek" from Nile's 2009 album Those Whom the Gods Detest is one of the benchmark examples of sustained extreme-tempo drumming in recorded metal. George Kollias performs the track at around 255 BPM, maintaining not just individual blast patterns but extended sections of that intensity without the kind of fatigue-driven degradation in timing or articulation that would afflict most drummers at that speed. His ability to sustain blast beats at 250+ BPM for long passages is a product of the same principle that underpins his gravity blast: total relaxation. Tension in the arms, wrists or hands is the enemy of both speed and endurance, and at these tempos it will cause fatigue within seconds. Kollias has described his approach in clinics as one of learning to remove effort rather than to add it — the motion becomes more efficient with practice until it feels as natural as a slower pattern. The alternating-foot double bass that underpins the blast adds another layer of demand: both feet must run in precise alternation at extreme speed, and the coordination between upper and lower body has to be so ingrained that it is effectively unconscious. "Sacrifice Unto Sebek" is the proof-of-concept for this approach — the track is intense from beginning to end, and Kollias plays it with a consistency that makes 255 BPM sound achievable even as it remains one of the hardest drumming feats on record. For drummers, the track is a master-class in stamina training: it teaches that building endurance for extreme tempos is a physiological process that happens over months and years, not weeks, and that injury prevention — regular rest, proper technique, avoiding tension — is not optional but foundational.

### How to Play

- Maintain a traditional blast pattern at 255 BPM through full sections — articulation must stay even, never smeared
- Keep the wrists, forearms and shoulders completely relaxed; tension is the primary enemy at these tempos
- Drive alternating-foot double bass in precise coordination underneath the blast — both feet must be fully independent
- Breathe regularly and consciously during extended blasting sections to maintain oxygen flow and prevent early fatigue
- Use efficient motion: minimise stick height and forearm travel so that less energy is required per stroke

### Key Elements

- Build blast-beat stamina incrementally over months — start at a tempo where the technique stays clean, then raise by 5 BPM only when that speed is fully stable
- Never sacrifice technique consistency for speed; a tight, even blast at 200 BPM builds the right muscle memory more effectively than a sloppy attempt at 250
- Take regular breaks during practice sessions and stop immediately if you feel tension or pain — injury at these tempos is a long-term setback
- Focus every session on relaxation rather than effort: if something feels hard it usually means tension, not weakness

## Teaching Points

George Kollias's style is defined by the radical application of relaxation and physics to extreme-metal drumming. Key practice principles across all his licks: trust the stick's rebound rather than muscling it back; feel polyrhythmic relationships physically before drilling them fast; build stamina by incrementally raising tempo only when technique is stable. Mastering these patterns builds not just speed but the fundamental discipline — tension-free motion, independent limbs, breath control — that underpins all advanced metal drumming.
