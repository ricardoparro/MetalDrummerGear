## Twilight of the Thunder God Double Bass Run

**Song:** Twilight of the Thunder God | **Album:** Twilight of the Thunder God (2008) | **BPM:** ~162 BPM | **Technique:** double bass | **Difficulty:** advanced

Amon Amarth's signature live anthem demands thunderous double bass passages at 162 BPM. Jocke Wallgren — who has performed this song thousands of times across arena and festival stages since joining in 2016 — brings a groove-anchored approach to the kick density, keeping ride and snare clarity above the continuous double bass foundation. The track's verse-to-chorus transitions shift weight from upper-limb-led playing to leg-led double bass instantly, a physical skill that requires deep independence between hands and feet.

### How to Play

- Alternate both kick pedals evenly at ~162 BPM with identical attack weight on each foot — imbalance between feet creates an uneven texture that undermines the anthemic groove
- Maintain a driving ride cymbal quarter-note or eighth-note pattern above the double bass to provide rhythmic clarity over the kick density
- Keep the snare backbeat on beats two and four as a groove anchor throughout double bass passages — the kick and snare work together, not in opposition

### Key Elements

- Start at 120 BPM and focus entirely on foot evenness — record yourself and listen to whether the left and right kick attacks are identical in volume and timing
- Build to 162 BPM in 5 BPM increments across multiple sessions; speed gains consolidated slowly are more durable than those chased quickly
- Practice the ride cymbal and double bass independently first, then layer them together — the hands often lose their pattern quality when the feet first come in at full tempo

## Runes to My Memory Melodic Death Groove

**Song:** Runes to My Memory | **Album:** With Oden on Our Side (2006) | **BPM:** ~148 BPM | **Technique:** main groove | **Difficulty:** advanced

The melodic death metal main groove from this Amon Amarth live staple features an eighth-note ride or hi-hat pulse carrying the song's melodic momentum while double bass bursts land precisely on the riff's rhythmic accent points. Wallgren uses the pre-chorus sections to build double bass density, creating a deliberate escalation toward the more expansive chorus. The groove's power lies in its selectivity — double bass appears where the riff demands it, not as a constant texture.

### How to Play

- Place double bass bursts at the riff's rhythmic accent points specifically — select rather than blanket kick placement reinforces the guitar-drum interplay
- Increase double bass density in pre-chorus sections as a deliberate build toward the more expansive chorus groove

### Key Elements

- Learn the ride pattern and snare backbeat without kick first — the upper-limb pulse is the foundation that the kick patterns build on top of
- Map the double bass placement points by listening to the guitar riff and identifying the specific accent beats where kick density increases
- Practice the pre-chorus build as a standalone four-to-eight bar phrase: start at the verse kick density and escalate to the chorus density within the phrase

## Fafner's Gold Blast Beat Section

**Song:** Fafner's Gold | **Album:** Berserker (2019) | **BPM:** ~160 BPM | **Technique:** blast beat | **Difficulty:** advanced

This blast beat passage from Amon Amarth's 2019 album Berserker shows how Wallgren integrates extreme technique into a melodic death metal context. The alternating kick-snare blast runs at ~160 BPM with ride or hi-hat driving the melodic-death-metal texture above it. Unlike pure extreme metal blasting, the blast must preserve the song's anthemic quality — entry and exit points are instant, with no acceleration ramp, maintaining the song's structural momentum.

### How to Play

- Execute alternating kick-snare blast at ~160 BPM with sufficient separation between each element that both register with distinct weight rather than blurring
- Drive the ride or hi-hat above the blast in a pattern that preserves the song's anthemic melodic-death-metal character
- Enter blast passages from groove sections at the correct tempo instantly with no acceleration ramp — the entry point is as important as the blast itself

### Key Elements

- Build the blast at 120 BPM and focus on snare authority rather than speed — a heavy, clear snare stroke at slow tempo builds the muscle pattern for a heavy, clear snare at 160 BPM
- Practice blast entry from a groove: play 8 bars of a groove at the song tempo, then enter the blast on bar 9 — the entry should feel sudden, not gradual
- Work on blast exit independently: run 8 bars of blast, then land a groove downbeat on bar 9 — the return to groove must be as clean as the blast itself
