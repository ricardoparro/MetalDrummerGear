## Epidemic of Hate Gravity Blast

**Song:** Epidemic of Hate | **Album:** Killing on Adrenaline (1998) | **BPM:** ~200 BPM | **Technique:** gravity blast | **Difficulty:** expert

Kevin Talley's gravity blast technique from Killing on Adrenaline uses the stick's weight and rebound rather than active wrist drive to generate speed at ~200 BPM. The stick is held loosely at the butt end and angled so gravity pulls it into the snare head, allowing the rebound to carry the stick back without arm or wrist assistance. The kick is locked under the snare to keep the blast articulate at extreme tempo. This technique allows sustained blasting without the muscular fatigue that active wrist-driven approaches generate.

### How to Play

- Hold the stick loosely at the butt end and angle it so gravity pulls it into the snare head
- Allow the rebound to carry the stick back rather than driving the return stroke with wrist or arm
- Lock the kick under the snare so the blast stays articulate even at extreme tempo

### Key Elements

- Practise the gravity stroke with one hand only until the angle and rebound feel natural before adding the second hand
- Keep a very loose grip — any tension in the hand prevents the rebound from doing its job
- Start at a tempo where every stroke rebounds cleanly, not the fastest you can manage — build tempo only after the stroke mechanics are consistent

## Killing on Adrenaline Two-Handed Blast

**Song:** Killing on Adrenaline | **Album:** Killing on Adrenaline (1998) | **BPM:** ~220 BPM | **Technique:** two-handed blast | **Difficulty:** expert

The title-track blast from this Dying Fetus classic pushes two-handed snare blasting to ~220 BPM with matched alternation between dominant and non-dominant hands. Both wrist mechanics must be identical — tone, timing, and consistency must match between hands at full speed. The double bass runs as a continuous engine beneath the blast, unifying the pattern rather than creating an independent rhythmic layer. Stick height must remain equally low on both sides to prevent either hand from lifting and slowing the pattern.

### How to Play

- Strike the snare with both hands in matched alternation, keeping wrist mechanics identical between dominant and non-dominant hands
- Drive the double bass as a continuous engine under the snare so the blast sounds unified rather than layered
- Keep stick height equally low on both sides so neither hand lifts and breaks the pattern

### Key Elements

- Practise the non-dominant hand alone with a metronome until it matches the dominant hand exactly in tone and consistency
- Add the double bass only after both hands are even — the feet amplify any existing hand imbalance
- Use a timer to build blast endurance in sets: 30 seconds on, 30 seconds rest, gradually extending the on-time

## Destroy the Opposition Groove Breakdown

**Song:** Destroy the Opposition | **Album:** Destroy the Opposition (2000) | **BPM:** ~155 BPM | **Technique:** death metal groove | **Difficulty:** advanced

From Dying Fetus's 2000 album, this groove breakdown section shows Talley's ability to lock into a deep, pocketed death metal groove after sustained blasting. The snare sits squarely in the pocket with uniform hit-to-hit dynamics, while the kick locks with the bass guitar to create a unified low-end chug. The discipline here is leaving space — Talley resists filling every gap, understanding that the groove's depth comes from what is not played as much as what is.

### How to Play

- Place the snare squarely in the pocket and let notes breathe rather than filling every gap
- Lock the kick with the bass guitar so the low-end chug stays tight rather than independent
- Keep snare dynamics uniform hit-to-hit so the groove stays deep rather than wandering in volume

### Key Elements

- Slow the groove to 80 BPM and listen for any inconsistency in snare volume before bringing tempo up
- Record with a bass guitar backing track to check whether the kick genuinely locks with the bass line
- Resist filling every gap — the discipline of leaving space is the defining characteristic of this style

## Teaching Points

Kevin Talley's style combines extreme blast technique — gravity blasting and two-handed matched blasting — with deep, pocketed groove. Key practice principles across all his licks: prioritise loose grip and rebound efficiency over active muscle force; build non-dominant hand consistency before adding double bass; and practise leaving space in groove sections as an active skill, not a default.
