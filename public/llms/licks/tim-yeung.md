## Bleed the Fifth Blast Beat Pattern

**Song:** Bleed the Fifth | **Album:** Bleed the Fifth (2007) | **BPM:** ~200 BPM | **Technique:** blast beat | **Difficulty:** expert

Tim Yeung's blast beat from Divine Heresy's debut title track is driven by clean single strokes between snare and ride at ~200 BPM, keeping each limb articulate rather than blurred into continuous noise. The kick locks in tight alternating double bass directly under the blast to add density without losing clarity. Snare stroke weight must remain consistent across long blast passages — evenness outranks maximum power at this tempo, and individual weak strokes stand out clearly even at speed.

### How to Play

- Drive the blast with clean single strokes between snare and ride, keeping each limb articulate rather than blurred
- Lock the kick in tight alternating double bass directly under the blast to add density without losing clarity
- Keep snare stroke weight consistent across long blast passages — evenness is more important than maximum power

### Key Elements

- Start blast practice at 100 BPM with a click — play for two minutes before raising tempo to build endurance first
- Record yourself and listen for snare evenness; individual weak strokes show up clearly in recordings even at full speed
- Practise the kick separately at blast tempo to verify both feet are producing identical tone before combining with hands

## Inferno of Violence Double Bass Drive

**Song:** Inferno of Violence | **Album:** Bleed the Fifth (2007) | **BPM:** ~195 BPM | **Technique:** double bass | **Difficulty:** expert

The double bass drive section from this Divine Heresy track operates at ~195 BPM with heel-up ankle-driven strokes throughout — using the full leg at this tempo produces fatigue and unevenness within bars. Pedal spring tension must allow fast rebound without excessive resistance that tightens the ankle joint. The left foot is typically the weaker element at high tempo, and left-right tone matching is the primary technical challenge — any tone imbalance between feet creates an uneven kick texture that undermines the track's density.

### How to Play

- Play heel-up with ankle-driven strokes — using the full leg at this tempo produces fatigue and unevenness within bars
- Set pedal spring tension to allow fast rebound without excessive resistance that tightens the ankle joint
- Keep both feet producing identical tone — left foot weakness at high tempo is the most common failure point

### Key Elements

- Practise feet-alone at 120 BPM for five minutes before raising tempo — build ankle stamina first
- Record the feet with a kick mic and compare left and right foot tone; match them before adding speed
- Loop eight-bar double bass passages rather than one-bar bursts to build the endurance the song demands

## Anarchists of the Underground Speed Groove

**Song:** Anarchists of the Underground | **Album:** Bleed the Fifth (2007) | **BPM:** ~185 BPM | **Technique:** speed groove | **Difficulty:** expert

This track from Bleed the Fifth demonstrates Yeung's ability to create a groove feel at extreme death metal tempo rather than pure blasting. The ride accent placement — rather than a static hi-hat — is what gives the pattern its forward momentum. Kick fires in riff-mirroring bursts to produce a groove feel rather than density-filling, and ghost notes remain compressed but present even at ~185 BPM — they are the first element to disappear when technique degrades under fatigue.

### How to Play

- Drive the groove from the ride rather than a static hi-hat — changing the ride accent placement is what gives the pattern its forward momentum
- Fire kick in riff-mirroring bursts rather than continuous double bass to produce a groove feel rather than density
- Keep ghost notes compressed but present at extreme tempo — they disappear first when technique degrades under fatigue

### Key Elements

- Learn the ride accent pattern in isolation before adding kick and snare — the upper-hand texture defines whether the pattern feels like groove or blast
- Practise the kick-riff mirroring by listening to the guitar track alone and mapping which attack points the kick follows
- Slow the full groove to 120 BPM and listen for ghost note audibility — only raise tempo once ghost notes remain present and consistent

## Teaching Points

Tim Yeung's style combines sustained extreme-tempo blasting with a groove orientation that keeps his playing musical rather than purely mechanical. Key practice principles across all his licks: develop ankle-driven heel-up double bass before raising tempo; build blast endurance through timed sets rather than maximum-tempo bursts; and prioritise ghost note presence as a technique quality indicator before increasing speed.
