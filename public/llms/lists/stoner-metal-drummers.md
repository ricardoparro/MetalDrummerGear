## Overview

The percussionists behind metal's fuzziest, most hypnotic riff-worship. Stoner metal grew out of the early-1990s Palm Desert scene in Southern California, where Kyuss fused Black Sabbath's blues-rock doom with hardcore energy and a maximally fuzzed-out, down-tuned guitar tone that became the genre's signature. Sleep pushed the template toward maximalist repetition on "Dopesmoker" (2003), Electric Wizard darkened it into its heaviest, most drone-adjacent extreme on "Dopethrone" (2000), and Windhand and High on Fire carried the sound into the 2000s and 2010s. Stoner metal drumming prioritizes hypnotic, trance-inducing repetition over technical display — locking into a groove and riding it, often across extended, meditative song structures, letting the riff's tonnage and the drummer's steady, blues-rock-informed swing do the work rather than speed or complexity.

Kyuss's Brant Bjork, Sleep's Chris Hakius, and Electric Wizard's rotating drummers do not currently have dedicated profiles in MetalForge's database, so these ten drummers are drawn from the progressive sludge, groove metal, and crushing extreme metal lineages that share stoner metal's foundational commitment to fuzzed-out repetition, blues-rock groove, and riff-driven power — Mastodon's Brann Dailor chief among them for his sludge-adjacent pedigree. They represent the closest working analogues to stoner metal's hypnotic, tone-worshipping rhythmic philosophy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Who are the best stoner metal drummers?**
A: Kyuss's Brant Bjork, whose loose, behind-the-beat groove on "Blues for the Red Sun" (1992) helped invent stoner metal's Palm Desert sound, and Sleep's Chris Hakius, whose extended, hypnotic repetition on "Dopesmoker" defined the genre's maximalist wing, do not currently have dedicated drummer profiles in MetalForge's database. Brann Dailor of Mastodon is the closest working analogue currently profiled, his sludge-adjacent pedigree sharing stoner metal's riff-worshipping foundation. Igor Cavalera of Sepultura and Vinnie Paul of Pantera follow closely for their own fuzzed-out, groove-first rhythmic philosophies.

**Q: What is stoner metal?**
A: Stoner metal fuses Black Sabbath's blues-rock doom with hardcore energy and a maximally fuzzed-out, down-tuned guitar tone, growing out of the early-1990s Palm Desert scene in Southern California where Kyuss and Fu Manchu built the genre's foundational sound. Sleep pushed the template toward maximalist repetition on "Dopesmoker" (2003), Electric Wizard darkened it into its heaviest extreme on "Dopethrone" (2000), and Windhand and High on Fire carried stoner metal's hypnotic, riff-worshipping sound into the 2000s and 2010s.

**Q: What makes stoner metal drumming unique?**
A: Stoner metal drumming prioritizes hypnotic, trance-inducing repetition over technical display — locking into a single groove and riding it, often across extended, meditative song structures built around a riff's tonnage rather than a drummer's speed or complexity. Where thrash and death metal drumming push tempo and technicality, stoner metal drumming borrows Black Sabbath's blues-rock swing and slows it further, using patient, steady pocket-playing to let fuzzed-out tone and repetition build a trance-like heaviness.

**Q: What bands define stoner metal?**
A: Kyuss is universally credited as stoner metal's founding band, with "Blues for the Red Sun" (1992) establishing the Palm Desert scene's fuzzed-out, down-tuned template. Sleep's "Dopesmoker" (2003) pushed the genre toward maximalist, one-riff repetition, while Electric Wizard's "Dopethrone" (2000) darkened stoner metal into its heaviest, most drone-adjacent wing. Fu Manchu and Fatso Jetson rounded out the Palm Desert scene, while Windhand and High on Fire — the latter formed by ex-Sleep guitarist Matt Pike — carried the genre's hypnotic riff-worship into later decades.
