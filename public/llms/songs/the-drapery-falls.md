## Technique Notes

"The Drapery Falls" from Opeth's Blackwater Park (2001) is often cited as Martin Lopez's most comprehensive showcase on the album — a nine-minute journey that takes the listener from hushed acoustic folk through brooding atmospheric passages and into crushing metal, all without ever losing the sense of a single, sustained musical idea. What Lopez does on this track — and what makes it such an important study — is demonstrate that a metal drummer does not have to choose between the extremes of loud and quiet. He operates in the entire dynamic range simultaneously, adjusting his weight and touch measure by measure as the arrangement demands, from nearly inaudible acoustic playing to full-power metal drumming and back again, all within the same performance.

The acoustic and semi-acoustic passages in "The Drapery Falls" are among the most delicate Lopez recorded with Opeth. His approach in these sections is closer to a jazz drummer accompanying a ballad than a metal player: brushstroke-light hi-hat, ghost notes that float rather than drive, snare hits with just enough weight to define the beat without breaking the mood. Then, when the band shifts into a heavier riff, Lopez shifts with them — the kick becomes more assertive, the snare cracks rather than whispers, and the groove suddenly has real mass behind it. The extraordinary quality of the performance is how seamless these transitions are. There is no abrupt gear-change, no moment where the groove feels inconsistent; he finds a way to move from one extreme to the other that feels as natural as breathing.

This track also demonstrates Lopez's approach to fills and decoration in a progressive context. Unlike many metal drummers who use fills as opportunities to show speed and power, his fills in "The Drapery Falls" function as phrase-endings — punctuation marks that close one section and open another. They are always musically placed, often landing right before a major structural change, and always proportional to the dynamics of the moment: a delicate fill in a quiet passage, a more powerful one when the full band is driving. This compositional awareness is the hallmark of a genuinely musical drummer.

For students, "The Drapery Falls" is an advanced study in dynamic control, acoustic-to-electric drumming transitions, and compositional fill placement. The official audio on Opeth's VEVO channel preserves the original studio recording's dynamic detail, making it possible to study exactly how Lopez adjusts his touch across the song's many contrasting passages. Learning even a portion of it teaches you to hear the difference between levels of musical pressure and to understand how a drum part can serve a progressive metal epic without dominating it or merely providing timekeeping. It is one of the most musically ambitious drum performances in heavy metal and remains a benchmark for anyone interested in playing with both power and genuine finesse.
