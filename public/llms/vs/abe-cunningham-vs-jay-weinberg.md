## Style & Technique Comparison

Abe Cunningham co-founded Deftones in Sacramento in 1988 as a teenager and has anchored the band's atmospheric alt-metal sound for over three decades, driving landmark records including the Grammy-winning "White Pony" (2000), "Diamond Eyes" (2010), and "Ohms" (2020) with a style built on dynamic restraint rather than raw aggression. His playing favors unconventional patterns and seamless transitions between crushing heaviness and ethereal delicacy — influenced by John Bonham's pocket, Stewart Copeland's unconventional patterns, and 311's Chad Sexton's syncopated funk-metal feel — prioritizing Deftones' emotional arc over technical showmanship.

Jay Weinberg joined Slipknot in 2014 at age 24, stepping into one of extreme metal's most scrutinized drum chairs following founding drummer Joey Jordison's departure, and delivered arena-scale power across three studio albums before parting ways in November 2023 and later joining Suicidal Tendencies. He blends the professional discipline he absorbed from his father, E Street Band drummer Max Weinberg, with hardcore-punk-honed intensity from his time in Against Me! and Madball, delivering sustained blast beats that had to authentically replicate Jordison's iconic parts while injecting his own explosive energy.

## Key Differences

The core divergence is philosophy: Cunningham builds Deftones' heaviness through space, dynamics, and atmosphere, while Weinberg built Slipknot's heaviness through sustained, unrelenting aggression and technical precision inherited from an already-iconic drum chair.

Gear tells a coincidental story of shared taste — both drummers built their sound on SJC Custom Drums kits driven by DW 9000 Series double pedals, despite playing in bands with almost opposite sonic philosophies. Cunningham's brass SJC snare and Zildjian K Custom cymbals are voiced for warmth and nuance; Weinberg's 48-ply brass "Crucible" snare and Zildjian A New Beat/K Custom mix are voiced for maximum cut and arena-scale sustain.

## Influence & Legacy

Cunningham helped define an entire alternative metal subgenre — his creative, non-traditional approach to drumming, drawing from post-punk and alt-rock rather than standard metal vocabulary, influenced countless nu-metal and alt-metal drummers who followed.

Weinberg carried one of metal's most scrutinized drum chairs for nine years, earning respect from fans who initially doubted anyone could replace Jordison, and built a following that carried into his post-Slipknot work with Suicidal Tendencies.

## Verdict

Abe Cunningham and Jay Weinberg represent opposite poles of heavy music's emotional range, and — coincidentally — both built their sound on SJC Custom Drums kits driven by DW 9000 Series double pedals. Cunningham spent 35-plus years proving that restraint, space, and atmosphere can be just as heavy as raw power, anchoring Deftones' genre-defining blend of crushing riffs and shoegaze textures. Weinberg spent nine years bringing arena-scale extreme metal intensity to one of the most scrutinized drum chairs in metal, honoring Joey Jordison's legacy while injecting his own hardcore-punk energy. Asking "what makes Deftones vs Slipknot drumming different?" comes down to atmospheric heaviness versus aggressive arena power.
