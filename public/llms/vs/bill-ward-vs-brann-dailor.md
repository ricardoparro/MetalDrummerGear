## Playing Style

Bill Ward co-founded Black Sabbath in 1968 and, alongside Ozzy Osbourne, Tony Iommi, and Geezer Butler, helped invent heavy metal's rhythmic vocabulary across the band's classic Ozzy-era albums, including "Black Sabbath" (1970), "Paranoid" (1970), and "Master of Reality" (1971). Brann Dailor, born March 19, 1975, in Rochester, New York, has driven Atlanta progressive sludge band Mastodon since 2000, developing a melodic, almost lead-instrument approach to drumming — flowing tom patterns and constant motion that weave through the music as countermelodies on albums like "Crack the Skye" (2009) and "Emperor of Sand" (2017), while also handling substantial lead vocal duties.

## Technique

Ward's technique is rooted in jazz — a swinging, open-handed feel inherited from Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich, played entirely on a single Ludwig Speed King pedal with no double bass, that prioritized groove and dynamics over speed and gave early Black Sabbath riffs their loose, hard-swinging heaviness. Dailor treats the kit as a singing instrument rather than a timekeeper — his toms are tuned in musical intervals to create actual melodies on songs like "Oblivion," and his modest single 22"x18" bass drum relies on creative placement and groove rather than raw speed, all while he sings complex vocal harmonies over some of metal's most technically demanding drum parts.

## Influence & Legacy

Ward is widely credited as one of the true originators of heavy metal drumming, and his playing on Black Sabbath's first six albums remains the foundational reference point the entire genre was built on. Dailor helped transform Mastodon from Atlanta sludge upstarts into one of progressive metal's most critically acclaimed and commercially successful acts, and his rare combination of technically demanding drumming with lead vocal duties — recognized with multiple Grammy nominations — has made him one of the genre's most distinctive modern voices.

## Verdict

Bill Ward and Brann Dailor sit at opposite ends of a direct generational lineage from Black Sabbath's doom-laden birth of heavy metal to Mastodon's progressive sludge evolution. Ward invented the genre's foundational jazz-swing vocabulary with Black Sabbath starting in 1968, prioritizing feel and groove over speed on a single-pedal kit. Dailor, drumming for Mastodon since 2000, pushed that same instinct for musicality further than almost anyone, turning his kit into a melodic, singing instrument while handling lead vocals simultaneously. Both prove that heavy metal's power has always come from feel and musicality first — Ward founded that idea, and Dailor carried it into prog-sludge's most ambitious modern form.
