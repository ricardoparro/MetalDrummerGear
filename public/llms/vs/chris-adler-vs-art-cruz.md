## Playing Style

Chris Adler co-founded Lamb of God (as Burn the Priest) in Richmond, Virginia in 1994 and spent 25 years as the band's drummer, inventing a groove-forward technical style that helped define the New Wave of American Heavy Metal on "Ashes of the Wake" (2004) and "Sacrament" (2006) before departing in 2019. Art Cruz, born in Downey, California, spent over a decade in deathcore and thrash with Winds of Plague and Prong before filling in for an injured Adler on Lamb of God's 2018 tour and being named the band's permanent drummer in 2019, debuting on the self-titled "Lamb of God" (2020) and "Omens" (2022).

## Technique

Adler's signature is his ride-bell-anchored groove — riding the bell with his right hand instead of the hi-hat while his left foot works independently for dynamic accents, all locked to syncopated double-bass patterns that gave Lamb of God its mechanical, riff-tight momentum. Cruz brings a blast-beat foundation and raw physical attack from his deathcore roots, executing Adler's notoriously complex back catalog live while adding heavier, more overt power to Lamb of God's newer, dynamically broader material.

## Influence & Legacy

Adler's ride-bell technique and syncopated kick vocabulary became foundational to groove metal, studied by a generation of heavy drummers, and his 2015 stint with Megadeth on the Grammy-winning "Dystopia" extended his reach into thrash. Cruz's influence is still being written, but inheriting and faithfully executing one of modern metal's most technically demanding chairs — while adding his own physicality — has already made him a respected case study in how to handle a high-profile drumming succession.

## Verdict

This is less a rivalry than a generational handoff. Chris Adler spent 25 years building Lamb of God's groove-metal identity from first principles, inventing a ride-bell-driven vocabulary that became a genre benchmark. Art Cruz inherited that famously difficult chair, learned the catalog under pressure, and has kept one of metal's hardest-working bands performing at full strength for two studio albums and counting. Adler is the innovator; Cruz is the guy who proved the songs could survive — and grow — without him.
