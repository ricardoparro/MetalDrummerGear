## Playing Style

Chris Adler co-founded Lamb of God in 1994 and spent 25 years defining its drumming identity — a precision-groove approach built on syncopated kick patterns locked to riffs, showcased across "New American Gospel" (2000), "Ashes of the Wake" (2004), and "Sacrament" (2006). Art Cruz joined in 2019 after filling in during Adler's tour absences, bringing a deathcore and thrash background from Winds of Plague and Prong into the same chair, first heard on Lamb of God's 2020 self-titled album and "Omens" (2022).

## Technique

Adler's signature is ride-bell-driven groove: riding the bell rather than the hi-hat while syncopated double bass churns underneath, producing the mechanical, riff-locked momentum that defined groove metal's New Wave of American Heavy Metal era. Cruz plays with more overt physical power and a blast-beat foundation from his deathcore roots, favoring front-of-the-beat attack that reinforces Lamb of God's heavier, more dynamic post-2020 material while still executing Adler's technically demanding back catalog faithfully.

## Influence & Legacy

Adler's ride-bell technique, innovative kick patterning, and Mapex/Meinl endorsements shaped a generation of groove and heavy metal drummers, and his tenure extended to a Grammy-winning stint with Megadeth on "Dystopia" (2016). Cruz inherited one of modern metal's most technically demanding drum chairs and made it his own, proving on "Omens" and "Illegals in Heaven" (2024) that Lamb of God's drumming standard continues at the highest level under new hands.

## Verdict

This is a generational handoff, not a rivalry. Chris Adler built Lamb of God's entire drumming vocabulary from first principles over two decades, setting groove metal's technical benchmark. Art Cruz stepped into that chair in 2019, honored the legacy, and added his own deathcore-forged power and aggression. Adler is the innovator who wrote the language; Cruz is the drummer proving it still hits as hard as ever.
