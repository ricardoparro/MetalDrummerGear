## Playing Style

Flo Mounier has been the only constant member of Canadian technical death metal pioneers Cryptopsy since joining in 1992, setting new standards for extreme metal drumming on landmark albums like "None So Vile" (1996) and "Whisper Supremacy" (1998) with sustained blast beats at 280+ BPM, complex polyrhythmic fills, and inhuman stamina. Bill Ward co-founded Black Sabbath in 1968 and, alongside Ozzy Osbourne, Tony Iommi, and Geezer Butler, helped invent heavy metal's rhythmic vocabulary across the band's classic Ozzy-era albums, including "Paranoid" (1970), "Master of Reality" (1971), and "Sabbath Bloody Sabbath" (1973).

## Technique

Mounier is widely regarded as one of the most technically proficient drummers in death metal, bringing jazz influences from Buddy Rich and Dave Weckl into extreme metal — his gravity-blast technique produces sustained speed with musical dynamics and ghost notes rather than pure brute force. Ward's technique is rooted in jazz as well, but pointed in the opposite direction — a swinging, open-handed feel inherited from Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich that prioritized groove and dynamics over speed, giving early Black Sabbath riffs their loose, hard-swinging heaviness. Both drummers channel jazz vocabulary into heavy music, but Mounier pushes it toward maximum velocity while Ward uses it to invent a slower, riff-locked pulse.

## Influence & Legacy

Mounier's "None So Vile" is regularly cited as one of the greatest extreme metal drum recordings of all time, and his teaching work directly influences thousands of death metal drummers worldwide through clinics conducted across three decades. Ward's jazz-informed swing and dynamic sensibility created the rhythmic vocabulary an entire genre was built on, and his playing on Black Sabbath's first six albums remains the foundational reference point every subsequent generation of metal drummers, including technical extremists like Mounier, ultimately builds on.

## Verdict

Flo Mounier and Bill Ward represent heavy music's rhythmic evolution from opposite ends of a five-decade timeline. Ward invented the genre's founding swing in 1968 with a single Ludwig Speed King pedal and a loose, jazz-rooted groove that gave Black Sabbath's earliest riffs their weight. Mounier took that same jazz lineage — by his own influences, Buddy Rich among them — and pushed it toward technical death metal's absolute physical limits, sustaining 280+ BPM blast beats on Cryptopsy's "None So Vile." Ward proved heavy metal could be built on feel; Mounier proved that same feel could survive extreme velocity.
