## Playing Style

Flo Mounier has been the only constant member of Canadian technical death metal pioneers Cryptopsy since joining in 1992, setting new standards for extreme metal drumming on landmark albums like "None So Vile" (1996) and "Whisper Supremacy" (1998) with sustained blast beats at 280+ BPM, complex polyrhythmic fills, and inhuman stamina. Bill Ward co-founded Black Sabbath in Birmingham in 1968 and, alongside Tony Iommi, Geezer Butler, and Ozzy Osbourne, invented heavy metal's rhythmic vocabulary across the band's classic Ozzy-era catalog, including "Paranoid" (1970), "Master of Reality" (1971), and "Sabotage" (1975).

## Technique

Mounier is widely regarded as one of the most technically proficient drummers in death metal, bringing jazz influences from Buddy Rich and Dave Weckl into extreme metal — his gravity-blast technique produces sustained speed with musical dynamics and ghost notes rather than pure brute force. Ward's technique is also jazz-rooted, inherited from Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich — a swinging, open-handed feel that prioritized groove and dynamics over speed, giving early Sabbath riffs their loose, hard-swinging heaviness. Both trace their playing back to jazz vocabulary, but Mounier channels it into extreme velocity while Ward channels it into foundational, mid-tempo groove.

## Influence & Legacy

Mounier's "None So Vile" is regularly cited as one of the greatest extreme metal drum recordings of all time, and his teaching work directly influences thousands of death metal drummers worldwide through clinics conducted across three decades. Ward's jazz-informed swing and dynamic sensibility created the rhythmic vocabulary an entire genre was built on, and his playing across Black Sabbath's first six albums remains the foundational reference point for heavy metal drumming, earning him a 2006 Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction.

## Verdict

Flo Mounier and Bill Ward represent two extremes of heavy music's jazz-informed lineage, separated by nearly three decades and an entire genre's evolution. Ward invented heavy metal's rhythmic vocabulary through loose, swinging feel in 1968, prioritizing groove over speed at every turn. Mounier took that same jazz foundation — Buddy Rich by way of Dave Weckl — and pushed it to technical death metal's absolute physical limits with Cryptopsy. Both prove metal drumming's deepest roots run through jazz, whether expressed as foundational groove or extreme velocity.
