## Style & Technique Comparison

Gene Hoglan, nicknamed "The Atomic Clock" for his metronomic precision, has played across Dark Angel, Death, Testament, Fear Factory, Strapping Young Lad, and Dethklok since the mid-1980s, driving extreme-tempo thrash and technical death metal with a combination of speed, power, and musicality. Dark Angel's "Leave Scars" (1989) (see [Leave Scars drum setup](https://metalforge.io/articles/leave-scars-drum-setup)) pushed tempos as high as 220 BPM years before that speed became commonplace in extreme metal.

Pete Sandoval, born in El Salvador in 1960, co-founded Terrorizer before joining Morbid Angel in 1988, where he pioneered and perfected the gravity blast technique on genre-defining albums like "Altars of Madness" (1989), "Blessed Are the Sick" (1991) (see [Blessed Are the Sick drum setup](https://metalforge.io/articles/blessed-are-the-sick-drum-setup)), and "Covenant" (1993) (see [Covenant drum setup](https://metalforge.io/articles/covenant-drum-setup)), setting the standard for death metal blast-beat drumming.

---

## Key Differences

The core divergence is metronomic versatility versus raw speed specialization. Hoglan's technique is built on inhuman timing and stamina — sustained double-bass endurance across marathon sets and varied genres, favoring metronomic consistency over raw top-end velocity. Sandoval's technique is centered on pure blast-beat speed — his gravity-assisted stick rebound achieved blast tempos that were, at the time, faster than nearly any other drummer in death metal.

Gear reflects each drummer's priorities. Hoglan's Pearl Reference kit and full-brass snare are voiced for tonal clarity across a wide range of tempos and genres. Sandoval's ddrum Dios kit and Mercury double pedal were built specifically for sustained blast-beat velocity.

---

## Influence & Legacy

Hoglan influenced progressive death metal and technical thrash across four decades and more than 20 bands, with his combination of speed, power, and musicality earning him a reputation as one of extreme metal's most respected and versatile drummers.

Sandoval's gravity blast innovation on "Altars of Madness" and "Blessed Are the Sick" essentially defined death metal's blast-beat vocabulary, cited as a direct influence by generations of extreme metal drummers who chased the speed and intensity he pioneered.

---

## Verdict

Gene Hoglan and Pete Sandoval represent extreme metal's two defining poles of speed drumming. Hoglan is "The Atomic Clock" — precision, stamina, and musicality across an unmatched breadth of bands and styles over four decades. Sandoval is the gravity blast pioneer — pure, relentless blast-beat velocity that set the tempo ceiling for death metal drumming in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Both answer "who plays faster?" from opposite ends of the same extreme metal speed lineage.

## FAQ

**Q: Who plays faster, Gene Hoglan or Pete Sandoval?**
A: Pete Sandoval's gravity blast technique, pioneered on Morbid Angel's "Altars of Madness" (1989) and "Blessed Are the Sick" (1991), is generally considered the faster pure blast-beat player, using gravity-assisted stick rebound to push tempos beyond what most contemporaries could sustain. Gene Hoglan is renowned more for metronomic precision and stamina across marathon sets and varied tempos — earning his "Atomic Clock" nickname — than for outright top-end blast speed, though Dark Angel's "Leave Scars" (1989) hit 220 BPM years ahead of the curve.

**Q: What is each drummer's signature technique?**
A: Gene Hoglan's signature is metronomic timing and stamina across extreme tempos and varied genres, plus his own gravity blast variations, which earned him the nickname "The Atomic Clock." Pete Sandoval's signature is the gravity blast — a gravity-assisted blast beat technique he pioneered and perfected with Morbid Angel that became a foundational death metal blast-beat vocabulary.

**Q: Who has influenced more drummers, Gene Hoglan or Pete Sandoval?**
A: Both are hugely influential, but in different ways. Gene Hoglan's versatility across Dark Angel, Death, Testament, and Dethklok influenced technical thrash and progressive death metal drummers seeking his blend of speed and musicality. Pete Sandoval's gravity blast on Morbid Angel's early albums directly shaped death metal's blast-beat vocabulary and is cited by generations of extreme metal drummers as foundational.
