## Playing Style

Hellhammer (born Jan Axel Blomberg, 1969) has driven Norwegian black metal since joining Mayhem in 1988, and his cold, blast-beat-driven performance on "De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas" (1994) remains the genre's defining recording; he has run a parallel career with Dimmu Borgir since 1999, bringing black metal aggression into symphonic arrangements. Bill Ward co-founded Black Sabbath in 1968 and, alongside Ozzy Osbourne, Tony Iommi, and Geezer Butler, helped invent heavy metal's rhythmic vocabulary across the band's classic Ozzy-era albums, including "Paranoid" (1970), "Master of Reality" (1971), and "Sabbath Bloody Sabbath" (1973).

## Technique

Hellhammer's technique is built around cold, relentless blast beats delivered with icy one-foot precision that became the template for second-wave black metal drumming — speed and repetition in service of atmosphere and aggression rather than technical display. Ward's technique is rooted in jazz — a swinging, open-handed feel inherited from Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich that prioritized groove and dynamics over speed, giving early Black Sabbath riffs like "Iron Man" and "Children of the Grave" their loose, hard-swinging heaviness. Where Hellhammer's blasts are a chaotic blizzard built for extremity, Ward's grooves are a patient, riff-locked pulse built for feel — two founding vocabularies for two entirely different eras of heavy music.

## Key Differences

Hellhammer's classic "De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas" rig was a developing-endorsement Pearl 5-piece shell pack with a Pearl 14x5.5" steel snare tuned bright for maximum crack, paired with Zildjian A Series cymbals; his modern rig has expanded to Pearl Reference and Pearl Masters Premium series kits for his Dimmu Borgir symphonic work. Bill Ward played a Ludwig Standard/Club Date kit (a 22" bass drum on "Master of Reality," growing to 24" with a second floor tom by "Sabbath Bloody Sabbath") with a Ludwig Acrolite 14x5" snare (later the deeper-shelled Ludwig Supraphonic 14x6.5" LM402) and Avedis Zildjian cymbals (14" Hi-Hats, 16"-18" Crashes, 20" Ride), later switching to Paiste 2002 & Giant Beat Series cymbals, driven by a Ludwig Speed King single pedal and Pro-Mark 5A/5B sticks. ## Influence & Legacy

Hellhammer defined the sound and speed vocabulary of second-wave black metal, and his three-decade-plus dual career with Mayhem and Dimmu Borgir has made him the genre's most in-demand extreme drummer for both raw and symphonic contexts. Ward's jazz-informed swing and dynamic sensibility created the rhythmic vocabulary an entire genre was built on, and his playing on Black Sabbath's first six albums remains the foundational reference point for heavy metal drumming — the common ancestor that black metal's blast-beat lineage, including Hellhammer's own playing, ultimately descends from.

## Verdict

Hellhammer and Bill Ward sit at opposite ends of heavy metal's 50-plus-year timeline, yet each defined the rhythmic vocabulary of his era. Ward invented the genre's founding swing in 1968, giving Black Sabbath's earliest riffs their loose, hard-hitting groove — a single-pedal, jazz-rooted approach built on feel rather than force. Hellhammer, arriving two decades later, pushed that lineage toward its coldest, fastest extreme, turning Mayhem's black metal into a benchmark for blast-beat precision. Ward built the foundation heavy metal drumming stands on; Hellhammer built one of its most extreme modern wings.
