## Playing Style

Hellhammer (born Jan Axel Blomberg, 1969) has anchored Norwegian black metal since joining Mayhem in 1988, and his cold, blast-beat-driven performance on "De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas" (1994) remains second-wave black metal's defining recording; he has also driven Dimmu Borgir's symphonic black metal since 1999, alongside stints in Arcturus and Shining. Bill Ward co-founded Black Sabbath in Birmingham in 1968 and, alongside Tony Iommi, Geezer Butler, and Ozzy Osbourne, invented heavy metal's rhythmic vocabulary across the band's classic Ozzy-era catalog, including "Paranoid" (1970), "Master of Reality" (1971), and "Sabotage" (1975).

## Technique

Hellhammer's technique is built around cold, relentless one-foot blast beats delivered with icy precision — speed deployed in service of black metal's atmosphere and aggression rather than technical display. Ward's technique is rooted in jazz, inherited from Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich — a swinging, open-handed feel (he famously crossed his right hand over to the hi-hat on a right-handed kit) that prioritized groove and dynamics over speed, giving early Sabbath riffs like "Iron Man" and "War Pigs" their loose, hard-swinging heaviness. Where Hellhammer chases pure velocity, Ward chases feel — two founding approaches to heavy music built on entirely different physical vocabularies.

## Key Differences

Hellhammer's classic "De Mysteriis Dom Sathanas" rig was a developing-endorsement Pearl 5-piece shell pack (22"x16" kick, 10"x9" and 12"x10" racks, 16"x16" floor tom) with a Pearl 14"x5.5" steel snare tuned bright for crack and Zildjian A Series cymbals, since expanded into Pearl Reference and Masters Premium series kits for his Dimmu Borgir symphonic work, driven by a Pearl Eliminator double pedal and Vic Firth American Classic 5B sticks. ## Influence & Legacy

Hellhammer defined black metal's blast-beat vocabulary and has remained the genre's most in-demand extreme drummer across a 35-plus-year, two-band career spanning Mayhem and Dimmu Borgir. Ward's jazz-informed swing and dynamic sensibility created the rhythmic vocabulary an entire genre was built on, and his playing across Black Sabbath's first six albums remains the foundational reference point for heavy metal drumming, earning him a 2006 Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction.

## Verdict

Hellhammer and Bill Ward sit at opposite ends of heavy metal's five-decade evolution, yet both are genre-founding figures whose playing still shapes how their instrument is taught. Ward invented the genre's rhythmic vocabulary in 1968 through jazz-trained swing and feel; Hellhammer took heavy music to its most extreme, atmosphere-driven speed a generation later in Norway's black metal scene. One built the foundation, the other pushed the ceiling — together they frame just how far heavy metal drumming has traveled.
