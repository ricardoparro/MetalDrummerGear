## Playing Style

Jaska Raatikainen co-founded Children of Bodom in 1993 and spent 26 years driving the Finnish band's signature blend of neoclassical melodies and melodic death metal, anchoring dense keyboard-and-guitar arrangements from "Something Wild" (1997) through the band's 2019 farewell. Bill Ward co-founded Black Sabbath in 1968 and, drawing on jazz heroes Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich, built an open, swinging feel that became the rhythmic template for heavy metal itself on "Black Sabbath" (1970) and "Paranoid" (1970).

## Technique

Raatikainen built his technique around clean, locked-in double bass and precise, driving blast beats, keeping Bodom's dense arrangements anchored with surgical consistency at extreme tempos. Ward's loose grip and behind-the-beat phrasing gave early Sabbath riffs a rolling, jazz-informed swing that no other rock drummer of the era was playing, relying entirely on single-pedal technique with no double bass at all.

## Influence & Legacy

Raatikainen gave Finnish melodic death metal its rhythmic identity, helping Children of Bodom become one of the genre's most globally successful acts and influencing a generation of Scandinavian metal drummers. Ward is widely credited as one of the true originators of heavy metal drumming — every extreme metal drummer who followed, including Raatikainen, built on the rhythmic vocabulary he created with Sabbath.

## Verdict

Jaska Raatikainen and Bill Ward sit at opposite ends of heavy metal drumming's five-decade evolution. Ward invented the jazz-swung, single-pedal foundation of heavy metal drumming in 1968, prioritizing feel and space over speed. Raatikainen took that lineage to its technical extreme, driving Children of Bodom's blast-beat-and-double-bass melodic death metal with surgical precision for 26 years. Both remain foundational voices in their eras — one wrote metal drumming's first chapter, the other pushed it toward its most demanding modern form.
