## Playing Style

Jaska Raatikainen co-founded Children of Bodom in 1993 alongside the late Alexi Laiho and remained the Finnish melodic death metal band's sole drummer for its entire 26-year run, anchoring dense keyboard-and-guitar arrangements from "Something Wild" (1997) through "Hatebreeder" (1999) and "Follow the Reaper" (2000) to the band's 2019 farewell "Hexed." John Otto has been Limp Bizkit's drummer and founding member since 1994, blending hip-hop-influenced grooves and funk-inspired beats that helped define the rap-metal sound on hits like "Rollin'" and "Break Stuff."

## Technique

Raatikainen built his technique around clean, locked-in double bass and precise, driving blast beats, keeping Bodom's dense arrangements anchored with surgical consistency at extreme tempos, composing fills that support Alexi Laiho's guitar and Janne Wirman's keyboard interplay rather than overwhelming it. Otto studied jazz at the Douglas Anderson School of the Arts, using syncopated patterns and funky ghost notes over a single-pedal setup to give Limp Bizkit's grooves their distinctive pocket rather than chasing extreme-metal speed.

## Key Differences

Raatikainen played a Pearl Masters Premium Maple kit with a Pearl Masters 14x5.5" Maple snare and Zildjian A Custom & K Custom Series cymbals (14" A Custom Hi-Hats, 17" & 18" A Custom Crashes, 20" K Custom Ride, 18" China), powered by a Pearl Eliminator double pedal and Vic Firth American Classic 5A sticks. ## Influence & Legacy

Raatikainen helped build Finland's melodic death metal scene into a global commercial force — Children of Bodom sold over two million albums worldwide, and his playing on "Hatebreeder" and "Follow the Reaper" influenced a generation of melodic death and metalcore drummers. Otto's jazz-meets-hip-hop grooves gave Limp Bizkit's sound instant recognizability, helping drive one of the best-selling records of the nu-metal era in "Chocolate Starfish and the Hot Dog Flavored Water."

## Verdict

Jaska Raatikainen and John Otto show just how differently precision and groove can define heavy music drumming. Raatikainen spent 26 unbroken years giving Children of Bodom's neoclassical melodic death metal a surgically consistent, high-velocity backbone. Otto's jazz-and-funk-schooled pocket made Limp Bizkit's rap-metal commercially unstoppable through disciplined, song-serving restraint rather than extreme-metal speed. Both are foundational figures in their respective scenes, operating at opposite ends of metal's tempo and technicality spectrum.
