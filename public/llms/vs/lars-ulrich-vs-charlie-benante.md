## Playing Style

Lars Ulrich co-founded Metallica in 1981 and helped invent the thrash metal template across "Kill 'Em All" (1983), "Ride the Lightning" (1984), and "Master of Puppets" (1986) — records that prioritized groove-driven power and mid-tempo stomp over raw velocity, letting Metallica's songwriting reach mainstream audiences no other thrash band matched. Charlie Benante joined Anthrax in 1983 and has appeared on all 11 of the band's studio albums, bringing a harder, hardcore-punk-informed intensity across "Spreading the Disease" (1985), "Among the Living" (1987), and "Persistence of Time" (1990) — records that pushed thrash's rhythmic vocabulary toward faster, more technical territory than Metallica ever attempted.

## Technique

Ulrich's technique favors feel and songwriting over speed — his mid-tempo stomps, syncopated snare placement, and instantly recognizable fills prioritized what served Metallica's riffs rather than technical display, anchoring some of metal's most enduring arrangements. Benante is widely regarded as the more technically advanced player: he pioneered the thrash "skank beat," developed blistering double bass patterns years ahead of most peers, and is also a left-handed drummer who plays a right-handed kit — a configuration that forced him to develop unconventional limb independence.

## Influence & Legacy

Lars Ulrich's influence is inseparable from Metallica's rise from Bay Area underground thrash to the best-selling metal band in history — regardless of technical debates, his feel and groove sensibility shaped how millions of fans and drummers understand metal drumming, and he became the first Danish musician inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. Charlie Benante influenced thrash's technical and hardcore-crossover wings, and his stature within the Big Four extended further in 2022 when he joined the reunited Pantera, filling the drum chair of his late friend Vinnie Paul — a testament to the respect he commands across metal's biggest stages.

## Verdict

Lars Ulrich and Charlie Benante represent the Big Four's two defining approaches to thrash drumming. Ulrich prioritized songwriting feel and groove, driving Metallica to become the genre's most commercially successful act. Benante prioritized technical velocity and hardcore-informed aggression, pushing Anthrax and thrash itself toward faster, more demanding territory. The "best Big Four drummer" debate ultimately comes down to what you value more: Ulrich's stadium-filling instinct for what a riff needs, or Benante's technical ceiling and speed.
