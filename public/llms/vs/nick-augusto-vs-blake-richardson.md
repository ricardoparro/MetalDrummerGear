## Playing Style

Nick Augusto joined Trivium in 2010, stepping in after Travis Smith's exit and bringing a thrash-rooted background from grindcore act Maruta and Metal Militia to one of modern metalcore's most demanding drum chairs. His two studio albums with the band, "In Waves" (2011) and "Vengeance Falls" (2013), showcased a powerful, speed-driven style that pushed Trivium toward heavier territory before he departed in 2014. Blake Richardson has held down Between the Buried and Me's drum seat since joining in early 2005, replacing Jason Roe and becoming central to the band's genre-defining evolution across landmark, Grammy-nominated records including "Colors" (2007), "The Great Misdirect" (2009), and "Colors II" (2023).

## Technique

Augusto's technique channels his grindcore and thrash roots into aggressive, high-tempo double-bass patterns and tight rhythmic precision, giving Trivium's "In Waves" era a speed and intensity distinct from the band's more melodic earlier records. Richardson's technique fuses death metal precision with jazz influences — complex blast beats, constantly shifting odd time signatures, and fill-heavy, melodically-conceived patterns inspired by Terry Bozzio, Dennis Chambers, and Tomas Haake, built to serve BTBAM's genre-hopping compositions rather than showcase raw speed alone.

## Influence & Legacy

Augusto's four years in Trivium produced two of the band's most commercially successful records — "In Waves" debuted at #13 on the Billboard 200 and "Vengeance Falls" reached #15 — establishing him as a respected name in 2010s metalcore before he moved on to Corrosion and Light the Torch. Richardson's two-decade tenure with Between the Buried and Me has made him one of the most creative and technically proficient drummers in modern progressive metal, earning Grammy nomination recognition and a reputation for serving the song first even amid extreme technical complexity.

## Verdict

Nick Augusto and Blake Richardson represent two different eras and philosophies of modern technical metal drumming. Augusto's Trivium tenure was a compact, high-velocity burst — thrash- and grindcore-informed aggression applied to metalcore songcraft across two commercially significant albums. Richardson has spent nearly two decades building a body of work that treats the drum kit as a compositional voice, blending death metal blast beats with jazz-informed melodicism across some of progressive metal's most celebrated records. The debate is metalcore intensity versus progressive-metal sophistication — both are among the most respected technical drummers to emerge from 2000s and 2010s modern metal.
