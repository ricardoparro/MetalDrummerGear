## Playing Style

Ray Luzier joined [Korn](/articles/korn-iii-drum-setup) in 2007, replacing founding drummer David Silveria and bringing rock-solid grooves with technical flair to the band's nu-metal foundation across "Korn III: Remember Who You Are" (2010), "The Path of Totality" (2011), and "The Paradigm Shift" (2013). Scott Travis joined [Judas Priest](/articles/painkiller-drum-setup) in 1989, replacing Dave Holland for the landmark "Painkiller" album — bringing an American double-bass-driven, thrash-influenced style that revitalized British heavy metal's biggest institution and has kept him in the band for over three decades.

## Technique

Luzier combines a sought-after session musician's versatility (built from years working with David Lee Roth and Army of Anyone) with the syncopated pocket-playing Korn's dense, detuned riffs demand — locking tightly with bassist Fieldy to hold down the band's signature groove. Travis is defined by relentless double bass precision and speed — his performance on "Painkiller"'s title track remains one of heavy metal's most celebrated drumming feats, a machine-like display that established the modern American power-drumming template within a classic British metal band.

## Verdict

Ray Luzier and Scott Travis are both technically accomplished replacement drummers who didn't just fill a seat — they outlasted the era they were hired to bridge. Luzier brought session-musician versatility and syncopated groove to Korn's nu-metal foundation, becoming the band's longest-tenured drummer. Travis brought thrash-influenced double bass power to Judas Priest's classic heavy metal, delivering one of the genre's most celebrated performances on "Painkiller" and remaining behind the kit for over three decades. Both prove that a replacement drummer can become the definitive one.
