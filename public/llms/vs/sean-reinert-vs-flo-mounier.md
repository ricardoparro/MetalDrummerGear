## Playing Style

Sean Reinert (1971-2020) helped invent technical death metal as a genre — his work on Death's "Human" (1991) and Cynic's "Focus" (1993) introduced jazz-fusion harmony and dynamic sensitivity into a scene still defining itself around raw brutality. Flo Mounier joined Cryptopsy in 1992 and became the band's only constant member, building a reputation as one of extreme metal's fastest and most technically proficient drummers through blast beats and gravity-blast innovations heard on "None So Vile" (1996) and "Whisper Supremacy" (1998).

## Technique

Reinert's jazz training gave him traditional-grip ghost notes, brush-like dynamic control, and polymetric phrasing that let him move between brutal intensity and jazz-informed delicacy within the same passage — a vocabulary almost unheard of in death metal at the time. Mounier's technique is built on raw speed and endurance — his gravity blasts and complex polyrhythmic patterns pushed blast-beat technique to new extremes, while his incorporation of jazz and fusion elements adds musicality without sacrificing Cryptopsy's trademark brutality.

## Influence & Legacy

Reinert's work on "Human" and "Focus" proved jazz vocabulary and extreme metal intensity were not mutually exclusive, laying a foundation the entire technical death metal scene built upon for three decades before his death in January 2020. Mounier has anchored Cryptopsy's catalog from "Ungentle Exhumation" (1993) through "As Gomorrah Burns" (2023), influencing countless death metal drummers with his blast-beat innovations and releasing instructional DVDs that codified his techniques for the next generation.

## Verdict

Sean Reinert and Flo Mounier are two of technical death metal's founding architects, arriving at the genre's cutting edge from opposite directions in the same early-1990s window. Reinert brought jazz sophistication and dynamic nuance that expanded what death metal could sound like emotionally. Mounier brought relentless speed and gravity-blast innovation that pushed the genre's physical ceiling. Together they represent the two poles — musicality and velocity — that define technical death metal drumming to this day.
