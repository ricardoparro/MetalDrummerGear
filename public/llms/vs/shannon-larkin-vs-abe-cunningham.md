## Style & Technique Comparison

Shannon Larkin, born in 1967 in Chicago, joined Godsmack in 2002 after stints with Wrathchild America, Ugly Kid Joe, and Amen, driving the band's thunderous hard rock machine across six studio albums including "Faceless" (2003), "IV" (2006), and "The Oracle" (2010) until his 2024 departure. His playing is defined by powerful, hook-driven rhythms that lock in with Tony Rombola's guitars, backed by seasoned dynamic control built for arena-scale hard rock shows.

Abe Cunningham co-founded Deftones in Sacramento, California in 1988 as a teenager and has remained the band's drummer for over three decades, developing an unconventional, atmospheric style that helped define alternative metal across "Around the Fur" (1997), "White Pony" (2000), and "Diamond Eyes" (2010). Rather than following typical metal drumming conventions, Cunningham creates parts that breathe and evolve with the music, drawing on influences like John Bonham, Stewart Copeland, and Chad Sexton to favor creative, unconventional patterns over technical showmanship.

Both drummers prioritize feel over speed — but Larkin's feel is muscular, groove-first arena power, while Cunningham's feel is dynamic, texture-first atmosphere.

## Key Differences

The clearest difference is musical context. Godsmack is a straightforward hard rock band built around riff-driven hooks, and Larkin's job is to lock in with that groove and deliver consistent, powerful impact night after night. Deftones layer heavy riffs with shoegaze-influenced atmosphere and dynamic shifts, and Cunningham's job is to serve that emotional arc — sometimes with restraint, sometimes with explosive heaviness, often within the same song.

Gear reflects the difference: Larkin's Pearl Reference Pure with bright Sabian AAX cymbals is voiced for cutting through arena mixes at consistent intensity. Cunningham's Tama Starclassic with a mixed Zildjian A/K Custom setup is voiced for tonal flexibility — bright enough for aggression, dark enough for atmosphere.

Career arcs also diverge: Larkin was a late addition to Godsmack (2002, replacing Tommy Stewart) who brought outside veteran experience to an established band. Cunningham has been in Deftones since its 1988 founding, making him one of alternative metal's longest-serving original drummers.

## Influence & Legacy

Shannon Larkin helped maintain Godsmack's commercial momentum through multiple platinum records, becoming one of the most recognizable faces in modern hard rock drumming across more than two decades behind the kit before his 2024 departure.

Abe Cunningham is regularly cited by younger alternative metal drummers as a primary influence — his dynamic, feel-first approach on tracks like "Digital Bath" and "Change (In the House of Flies)" proved that restraint and texture could be as powerful as raw aggression, and his Grammy-winning work on "Elite" (from White Pony) remains a genre landmark.

## Verdict

Shannon Larkin and Abe Cunningham represent two paths through legacy-era alternative and hard rock drumming. Larkin is the groove authority — a veteran session-honed player whose powerful, hook-driven rhythms gave Godsmack an unshakeable arena rock foundation for over two decades. Cunningham is the texture specialist — a founding member whose unconventional, dynamics-first playing has been Deftones' heartbeat since the band formed in a Sacramento classroom in 1988. Comparing them is comparing "groove metal" muscle to "alt-metal" atmosphere — feel over speed, in two very different flavors.
